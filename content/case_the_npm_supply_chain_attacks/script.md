NARRATOR: On the morning of the eighth of September, twenty twenty-five, a software developer in Europe opened his email and found a message from the people who run the world's largest collection of free, reusable code. The node package manager. Everyone in the JavaScript world just calls it the N-P-M registry. The message said his account security was out of date. Refresh your two-factor authentication, it said, or your account gets locked in a few days. There was a link. The page it opened looked exactly like the real login screen. He typed his username. He typed his password. And then, because the site asked for it, he read the six-digit code off his authenticator app — the one-time number that's supposed to be the one thing a thief can never have — and typed that in too.

NARRATOR: It took the attacker on the other end maybe a minute to use all three. And in that minute, they didn't break into one developer's account. They got their hands on a tiny corner of the internet that, it turns out, almost everything is quietly standing on. By that afternoon, code written by these attackers was landing on developers' machines around the world at more than two billion downloads a week, added up across the packages they'd seized. Two billion. From one phishing email.

NARRATOR: This is the story of that day — and of something stranger that crawled out of the same ecosystem a week later, malware that didn't need a human to spread it at all. But to see how a single stolen login could reach two billion downloads, you have to understand a strange, invisible feature of how modern software actually gets built. So let me take you back seven years first, to the first time the world got a proper warning. Almost nobody listened.

QUESTION: How much of the code in the app on your phone do you think was actually written by the company whose name is on it?

NARRATOR: The honest answer, for most modern software, is a small fraction. Maybe a few percent. The rest is borrowed. A developer building something today doesn't write everything from scratch — that would take years. They reach for free, open-source building blocks other people have already written and shared. Need to print coloured text in a terminal? Somebody solved that; you pull in their little package. Need to handle dates, or parse a web address, or strip formatting codes out of a string? There's a package for it. You type one command, and it arrives. And here's the part that matters: each of those packages was built using other packages, which were built using still other packages, down and down. Install one thing you chose on purpose, and you might pull in a thousand things you never chose and never read. Layers of code, strangers trusting strangers, all the way down. The whole chain has a name: the software supply chain. Most developers, most of the time, never think about it. Which is exactly the problem.

NARRATOR: Twenty eighteen. There was a small, popular package — a humble utility for moving data through a program in a tidy stream. It had been written years earlier by a developer named Dominic Tarr, and like a thousand other little open-source projects, it had quietly become load-bearing. Millions of downloads. Depended on by serious software. And its author had moved on. He'd written it for fun, for free, years before; he didn't use it anymore, didn't get paid for it, and was tired of being on the hook for it. So when a friendly stranger turned up online and offered to help maintain it, Dominic did what an exhausted volunteer does. He said yes. He handed the keys over — the right to publish updates, under his package's trusted name, to everyone who used it.

NARRATOR: For a while, nothing happened. The new maintainer did some real work, built up trust. And then, quietly, he added a new dependency — a little package underneath — that contained hidden, encrypted, malicious code. It was surgical. It didn't attack everyone. It woke up only inside one specific other application: a bitcoin wallet. On machines running that wallet, it tried to steal the keys to people's cryptocurrency. The whole thing sat there, downloaded eight million times, for over two months before anyone noticed. And it was noticed by accident — the attacker had used a slightly old, deprecated way of decrypting their payload, that triggered a stray warning message in an unrelated tool, and one curious developer pulled on that thread until it all unravelled.

NARRATOR: That was the warning shot. The lesson sitting right there in twenty eighteen: the weakest point in all this borrowed code isn't the code. It's the human being who holds the keys to publish it. Tired, unpaid, trusting. Hand those keys to the wrong person — or have them stolen — and you don't compromise one app, you compromise everyone downstream who ever typed "install". The industry nodded, said "yes, supply-chain risk, very serious", and went back to typing "install".

NARRATOR: Now come back to September twenty twenty-five, and meet the maintainer who got that phishing email. In the JavaScript world he's known by a handle — Qix, spelled Q-I-X. And he is not some hobbyist with one obscure package. He maintains a set of the most-used building blocks in the entire ecosystem. Tiny ones. Unglamorous ones. The kind nobody thinks about. There's one called chalk, whose entire job is to make text in a terminal show up in colours. There's debug, a little logging helper. There's strip-ansi, and ansi-styles, and color-convert, and supports-color, and wrap-ansi — a family of microscopic utilities that each do one boring thing. None of them is exciting. None is something you'd brag about depending on. And every one of them is buried, invisibly, in the foundations of enormous swathes of the web — pulled in not because developers chose them, but because the things they did choose depend on them, several layers down.

NARRATOR: Chalk alone gets downloaded around three hundred million times a week. Across the full set of packages this one maintainer controlled, the combined reach was more than two billion downloads every single week. So when the attacker captured his username, his password, and that live one-time code, and logged in as him, they weren't standing in front of one door. They were standing in front of the supply room the whole house gets its supplies from.

NARRATOR: And they moved fast. In the early afternoon, U-T-C time, they published brand-new versions of around eighteen of these packages. From the outside, the versions looked completely normal — a routine update, the kind that lands in projects automatically all day long. But tucked inside was new code nobody asked for. And this payload was clever about where it did its damage. It wasn't trying to wreck your computer. It was built to wake up inside a web browser, sit there silently, and watch for one specific thing: cryptocurrency.

QUESTION: If you were going to steal cryptocurrency from people, but you could only run a little bit of code inside their web browser, how would you actually do it?

NARRATOR: You wouldn't smash and grab. You'd be patient and quiet. This malware hooked itself into the part of the browser that talks to crypto wallets — the bridge a wallet like MetaMask uses to let a website request a payment. And it watched the traffic flowing across that bridge. When it saw a cryptocurrency transaction being set up, it did one tiny, devastating thing: it swapped the destination. You think you're sending money to the address you carefully copied. The screen even shows the right-looking address. But in the half-second between you confirming and the transaction going out, the malware quietly rewrites the recipient to a wallet the attackers control. Your money leaves. It just doesn't go where you think. That pattern has a name — a crypto-clipper, or a wallet-drainer: code that sits between you and your money and silently changes the address. The same idea the attacker reached for in twenty eighteen, except now it was riding inside not one obscure package but eighteen of the most widely installed packages on earth.

NARRATOR: So picture the blast radius. Every developer who ran an install or a build that afternoon, on a project that depended on chalk or debug or any of the others — even four or five layers down, even though they'd never heard of half these packages — could have pulled the poisoned version straight into their software. And shipped it to their users. The reach was genuinely unprecedented. Security researchers who've watched this space for decades said they'd never seen a single compromise positioned to touch so much of the internet at once.

NARRATOR: And then comes the twist that makes this story almost reassuring — and, a second later, much more frightening. The community caught it fast. Within roughly two hours, other developers spotted the strange code, raised the alarm, and the maintainer — mortified, very publicly owning his mistake — pulled the malicious versions and pushed clean ones. The poisoned window slammed shut. And because of that speed, the actual theft was tiny. Reports put what the attackers drained at a few hundred dollars. A few hundred dollars. From an attack that, for two hours, had its hands on two billion downloads a week.

NARRATOR: Sit with the gap between those two numbers. The damage was small purely because they got caught quickly. The reach — the potential — was almost limitless. They didn't fail because the defences were good. They "failed" because they were lucky enough to be noticed. Change one thing — make the payload quieter, make it wait a few days before activating, make it not announce itself with weird browser behaviour — and the same login, the same eighteen packages, the same two billion downloads, could have been a catastrophe far worse than hundreds of dollars. The phishing email worked perfectly. The only thing that saved everyone was that the worst of it got spotted before it could ripen.

NARRATOR: Now. If that were the whole story, the lesson would be: be careful, train people not to click, the system mostly self-corrects. But it isn't the whole story. Because barely a week later, something else appeared in the same ecosystem — and it didn't need a phishing email at all. It didn't need a human clicking publish. It published itself.

NARRATOR: It got a name, borrowed from science fiction: Shai-Hulud. The great sandworm from Dune. And the name fit, because this thing burrowed. It started life, like the others, as malicious code hidden inside an npm package. But this code had a routine that ran automatically the moment the package was installed — a little script that fires on installation, which is a completely normal, legitimate feature packages use to set themselves up. Except this one's setup job was to go hunting. The moment it landed on a developer's machine, it started searching for secrets. Not files, not photos — credentials. The access tokens and keys developers keep lying around so their tools can log in automatically. Their npm publishing token. Their account tokens for code hosting. Their keys to cloud services. To do the hunting, it even pulled down a real, well-known, open-source secret-scanning tool — the kind security teams use to find leaked credentials before the bad guys do — and turned it on its victim. Using the defenders' own tool to rob them.

NARRATOR: It scooped up whatever secrets it found and shipped them off — quietly creating a new public code repository under the victim's own account and dumping the stolen credentials there, lightly disguised, for the attacker to collect. That alone is bad. But it's the next step that put this in a different category entirely. Remember the npm publishing token it just stole, the one that lets a developer publish updates? The worm used it. Automatically, no human involved, it logged in as the victim, found every other package that victim was allowed to publish, and injected a copy of itself into each one. Then it published those poisoned versions to the world.

QUESTION: Do you see what just happened? Walk it through one more time, slowly.

NARRATOR: A developer installs an infected package. The worm wakes up, steals that developer's publishing keys, and uses them to infect every other package that developer maintains. Now those are poisoned too. So the next developer who installs one of them — maybe someone who maintains ten more popular packages — gets infected, gets their keys stolen, and infects their ten. And so on. Each victim becomes a launch pad for the next wave, automatically, without anyone at a keyboard deciding anything. That is the definition of a worm: software that copies itself from host to host on its own. We've had worms for decades — but they spread through networks and operating systems. This one spread through the supply chain. Through trust. Through the very mechanism the whole ecosystem uses to share useful code. In its first day, it jumped to a couple of hundred packages. A later, more aggressive version, a couple of months on, took over the better part of eight hundred. A self-propagating supply-chain worm. That had never really existed at this scale before.

NARRATOR: And that's the leap I want you to feel. The September phishing attack was scary because of how far one stolen human login could reach. The worm is scary because there's no human to catch. You can train people not to click suspicious emails. You can't train a thousand machines not to run an install script. A human attacker has to pick targets, do work, take risks, and can be spotted and stopped — like Qix was, in two hours. A worm just runs. It works while everyone's asleep. It doesn't get tired, doesn't get greedy in a way that tips its hand, doesn't need a phishing email to land. It needs one infected package and an ecosystem built on automatic trust, and it does the rest itself. That's a genuinely new and worse class of threat, and it came from the same soft spot the eighteen packages did: the supply chain.

NARRATOR: So step back from the day-to-day drama, because there's a bigger shape here — the part that matters long after the specific package names are forgotten.

NARRATOR: Both attacks — the phishing and the worm — are the same kind of attack in two different costumes. Neither broke in by finding a clever bug. Nobody discovered a flaw in chalk; the code did exactly what it was written to do. They attacked the supply chain instead — the long, mostly invisible chain of trust that delivers code from the person who wrote it to the millions of machines that run it. They poisoned the well rather than attacking each person who drinks from it. And if that pattern sounds familiar, it should: it's the same family as the attack that nearly slipped a backdoor into a core piece of the Linux ecosystem around the same era — where, instead of a phished login or an automated worm, a patient attacker spent months as a helpful volunteer, earning a maintainer's trust by hand, the slow human con. Different doorway in. Same house. The supply chain itself has become the target, because it's the highest-leverage place to strike: compromise one upstream link and you compromise everyone downstream at once.

NARRATOR: And once you see it that way, the weak points stop being mysterious. What actually failed in September wasn't a firewall and it wasn't an algorithm. It was a person's login, and the fact that his way of proving who he was could be stolen wholesale in a single phishing form. He had two-factor authentication — on top of your password, you confirm with a second factor, like a code from your phone. It's good. It stops most attacks. But the kind he had could be phished: the fake site simply asked for the live code and relayed it on. There's a stronger, phishing-resistant kind of multi-factor login — one tied to the real website's identity, so a fake look-alike page literally can't use what you give it — and on an account controlling two billion downloads a week, its absence was the whole ballgame. The lesson the industry keeps relearning: a maintainer's account is a single point of failure for everyone downstream, and how that one human proves who they are — their authentication — is therefore everyone's problem, not just theirs.

NARRATOR: Then there's the part that's about all of us, not just the maintainers. Every project any of us builds pulls in a sprawling tree of code we've never read — dependencies of dependencies of dependencies, the deep nested layers the worm rode through, the layers that quietly carried chalk into projects that had never heard of it. We trust all of it, implicitly, every time we type install. That tree is enormous and almost nobody audits it. Which means the obvious-in-hindsight defences are the boring-sounding ones. Pin your dependencies — lock them to specific, known versions, so a malicious new release isn't pulled in automatically just because it's newer. Use the integrity checks the tooling already offers — cryptographic fingerprints that refuse to install a package if its contents don't match what you locked, catching a swapped-out version at the door. Actually review what you're adding, and lean on provenance — verifiable evidence of where a package really came from and who really built it — rather than trusting a name and a version number. None of that is glamorous. All of it is the difference between a poisoned package sliding silently into your build and getting stopped cold.

NARRATOR: And maybe the deepest point, the one the syllabus would put plainly: your dependencies are part of your software. Not an afterthought, not someone else's problem — part of the thing you're building and responsible for maintaining over its whole life. Choosing them, locking them, checking them, updating them carefully and watching what those updates bring in — that's not optional hygiene bolted on at the end. It's a core part of building and maintaining software, every bit as much as the code you type yourself. The two billion downloads didn't reach so far because the attackers were brilliant. They reached so far because, for everyone downstream, those eighteen tiny packages were treated as furniture — always there, never examined, completely trusted. Trust at that scale, unexamined, isn't a convenience. It's the attack surface.

NARRATOR: One developer, one tired moment, one convincing email. Eighteen packages nobody thinks about. Two billion downloads a week. And a week later, a worm that didn't even need the developer — just the trust. That's what a software supply-chain attack looks like now. The code was never the weak link. The chain was.
