NARRATOR: Somewhere in the world, in the autumn of twenty eighteen, there is a person who owns a fortune in Bitcoin. We don't know their name. We don't know which country they're in. We only know that they hold at least a hundred Bitcoin in a wallet on their computer — at the prices of the time, well over half a million dollars — and that, without ever clicking a bad link or opening a suspicious attachment, without doing a single thing wrong, they were minutes from having every last coin silently drained out of that wallet and sent to a stranger they would never meet. The thief was not in their house. The thief was not on their network. The thief had reached this person through a chain of trust so long, and so ordinary, and so invisible, that nobody in the entire chain — not one single human being — had any idea they were part of a robbery. To understand how that's even possible, we have to start somewhere that seems impossibly far away from a Bitcoin wallet. We have to start with a tired man giving away something for free, because he was kind, and because he was exhausted, and because that is simply how this whole world is built.

NARRATOR: This is the story of the software supply chain — the most important part of modern software that almost nobody outside the industry has ever heard of. And it's the story of how, over and over, the same soft spot got hit in the same way, by very different people for very different reasons, until the whole industry had to admit that the thing it had been quietly standing on this entire time was never as solid as it looked.

QUESTION: How much of the code in the app on your phone do you think was actually written by the company whose name is on it?

NARRATOR: The honest answer, for nearly all modern software, is a small fraction. Maybe a few percent. The rest is borrowed. A developer building something today does not write everything from scratch — that would take years, and frankly nobody would bother. Instead they reach for free, open-source building blocks that other people have already written and shared with the world. Do you need to print coloured text in a terminal window? Somebody already solved that years ago; you pull in their little package and you're done in seconds. Need to handle dates, or read a web address apart into its pieces, or pad a number out with leading zeros? There is a package for it. You type one short command, and it arrives on your machine, and it just works. This is the great gift of open source, and it is genuinely one of the most beautiful arrangements in all of technology — millions of strangers, freely sharing the solutions to small problems so that nobody ever has to solve the same small problem twice.

NARRATOR: But here is the part that matters, the part that turns the gift into a loaded gun. Each of those packages you pull in was itself built using other packages, which were built using still more packages, down and down. You install one single thing you chose on purpose, that you read about and decided you wanted — and behind it, invisibly, comes a tree of hundreds, sometimes thousands, of other packages you never chose, never heard of, and will certainly never read. Strangers trusting strangers trusting strangers, all the way to the bottom. The whole structure has a name. It is called the software supply chain. And the uncomfortable truth of it is this: when you install a package, you are not just trusting the person who wrote it. You are trusting every person who wrote every package underneath it, and every person those people decided to trust, forever, automatically, usually without ever knowing their names. Most developers never think about this at all. Which is exactly the problem.

NARRATOR: The first time the world really felt the ground move under that chain was in March of twenty sixteen, and it had nothing to do with a hacker. It was a fight. There was a programmer named Azer Koçulu — a prolific, well-liked open-source developer who had published hundreds of little packages to the main JavaScript registry, the place everyone calls N-P-M, the node package manager. One of his packages happened to be named "kik." And a company — the messaging app also called Kik — wanted that name for themselves. There was a trademark dispute. Lawyers got involved. And when the people who run N-P-M sided with the company and took the name away from him and handed it over, Azer was furious. He felt the whole open-source bargain had been betrayed — that the work he'd given away for free could just be confiscated. So he made a decision that, in hindsight, reads like a small act of rebellion that detonated like a bomb. He unpublished everything. Every single package he had ever put on N-P-M — all two hundred and seventy-three of them — he pulled down, all at once, deleting them from the registry the entire world depended on.

NARRATOR: And among those two hundred and seventy-three packages was one called left-pad. Left-pad is eleven lines of code. Eleven. Its entire job in life is to take a piece of text and add spaces or zeros to the front of it until it's a certain length. That's it. That's the whole thing. It is so trivial that any first-week programmer could write it in a couple of minutes without thinking. And yet, because it was sitting right there, free, already written, already tested, thousands upon thousands of projects had simply reached out and pulled it in rather than write their own three-line version. And not small projects, either. Major pieces of software depended on it — the Babel tool that nearly the entire JavaScript world uses to translate modern code, the React framework that runs huge swathes of the web, parts of Node itself. And underneath those, everything that depended on them. Facebook. PayPal. Netflix. Spotify. The moment left-pad vanished from the registry, builds across the entire internet started failing. Developers all over the planet, who had never heard of left-pad, who had certainly never typed its name, suddenly found their software refusing to build, throwing the same baffling error — a four-oh-four, file not found — because eleven lines of code, four or five layers down a dependency tree they'd never looked at, had simply ceased to exist.

NARRATOR: It was chaos. And it was fixed in the least elegant way imaginable: the people running N-P-M took the extraordinary step of un-deleting the package — restoring left-pad against its own author's wishes, within a couple of hours, just to stop the bleeding. And afterwards they changed the rules so you could no longer pull down a package, once it had been up for a day, if other software relied on it. The hole got patched. But the lesson underneath it did not really land. Because everyone told the story as a comedy — ha, the internet broke because one guy deleted eleven lines of code. They laughed at the absurdity and missed the actual point. The point was not that the package was tiny. It was that the entire towering structure of modern software was resting, without anyone really noticing, on thousands of tiny, unguarded pieces, each one held in place by a single individual who could do whatever they wanted with it. Azer Koçulu broke the internet by accident, out of anger. The obvious next question — the one almost nobody asked out loud in twenty sixteen — was: what happens when someone does it on purpose?

NARRATOR: Two years later, we found out.

NARRATOR: To tell you what happened, I have to introduce you to a man named Dominic Tarr. And I want to be very clear before I do, because this matters: Dominic Tarr is not a villain in this story. He is, if anything, one of its quiet heroes — and also, through no real fault of his own, the hole the whole attack walked through. Dominic was a well-respected, genuinely beloved figure in the open-source JavaScript community. He'd written a lot of code, given a lot of it away, helped a lot of people. And among the many packages he maintained was one called event-stream. Event-stream is a utility — a tidy little tool for moving data through a program in a stream, piece by piece, instead of all at once. Useful, unglamorous plumbing. The kind of thing serious software quietly relies on. And by twenty eighteen, event-stream had quietly become load-bearing. It was being downloaded around two million times a week. It sat, invisibly, underneath thousands of other packages, which sat underneath thousands more.

NARRATOR: But here's the human truth at the centre of it. Dominic Tarr had written event-stream years earlier, for fun, to learn something, in his spare time. He didn't get paid for it. Not a cent. He didn't even use it anymore — hadn't, for years. He'd moved on with his life, the way people do. And yet, because thousands of strangers had built their software on top of his old hobby project, he was still, technically, on the hook for it. Every bug report, every feature request, every "hey, can you fix this" landed in his lap, for a thing he no longer cared about and got nothing from. If you have ever maintained anything popular and free, you know exactly how that feels. It is a slow, thankless weight that never lets up. He was, in his own words, getting literally nothing from it.

NARRATOR: So in twenty eighteen, when a stranger turned up online and offered to help — someone using the handle right-nine-control, written as r-i-g-h-t, nine, c-t-r-l — and said, in effect, hey, I'd like to maintain this, I'll take some of this off your hands, Dominic did the most natural, most human, most reasonable thing in the world. He said yes. Later, when the whole thing had blown up and people were demanding to know how this could have happened, Dominic explained it with a plainness that's almost painful. He said: "He emailed me and said he wanted to maintain the module, so I gave it to him. I don't get anything from maintaining this module, and I don't even use it anymore, and haven't for years." That's it. That's the entire breach. A tired volunteer, handed a way to put down a burden he never asked to carry, took it. And in taking it, he handed a complete stranger the publishing keys to a package that two million machines pulled down every week.

QUESTION: Be honest — if you were exhausted, unpaid, and someone genuinely seemed to want to help with a project you didn't even use anymore, what would you have done?

NARRATOR: You'd have said yes. Almost anyone would. And that's the whole horror of it. Nothing Dominic did was foolish. Handing over commit and publishing access to a willing contributor was, at the time, completely normal — it's how open source had always worked, how it spread the load, how exhausted maintainers survived. He had no way to know. The trap was built precisely out of his decency. And right-nine-control understood that perfectly, because for a while, they played the part flawlessly. They didn't immediately do anything bad. They did real work first. They made genuine, useful contributions — improving the package, building up a track record, earning trust the slow and patient way. They wanted to look, for as long as it took, exactly like the helpful volunteer Dominic believed he'd found.

NARRATOR: And then, once the keys were truly theirs, they made their move. In September of twenty eighteen, right-nine-control added a new dependency to event-stream — a small package underneath it, called flatmap-stream. Now on the surface, this was nothing. Adding a dependency is the most ordinary thing a maintainer does. It happens thousands of times a day across the ecosystem. flatmap-stream did a real, plausible job; it looked like an honest little helper package. And because event-stream now depended on it, every single one of those two million weekly downloads of event-stream quietly pulled flatmap-stream down too, automatically, no questions asked. The attacker hadn't compromised event-stream's code directly, where Dominic or anyone else might glance at it. They'd done something sneakier. They'd hidden the weapon one layer deeper — in a brand-new package that almost nobody would ever think to inspect, that simply came along for the ride.

QUESTION: So if anyone can read open-source code, and millions of people are downloading this thing, how do you hide a robbery inside it without someone spotting it?

NARRATOR: This is where the craft becomes genuinely chilling, because the attacker assumed people might look — and built something designed to give them nothing to see. The malicious code in flatmap-stream was not sitting there in plain, readable form. It was encrypted. Hidden away inside what looked like an innocent test file — a bit of sample data, the boring stuff every project ships, the stuff nobody ever reads. And it was locked with proper, serious cryptography — A-E-S two-fifty-six, the same family of encryption that protects real secrets. So even if a curious developer pulled the package apart and stared straight at the payload, they would see only an unreadable wall of scrambled bytes. Meaningless noise. Nothing to raise an alarm.

NARRATOR: But encryption has a problem, if you're the attacker. To run your hidden code, you have to decrypt it. And to decrypt it, you need the key. So where do you hide the key to your weapon, on the victim's own machine, in a way that doesn't give the whole game away? This is the part that, when you understand it, genuinely takes the breath away. The attacker did not ship the key at all. Instead, they wrote the malicious code so that it would build its own decryption key out of something on the victim's computer — specifically, out of a single line of text that would only exist on the exact machine they wanted to rob. They used, as the key, the description field from a particular project — the short human-readable sentence that every package carries to say what it is. And the project they were hunting for had a description that read: "A Secure Bitcoin Wallet."

NARRATOR: Let that sink in. The weapon could only unlock itself on a computer where that precise phrase was present. On your machine, on my machine, on the machines of the millions of ordinary developers who pulled event-stream down every week, the key simply wasn't there. The encrypted payload sat inert, locked, harmless, unreadable — a sealed box with no key in sight. The malware deliberately did nothing, for almost everyone, almost all the time. It was waiting. It was hunting for one specific kind of victim, and it would ignore the entire rest of the planet until it found them.

QUESTION: So who, exactly, had "A Secure Bitcoin Wallet" written on their machine?

NARRATOR: The developers of a product called Copay. Copay was a real, well-known, open-source Bitcoin wallet, made by a company called BitPay. And this is the masterstroke of the whole operation, the thing that lifts it from a clever hack into something genuinely dark. The attacker was not trying to rob the developers of Copay. The developers were just the doorway. The attacker was using the Copay project as the key to unlock code that would then poison Copay itself — so that the poisoned version of the wallet would be built, packaged up, signed, and shipped out to Copay's actual users. Real people. People who would download a wallet from a reputable company, install it, trust it, and put their Bitcoin in it — having no idea that buried in its foundations, several layers down a dependency tree nobody had fully read, was a piece of code lying in wait for them.

NARRATOR: And here is what that code did, once it finally woke up inside a Copay build. It quietly reached into the part of the wallet that handles your cryptographic keys — the secret numbers that prove the coins are yours and let you spend them. The attackers had written it to specifically tamper with the function that fetches those keys, swapping in their own malicious version that copied the keys out and sent them off to a server the attackers controlled, at an address out on the internet. But they were careful — surgically, almost paranoidly careful. The code only armed itself on the real Bitcoin network, never on the test networks developers use for safe experiments, so it wouldn't trip during testing. It only ran during the specific commands used to build a real, shippable release. And it didn't bother with small fish. It was written to harvest the keys only from wallets holding more than a hundred Bitcoin, or more than a thousand Bitcoin Cash — going after the genuine whales, the people with fortunes, and ignoring everyone else so as not to draw attention by causing a flood of small thefts.

NARRATOR: Every layer of that was a deliberate choice, designed to keep the thing invisible as long as possible. And it worked. The malicious flatmap-stream sat in the supply chain, riding inside event-stream, pulled down something like eight million times over roughly two and a half months — and for that entire stretch, nobody noticed.

NARRATOR: So how was it finally caught? Not by a security company. Not by N-P-M. Not by any of the giant corporations standing downstream. It was caught by a student. By chance. There was a computer science student at California State University, Fullerton named Ayrton Sparling — online he went by the handle FallingSnow. And the thread that unravelled the whole thing was almost comically small. A different developer had earlier noticed that some tool in the ecosystem was throwing a strange little warning — a complaint that the code was using an old, deprecated, out-of-fashion way of doing cryptography. Just a warning message. The kind of thing you'd normally shrug off. But it was odd. Why would this code be doing cryptography at all? Ayrton Sparling pulled on that loose thread. He traced the odd behaviour down into flatmap-stream, looked hard at what was actually in there, realised what he was staring at, and in November of twenty eighteen he opened an issue on the event-stream project — issue number one hundred and sixteen — essentially shouting: there is malicious code in here, this package has been compromised, look at this.

NARRATOR: And then it all came apart very fast. Once people knew where to look, the encrypted payload, the Copay targeting, the key-stealing — all of it was decoded and laid bare within days. N-P-M's security team was notified, and on the twenty-sixth of November twenty eighteen they removed the malicious package and took control of event-stream away from the attacker. Copay confirmed that the poison had indeed made it into several of their released versions and rushed out clean ones, urging anyone affected to move their funds. The crisis was, in the immediate sense, over. The attacker — right-nine-control, whoever they really were — vanished. The account went dark. To this day, nobody has been publicly, definitively identified as the person behind it. They built trust for months, struck once, surgically, at the richest crypto wallets in the world, and walked away into the dark.

NARRATOR: And Dominic Tarr — the man whose only crime was being a kind, tired volunteer — was left holding it. He wrote a public statement afterwards, and it's worth sitting with what he said, because it's the real lesson of the whole affair stated more honestly than any security report. He pointed out that he was an unpaid volunteer, that he got nothing from this, that giving publish access to people who offered to help was simply how the community had always worked. And then he put his finger on the genuine wound underneath it all. We are, he said, in a strange situation where huge amounts of critical software are maintained by people who've lost interest, or are burning out, or don't even use the thing anymore — because, in his words, you can easily share the code, but nobody wants to share the responsibility for maintaining it. That is the sentence the whole industry needed to hear. The weakest point in the supply chain was never the code. It was the human being holding the keys — tired, unpaid, and trusting.

NARRATOR: Now, you might think a near-miss like that — eight million downloads, a fortune almost stolen, a beloved maintainer publicly humiliated — would have permanently changed how the world treated this risk. It didn't. Or rather, it changed it slowly, while the attacks kept coming, each one a fresh variation on the exact same theme. Because the lesson of event-stream is not really about Bitcoin, or about that one attacker. The lesson is about the shape of the weak spot. And once you can see the shape, you start seeing it everywhere.

NARRATOR: Take what happened in October of twenty twenty-one, to a package called ua-parser-js. Its job is to look at the little identifying string a web browser sends about itself and work out what browser, what operating system, what device it is. Dull, useful, everywhere. Nearly eight million downloads a week, with well over a thousand other packages depending on it. And one day, the developer who maintained it, a man named Faisal Salman, discovered to his horror that his N-P-M account had been hijacked. Somebody had gotten control of his login — and there's good reason to think the account had no real second layer of protection on it — and used it to publish poisoned versions of his package directly. No patient months of earning trust this time. No clever encryption. They simply stole the maintainer's identity and pushed malware out under his trusted name. And the malware was nakedly greedy: it checked whether you were on Windows or Linux and installed a cryptocurrency miner to quietly burn your computer's power generating coins for the attacker, and on Windows it also dropped a program to steal your saved passwords — from browsers, from email, the lot. For a few hours, before it was caught and ripped out, anyone who ran an install touching ua-parser-js was at risk. Same soft spot — a single maintainer's account, controlling a package the whole world pulls down. Different way in: not a con, just a stolen password.

NARRATOR: And then there's the strangest variant of all, from March of twenty twenty-two, because this one wasn't even a criminal. It was a protest. There's a popular package called node-ipc, used for letting parts of a program talk to each other, downloaded over a million times a week. Its maintainer, deeply angry about Russia's invasion of Ukraine, pushed out new versions of his own package containing code that checked the location of the computer it ran on — and if it decided the machine was in Russia or Belarus, it would try to overwrite the user's files, wiping them and replacing their contents with a heart symbol. He also bundled in a module that dropped a peace message onto people's desktops. People started calling this new thing protestware — software sabotaged on purpose, by its own author, to make a political point. And whatever you think of the cause, the mechanism should chill you, because it's the same one every time: a maintainer with the keys to a package the world trusts, reaching down through the supply chain to do something the people downstream never agreed to and never saw coming. The vulnerability was even given an official tracking number, the way a real security flaw is — because that's exactly what it was.

NARRATOR: So step back and look at the four of them together, because the pattern is the whole point. Azer Koçulu, in twenty sixteen, broke the world by deleting eleven lines of code in anger. The event-stream attacker, in twenty eighteen, conned a trusting volunteer out of his keys and used them to hunt for Bitcoin fortunes. The ua-parser-js attacker, in twenty twenty-one, stole a maintainer's login and pushed out a cryptominer. And the node-ipc maintainer, in twenty twenty-two, weaponised his own package as a protest. Four completely different people. Four completely different motives — rage, greed, greed again, and political conviction. And every one of them walked through the exact same door. A tiny, trusted package. A long chain of transitive dependencies — things you pull in not because you chose them, but because something you chose depends on something, that depends on something, that depends on them. And blind trust, all the way down. Nobody broke a firewall. Nobody cracked an encryption algorithm. Nobody found a clever bug. The code, in every case, did exactly what it was written to do. They didn't attack the software. They attacked the chain of trust that delivers it — they poisoned the well, instead of attacking everyone who drinks from it.

NARRATOR: And once you see it that way, the weak points stop being mysterious. The deepest one is the human being holding the publishing keys to a package the world depends on. Sometimes you steal that person's login, the way it happened to ua-parser-js. Sometimes you befriend and exhaust them until they hand the keys over willingly, the way it happened to Dominic Tarr. Sometimes that person is the attacker, the way it was with node-ipc. But in every case, a single maintainer's account turns out to be a single point of failure for everyone downstream of them. How that one human proves who they are, how carefully they guard their keys, who they trust with publishing rights — these are not that person's private business. They are everyone's business. Every project that ever pulled their package down has staked its safety on a stranger they've never met.

NARRATOR: And the second weak point is the rest of us — everyone downstream, doing the trusting. Because every project any of us builds pulls in a sprawling, branching tree of other people's code, layer upon layer, the vast majority of which we have never read and never will. The deep nested dependencies the event-stream attack rode through. The transitive packages that quietly carried left-pad into the foundations of companies that had never heard of it. We accept all of it, implicitly, every single time we type install. That tree is enormous, and almost nobody audits it. We treat those packages like furniture — always there, never examined, completely trusted. And trust at that scale, unexamined, is not a convenience. It is the attack surface.

NARRATOR: Which is why the defences, when you finally name them, sound almost insultingly boring next to the drama of the attacks. Pin your dependencies — lock them down to specific, known versions you have checked, so that a malicious new release can't just be pulled in automatically tomorrow simply because it's newer. Use the integrity checks the tooling already gives you for free — cryptographic fingerprints that refuse to install a package if its contents don't exactly match what you locked, so a swapped-out poisoned version gets stopped at the door. Actually look at what you're adding before you add it, and prefer ways of verifying where a package genuinely came from and who genuinely built it, rather than trusting a familiar name and a version number. None of that is glamorous. None of it makes a good story. But every one of those boring habits is the precise difference between a poisoned package sliding silently into your software and being stopped cold.

NARRATOR: And maybe the deepest point, the one worth carrying out of all this, is the simplest. Your dependencies are part of your software. Not an afterthought. Not someone else's problem. Not free magic that arrives when you type install and never think about again. The code other people wrote, that your software stands on, is your software — yours to choose carefully, lock down, check, and watch over its whole life. Dominic Tarr said it himself, from the inside: people are happy to share the code, but nobody wants to share the responsibility. That gap — between how eagerly we take, and how little we tend — is the gap every one of these attacks lived in.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A tired volunteer who built something for fun, gave it to the world for free, stopped using it, and then — out of pure decency — handed the keys to a stranger who asked nicely. A weapon hidden one layer deeper than anyone would look, encrypted so it couldn't be read, that would only unlock on the machines of the people who make a Bitcoin wallet, that only fired during a real release, that only robbed the very richest — riding quietly inside eight million downloads for two and a half months. A student, by chance, pulling a loose thread until the whole thing unravelled. And before it and after it, the same shape again and again: an angry man deleting eleven lines and breaking the internet, a stolen login pushing out a cryptominer, a furious maintainer wiping files to make a point. Different people, different reasons, the same door.

NARRATOR: We built the most extraordinary thing — a global library where strangers freely share their work so nobody has to solve the same problem twice. And we built it, almost without noticing, on a foundation of pure trust: trust in people we've never met, holding up bolts in the dark, for nothing, in their spare time. That trust is the best thing about it. And it is the only thing the attackers ever needed. The code was never the weak link. The chain was.
