NARRATOR: This is the Secure Software Architecture summary — the cram episode, the everything pass. The job: sweep the whole module top to bottom, refresh every dot-point in a couple of sharp sentences, and dump every mnemonic in one go so you can offload the lot in the first minute of the exam. Six chapters, the biggest module. Get a pen. We start with the exam dump.

NARRATOR: One hook sits above all others: CIA-AAA. Say it "see-eye-ay, triple-A." Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — that's CIA, what we protect. Authentication, Authorisation, Accountability — that's the triple-A, how we protect it. The second you sit down, write CIA-AAA in the margin, because almost every security question is one of these six properties being protected or broken. Then the rest of the marquee set: "Hash is one-way, Encrypt is two-way." V-S-E — Validate, Sanitise, handle Errors. "Static is Source, Dynamic is Doing" — the testing distinction. And the vulnerability master list, B-X-I-R. Write CIA-AAA first, then those, then we go through the whole module so each one means something.

NARRATOR: The module arc, because the shape is the memory aid. Six chapters in three movements. The mindset — chapter fourteen: why security matters, building it into the lifecycle, designing it for real users. The principles — chapter fifteen: CIA-AAA and cryptography. The practice on data — chapter sixteen: defensive input handling and privacy. The systems — chapter seventeen: secure A-P-Is and secure execution. The testing and the bugs — chapter eighteen: the testing toolbox and the vulnerability catalogue. The context — chapter nineteen: teams, enterprise benefits, ethics and law, evaluation. Mindset, principles, data, systems, testing, context. That's the module.

NARRATOR: Chapter fourteen, the business case for security. The skill is to justify in business language, not code. The two syllabus benefits: protect data — confidentiality and integrity — and minimise cyber attacks and vulnerabilities. Mnemonic D-A: protect Data, prevent Attacks. The framing: security is a business enabler, not just a cost — it builds trust, regulatory compliance, market differentiation. Breach cost has two parts: direct — fines, forensics, recovery, notification — and indirect — lost revenue, churn, reputation — and the indirect usually dominates. Prevention is far cheaper than response: roughly thirty thousand dollars of prevention versus eight hundred thousand for a breach. Risk equals Likelihood times Impact — prioritise the highest scores. The trap: "we're too small to be targeted" — attacks are automated and indiscriminate, and small firms are least able to survive a breach.

NARRATOR: Chapter fourteen, integrating security into the S-D-L-C. The eight steps — same as Year eleven, now with a security lens — Requirements definition, Specifications, Design, Development, Integration, Testing and debugging, Installation, Maintenance. Mnemonic: "Really Smart Developers Don't Ignore Testing In Maintenance." Security-by-design means security in every phase, not bolted on. Shift left means move security effort earlier, where flaws are cheap — roughly sixty-three times cheaper to fix at design than in production; that's "prevention cheaper than response" inside the lifecycle. Threat modelling: model the system, identify threats, assess risk by Likelihood times Impact, choose countermeasures, validate. Threat categories, mnemonic STRIDE: Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege. The trap: bolt-on security added at the end is expensive and ineffective; and testing is just one of eight phases, not the whole of security.

NARRATOR: Chapter fourteen, user-centred security design. The security-usability paradox: difficult security gets bypassed, and the workarounds create new vulnerabilities — so usability is itself a security requirement; more friction does not mean more secure. End users vary in skill, devices, and ability, so secure design must be inclusive or it excludes people and gets circumvented — accessibility applied to security. The guiding principle: "secure default, easy right path" — the safe option is on by default and the secure way is the easiest way. Supporting principles: transparency, progressive security, recoverable security, contextual guidance. And you model both user personas, for legitimate use, and abuse cases, for malicious use — abuse cases are STRIDE pointed at your features. The trap: blaming users for a design failure — telling people not to write passwords down fixes nothing.

QUESTION: First retrieval, the highest-value one. Pause the player and write down all six of CIA-AAA, and for each say in a few words what it means. Then play.

NARRATOR: CIA-AAA. Confidentiality — prevent unauthorised reading or disclosure; secrecy. Integrity — prevent unauthorised modification or tampering; accuracy. Availability — keep the system reachable for authorised users; access. Authentication — verify who you are, comes first. Authorisation — control what you're allowed to do, comes second. Accountability — log actions so they're traceable to a user. CIA is what we protect; AAA is how we protect it. Six terms with a meaning each — the foundation of the whole module.

NARRATOR: Chapter fifteen, the conceptual heart — the CIA triad and AAA in full. Confidentiality: controls are encryption at rest and in transit, and access control; threats are breaches, eavesdropping, social engineering, insiders. Integrity: controls are hashing, digital signatures, input validation; threats are tampering, injection, man-in-the-middle. Availability: controls are rate limiting, redundancy and failover, circuit breakers, backups; threats are denial of service, resource exhaustion, hardware failure. Authentication: factors are something you Know, something you Have, something you Are, somewhere you Are — mnemonic Know, Have, Are, plus Where; combine two or more for multi-factor authentication; passwords are stored as a salted hash. Authorisation: role-based access control and least privilege. Accountability: audit logging, non-repudiation, forensics. The pipeline: Authenticate, Authorise, Act, Log. The answer-shape for any control question: name the property, name the control, state the effect. The trap: "logged in is not allowed" — authenticated is not authorised; confusing those, or confusing confidentiality with integrity, is exactly what the marker exploits.

NARRATOR: Chapter fifteen, cryptography part one — hashing, encryption, password storage. Encryption is two-way: reversible with a key, plaintext to ciphertext and back — it delivers confidentiality; use it when you need the data back. Hashing is one-way: irreversible, producing a fixed-size fingerprint — it delivers integrity and verification; use it when you only need to check a match. The marquee line: "Hash is one-way, Encrypt is two-way." Hash properties: one-way, deterministic, fixed-size, and the avalanche effect — one character changes and the whole hash changes. Password storage is the headline application: never plaintext, and not encryption — a reversible key is a liability and you never need to recover a password, only verify it. So you hash. But plain hashing isn't enough, so the recipe is "Hash it, Salt it, Slow it." A salt is a unique random per-user value combined with the password before hashing — it makes identical passwords differ and defeats precomputed rainbow tables; "salt stops the rainbow." Slow hashing — bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2 — beats brute force, because fast hashes like S-H-A-two-fifty-six are too fast. The order: password, then salt, then hash. The golden rule: never roll your own crypto — use audited libraries.

NARRATOR: Chapter fifteen, cryptography part two — keys, T-L-S, signatures, sandboxing. Symmetric encryption uses one shared key both ways, fast for bulk, but with a key-distribution problem — Symmetric is the Same key. Asymmetric uses a key pair, public shared and private secret, which solves sharing but is slow — Asymmetric is A pair. Hybrid encryption is the H-T-T-P-S move: asymmetric once to agree a fast symmetric session key, then symmetric for all bulk traffic. Key management lifecycle, mnemonic "Good Spies Rotate Disguises": Generate, Store, Rotate, Destroy. A certificate binds a public key to an identity, signed by a certificate authority; the browser checks that signature to authenticate the server and obtain its public key — the certificate authenticates, it does not encrypt. The T-L-S handshake: certificate, verify against trusted authorities, asymmetric to agree a symmetric session key, symmetric for bulk. A digital signature is the inverse of encryption — sign with the private key, verify with the public key; mechanically, hash the document then encrypt the hash with the private key — giving authenticity, integrity, and non-repudiation, which is accountability. Sandboxing runs code in a restricted isolated environment — a playpen the code can't climb out of — for least privilege and containment. The trap: signing is not encrypting — a signed document is still readable; and strong crypto can still fail through buggy implementation, which is Heartbleed.

NARRATOR: Chapter sixteen, defensive data input handling — validation, sanitisation, error handling. The governing rule: never trust input — treat all external data as hostile. The three practices, mnemonic V-S-E: Validate, Sanitise, handle Errors. Validation checks input against rules and rejects what doesn't conform — it's a gate; and allow-list beats block-list, because defining what's good is finite while defining what's bad is infinite and evadable. Sanitisation transforms or escapes input so it's safe to use — it cleans rather than rejects, for example H-T-M-L-escaping a less-than sign. Validation rejects, sanitisation cleans — the key distinction. S-Q-L injection is untrusted input concatenated into a query so it becomes S-Q-L; the fix is a parameterised query, passing the value separately so the database treats it as data. Cross-site scripting is untrusted input echoed into a page so it becomes script; the fix is output encoding plus a Content Security Policy. Injection and cross-site scripting are the same disease — input becoming code — in two different interpreters. Error handling is a security control: log it private, show it generic, fail securely — a login error says "invalid username or password," never which.

QUESTION: Retrieval. Pause and say the difference between validation and sanitisation in one sentence each, then name the fix for S-Q-L injection. Then play.

NARRATOR: Validation checks input against rules and rejects what doesn't conform — it's a gate that turns bad input away. Sanitisation transforms or escapes input so it's safe to use — it doesn't reject, it cleans. Validation rejects, sanitisation cleans. The fix for S-Q-L injection is a parameterised query, also called a prepared statement: a placeholder with the value passed separately, so the database treats it strictly as data, never as command. Confusing validation with sanitisation, or trusting a block-list, is the lost mark.

NARRATOR: Chapter sixteen, privacy by design. The three named principles, mnemonic PER: Proactive not reactive, Embed privacy into design, Respect for user privacy. The crucial distinction: privacy is not security. Security protects data from unauthorised access — keeping attackers out. Privacy governs whether you should collect it at all, and how it's used, retained, and consented — you can have perfect security and still violate privacy by harvesting and selling data. Proactive means anticipate and prevent privacy harm by design — run a privacy impact assessment before collecting. Embed means privacy built into the architecture and on by default. Respect means transparency, genuine consent, and data-subject rights — access, correct, delete. Data minimisation is "collect less, keep less" — data you never collected or already deleted can't be breached. Purpose limitation: use data only for its collected purpose. The legal layer: G-D-P-R in the E-U, which made privacy-by-design a legal requirement, and the Australian Privacy Principles under the Privacy Act. The trap: privacy equals security — no, they're distinct; and consent theatre, the pre-ticked "agree to everything," is not genuine consent.

NARRATOR: Chapter seventeen, secure A-P-I design. An A-P-I is a door opened on purpose to the internet, so every request is untrusted — check before acting. Secure it with A-A-R-T: Authenticate the caller, Authorise the action, Rate-limit, Transport-secure. Authentication options: an A-P-I key identifies the app; a J-W-T is a signed stateless token carrying identity and permissions — signed, not encrypted, so no secrets in the payload; OAuth two is delegated authorisation, "log in with." Authorisation must happen on every request — role-based access plus an ownership check plus least privilege — because authenticated is not authorised; forgetting this is broken access control, where a logged-in user changes the I-D in the U-R-L and reads someone else's data. Rate-limiting caps requests per window to protect availability and slow brute force — exceed it and you get a four-two-nine. Transport-secure means everything over T-L-S. And sessions, because H-T-T-P is stateless: the server issues a session I-D after login, secured with S-A-F-E — Strong random I-Ds, cookie Attributes — HttpOnly, Secure, SameSite — a Fixed lifetime, and re-issue on Escalation, regenerating the I-D on login to stop fixation. The same-origin policy is the browser rule that a page's JavaScript can only read responses from its own origin; C-O-R-S is a controlled relaxation of that — and C-O-R-S is not access control. The trap: authentication without authorisation — broken access control — is the classic A-P-I breach.

NARRATOR: Chapter seventeen, secure execution and resource management. Two dot-points: efficient execution — memory, session, exception management — and protecting against file and side-channel attacks. The three execution areas, mnemonic M-S-E: Memory, Session, Exception. Memory: a buffer overflow writes past a fixed buffer and corrupts adjacent memory; an over-read returns more bytes than it should and leaks adjacent memory — that was Heartbleed, T-L-S trusting a client-supplied length without validating it, leaking private keys; managed languages bounds-check arrays, but leaks, exhaustion, and over-reads remain — so validate sizes and never trust an input length. Session: strong random I-D at login, validate per request, timeout, regenerate on login to stop fixation, invalidate on logout server-side; failures are session hijacking — stealing a live I-D — and session fixation — planting a known one. Exception: fail securely — leaking a stack trace is information disclosure, and a caught security-check that continues fails open and grants access, so log private, show generic, fail closed. File attacks are path traversal — dot-dot-slash sequences climb out of the intended folder to sensitive files; "dot-dot-slash climbs out" — so canonicalise the path and verify it stays inside the base directory. Side-channel attacks extract secrets by measuring an indirect effect — timing, power, sound — not the algorithm; the classic is a timing attack on an early-exit comparison, defeated by a constant-time comparison.

NARRATOR: Chapter eighteen, security testing fundamentals. Two dot-points: the five testing strategies, and surviving a breach. The five methods, cheap-and-broad to expensive-and-deep — the chant is "Code Review, S-A-S-T, D-A-S-T, Vulnerability Assessment, Penetration." Code review is a human reading source line by line, uniquely catching flawed logic and intent no tool sees — it caught the x-z backdoor. S-A-S-T, static application security testing, analyses source without running it, finding insecure patterns early and by exact line, but with false positives. D-A-S-T, dynamic, attacks the running app from outside, finding real exploitable runtime flaws, but late and with no line number. Mnemonic: "Static is Source — not running; Dynamic is Doing — running." Vulnerability assessment is a broad scan cataloguing known weaknesses — breadth, which would have caught Equifax's missing patch. A penetration test is a skilled human actively exploiting and chaining weaknesses — depth. Surviving a breach, mnemonic H-B-D: Harden — shrink the attack surface beforehand; Breach-handle — detect, contain, eradicate, recover, review, planned in advance; and Disaster-recover within business continuity. Business continuity keeps the whole business running through a disruption; disaster recovery is the subset that restores I-T systems and data — and an untested backup is a guess.

QUESTION: Retrieval. Pause and list all five security testing strategies, and say the one-line difference between S-A-S-T and D-A-S-T. Then play.

NARRATOR: Code Review, S-A-S-T, D-A-S-T, Vulnerability Assessment, Penetration. S-A-S-T is static — it reads the source without running it, finding insecure patterns early and by exact line. D-A-S-T is dynamic — it attacks the running application from outside, finding real runtime and configuration flaws but late and without a line number. "Static is Source, Dynamic is Doing." Confusing those two is one of the eight marker traps.

NARRATOR: Chapter eighteen, the web application vulnerability catalogue. The user-action vulnerability master list, mnemonic B-X-I-R: Broken authentication and session, Cross-site, Invalid forwarding and redirect, Race conditions. Cross-site scripting injects a malicious script that runs in another user's browser — untrusted input echoed into a page without encoding; types are reflected, stored, and D-O-M-based; fix with output encoding, a Content Security Policy, and HttpOnly cookies. Cross-site request forgery — "see-surf" — injects no script; it tricks a logged-in user's browser into sending a forged request to a site where they're authenticated, riding the auto-attached session cookie; fix with an anti-C-S-R-F token and SameSite cookies. The key distinction: cross-site scripting injects a Script — run code as you; cross-site request forgery rides a Session — act as you. Broken authentication and session management: weak passwords, no login rate-limit, session hijacking — steal a live I-D — and session fixation — plant a known one; fix with the S-A-F-E session rules plus multi-factor and rate-limited logins. Race conditions: correctness depends on the timing of concurrent operations — the classic is T-O-C-T-O-U, time-of-check to time-of-use, like a double-spend where two withdrawals both pass the balance check before either deducts; fix by making check-and-act atomic. The trap: "my code is single-threaded so no races" — wrong, the web server is concurrent. Invalid forwarding is an open redirect — redirecting to an unvalidated user-supplied U-R-L — fixed by allow-listing internal destinations.

NARRATOR: Chapter nineteen, security in development teams. The dot-point is to describe the benefits of collaboration for safe software, three named, mnemonic V-D-Q: Views — various points of view; Delegate by expertise; Quality of the solution. Various points of view: a solo developer reviews with the same blind spots they wrote with, so flaws hide in the gap; a different perspective reaches that gap — which is why code review and pair programming work, and why solo self-review is structurally weak. Delegate by expertise: security is too big for one person, so assign crypto, A-P-I authorisation, input handling to whoever has depth — shared responsibility with security champions and collaborative threat modelling. Quality of the solution: diverse review plus specialist expertise means fewer vulnerabilities, and review comments teach juniors, raising the whole team over time. The nuance and trap: collaboration is not automatically more secure — the bystander effect means review becomes "everyone's job" and no one checks, so assign one accountable reviewer; and trust can be exploited, as the x-z backdoor showed. Benefits need structured collaboration, not headcount.

NARRATOR: Chapter nineteen, enterprise security benefits. The dot-point is to explain the benefits to an enterprise of secure development, five named, mnemonic PIP-PB — "Pip gets a peanut-butter": Products or services improved, Influence on future development, improved work Practices, Productivity, Business interactivity. These are organisational and commercial benefits, not technical — the core trap. Products and services: secure-by-design becomes a marketable value proposition. Influence on future development: secure patterns and incident lessons feed forward so every later project starts from a more secure baseline — shift-left scaled across the organisation, and the most-missed of the five. Improved work practices: review and threat-modelling discipline lifts general engineering quality. Productivity, counter-intuitively: security speeds you up, because prevention beats firefighting and automation lowers friction, so you ship more — proving the "enabler not just a cost" claim from chapter fourteen. Business interactivity, the most abstract: safely connecting and transacting with customers and partners — only possible when security earns trust. Equifax is the counterfactual: a breach lost all five. Always land the answer on the enterprise, never the codebase.

NARRATOR: Chapter nineteen, security ethics and legal considerations. The verb is evaluate — weigh more than one side and reach a justified judgement, not just describe. The six issues, mnemonic E-D-P-C-I-D, said "Ed Picid": Employment, Data security, Privacy, Copyright, Intellectual property, Digital disruption. Employment: creates security careers but automation displaces and demands reskilling — a shift in work. Data security: an ethical duty of care plus legal mandatory breach-notification. Privacy: whether you should collect at all, distinct from data security, governed by G-D-P-R and the Australian Privacy Principles. Copyright: automatic legal protection of original works including source code, honoured via licences — permissive versus copyleft. Intellectual property: the broader category — copyright is just one type, alongside patents and trade secrets — and that's the trap, copyright is one type of I-P. Digital disruption: enables new services but displaces old industries and concentrates power. The through-line: responsible disclosure — report privately to the vendor first, agree a fix window, publish after the patch, and never exploit, because unauthorised access can be illegal even with good intent. The trap: equating copyright with intellectual property, and describing when the verb is evaluate.

NARRATOR: Chapter nineteen, evaluating security programs — the last teaching lesson. The dot-point is to evaluate the strategies used to manage code security: where chapter eighteen was apply the five methods, this is evaluate them — measure effectiveness and improve. Evaluation is a continuous loop of three stages, mnemonic M-A-I, said "my": Measure, Audit, Improve. Measure with security metrics — good ones are actionable and outcome-based, not activity. Examinable ones: mean time to detect, mean time to respond, vulnerability remediation time, patch coverage, code-review coverage, phishing-test failure rate. Audit is an independent systematic check against a standard — you can't grade your own homework; standards are I-S-O two-seven-thousand-one, the N-I-S-T framework, and P-C-I-D-S-S. Improve means act on findings and feed lessons back — continuous improvement, the same shape as the "influence on future development" benefit. The loop is continuous because the threat landscape never stops moving. The trap: vanity metrics — "five million attacks blocked" looks impressive but doesn't show you're safer; measure outcomes, not activity.

NARRATOR: Now the consolidated trap pass — the eight distinctions the marker exploits, in one block. Authentication versus authorisation: who you are versus what you can do — "logged in is not allowed." Confidentiality versus integrity: reading versus changing — they fail independently. Hash versus encrypt: one-way versus two-way. Validation versus sanitisation: reject versus clean. S-A-S-T versus D-A-S-T: source-not-running versus app-running. Cross-site scripting versus cross-site request forgery: inject a script versus forge a request. Privacy versus security: should we hold it at all versus is the access protected. Copyright versus intellectual property: copyright is one type of I-P. Those eight, plus: never bolt security on, never trust the client, never roll your own crypto, never put secrets in a J-W-T, never treat C-O-R-S as access control, never assume single-threaded means no race, never use vanity metrics.

NARRATOR: To finish, a short exam-style set — real N-E-S-A verbs, model answers after each. Last self-test.

QUESTION: Question one. Distinguish between authentication and authorisation, and give an example of a vulnerability that arises from confusing them. Pause, attempt it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: authentication verifies who a user is — for example checking a username and password — and it comes first. Authorisation controls what an authenticated user is permitted to do — for example role-based access and ownership checks — and it comes second. Confusing them produces broken access control: a logged-in user changes the I-D in a U-R-L and accesses another user's data, because the system authenticated them but never authorised the specific action. The principle is "authenticated is not authorised." Naming both definitions, the order, and broken access control is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two. Explain why passwords should be hashed and salted rather than encrypted. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: passwords should be hashed because hashing is a one-way function — even if the database is stolen, the hashes can't be reversed to recover the passwords, and you never need to recover a password, only verify a match by re-hashing the entered one. Encryption is two-way and reversible, so the decryption key becomes a liability — steal the key and steal every password. Plain hashing isn't enough, so you also salt — a unique random per-user value that makes identical passwords differ and defeats precomputed rainbow tables, "salt stops the rainbow" — and you use a slow hash like bcrypt to beat brute force. "Hash it, salt it, slow it." Stating one-way versus two-way, the salt's role against rainbow tables, and slow hashing is the full-mark answer.

QUESTION: Question three. Compare static and dynamic application security testing, and outline a situation where each is the better choice. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: static application security testing, S-A-S-T, analyses source code without running it — "Static is Source." It finds insecure patterns early and reports the exact line, so it's cheap to fix, but it produces false positives and can't see runtime or configuration flaws. Dynamic application security testing, D-A-S-T, attacks the running application from outside — "Dynamic is Doing." It finds real, exploitable runtime and configuration flaws, but late and with no line number. So S-A-S-T is the better choice early, on every commit, to catch coding flaws by line; D-A-S-T is better against a deployed staging build, to confirm what's actually exploitable. A weak answer just says one is "before running" and the other "after" — the mark is naming the trade-off, false positives and early-by-line versus exploitable-but-late, and matching each to its situation.

QUESTION: Question four, the harder one. A company stores customer health data, exposes a public A-P-I, and has had no security audit in two years. Evaluate three weaknesses and recommend a fix for each, naming the CIA-AAA property at stake. Pause the player, plan an answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: first, health data demands confidentiality, so it must be encrypted at rest and in transit over T-L-S, with role-based access control so staff see only what they need — least privilege. Second, the public A-P-I is untrusted, so secure it with A-A-R-T: authenticate every caller, authorise the specific action on every request to prevent broken access control, rate-limit to protect availability, and require T-L-S — and validate every input parameter, V-S-E, to stop injection, protecting integrity. Third, no audit in two years means security is unmeasured, so apply M-A-I: an independent audit against a standard like I-S-O two-seven-thousand-one, measuring outcome metrics like mean time to respond and vulnerability remediation time, then improving continuously — that's accountability and the evaluation loop. A full-mark answer names a real weakness, a concrete named fix, and the CIA-AAA property at stake for each, and reaches a justified judgement. That's the whole of Secure Software Architecture — make software trustworthy — and next in the course it's made smart, in Software Automation, where CIA-AAA accountability, privacy minimisation, and the ethics toolkit all return as A-I data ethics.
