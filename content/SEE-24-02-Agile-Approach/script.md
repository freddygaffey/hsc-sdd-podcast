NARRATOR: Recap first, and today it's a direct setup, because this episode is the other half of a contrast. Last episode: Waterfall, the linear, sequential development approach. Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance, run as strict stage-gates with heavy documentation and formal sign-off at each. The hook was "Waterfall Falls once" — you go down the stages in order, and going back up is expensive. Strengths: predictability, comprehensive documentation, control. Weaknesses: rigidity to change, late feedback, late testing, all-or-nothing delivery. It suits stable, regulated projects and fails on uncertain, fast-moving ones — Healthcare-dot-gov plus a fixed deadline plus weak testing was the meltdown.

NARRATOR: And the chain before that: implementation methods, DiP-PP — Direct, Phased, Parallel, Pilot, the four cut-over strategies, matched by risk to the scenario. Ideation and modelling, B-M-S and the data dictionary. Requirements, FUN versus PERF and TECO feasibility. Now, today. Waterfall is the rigid, plan-everything-up-front approach; Agile is its direct opposite, invented precisely to fix Waterfall's weaknesses. The hook, paired with last episode's: "Agile Goes around." Waterfall falls once; Agile goes around. Waterfall is a straight line down; Agile is a loop you ride again and again. That single contrast is the spine of this chapter. Today's outcome is S-E-twelve-oh-six.

NARRATOR: Here's the core idea. Agile develops software iteratively and incrementally. Instead of all the requirements, then all the design, then all the build, then all the testing — the waterfall line — Agile does a little of each, repeatedly, in short cycles. Each cycle produces a small, working, potentially shippable piece of software; you show it to the client, get feedback, and fold that feedback into the next cycle. So the rate of developing — that exact syllabus phrase — is incremental and continuous: working software delivered frequently, not in one big drop at the end. The philosophy values responding to change over following a fixed plan, and working software over comprehensive documentation. That doesn't mean no plan and no documents — that's the trap, and we'll hit it — it means the plan and docs are lighter and evolve.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll be able to explain the iteration workflow and what a sprint is; write a user story in the correct format; name the Agile ceremonies; explain method tailoring; and — the headline skill — compare Agile against Waterfall, which is the most-asked extended question in this whole chapter. Let's build it.

NARRATOR: First, the iteration — the loop Agile goes around. The basic unit is the iteration, usually called a sprint, and it's time-boxed: a fixed length, typically two weeks, that cannot be extended. Time-boxed matters — if the work isn't done when the sprint ends, the sprint still ends, and the unfinished work goes back in the queue. The point is a steady, predictable rhythm. Within each sprint you plan, build, test, and review a small increment of working software; then the next sprint starts and you go around again. The prioritised list of everything still to build is the product backlog; the slice you commit to for this sprint is the sprint backlog. Over time you learn your velocity — how much you reliably get done per sprint — which lets you forecast. So: short fixed cycles, each producing working software, going around the loop.

NARRATOR: Second, user stories — how Agile captures requirements, and it's examinable as a format you reproduce. A user story is a short description of a feature from the user's point of view, in a fixed template: "As a [type of user], I want [some functionality], so that [some benefit]." Three parts: the role, the want, the why. For example: "As a high school student, I want to log into the school portal with my student I-D, so that I can access my grades securely." Notice it doesn't say how to build it — it captures who, what, and why, leaving the technical solution open. Each user story carries acceptance criteria — the specific, testable conditions that define when it's done. There's your callback to requirements: acceptance criteria are the measurable-and-testable idea from episode one, applied per story. The marker wants the exact "As a, I want, so that" structure plus acceptance criteria. Worked user stories with acceptance criteria are in Listing one. A handy quality check for stories is INVEST — Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable — a good story has all six.

QUESTION: Quick check. Turn this requirement into a properly formatted user story with one acceptance criterion: "Teachers need to enter grades for a whole class quickly." Pause, write the story, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's a strong version. "As a teacher, I want to enter grades for multiple students at once, so that I can efficiently update records after marking." Acceptance criterion: "the system saves all entered grades together and logs each change with a timestamp and the teacher's identity." Hear the structure — role, want, benefit, then a testable condition. The three-part format plus a testable criterion is the mark. The weak version just writes "teachers enter grades fast" — no role, no benefit, no testability.

NARRATOR: Third, the ceremonies — the structured meetings that make a sprint work. Four of them, and here's the hook: P-S-R-R. Plan, Standup, Review, Retro. Plan is sprint planning at the start: the team picks backlog stories that fit its capacity, estimates the effort — often in story points on a Fibonacci-ish scale, one, two, three, five, eight, thirteen — and breaks them into tasks. Standup is the daily standup, a strict fifteen-minute sync where each person answers three questions: what did I do yesterday, what will I do today, what's blocking me. Its job is to surface blockers fast, not solve them in the meeting. Review is the sprint review at the end: the team demonstrates working software to stakeholders and gathers feedback — the feedback loop Waterfall lacks, happening every two weeks instead of once at the end. Retro is the sprint retrospective: the team reflects on its own process — what to start, stop, and continue — to work better next sprint. So P-S-R-R: Plan the sprint, Standup daily, Review with stakeholders, Retro on the process.

QUESTION: Pause-the-player retrieval, the highest-value one here. Without looking, write down the four Agile ceremonies from P-S-R-R, and one sentence on what each is for. Pause now, dump all four, then play to check.

NARRATOR: P-S-R-R. Plan — sprint planning — select and estimate this sprint's stories. Standup — the daily fifteen-minute sync to surface blockers. Review — demonstrate the working increment to stakeholders and get feedback. Retro — the retrospective, where the team improves its own process. Plan, Standup, Review, Retro. Four with a purpose each is the ceremonies mark — and notice the distinction the exam loves: Review is about the product, Retrospective is about the process. Don't swap them.

NARRATOR: Fourth, method tailoring — a specific syllabus phrase, so know it precisely. Method tailoring means adapting Agile practices to fit your team, project, and context, rather than following one framework rigidly. Agile isn't a single fixed recipe: there's Scrum, with its sprints and roles like Product Owner and Scrum Master, and there's Kanban, which focuses on continuous flow and limiting work-in-progress rather than fixed sprints. Tailoring is choosing and adjusting practices — maybe shorter sprints, maybe skipping a ceremony that doesn't add value, maybe blending Scrum and Kanban — to suit the situation. The exam point: tailoring is a strength, Agile's flexibility, but it requires judgement; it's not licence to drop everything you find inconvenient. Which leads straight to the trap.

NARRATOR: Here's the big Agile trap, and the marker loves catching it: "Agile means no planning and no documentation." Wrong. Agile values working software over comprehensive documentation, and responding to change over following a fixed plan — but "over" doesn't mean "instead of." Agile still plans, every single sprint; it still documents, just lightly and continuously instead of in giant upfront tomes; it still has structure, the ceremonies. A team using "we're agile" as an excuse to wing it with no planning, no tests, no records isn't doing Agile — it's chaos with a buzzword. The related failure has a name: cargo-cult Agile, going through the motions of the ceremonies — standing up daily, holding retros — without the underlying mindset of genuine collaboration and adaptation, so you get the meetings but none of the benefit. In an exam, never write "Agile has no documentation." Write "Agile uses lighter, evolving documentation produced as needed."

NARRATOR: Now the headline: comparing Agile and Waterfall, the extended-response gold. Set them side by side. Structure: Waterfall is sequential phases; Agile is iterative sprints. Planning: Waterfall plans everything up front; Agile plans incrementally, sprint by sprint. Change: Waterfall resists change once a phase is signed off; Agile embraces it at every iteration. Feedback: Waterfall gets client feedback late, near the end; Agile gets it every sprint. Documentation: Waterfall is heavy; Agile is light and evolving. Delivery: Waterfall delivers all value at the end; Agile delivers working increments continuously. Suitability: Waterfall suits stable, well-understood, regulated projects; Agile suits uncertain, fast-moving, user-facing projects where requirements will evolve. The full comparison is a table in Listing two. The one-line anchor is the pair of hooks: Waterfall Falls once — linear, one pass; Agile Goes around — looping, many passes.

NARRATOR: Let me interleave deliberately, because Agile leans on things you've already learned. Backward to Software Automation, the very first episode: DevOps and C-I-C-D — continuous integration and continuous deployment — are what make Agile practical at speed. You can't deliver working software every two weeks by hand; you need automated build, test, and deploy pipelines firing on every change. So the automation you learned in S-A is the engine under Agile's frequent delivery. Backward to Secure Software Architecture: Agile's "embrace change" carries a security risk — moving fast can mean skipping security, which is why security-by-design and shift-left matter even more in Agile. You bake security into the definition-of-done for each story rather than bolting it on at a final gate that Agile doesn't have. The thread: Agile is enabled by automation, must consciously keep security, and gets combined with Waterfall next.

NARRATOR: Worked example, because "explain the iteration workflow" and "compare the two approaches" both reward concreteness. Picture building the school portal in Agile. Sprint one: plan — pick the login story and the view-grades story from the backlog, estimate them, break into tasks. Build and test them. At the daily standups, a developer flags she's blocked waiting on the database schema — surfaced and unblocked the same day. Sprint review at the end: demo login and grade-viewing to the teachers, who say "actually, parents need this too" — feedback you got in two weeks, not at month six. Retro: standups ran long, so the team agrees to tighten them. Sprint two: the backlog now includes the new parent story, and you go around again. That's the loop — and notice the "parents need this" change cost almost nothing in Agile, whereas in Waterfall, discovered after sign-off, it'd be a painful, expensive backflow. That contrast is the comparison answer in story form.

NARRATOR: Now the weak-versus-strong contrast for the comparison question. Weak answer to "compare Waterfall and Agile": "Waterfall is older and slower, Agile is newer and faster." Hear it — named contrasts across structure, change, feedback, and suitability, not just "old versus new." That's the extended-response mark.

NARRATOR: Lock in today. Agile is iterative and incremental — short, time-boxed sprints, each delivering working software, going around the loop. "Agile Goes around" against "Waterfall Falls once." Requirements are user stories — "As a, I want, so that" — each with testable acceptance criteria. The ceremonies are P-S-R-R: Plan, Standup, Review, Retro — Review is the product, Retro is the process. Method tailoring is adapting Agile practices to your context. Strengths: adaptable to change, early and continuous feedback, frequent delivery. Weaknesses: less predictable final scope and cost, lighter documentation that can hurt in regulated settings, and high client involvement required. Traps: "Agile means no planning or documentation" — false, it plans every sprint and documents lightly; and cargo-cult ceremonies with no real adaptation.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions, recall up to extended. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Explain the iteration workflow in Agile development. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: Agile develops software in short, time-boxed iterations called sprints, typically around two weeks. Each sprint begins with sprint planning, where the team selects and estimates user stories from the prioritised product backlog that fit its capacity. During the sprint, the team builds and tests a small increment of working software, holding a brief daily standup to synchronise and surface blockers. At the end, a sprint review demonstrates the increment to stakeholders to gather feedback, and a retrospective reflects on improving the team's process. The next sprint repeats the cycle — Agile goes around the loop — delivering working software continuously rather than all at once. Describing the time-boxed cycle of plan, build-and-test, review, retro, and repeat is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, format. Write a user story for a feature that lets parents receive grade notifications, including one acceptance criterion. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: "As a parent, I want to receive an email notification when my child's grades are updated, so that I can stay informed about their academic progress." Acceptance criterion: "when a teacher saves a new grade for the student, the system sends an email to the linked parent within five minutes, and only to verified parent accounts." The mark is the correct three-part structure — role, want, benefit — plus a specific, testable acceptance criterion.

QUESTION: Question three, application. Explain what method tailoring means in Agile and why it's useful. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: method tailoring is adapting Agile practices, ceremonies, and frameworks — such as Scrum or Kanban — to suit a particular team, project, and context, rather than applying one framework rigidly. For example, a team might shorten its sprint length, adjust which ceremonies it runs, or blend Scrum's sprints with Kanban's continuous-flow approach. It's useful because no single set of practices fits every situation, so tailoring lets a team keep the practices that add value and drop or modify those that don't, improving efficiency and fit — though it requires judgement and shouldn't become an excuse to abandon discipline. Defining tailoring as context-driven adaptation and justifying it by fit-and-flexibility is the mark.

QUESTION: Question four, the extended one. Compare the Waterfall and Agile development approaches, and recommend which is more appropriate for a project with rapidly changing requirements. Pause, plan across both episodes, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: Waterfall is a sequential approach in which each phase — requirements, design, implementation, testing, deployment, maintenance — is completed and signed off before the next begins, with all requirements planned up front. This makes it predictable and comprehensively documented, with a clear audit trail, but rigid: changes discovered late are expensive because the model doesn't flow back, and client feedback and testing come only near the end. Agile is iterative and incremental, delivering working software in short time-boxed sprints with continuous client feedback through sprint reviews and lighter, evolving documentation. This makes it highly adaptable to change and fast to deliver value, but its final scope and cost are less predictable, its lighter documentation can be a problem in regulated settings, and it demands sustained client involvement. The hooks capture it: Waterfall falls once, Agile goes around. For a project with rapidly changing requirements, Agile is more appropriate, because its iterative structure embraces change at every sprint and surfaces it through continuous feedback, whereas Waterfall would force costly backflows each time a requirement shifts. An answer that contrasts the two across structure, change-handling, feedback, documentation, and suitability, and then justifies the Agile recommendation specifically against the changing-requirements condition, earns full marks.
