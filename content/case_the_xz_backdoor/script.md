NARRATOR: In late March two thousand twenty-four, a software engineer at Microsoft named Andres Freund was doing something profoundly boring. He was running performance tests on a database — measuring, in fine detail, how fast it ran on his Linux machine. The kind of work that's mostly waiting and squinting at numbers. And in that squinting, he noticed something almost anybody else would have shrugged off. When he logged into the machine over a secure remote connection — over S-S-H, the standard way administrators connect to servers — the login was taking about half a second longer than it should. Logins that used to take a tenth of a second were taking six tenths. Half a second. On a process you do a hundred times a day without thinking.

NARRATOR: Most people would assume their computer was just a bit busy and move on. Freund didn't. Something nagged at him. Why would logging in suddenly cost more processor time? So he pulled the thread. He dug into what the login process was doing under the hood, and found the extra time was being burned in an unexpected place — a compression library called liblzma, part of a small utility called xz. And what he uncovered, by following that half-second, is now regarded as one of the most patient, most nearly-successful attacks the open-source world has ever seen. He'd stumbled onto a backdoor — a secret hidden entrance — deliberately planted in software on its way into nearly every Linux computer on the planet.

NARRATOR: To see how big a deal that is, you have to know what xz is. It's a tiny, unglamorous tool for compressing files — squeezing data smaller. It is not exciting. But it is everywhere. It's woven so deeply into the plumbing of Linux that countless other programs quietly depend on it, including, through a chain of connections, the S-S-H login server — the very thing that guards remote access to most of the servers running the internet. So this dull little compression tool sat, unnoticed, in a position of extraordinary trust. Poison xz, and you could reach into the front door of a vast number of the world's machines.

NARRATOR: And xz wasn't built by a big company with a security team. For years it was maintained, essentially, by one person — a volunteer named Lasse Collin — in his spare time, for free, out of a sense of duty, because the world needed someone to. This is the open-source bargain, and it's a beautiful one: enormous amounts of the software civilisation runs on are maintained by individual volunteers, gifting their labour to everyone. But it also means the security of something millions of machines depend on can rest on the shoulders, and the goodwill, and the exhaustion, of a single tired human being.

NARRATOR: Which is exactly what the attacker was counting on.

NARRATOR: This is the part that lifts the xz story above an ordinary hack into something closer to a spy thriller. The attack didn't begin with code. It began with a relationship. Back in twenty-twenty-one, a new contributor appeared in the xz project, using the name Jia Tan. And Jia Tan did everything right. They started small — modest, helpful contributions, fixing little things, being useful. Over months they kept showing up: polite, competent, reliable. Slowly, they earned the maintainer's trust, the way any good open-source contributor does.

NARRATOR: And then, around them, other voices appeared. People on the project's mailing list — accounts we now believe were sock puppets, fake personas, very possibly the same operation — began applying a particular kind of pressure. They complained, in reasonable-sounding tones, that xz wasn't being updated fast enough. That the lone maintainer was too slow. That the project needed fresh hands to keep up. They framed it as concern for the project. But the effect was relentless: pressure on one overworked volunteer, hinting that he was letting people down, that he should hand over control.

QUESTION: Put yourself in that maintainer's shoes for a second. You're doing this alone, unpaid, in your spare time. People are pushing you, saying you're too slow, the project's stagnating. And here's this contributor — Jia Tan — who's been helpful and steady for years and is offering to take some of the weight off. What do you do?

NARRATOR: He let them in. Of course he did. It was the humane, reasonable thing to do. Worn down, helped along, Lasse Collin gradually handed Jia Tan more responsibility, until Jia Tan had real authority over the project — the power to make releases, to ship the official packaged versions the rest of the world would download and install. This whole arc took years. Years of patience, of building credibility one good commit at a time, of slow social pressure, all aimed at one goal: to be trusted with the keys.

NARRATOR: And once they had the keys, in early twenty-twenty-four, they used them.

NARRATOR: The backdoor itself was a marvel of misdirection. The malicious code wasn't sitting plainly in the source for anyone to read — too easy to spot. Instead, it was hidden in the build process: the machinery that turns source code into the finished, installable program. And it was disguised as test data — chunks of deliberately corrupted-looking files that any reviewer would dismiss as exactly what they appeared to be, junk for testing compression. But during the build, a hidden script would quietly extract the real payload from those innocent-looking files, stitch it together, and weave it into the final library. The poison only assembled itself at the last moment, in the packaged release — so anyone reading the public source code saw nothing wrong.

NARRATOR: And what did the finished backdoor do? It lay in wait inside the S-S-H login process, watching for a secret signal — a specific cryptographic key that only the attacker possessed. To everyone else, the system behaved normally. But to whoever held that key, the backdoor unlocked the door: it would let them run their own commands on the machine, bypassing all the usual checks, with full control. A skeleton key to a substantial portion of the servers on the internet, about to be shipped to the world inside a routine update.

NARRATOR: It got close. Frighteningly close. The poisoned versions had already reached the testing branches of major Linux distributions — the pre-release versions that would, within weeks, have rolled out to everyone. By most estimates we were weeks away from this backdoor sitting on a meaningful fraction of the world's servers.

NARRATOR: And the only reason it didn't was that the backdoor was very slightly too slow. The extra work it did, hooking into the login process, cost about half a second. And one engineer, doing something unrelated, was paying close enough attention to wonder why. The whole operation — years of patient infiltration, a near-flawless technical hide — was undone by a performance benchmark and a curious mind. When he disclosed it, on the twenty-ninth of March, the response was immediate and global. The backdoored versions were pulled, distributions reverted, the world exhaled. The vulnerability was catalogued as C-V-E twenty-twenty-four, dash, three-zero-nine-four, and rated as severe as the scale allows.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So what does this near-miss teach? More than almost any other story in security, because it attacks something we don't usually think of as attackable: trust itself.

NARRATOR: This is the cleanest example you will ever find of a software supply-chain attack. We tend to picture an attacker breaking into our system from outside. But your software isn't just the code you wrote — it's a vast tree of other people's code, dependencies pulled in from across the world, each depending on still more. That whole tree is your supply chain, and every link in it is a way in. The attacker here didn't break down your door. They poisoned something upstream that you would willingly invite inside, with your blessing, as part of a routine update. The lesson: the software you depend on is an attack surface, and your system is only as secure as everything you build it on top of.

NARRATOR: It also strikes at the heart of the open-source trust model. Open source is, overwhelmingly, a force for good and for security — anyone can read the code, so bugs and malice can be caught in the open, by many eyes. That openness is exactly how this attack was eventually caught. But the same model assumes good faith from contributors, and rests on volunteers who can be befriended, pressured, exhausted, and manipulated. The attacker didn't defeat the technology. They exploited the human community around it — the maintainer dynamics, the goodwill, the loneliness of unpaid work holding up critical infrastructure. Securing software, it turns out, is not only a technical problem. It's a social one.

NARRATOR: Which is why this is also a story about code review and build integrity. The malice was hidden in the build process and disguised as test data because the attacker knew people review source code, but rarely scrutinise the machinery that compiles it, and almost never inspect binary test files. Strong security means reviewing not just what the code says, but how it's assembled into the thing you run — making sure the program you ship is genuinely built from the source everyone reviewed, with nothing slipped in along the way. Build integrity, reproducible builds, careful review of who has authority to make a release: these aren't bureaucracy. They're the controls this attack was designed to slip past.

NARRATOR: And finally, notice when in the life of the software this happened. It wasn't a flaw in how the code was originally written. It was malice injected during maintenance — into a mature, trusted project, through the slow erosion of who controlled it. Security isn't a phase you complete at the start and tick off. It has to live across the entire development life cycle, including the long, quiet years afterward — because that boring, established, who's-going-to-bother-attacking-this stage is exactly where a patient adversary chooses to strike. The xz backdoor was nearly the most consequential breach in the history of computing, and the only thing between us and it was one person who refused to ignore half a second.
