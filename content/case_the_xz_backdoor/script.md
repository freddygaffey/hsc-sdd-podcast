NARRATOR: It is late March, two thousand twenty-four, and a software engineer named Andres Freund is sitting at his desk doing some of the most boring work in all of computing. He works for Microsoft, on the database called PostgreSQL, and what he is doing right now is performance testing. Benchmarking. He is running a piece of software over and over again on his Linux machine and watching, in fine detail, how fast it runs and where the time goes. This is the kind of work that is mostly waiting and squinting at numbers. Nine hundred and ninety-nine times out of a thousand, you find nothing, you adjust something, and you run it again.

NARRATOR: And in that squinting, Freund notices something almost anyone else on Earth would have shrugged off and forgotten in a heartbeat. When he logs into the machine over a secure remote connection — over S-S-H, Secure Shell, the standard way administrators connect to servers all over the world — the login is taking longer than it should. Not a lot longer. About half a second. Five hundred milliseconds of extra delay on a process that he, like every other engineer, does dozens of times a day without ever thinking about it. The login that used to feel instant now had a tiny, almost imperceptible hesitation in it.

NARRATOR: Five hundred milliseconds. The blink of an eye is about a hundred milliseconds, so call it five blinks. That is the entire margin we are talking about. That is the thread the whole world was hanging by, although no one knew it yet.

NARRATOR: Most people, noticing that, would assume the machine was a little busy and move on with their lives. Freund didn't. Something nagged at him. Why would simply logging in suddenly cost more processor time than it used to? It made no sense. So he did the thing that, in hindsight, saved an incalculable amount of grief: he pulled the thread. He got out his tools — including one called Valgrind, which watches exactly what a program does with memory while it runs — and he started taking the login process apart to find out where that half-second was disappearing to.

NARRATOR: And the time was being burned in the strangest possible place. Not in the S-S-H code itself. It was being burned inside a compression library called liblzma — part of a small, dull, deeply unglamorous utility called xz. A tool whose entire job in life is to squeeze files smaller. There was no good reason on Earth for the file-squeezing library to be doing extra work during a login. None.

NARRATOR: What Andres Freund had stumbled onto, by following five hundred milliseconds down a rabbit hole on a quiet afternoon, is now regarded as one of the most patient, most technically beautiful, and most nearly successful attacks the open-source world has ever seen. He had found a backdoor. A secret, hidden entrance, deliberately planted in software that was, at that very moment, on its way into nearly every Linux computer on the planet. And the people who put it there had spent the better part of three years getting it into position.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how that backdoor was built, the human being it was built around, and the half-second that gave it away.

QUESTION: Before any of that makes sense, we have to answer one thing: why would anyone go to enormous lengths to poison a tool whose only job is making files smaller?

NARRATOR: Because of where xz sits. To understand the scale of this, you have to understand that xz is not important the way a famous app is important. It is important the way a single bolt holding up a bridge is important. It is tiny. It is invisible. Almost no one has ever heard of it. And it is absolutely everywhere. The xz tools and their compression library, liblzma, are woven so deeply into the plumbing of Linux that countless other programs quietly depend on them, often without anyone realising. Linux, remember, is the operating system that runs the overwhelming majority of the servers that make up the internet — the web servers, the cloud, the back ends of the apps on your phone. The machines that run civilisation, basically, run Linux. And buried in their plumbing is xz.

NARRATOR: Now here is the part that turns a boring compression tool into the most dangerous thing in the building. Through a chain of dependencies — one piece of software relying on another, relying on another — liblzma gets pulled into the same running process as the S-S-H login server on a great many systems. We'll come back to exactly how that chain works, because it is genuinely clever, but hold the shape of it for now. The dull little file-squeezer ends up living inside the front door guard of the internet. So if you could poison xz — if you could get your own code into liblzma — you would be sitting right next to the mechanism that decides who is allowed to log into a huge fraction of the world's servers. You wouldn't be picking the lock. You'd be the lock.

NARRATOR: That is the prize. That is why someone would spend years on a file-squeezer.

NARRATOR: And to see how they got there, you have to meet the man who, for nearly twenty years, quietly held that bolt in place. His name is Lasse Collin. And xz, for most of its life, was essentially a one-man operation. Lasse maintained it in his spare time, for free, out of a sense of duty — because the world needed someone to, and he was the someone. This is the open-source bargain, and at its best it is one of the most beautiful arrangements in all of technology: enormous amounts of the software that everything runs on are maintained by individual volunteers, gifting their labour to all of humanity, asking nothing in return. Most of the time it works astonishingly well.

NARRATOR: But it has a shadow side, and the xz story is the shadow side made flesh. It means that the security of something millions upon millions of machines depend on can rest entirely on the shoulders, and the goodwill, and the energy, and the mental health, of a single tired human being. One person. Unpaid. Doing it after work. Because no one else stepped up.

NARRATOR: And by the early twenty-twenties, Lasse Collin was running on empty. He has said as much, in his own words, in public, on the project's mailing list. He had long-term mental health struggles. He had limited time and limited energy. He was, by his own account, a volunteer doing an unpaid hobby project that the entire world had quietly come to rely on, and he was exhausted by it. He was, in other words, the perfect target. Not because he did anything wrong — because he was overstretched, and alone, and kind.

NARRATOR: Which is exactly what the attacker was counting on.

NARRATOR: This is the part that lifts the xz story above an ordinary hack into something closer to a long con — a heist film where the break-in doesn't happen until the very last act. Because the attack did not begin with code. It began with a relationship. In October of two thousand twenty-one, a new contributor appeared in the xz project, using the name Jia Tan — written online with the handle J-T-seventy-five. And Jia Tan did everything right. Everything. The very first contribution, at the end of October twenty twenty-one, was tiny and harmless. Over the following months Jia Tan kept showing up — submitting modest, genuinely useful patches, fixing small things, being helpful, being competent, being utterly reliable. By February of twenty twenty-two, Jia Tan's first real changes were being merged into the project. A model contributor. The kind every overworked maintainer dreams of.

NARRATOR: And then, around Jia Tan, the pressure began.

NARRATOR: Starting in the spring of twenty twenty-two, other voices appeared on the project's public mailing list. New accounts. People we now believe, with very high confidence, were sock puppets — fake personas, fabricated identities, very possibly all operated by the same person or group running Jia Tan. And these accounts began applying a specific, surgical kind of pressure. Not insults. Not threats. Something far more effective against a tired, conscientious volunteer: disappointment.

NARRATOR: One of them used the name Jigar Kumar. In April of twenty twenty-two, Jigar Kumar wrote, complaining about the pace of the project: "Patches spend years on this mailing list. There is no reason to think anything is coming soon." A month later, in May: "Over one month and no closer to being merged. Not a surprise." And in June, the knife: "Progress will not happen until there is new maintainer. The current maintainer lost interest or doesn't care to maintain anymore."

NARRATOR: Listen to what that is doing. It is telling a man who is giving his free time to the world that he is failing the world. That he is the bottleneck. That the kindest thing he could do is step aside and let fresh hands take over. And it is all framed as concern — concern for the project, concern for the users, concern, eventually, even for him.

NARRATOR: Lasse Collin answered, and his reply is one of the most quietly heartbreaking things in this whole affair. In June twenty twenty-two he wrote: "I haven't lost interest but my ability to care has been fairly limited mostly due to longterm mental health issues. It's also good to keep in mind that this is an unpaid hobby project." That is a human being, under pressure, telling strangers the truth about his own exhaustion. And the attackers had built that pressure specifically to extract exactly that admission — to manufacture the moment where stepping back would feel like relief.

NARRATOR: And the moment they had that opening, another sock puppet moved in to close it. An account using the name Dennis Ens, just days later, wrote what reads on the surface like sympathy and underneath is a push: "I am sorry about your mental health issues, but its important to be aware of your own limits. Trying to maintain both means neither are maintained well." Be aware of your limits. Let go. There's someone right here who can help.

QUESTION: Put yourself in Lasse Collin's shoes for a second. You're doing this alone, unpaid, in your spare time, and you're genuinely struggling. People are telling you, over and over, that you're too slow, that the project is dying on your watch, that you're letting everyone down. And standing right there is Jia Tan — who has been helpful and steady and reliable for months, who clearly cares, and who is offering to take the weight off your shoulders. What do you do?

NARRATOR: He let them in. Of course he did. It was the humane, reasonable, generous thing to do, and there was no way for him to know it was a trap, because the trap had been built out of his own decency. Worn down, helped along, reassured, Lasse Collin gradually handed Jia Tan more and more responsibility. By the end of twenty twenty-two — around December — Jia Tan had commit access. The authority to put code directly into the project. And the sock puppets, their job done, went quiet. Jigar Kumar, Dennis Ens — once Jia Tan was inside, they simply faded away, like stagehands slipping off after the set is built.

NARRATOR: Over the following year, Jia Tan kept playing the long game. More good commits. More trust. More authority — including, crucially, the power to cut releases: to build and publish the official, packaged versions of xz that the rest of the world would download, trust, and install. This whole arc — from the first tiny harmless patch to holding the keys to the kingdom — took roughly two and a half years. Two and a half years of patience. Of building credibility one honest commit at a time. Of slow, deniable social pressure applied by invented people. All of it aimed at one single goal: to be trusted with the keys to a piece of the internet's plumbing.

NARRATOR: And in early twenty twenty-four, with the keys finally in hand, they used them.

QUESTION: So how do you actually hide a backdoor in software that thousands of people can read? Anyone can look at open-source code. How do you put poison in it without someone seeing?

NARRATOR: This is where the technical craft becomes genuinely stunning, and it hinges on a distinction most people don't know exists. When you use open-source software, there are really two versions of it floating around. There is the source code in the public repository — on GitHub, say — which anyone in the world can read, line by line, scrutinise, and review. That's the version everyone trusts because everyone can see it. And then there is the release tarball — the packaged-up bundle that the maintainer actually builds and publishes, the thing the Linux distributions download and turn into the software on your machine. In an ideal world, those two are identical: the tarball is just the repository, zipped up.

NARRATOR: They are not always identical. And Jia Tan knew that. The attacker's masterstroke was to make sure the malicious code was never in the public repository where people read it — and was only in the release tarball that people actually install. The poison was hiding in the gap between what everyone reviews and what everyone runs.

NARRATOR: But it gets cleverer, because even in the tarball, the bad code wasn't sitting there in plain, readable form for someone to stumble on. It was disguised as test data. Now, every serious software project ships with test files — sample inputs used to check that the program works correctly. And a compression tool, naturally, needs test files that are deliberately broken or corrupted, so it can check that it handles damaged input gracefully. Files with names like "bad corrupt lzma two" — junk, by design. Garbage that is supposed to look like garbage. Any reviewer glancing at a binary test file full of corrupted-looking compression data would see exactly what they expected to see — meaningless noise for testing — and move on. Who reads the test garbage?

NARRATOR: But it wasn't garbage. Hidden inside those innocent-looking corrupted test files was the real payload — the malicious code — chopped up and concealed so it read as noise. And the trigger that pulled it all together lived in the build process: the machinery that turns source code into the finished, installable program. Buried in a build script — a configuration file written in an old, crusty, almost-nobody-reads-it language called m4, in a file innocuously named "build to host" — was an instruction. During the build, and only during the build, that script would quietly reach into the fake test files, extract the hidden payload, stitch the pieces back together, and weave the resulting malicious code into the final compiled library.

NARRATOR: So follow the misdirection, because every layer was chosen to defeat a different kind of inspection. The source code people read? Clean. The test files people ignore? That's where the bomb is. The build script people skim? That's the fuse. The poison only assembles itself at the very last moment, during compilation, inside the packaged release that gets shipped to the world. Read the public source all day long and you would see nothing wrong, because the wrong thing wasn't there. It only existed in the finished product.

QUESTION: All right — so the poison gets into the compiled library. But xz squeezes files. How does poisoning a file-compression library let you take over the login system of the entire internet?

NARRATOR: This is the chain of dependencies I asked you to hold onto earlier, and it is a chain nobody intended to build. It happened by accident, through a series of perfectly reasonable decisions, which is what makes it so unnerving. Here is the path. On many Linux systems, the S-S-H login server — the program called sshd that guards remote access — is patched by the distribution to talk to systemd, which is the master program that manages services on the machine. To talk to systemd, sshd has to link against a library called libsystemd. And libsystemd, in turn, depends on liblzma — our compression library — to handle compressed data. So the chain runs: the login server pulls in libsystemd, libsystemd pulls in liblzma, and liblzma is xz.

NARRATOR: Read that chain again and feel the horror of it. Nobody ever decided that the file-compression library should be inside the login server. No one designed that. It is an emergent accident — three sensible links of a dependency chain that, added together, drag a humble compression tool right into the beating heart of the system that authenticates every remote login. The attacker didn't create that connection. They found it. They noticed that if you poison liblzma, your code ends up running inside sshd, the most security-critical process on the machine, on millions of servers — all by riding a dependency chain that the world had built for it, link by innocent link.

NARRATOR: And once the malicious code was running inside that process, it used a genuinely sophisticated technique to take control. There is a mechanism in the way Linux programs work called an IFUNC — an indirect function — which is, legitimately, a way for a program to choose at the last second which version of a function to use, depending on the hardware it finds itself on. It's a normal optimisation. The backdoor hijacked that mechanism. It used IFUNC resolution to quietly hook itself into the login process and reach out and replace one specific, critical function in the S-S-H server — the function involved in checking a login's cryptographic credentials, called R-S-A public decrypt. The legitimate function that helps decide whether your key is valid was swapped, at runtime, for the attacker's version.

NARRATOR: And here is what the attacker's version did. It lay in wait, watching every login attempt, looking for one specific secret signal — a particular cryptographic key that only the attacker possessed, an Ed448 key, mathematically impossible for anyone else to forge. To the entire rest of the world, the system behaved completely normally. Every legitimate user logged in exactly as before. There was no obvious change, no broken feature, nothing to notice — except, of course, that faint half-second of extra work the hook was doing on every connection. But to whoever held that one secret key, the backdoor swung the door wide open. It would take their input and pass it straight to the system to be executed — letting them run any command they wanted on the machine, with full control, bypassing every password, every key check, every protection. A skeleton key to a substantial fraction of the servers on the internet. And it was about to be shipped to the entire world inside a perfectly routine software update.

NARRATOR: And it got close. Frighteningly, genuinely, weeks-away close.

NARRATOR: The poisoned versions were given the numbers five point six point zero and five point six point one. Version five point six point zero went out on the twenty-fourth of February, twenty twenty-four — the day after the backdoor was committed. Five point six point one followed on the ninth of March, tidying up the attack. And these were not obscure releases sitting in a corner. They had already made their way into the testing and development branches of the major Linux distributions — the pre-release versions that real engineers run, and that, within a matter of weeks, would have flowed downstream into the stable releases that everyone runs.

NARRATOR: The roll call is chilling. The bad versions reached Debian's unstable and testing branches — Debian sid. They reached Fedora Rawhide and the Fedora forty beta. They reached openSUSE Tumbleweed. They reached Kali Linux, the distribution security professionals themselves use. They reached Arch Linux. These are some of the most important and most widely used flavours of Linux on Earth, and the backdoor was already inside their pipelines, lined up and waiting for the conveyor belt to carry it the last short distance into the hands of hundreds of millions of machines. By most expert estimates, we were weeks — maybe only weeks — away from this backdoor sitting on a meaningful fraction of the world's servers, with a foreign actor holding the only key.

NARRATOR: And the only reason — the entire reason — that it didn't happen, is that the backdoor was very, very slightly too slow.

NARRATOR: Remember the half-second. All that work the hook was doing — hijacking the function, watching the logins — cost about five hundred milliseconds. And one engineer, Andres Freund, doing something completely unrelated, benchmarking a database, happened to be paying close enough attention to wonder why his login had gotten a touch slower. The most patient, most sophisticated supply-chain attack in the history of software — two and a half years of social engineering, an exquisitely layered technical hide, a near-flawless escape from every kind of code review — was undone by a performance benchmark and a man who refused to ignore an anomaly that everyone else on Earth would have waved away.

NARRATOR: He chased it down, confirmed what he was looking at, and on the twenty-ninth of March, twenty twenty-four, he sent an email to a public security mailing list, calmly laying out what he had found. And the response was instant and global. Within hours, the security world understood the magnitude of what had nearly happened. The backdoored versions were pulled. Distributions ripped them out and reverted to clean releases. Investigators descended on every commit Jia Tan had ever made. The whole edifice of trust that the attacker had spent two and a half years constructing collapsed in a single day. The vulnerability was catalogued as C-V-E twenty twenty-four, dash, three-zero-nine-four, and rated ten out of ten — as severe as the scale goes. The world exhaled.

NARRATOR: As for who Jia Tan actually was — we don't know. The persona evaporated. The patience, the discipline, the resources, the multi-year time horizon, the deep technical sophistication, the willingness to invest years for a payoff: nearly everyone who has studied it believes this was not a lone hobbyist or an ordinary criminal, but a well-funded, professional operation, very likely a nation-state intelligence service. But there has been no confirmed attribution. The name Jia Tan is almost certainly fiction. The human being behind it walked away into the dark, and the rest of us are left holding the pieces of what they nearly pulled off.

QUESTION: So what does a near-miss actually teach us? Nothing blew up. The backdoor was caught. Why does this matter more than the attacks that succeeded?

NARRATOR: It matters more precisely because of how close it came, and because of what it tells us about where we are all standing. This is the cleanest example you will ever encounter of a software supply-chain attack. We tend to picture an attacker as someone breaking into our system from the outside — battering down the front door. But your software is not just the code you wrote. It is a vast, branching tree of other people's code: the libraries you depend on, and the libraries those libraries depend on, pulled in from all over the world, layer upon layer, most of which you have never read and never will. That entire tree is your supply chain. And every single link in it is a potential way in. The xz attacker didn't break down anyone's door. They poisoned something far upstream — something that you, and your operating system, and the login server itself, would all willingly invite inside, with full trust, as part of a routine, signed, official update. The hard lesson is this: the software you depend on is itself an attack surface, and your system is only ever as secure as the least secure thing you have built it on top of, all the way down.

NARRATOR: It also strikes at something deeper than technology, and this is the part I most want you to sit with. The attack didn't defeat a firewall or crack an encryption algorithm. It attacked trust itself. Open source runs on trust and good faith — the assumption that the people contributing are who they say they are and want what they say they want. That assumption is usually correct, and it is exactly why open source is, on the whole, more secure than closed software: anyone can read the code, so bugs and malice can be caught in the open, by many eyes. That openness is literally how this was eventually found. But the same model rests on volunteers who are real human beings — who can be befriended, flattered, pressured, exhausted, and manipulated. The attacker didn't out-code the community. They exploited the human dynamics around it: the loneliness of an unpaid maintainer, the social pressure of strangers' disappointment, the simple human instinct to accept help from someone who has been kind to you. Securing software, the xz story tells us, is not only an engineering problem. It is a social one.

NARRATOR: And notice exactly where, in the life of the software, this happened — because this is a point students and professionals alike consistently miss. This was not a flaw in how the code was originally written. The original xz, written by Lasse Collin, was fine. The malice was injected during maintenance — into a mature, established, deeply trusted project, through the slow erosion of who controlled it. Security is not a phase you complete at the start of a project and then tick off and forget. It has to live across the entire life of the software, including the long, quiet, boring years afterwards — because that established, who-would-ever-bother-attacking-this stage is exactly the stage a patient adversary chooses. They didn't strike when xz was new and watched. They struck when it was old and trusted and tired.

NARRATOR: And it is, finally, a story about why we review not just what code says, but how it is assembled. The malice was hidden in the build process and disguised as test data for one precise reason: the attacker understood that people review source code, but they rarely scrutinise the machinery that compiles it, and they almost never inspect binary test files. The defence this attack was designed to slip past has a name — build integrity. Making sure that the program you actually ship is genuinely built from the exact source that everyone reviewed, with nothing slipped in along the way. Reproducible builds. Careful control over who has the authority to cut a release. These things sound like bureaucracy. They are not. They are the precise locks this attacker spent two and a half years learning to pick.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A piece of the internet's invisible plumbing, maintained for free by one exhausted volunteer. A fake contributor who spent two and a half years being helpful, while invented strangers wore the real maintainer down with manufactured disappointment, until he handed over the keys. A backdoor hidden not in the code everyone reads but in the test files everyone ignores, assembled only at build time, riding an accidental dependency chain straight into the login server of the world. A skeleton key to hundreds of millions of machines, lined up in the release pipelines of nearly every major Linux distribution, weeks from going out to everyone. And the only thing standing between us and that — between the open internet and a foreign hand holding the master key to it — was one engineer who noticed that his login had gotten half a second slower, and could not let it go.

NARRATOR: Think about how thin that margin is. Two governments' worth of patience and craft, the most sophisticated supply-chain attack ever attempted, brought down by five hundred milliseconds and one curious mind. If the attacker's code had been just a little more efficient — if it had cost fifty milliseconds instead of five hundred — Andres Freund very likely never notices, and the backdoor ships, and we are living in a very different and much darker version of the present. We did not catch this because our defences were strong. We caught it because of luck, and one person's stubbornness about an anomaly.

NARRATOR: And that should change how you think about the systems you depend on. The internet does not run on the gleaming infrastructure of trillion-dollar companies alone. A staggering amount of it rests on the unglamorous, unpaid, unnoticed work of a handful of volunteers, holding up critical bolts in the dark, asking for nothing — and exhausted, and alone, and exactly the people a patient adversary will come for next. The xz backdoor was nearly the most consequential breach in the history of computing. It failed by half a second. Build your systems, and choose what you build them on, as if the next one won't.
