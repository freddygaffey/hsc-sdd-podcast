NARRATOR: Chapter twenty, first concept — A-I versus M-L. "A-I is the goal, M-L is one way." A-I is the broad field of building systems that behave intelligently; they may or may not learn. M-L is the subset of A-I that learns patterns from data and improves with experience. The dividing line is learning: a rule-based expert system follows hand-written rules and never learns; an M-L classifier learns its rules from data. Every M-L is A-I; not every A-I is M-L. The automation approaches: DevOps automates the build-test-deploy pipeline — we cash that in when we bridge to the project module. R-P-A is task-level, a robot mimicking a human at the user interface — brittle, it breaks when the interface changes. B-P-A is process-level, redesigning the whole workflow with deep integration and human approvals. And M-L augments automation by adding judgement — automation provides the hands, M-L provides the eyes.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty, training models — S-U-S-R, the four ways to train. Supervised: labelled data, input paired with the correct answer, learn-with-an-answer-key — spam filters, diagnosis, price prediction. Unsupervised: no labels, find the hidden structure yourself — clustering, customer segmentation. Semi-supervised: a few expensive labels plus a large unlabelled pool, used together — medical imaging. Reinforcement: no fixed dataset, an agent learns by trial and error from a reward signal — games, robotics, self-driving. The one question separating supervised from unsupervised: do you have labels? And the seed planted back there — supervised learning is only as good as its labels, garbage labels in, bias out — which paid off in the bias episode as labeler and historical bias.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty, applications — F-A-I: Forecasting and data analysis, Assistants, Image recognition. Forecasting is supervised regression on labelled history — it assumes the future resembles the past, which is why Zillow's house-pricing model over-bought and the unit collapsed when the market shifted. Assistants understand and respond to human language, and must cope with messy human behaviour. Image recognition runs on deep neural networks, usually supervised on large labelled image sets. The answer move students miss: name the application, the technique underneath, and the data it needs.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty, the design-and-analysis models — a tree is readable if-then questions; a net is weighted neurons. A decision tree predicts by asking a sequence of test questions, following branches root to leaf — R-N-L, Root, Node, Leaf — and it's interpretable: you can state exactly why it decided. When you trace one, give the path, not just the leaf. A neural network is many simple neurons in weighted layers; powerful but opaque, a black box. The exam question is the comparison — explainability versus power — and that trade-off ties straight to accountability and bias, because you can't answer for a decision you can't read.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-one, regression and the algorithm types — L-L-K: Linear regression, Logistic regression, K-nearest neighbour, plus polynomial. Linear predicts a number by fitting the best straight line. Logistic — despite the name — is classification: it predicts a yes-or-no using the sigmoid, the same activation a neuron uses. Polynomial fits a curve, and it's how you overfit. K-nearest neighbour predicts by finding the most similar training examples and voting. The marquee line: linear predicts a number, logistic predicts a yes-or-no. Built as object-oriented fit-and-predict classes — fit learns the parameters, predict applies them. And overfitting: a model that memorises the training data's noise and fails on new data — catch it with a train-test split, never evaluate on training data.

NARRATOR: One trap worth modelling right here, because it costs marks every year. Asked "what does logistic regression predict," a weak answer says "a number, because it's regression" — that loses the mark; it reads the name and stops. The strong answer: "logistic regression performs classification — it predicts a category, a yes-or-no, using the sigmoid function to output a probability; linear regression predicts the number." The mark is in naming classification and the sigmoid, not in the word regression.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-one, neural networks — P-L-A: Predict, Loss, Adjust, repeated over many epochs. A perceptron is a single neuron — weighted sum of inputs plus a bias, then an activation — roughly one logistic regression unit. A network stacks neurons into input, hidden, and output layers; layers learn features, edges then faces. The forward pass runs inputs through to a prediction — that's what "trace" questions test. Training is the P-L-A loop: predict with a forward pass, measure the loss against the known label, adjust every weight to reduce the error — technically backpropagation plus gradient descent. A big network overfits like a high-degree polynomial, so still use a train-test split.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-two, impact — S-P-E-E, "spee": Safety and access, People and skills, Efficiency and Environment, Economy and wealth. Five dimensions in four buckets. Safety: removes humans from hazards but adds new ones; access: enabling when inclusive, excluding when not. People and skills: employment is transformed, not just subtracted — displacement, new roles, reskilling. Efficiency and Environment: more output and less production waste, but a hardware footprint, e-waste, and the heavy energy and water cost of training large A-I models. Economy and wealth: a bigger economy but unequal distribution. The verb is assess, which is evaluate: always two-sided, always a judgement.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-two, human behaviour — P-S-C-B: Psychological responses, acute Stress response, Cultural protocols, Belief systems. The flip: not automation's impact on humans, but human behaviour's influence on the system, because these systems learn from human data and need human acceptance. Psychology: trust, control, cognitive load — design for transparency and override. Stress: distorts behaviour and data, so a model trained only on calm data fails the stressed user when they need it most. Culture: norms and privacy expectations vary, including Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property protocols — localise and test across cultures. Belief: technology, privacy, human-agency, and fairness beliefs decide acceptance — offer choice, oversight, and data minimisation. The thesis: A-I adapts to the human, not the human to the A-I.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-two, bias — S-H-L-M, "SHaLuM": Sampling, Historical, Labeler, Measurement. Sampling: groups missing from the data. Historical: a faithful record of an unjust past — and more data won't fix it. Labeler: the humans' labels carry their lens. Measurement: a flawed or unequal proxy. The myth to kill: the algorithm is not neutral, it mirrors and amplifies its data. Mitigation is D-R-F: Diverse data at the source, Reweight in training, Fairness metrics measured per group — because high overall accuracy can still hide deep unfairness to a minority. Plus accountability: model cards and data provenance make bias visible and answerable. That's the full module reloaded. Now we earn the marks — integrated questions.

NARRATOR: Now the integrated scenario, the centrepiece — real H-S-C extended questions span the whole module in one stimulus. The stimulus: a government employment agency wants an automated tool to screen job applicants and recommend who to interview. They have ten years of the agency's own past hiring data. Walk the whole module through it. First, the technique. This is supervised learning, S-U-S-R — labelled data, past applicants paired with the known outcome, hired or not. The task is classification — a yes-or-no recommendation — so the natural algorithm is logistic regression from L-L-K, possibly a neural network if the data's complex; if so, expect the explainability cost. Build it as an object-oriented fit-and-predict model and guard against overfitting with a train-test split — never judge it on the data it trained on.

NARRATOR: Same scenario, now the human and ethics layers, where the marks concentrate. Human behaviour, P-S-C-B: applicants and hiring managers respond psychologically to an automated screen — they'll distrust an opaque reject, so the tool should explain its recommendations and keep a human override; and cultural and belief factors mean backgrounds vary, so a one-size design will misjudge some. Then bias, S-H-L-M, the heart of it: training on ten years of the agency's own hiring history is a textbook recipe for historical bias — if the agency historically favoured certain groups, the model learns and amplifies that, exactly like the Amazon recruiting tool that penalised résumés mentioning "women's" and was scrapped. There's sampling bias if some groups are under-represented in the applicant history, and labeler bias in how "good candidate" was judged. The algorithm isn't neutral — it mirrors the data. Mitigate with D-R-F: more diverse and contemporary data, reweight under-represented groups, fairness metrics measured per group, not just overall accuracy. Then impact, S-P-E-E: this is an automated decision about people's livelihoods — the People-and-skills and individual-equity dimensions — so weigh the efficiency gain against the harm of unfair rejection. And accountability: publish a model card, document data provenance so the tool can be audited, and keep a human in the loop for the final decision. That single answer — technique, then human factors, then bias, then impact, then accountability — uses six of the eleven mnemonics, and that breadth is what an extended question rewards.

QUESTION: Your turn to integrate. A hospital wants an A-I to help triage patients in the emergency department. In one structured answer, identify the training model and a suitable algorithm, then evaluate two ethical risks and how you'd address them. Pause the player, plan across the module, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Technique: supervised learning, S-U-S-R — labelled historical cases, symptoms paired with urgency outcomes; a classification task, so logistic regression or a neural network, built as an object-oriented model and validated on a held-out test set to avoid overfitting. Ethical risk one, bias, S-H-L-M: if the training data under-represents certain groups, that's sampling bias; if it reflects unequal historical care, historical bias — the model could under-triage a group, a life-threatening unfair outcome; address it with D-R-F, diverse data, reweighting, and per-group fairness metrics, plus a model card disclosing per-group performance. Ethical risk two, the acute stress response and human factors from P-S-C-B: an emergency is high-stress, so a model trained on calm, complete data may fail when staff and patients are stressed and inputs are rushed and incomplete — exactly when it's needed most; address it by training on realistic high-pressure data, keeping a clinician override so the A-I assists rather than replaces — meaningful human oversight and accountability — and designing a clear, low-cognitive-load interface. Judgement: the tool can improve speed and consistency, an efficiency gain under S-P-E-E, but only if bias and stress-failure are actively managed and a human stays accountable for the decision. Technique plus two evaluated risks with concrete fixes and a judgement — full marks.

NARRATOR: Now the traps, gathered in one place, because cross-topic confusion is where marks leak in this module. Trap one: A-I equals M-L. No — M-L is a subset of A-I; the dividing line is learning. Trap two: supervised equals unsupervised, or reinforcement is supervised. The only question for supervised versus unsupervised is do you have labels; and reinforcement learns from a reward after acting, not from a labelled answer key. Trap three: logistic regression predicts numbers. No — despite the name, logistic is classification, a yes-or-no; linear predicts the number. Trap four: "linear is a line, logistic is a curve" — wrong, polynomial is the curve, and polynomial is how you overfit. Trap five: a neural network is a brain — no, it's layers of weighted sums and activations, maths not thought. Trap six, in a trace: stating only the leaf — give the whole path. Trap seven, in an impact answer: being one-sided, "robots take jobs" — assess means both sides and a judgement, and employment is transformed not just subtracted. Trap eight: treating all users as uniform — human behaviour is varied and context-dependent. And trap nine, the big one: "the algorithm is neutral" — it mirrors and amplifies its data; and "just collect more data fixes bias" — not if the history itself is biased.

QUESTION: Quick-fire distinguish round, three in a row, the kind that opens an exam. Pause after each and answer before I do. First: distinguish artificial intelligence from machine learning.

NARRATOR: Model answer: artificial intelligence is the broad field of building systems that behave intelligently, which may or may not learn; machine learning is a subset of A-I in which the system learns patterns from data and improves with experience. The dividing line is learning — hand-written rules versus patterns learned from data. "A-I is the goal, M-L is one way."

QUESTION: Second: distinguish linear regression from logistic regression.

NARRATOR: Model answer: linear regression predicts a continuous number by fitting the best straight line — for example, predicting a house price. Logistic regression, despite its name, performs classification — it predicts a category, a yes-or-no, using the sigmoid function to output a probability — for example, spam or not spam. Linear predicts a number; logistic predicts a yes-or-no. The trap is the name: logistic is not number-prediction.

QUESTION: Third: distinguish historical bias from sampling bias.

NARRATOR: Model answer: sampling bias occurs when the training data does not represent the real population because some groups are missing or under-represented — for example, a face-recognition model trained mostly on one skin tone. Historical bias occurs when the data accurately reflects a past that was itself unequal, so the model learns and perpetuates that discrimination — for example, a hiring model trained on a male-dominated hiring history. The key difference: sampling bias is about who's in the data; historical bias is about the data being a faithful record of an unjust world — and more data won't fix historical bias.

QUESTION: Question one, the extended one. A retail company wants to build a machine learning system that recommends products to customers and automatically adjusts prices. Recommend an appropriate training model and algorithm, then evaluate the social, ethical, and bias considerations of deploying it. Pause, plan across the module, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Technique: supervised learning, S-U-S-R — labelled history of customer behaviour and purchases. Recommendation can use K-nearest neighbour from L-L-K, finding similar customers; the price-adjustment is a forecasting and regression problem — linear or polynomial regression predicting a numeric price, watching for overfitting with a train-test split. Build it as object-oriented fit-and-predict models. Now the evaluation. Bias, S-H-L-M: if the data over-represents some customer groups that's sampling bias, and historical purchasing patterns may carry historical bias, so recommendations and prices could disadvantage certain groups — charging more or offering worse deals to a particular demographic; mitigate with D-R-F, diverse data, reweighting, and fairness metrics across groups. Human behaviour, P-S-C-B: customers respond psychologically and by belief — opaque, personalised pricing erodes trust and raises fairness and privacy concerns, so be transparent and minimise data collection. Impact, S-P-E-E: efficiency and economic gains for the company, but a distribution-of-wealth and individual-fairness concern if the system exploits vulnerable customers. Accountability: document the model with a model card and keep the pricing logic auditable. Judgement: the system can increase sales and efficiency, but only with active bias mitigation, transparency, and fairness safeguards is its deployment ethically justified. A full-mark answer names the technique, then evaluates bias, human, and impact factors two-sidedly to a judgement.

QUESTION: Question two. Compare decision trees and neural networks, referring to explainability. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: a decision tree predicts by following a sequence of readable if-then questions from root to leaf, so it is interpretable — you can state exactly why it reached a decision — but it is less powerful on complex tasks. A neural network is many weighted neurons in layers; it is powerful, especially for tasks like image recognition, but it is a black box — its decision is spread across many weights with no human-readable rule. So the trade-off is explainability versus power: trees are transparent but limited, networks are powerful but opaque. Because explainability supports accountability and helps detect bias, an interpretable model like a tree is often preferred for high-stakes or regulated decisions. Naming the mechanism of each and stating the explainability-versus-power trade-off with its consequence is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Identify the four sources of bias in machine learning and, for one, explain a mitigation. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the four sources are sampling bias, historical bias, labeler bias, and measurement bias — the hook is S-H-L-M. Take sampling bias, where groups are under-represented in the training data: a mitigation is to collect more diverse and representative data using stratified or quota sampling so every group is properly counted — the D in D-R-F — and to verify the result by measuring fairness across groups rather than relying on overall accuracy. Naming all four sources and giving a concrete, source-appropriate mitigation is the mark.
