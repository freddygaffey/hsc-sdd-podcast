NARRATOR: It is a Friday afternoon in May. A nurse in an English hospital walks up to a computer to pull up a patient's blood results. She moves the mouse. And the screen turns red. Not an error message. Not a frozen window. A wall of red, with a padlock, and a sentence that does not belong in a hospital: Oops, your important files are encrypted. Below it, two countdown clocks are ticking. One counts down to the moment the ransom doubles. The other counts down to the moment your files are deleted forever. And in the middle of it all, a demand. Pay three hundred dollars. In Bitcoin. To strangers you will never meet.

NARRATOR: She tries another machine. Red. The one at the nurses' station. Red. Down the corridor, in radiology, in pathology, in the room where they run the M-R-I scanner, screen after screen is going dark and then bleeding red. This is not one broken computer. This is the building. And it is not only this building.

NARRATOR: That same afternoon, the same red screen was appearing on machines in Spain, in Russia, in Germany, in China, in factories and phone companies and government offices on nearly every continent on Earth. It was spreading faster than anyone could track. Within a single day it would touch something on the order of two to three hundred thousand computers across roughly a hundred and fifty countries. People who study malware for a living sat at their desks and watched a map light up in real time, and many of them said the same thing out loud: I have never seen anything move this fast.

NARRATOR: The thing on the map had a name. WannaCry. And on the twelfth of May, two thousand seventeen, it did something that ransomware had almost never done before. It walked into hospitals, and it turned off the machines that keep people alive.

NARRATOR: This is the story of that day. It is a story about a weapon built by a superpower and then stolen. About a patch that already existed and a door that nobody bolted. And about a twenty-two-year-old who, almost by accident, from his bedroom, stopped it for ten dollars and sixty-nine cents. To understand how all of that fits together, we have to start with what WannaCry actually was, because it was not one thing. It was two terrible things welded into one.

QUESTION: So what is it, exactly, that makes a piece of software able to take a hospital hostage and spread across the planet in an afternoon?

NARRATOR: Start with the first half. The half that holds you to ransom. This is ransomware. Malicious software that finds your files and encrypts them. Encryption is just maths. It scrambles your documents, your databases, your patient records, your scans, using a secret key. With the key, the scrambling reverses and everything comes back. Without the key, your files are noise. Random bytes. And the whole, vicious idea of ransomware is simple. The attacker takes your key, and they will sell it back to you. WannaCry generated a unique key for each machine it hit, encrypted that machine's files, and then locked the key away behind encryption that only the attackers could undo. Your data was still sitting right there on the disk. You just could not read a single byte of it.

NARRATOR: By twenty-seventeen, ransomware was, sadly, ordinary. Criminals had been running this hustle for years. But ransomware normally has a weakness, and the weakness is you. It needs a human to make a mistake. Somebody clicks a link in an email that looks like an invoice. Somebody opens an attachment from a delivery company they never ordered from. One bad click, one infected machine. To take down an entire hospital, the old way, you would need every single person in that hospital to click the same bad link on the same afternoon. That does not happen. Which is exactly why the second half of WannaCry was so dangerous.

NARRATOR: Because WannaCry did not need you to click anything.

NARRATOR: The second half was a worm. And a worm is malware that moves on its own. No email. No click. No human. Once a worm is on a single machine inside a network, it looks around. It scans for other computers it can reach. It probes them on the network, looking for the ones that are vulnerable, the ones still listening on the file-sharing port with the unpatched flaw. It finds them, forces its way in, copies itself across, and then those new machines start scanning too. One becomes ten. Ten become a hundred. A hundred become a thousand. It is a chain reaction, and it runs at the speed of the network, which is to say, in minutes. WannaCry scanned not only the local network but reached out across the wider internet too, hunting for any exposed machine anywhere with that port open to the world. This is why it could walk into a hospital where exactly one machine got infected and, within the hour, own the whole place. The nurse did not click anything. Nobody in that hospital did anything wrong that afternoon. The worm let itself in.

QUESTION: But a worm cannot just wish its way onto a fresh computer. To force itself across a network with nobody clicking anything, it needs a key to the lock. A flaw it can use to run its own code on a stranger's machine, over the wire, uninvited. So where did a piece of criminal ransomware get a weapon that powerful?

NARRATOR: Here is where this stops sounding like a crime story and starts sounding like a spy novel. Because the weapon WannaCry used was not built by criminals. It was built by the United States government. By the National Security Agency. The N-S-A.

NARRATOR: The weapon has a name too. EternalBlue. And to feel why it was so devastating, you need to understand the lock it picked. Windows computers have an old, built-in way of sharing files and printers with each other across a local network. The protocol is called S-M-B. Server Message Block. When you open a shared folder on a colleague's machine, or send a document to the printer down the hall, you are very likely speaking S-M-B. It listens, quietly, on network port four-four-five, on millions and millions of Windows machines, by default, all the time. It is plumbing. Nobody thinks about it. And in the oldest version of that plumbing, version one of S-M-B, there was a flaw.

NARRATOR: The flaw lived deep in the part of Windows that handles those file-sharing messages, in the kernel, the most privileged, most trusted core of the operating system. EternalBlue exploited a chain of bugs in how that code parsed incoming requests. Let me give you the shape of it, because the precision is the point. When an S-M-B request comes in carrying certain extended file attributes, Windows has to convert them from an old format into a newer one, and to do that it first calculates how much memory to set aside. There was an arithmetic mistake in that calculation, an integer overflow, that made Windows reserve a chunk of memory that was too small. Then the attacker sends more data than that chunk can hold. The data spills out past the edge, overwriting memory that belonged to something else. That is a buffer overflow, and it is happening inside the kernel.

NARRATOR: The attackers compounded it with a second trick, exploiting a subtle disagreement between two kinds of S-M-B transaction message about how big a request was supposed to be. Send one type immediately before the other, in just the wrong order, and Windows allocates memory based on one and copies based on the other. And then a third technique, spraying the memory with carefully arranged data so that when the overflow corrupts the kernel, it corrupts it in a controlled, predictable way, landing the attacker's own instructions in a place where the machine will run them. Put those together and you have the holy grail of attacks. Remote code execution. With no login. No password. No permission. No click. You send a stranger's computer a few malformed network packets, and you are now running your own program inside the most trusted part of their operating system. That flaw was eventually catalogued with the identifier C-V-E twenty-seventeen, dash, zero-one-four-four.

NARRATOR: And once EternalBlue had kicked the door open, a second N-S-A tool walked through it. This one was called DoublePulsar. A backdoor. A quiet loader that sits in memory, waiting, and lets the attacker inject whatever payload they like into the compromised machine. EternalBlue was the break-in. DoublePulsar was the delivery van. Together they were a complete, professional, intelligence-grade attack chain. And that is exactly what they were built to be, by a government, for use against its targets.

NARRATOR: The N-S-A had found this flaw in Windows. And then it made a choice. It did not tell Microsoft. It did not say, there is a hole in the file-sharing service that runs on hundreds of millions of computers, including American hospitals and American businesses, you should fix it. Instead, it kept the flaw secret, and it kept the exploit, because a secret hole that nobody else knows about is a weapon. For years, EternalBlue sat in the N-S-A's arsenal, a skeleton key to a huge fraction of the world's computers.

NARRATOR: And then they lost it.

QUESTION: So how does a secret weapon, built and hoarded by the most powerful signals-intelligence agency on the planet, end up in the hands of whoever wants it?

NARRATOR: In the summer of twenty-sixteen, a group nobody had heard of surfaced online, calling themselves the Shadow Brokers. Their broken-English announcements claimed they had stolen a cache of cyber-weapons from an elite N-S-A hacking unit, and they wanted to sell it. At first it read like a hoax or a scam. It was not. Over the following months they leaked tool after tool. And on the fourteenth of April, two thousand seventeen, the Shadow Brokers released their fifth and most damaging dump. Inside it were dozens of exploits and hacking utilities. Inside it was EternalBlue. Inside it was DoublePulsar. The government's skeleton key was now a free download, sitting on the public internet, available to any criminal, any vandal, any teenager, anyone at all.

NARRATOR: The clock was now running. And somebody, somewhere, did the obvious, terrible thing. They took EternalBlue, the worm-grade exploit that could leap from machine to machine with nobody clicking anything, and they bolted it onto a piece of ordinary ransomware that locks your files and demands Bitcoin. The self-spreading weapon married the money-making payload. WannaCry was born. And then, on the twelfth of May, they let it go.

NARRATOR: Now, here is the detail that turns this whole story from a tragedy into something that should make you grind your teeth. Because EternalBlue did not have to work. Microsoft had already closed the hole.

NARRATOR: A full month before WannaCry, on the fourteenth of March, two thousand seventeen, Microsoft had released a security update. Its catalogue number was M-S seventeen, dash, zero-one-zero. And it patched, precisely, the S-M-B flaw that EternalBlue relied on. Microsoft rated it critical. They knew. In fact, Microsoft knew so well how dangerous this was that, when WannaCry hit, they did something almost unheard of. They went back and released emergency patches for versions of Windows that were so old they were officially out of support, like Windows X-P, machines they had stopped protecting years earlier. They threw a lifeline to systems they had already abandoned, because the danger was that severe.

NARRATOR: So sit with the arithmetic of it. The fix existed. The fix was free. The fix had been available for almost two months by the day of the attack. And every single one of the hundreds of thousands of machines WannaCry infected was a machine that had not installed it. According to Kaspersky's analysis, ninety-eight percent of the infected computers were running Windows 7, a version Microsoft fully supported and had patched back in March. The lock had been fitted. The bolt was sitting right there beside the door. And on those machines, nobody had thrown it.

QUESTION: Why not? If the patch was free and the warning was clear, how does this happen to two hundred thousand machines at once?

NARRATOR: Because patching, in the real world, is hard in a way that has nothing to do with the patch. Large organisations run thousands of computers, and they often do not have a complete, accurate list of every one of them. Some machines run software that breaks if the operating system underneath it changes, so updates get postponed, then postponed again. Some run on hardware so old it cannot easily be touched. Some sit inside medical devices or factory controllers where a reboot means real disruption, so they are left alone for years. And in the National Health Service, in particular, there were a great many ageing machines, many still running old Windows, scattered across hundreds of separate organisations with no single hand on the wheel. The patch had been published. Getting it onto every last machine, before an attacker weaponised the hole, was the part that did not happen.

NARRATOR: And so on the twelfth of May, the worm found all those open doors at once.

NARRATOR: The first detections came around quarter to eight in the morning, U-T-C. From there it was a wildfire. In England and Scotland, the National Health Service was hit hard, and the world watched a health system stagger in real time. Of the two hundred and thirty-six hospital trusts in England, at least eighty were affected. Thirty-four of them were directly infected and locked out of their own devices. Beyond the hospitals, hundreds more primary-care organisations were caught, including hundreds of G-P surgeries, the family doctors. Staff lost access to patient records, to test results, to the systems that book appointments. Some sites lost the computers that drove diagnostic equipment, the machines behind scans and bloodwork. Ambulances were diverted away from affected hospitals, because a hospital that cannot see your records cannot safely take you. Around nineteen thousand appointments and operations were cancelled. This was not data on a spreadsheet. This was a child's surgery postponed. A cancer scan that did not happen that week. Care, denied, because the computers had been taken hostage.

NARRATOR: And it was global. In Spain, the telecoms giant Telefónica was hit. In France and Britain, Renault and Nissan halted car-manufacturing lines, because the worm reached the factory floor. The German railway operator, Deutsche Bahn, watched WannaCry's red screen appear on the departure boards in its stations. FedEx was struck. Honda. Universities, banks, government ministries, in country after country after country. The estimates of the total economic damage ran from hundreds of millions of dollars into the billions, with one widely cited figure reaching as high as four billion. All of it unleashed by a tool a government built and then could not keep hold of.

NARRATOR: WannaCry could have run for weeks. It could have been one of the defining catastrophes of the decade. And the reason it was not comes down to one person, on holiday, who could not stop poking at it.

NARRATOR: His name is Marcus Hutchins. In May twenty-seventeen he was twenty-two years old, living in the seaside town of Ilfracombe, in Devon, in the south-west of England. Online, the security world knew him only by a handle. MalwareTech. He worked for a firm called Kryptos Logic, and he spent his days doing exactly what his handle suggests. Taking malware apart to see what makes it tick. That Friday, he was actually on vacation. But word of WannaCry was tearing across the internet, and he could not leave it alone. So he sat down in his bedroom, pulled a live sample of the worm into his analysis lab, and started reverse-engineering it. Reading what it does, step by step, instruction by instruction.

NARRATOR: And he noticed something strange. Before WannaCry did any of its damage on a freshly infected machine, before it encrypted a single file or scanned for a single neighbour, it did one curious thing first. It tried to phone home to a particular web address. A long, ridiculous, nonsensical string of letters. Something a cat would type if it walked across a keyboard. Hutchins looked at this domain in the code, and he did the simplest thing in the world. He checked whether anyone owned it.

NARRATOR: Nobody did. The domain was unregistered. It did not exist.

NARRATOR: So Hutchins, mostly out of professional habit, partly out of curiosity, did what malware researchers do when they find an unregistered domain in a live sample. He bought it. Ten dollars and sixty-nine cents. He registered that absurd string of letters in his own name and pointed it at a server, intending only to watch the traffic, to see how many infected machines around the world would try to call this address, like setting up a microphone to count the screams.

NARRATOR: And WannaCry stopped.

NARRATOR: Not slowed. Stopped. Across the world, the spread fell off a cliff. The halt registered in the data at around three in the afternoon, U-T-C, just hours after the first detections that morning. Hutchins, by complete accident, had flipped a switch he did not know was there.

QUESTION: How can registering a meaningless web address, for the price of a sandwich, switch off a worm tearing through a hundred and fifty countries?

NARRATOR: Because that phone-home was a kill switch, hard-wired into the malware itself. WannaCry's logic, on every machine, went like this. First, try to reach that nonsense domain. If you cannot reach it, if the address does not resolve, carry on. Encrypt the files. Spread to the neighbours. But if you can reach it, if the domain answers, stop. Do nothing. Shut down. As long as that domain did not exist, every copy of WannaCry failed to reach it, and therefore every copy kept attacking. The instant Hutchins registered the domain and stood up a server to answer, the address suddenly existed. And now every fresh copy of WannaCry, everywhere on Earth, dutifully checked the address, found that it answered, and obediently switched itself off. One man, one ten-dollar domain, and the global engine of the worm seized up.

NARRATOR: Nobody is entirely certain why the authors built that switch in. The leading theory is that it was an anti-analysis trick. Researchers often run malware inside sandboxes, controlled fake environments that, to fool the malware, answer every web request with yes, I am here. The thinking goes that WannaCry's authors used the unregistered domain as a tripwire. If even a domain that should not exist answers, I must be inside a researcher's trap, so I will lie low. They built it to hide from people like Marcus Hutchins. And it became the very lever Marcus Hutchins used to bring the whole thing down.

NARRATOR: The fight was not quite over, and this is the part people forget. Registering the domain did not undo a single infection. Every machine already encrypted stayed encrypted. The kill switch only stopped new attacks. And it was fragile, because it depended entirely on that one domain staying reachable on the internet. Whoever was behind WannaCry, or others hoping to restart the chaos, understood that immediately. About a week later, on the nineteenth of May, attackers tried to take the kill-switch domain offline by drowning it in junk traffic, a denial-of-service attack using a variant of the Mirai botnet, an army of hijacked internet-connected devices all hammering that single address at once. Because if they could knock the domain off the internet, even for a while, then WannaCry would once again fail to reach it, follow its own logic, and wake back up. Hutchins and his colleagues at Kryptos Logic fought to keep it alive. Within days they moved the domain behind a cached, distributed version of the site, infrastructure built to soak up enormous floods of traffic without falling over. The kill switch held. The worm stayed dead.

[pause]

NARRATOR: There is a bitter coda to Marcus Hutchins' story, and it deserves telling, because heroes in this field are rarely simple. A few months later, in August twenty-seventeen, Hutchins flew to Las Vegas for a security conference. As he prepared to fly home, the F-B-I arrested him. The charges had nothing to do with WannaCry. They concerned a piece of banking malware called Kronos, which prosecutors said he had helped create years earlier, as a teenager, before he turned his skills to defence. He eventually pleaded guilty to two charges. And the judge, weighing the young man's history against what he had become and what he had done that Friday in May, sentenced him to time served and supervised release. No prison. The man who stopped WannaCry walked free. The boy who once wrote malware, and the man who saved the world's hospitals from it, were the same person. People are not always one thing.

NARRATOR: As for who launched WannaCry in the first place, investigators followed the fingerprints in the code, the reused fragments, the patterns, and at the end of twenty-seventeen the governments of the United States and the United Kingdom publicly attributed the attack to North Korea, to a state-linked group known as the Lazarus Group. North Korea denied it. But for all its destruction, WannaCry was, in money terms, a flop for its makers. Because of the kill switch and the chaos, only a few hundred victims ever paid, just over three hundred and twenty payments, totalling around a hundred and thirty thousand dollars in Bitcoin. A few billion dollars of damage to the world. A hundred and thirty thousand dollars in the attackers' pockets. A grotesque, lopsided ledger.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So let us pull the lessons out of this, because every link in this chain is a place where it could have been stopped, and each one is a principle worth carrying with you.

NARRATOR: Start with the most maddening one, because it is the most important. Patch management. The fix, M-S seventeen, dash, zero-one-zero, had existed for two months. It would have stopped WannaCry dead. Every infection was an unpatched machine. And that is the brutal pattern running through so many real-world disasters. The most devastating attacks usually do not exploit some exotic, unknown flaw. They exploit a known vulnerability for which a fix already exists, against organisations that had not applied it yet. The glamorous part of security is finding new holes. The part that actually saves you is the unglamorous discipline. Knowing every single machine you run. Keeping them updated. Applying patches promptly, before an attacker weaponises a hole the vendor already closed. On that Friday in May, patch management was, quite literally, the line between a hospital that worked and one that turned people away.

NARRATOR: Then think about what WannaCry actually destroyed. Ransomware does not steal your data. It denies you access to it. The data was still sitting on those hospital disks the whole time, encrypted, present, and utterly useless. The thing it attacks is availability. Information security rests on three pillars. Confidentiality, keeping data secret. Integrity, keeping data correct and unaltered. And availability, being able to reach your data when you need it. We talk most about the first two. WannaCry is the argument for the third. Because when a hospital loses availability, surgeries get cancelled and ambulances get turned away. Downtime is not a line item on a spreadsheet. It can be a missed diagnosis, a delayed operation, real harm to real people. Availability is not a footnote. It is a pillar.

NARRATOR: Then the lesson about what you do when prevention fails. And it will fail, because no defence is perfect. The question that separates a survivable incident from a catastrophe is brutally simple. Can you recover? With ransomware, the answer comes down to one thing. Backups. If your files are encrypted and held hostage, but you have a recent, clean, offline copy, then the attacker's entire business model collapses. You do not negotiate. You do not pay. You wipe the infected machines, you restore from backup, and you carry on. Ransomware, in its deepest sense, is an attack on organisations without good backups. Which is why the discipline that matters here has a name. Business continuity and disaster recovery. Planning, in advance, how you keep operating and how you restore your systems after a disaster, with backups that are recent, that are tested, and that are isolated, kept somewhere the worm cannot reach and re-encrypt them. The organisations that recovered fastest from WannaCry were the ones who could restore what they had and refuse to deal. Backups are not a chore for the day a hard drive dies. They are your answer to extortion.

NARRATOR: And then there is the thread that ties the whole story together. Look at the chain of failure. A government found a flaw and hoarded it as a weapon instead of disclosing it so it could be fixed. The weapon was stolen and dumped on the open internet. Criminals strapped it to ransomware. And it tore through machines that had never installed a patch that had been available for two months, on networks without tested, isolated backups to fall back on. Every one of those was a separate failure, and every one of them was a chance to stop this. The flaw could have been responsibly disclosed. The patch could have been applied. The backups could have made the ransom meaningless. WannaCry succeeded because, on a global scale, all of those defences failed at the same time.

NARRATOR: So the lesson is not any single fix. It is defence in depth. The understanding that prevention will sometimes fail, and that your job is to make sure that when it does, you can still detect the attack, contain it, and recover from it. You build layers, because you assume any one of them can be breached. Because here is the asymmetry at the dark heart of security, the thing WannaCry made impossible to ignore. The attacker only has to find one way in, on one day. You have to hold every door, every machine, every day. The patch from March was already on the shelf. The kill switch cost ten dollars and sixty-nine cents. The difference between catastrophe and a footnote can be that thin. And the only way to stay on the right side of it is to do the boring, relentless, unglamorous work, before the screen turns red.
