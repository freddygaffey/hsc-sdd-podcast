NARRATOR: On the afternoon of Friday the twelfth of May, two thousand seventeen, screens started going red in hospitals across England. A doctor would walk up to a computer to pull a patient's records, and instead there'd be a message, stark against a red background, with a countdown timer ticking down. Your files have been encrypted, it said. To get them back, pay six hundred dollars in Bitcoin. And the timer counted toward the moment the price would double — and then toward the moment your files would be gone forever.

NARRATOR: This wasn't one computer. Within hours it was thousands. Across the United Kingdom's National Health Service, machine after machine fell dark and red. Hospitals couldn't access patient histories. Appointment systems froze. Some sites lost the equipment that ran scans and tests. Ambulances were diverted away from affected hospitals, because those hospitals could no longer safely take patients. Surgeries were cancelled. Tens of thousands of appointments, gone. This wasn't an abstract data breach in a corporate server room. This was people who needed medical care not getting it, because the computers had been taken hostage.

NARRATOR: And it wasn't only Britain. The same red screens were blooming across the world that day — in Spain, where the telecoms giant Telefónica was hit; in Russia; in factories that halted their production lines; in companies on nearly every continent. By the time it was contained, it had infected something like two hundred thousand computers across roughly a hundred and fifty countries, in a matter of days. The world was watching the fastest-spreading malware attack in a very long time, happening live.

NARRATOR: The thing tearing through all those machines had a name: WannaCry. And to understand how it moved so fast, you have to see that WannaCry was two nasty things bolted together. The first was ransomware — malicious software that encrypts your files and demands payment for the key to unlock them. By twenty-seventeen, that was sadly common. Ransomware normally spreads the boring way: someone clicks a bad link in an email, or opens an infected attachment, and infects their own machine. One careless click, one machine.

NARRATOR: But WannaCry didn't need you to click anything. That's the second, far more dangerous half of it. WannaCry was also a worm — malware that spreads by itself, machine to machine, with no human help at all. Once it was on one computer inside a network, it reached out across that network, found other vulnerable computers, and infected them too — and those infected more, and more, in a chain reaction. No clicking. No email. Just one infected machine quietly turning into ten, then a hundred, then a thousand, automatically, in minutes.

QUESTION: For a worm to leap from one machine to the next without anyone clicking anything, it needs a way in — some flaw it can use to force its way onto a fresh computer over the network. So where did WannaCry get a weapon that powerful?

NARRATOR: Here's where the story takes a turn that sounds like fiction. The weapon WannaCry used was called EternalBlue, and it wasn't built by ordinary criminals. It was built by a national intelligence agency — the United States National Security Agency. EternalBlue exploited a flaw in a Windows feature called S-M-B — the system Windows computers use to share files and printers over a local network. The flaw let an attacker send specially crafted messages to that file-sharing service and, through it, run their own code on the target machine, remotely, with no login and no permission. A perfect tool for jumping silently from computer to computer. The N-S-A had discovered this flaw and, instead of reporting it so it could be fixed, quietly kept it as a cyber-weapon of its own.

NARRATOR: And then they lost control of it. In early twenty-seventeen, a mysterious group calling themselves the Shadow Brokers got hold of a cache of the N-S-A's hacking tools and dumped them onto the public internet for anyone to grab. EternalBlue, a weapon built by a superpower, was suddenly free for the taking. It didn't take long for someone to bolt it onto a piece of ransomware and let it loose. The government's secret skeleton key had been stolen and handed to the world, and the world used it to lock hospitals.

NARRATOR: But here is the detail that turns this from a tragedy into an infuriating one. Microsoft had already fixed the flaw. A full month before WannaCry struck — back in March twenty-seventeen — Microsoft released a security patch, known as M-S seventeen, dash, zero-one-zero, that sealed exactly the hole EternalBlue used. They'd even patched versions of Windows so old they were officially out of support, because they knew how dangerous this was. The fix existed. It was free. It had been available for weeks. Every single computer WannaCry infected had not installed an update that had been sitting there, ready, for a month. The reasons were the usual ones — old systems that couldn't easily be updated, organisations that put patching off, sprawling networks where nobody had a complete picture of what needed fixing. The N-H-S, in particular, had a great many ageing machines. The door had a lock and a fitted bolt sitting right beside it, and it simply hadn't been thrown.

NARRATOR: WannaCry might have run on far longer if not for a young security researcher in England — Marcus Hutchins — who spent that Friday tearing the malware apart to see how it worked. And he noticed something odd. Before WannaCry did its damage on a new machine, it tried to contact a particular web address — a long, random-looking, nonsensical domain name that, he checked, nobody had registered. It didn't exist. So, almost on a whim, for about ten dollars, he registered it himself, just to watch what would happen.

NARRATOR: And WannaCry stopped.

NARRATOR: He had, by complete accident, found a kill switch. The malware's logic was: try to reach that nonsense address; if you can't, carry on infecting; but if you can, stop. Nobody is entirely sure why the authors built that in — perhaps as an anti-analysis trick. But the moment Hutchins registered the domain, every new copy of WannaCry around the world checked the address, found that now it did exist, and obediently switched itself off. One man, one ten-dollar domain registration, and the global spread of the worm slammed to a halt. A staggering piece of luck dressed up as a save.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So let's pull out what this dense story teaches.

NARRATOR: Start with the most maddening lesson, because it's the most important: patch management. The patch — M-S seventeen-oh-one-zero — had existed for a full month, and it would have stopped WannaCry dead. Every infection was an unpatched machine. That's the brutal truth running through so many real breaches: the most devastating attacks usually exploit known vulnerabilities for which a fix already exists. The hard part of security isn't discovering some exotic new flaw — it's the unglamorous discipline of keeping systems updated, knowing every machine you run, and applying patches promptly, before an attacker weaponises a hole the vendor has already closed. On that Friday, patch management was quite literally the difference between a hospital that worked and one that didn't.

NARRATOR: Then the property under attack. Ransomware doesn't steal your data; it denies you access to it. The property it destroys is availability — the data is still there, encrypted, but you can't reach it when you need it. WannaCry proves availability is no abstract corporate concern. When a hospital loses availability, surgeries get cancelled and ambulances get turned away. Downtime isn't just lost revenue on a spreadsheet; it can be lost care, lost safety, real harm to real people. That's why availability sits alongside confidentiality and integrity as a pillar of security, not a footnote.

NARRATOR: Then the lesson about what you do when prevention fails — because no defence is perfect, and the question that separates a survivable incident from a catastrophe is: can you recover? With ransomware specifically: if your files are encrypted and held hostage, but you have a recent, clean, offline backup, the attacker's leverage evaporates. You don't pay. You wipe the machine, restore from backup, and carry on. Ransomware is, in a deep sense, an attack on organisations without good backups. So the controls that matter here are business continuity and disaster recovery — planning in advance how you keep operating and how you restore systems after a disaster, with reliable, tested, isolated backups at the heart of it. The organisations that recovered fastest were the ones who could restore what they had and refuse to negotiate. Backups aren't a chore for the day a hard drive fails. They are your answer to extortion.

NARRATOR: One last thread ties it together. This was malware — ransomware delivered by a self-spreading worm, riding a stolen government exploit through unpatched machines. Every layer of that chain was a place it could have been stopped. The flaw could have been responsibly disclosed instead of hoarded. The patch could have been applied. Tested backups could have made the ransom irrelevant. WannaCry got through because, at a global scale, all three failed at once. The lesson isn't any single fix. It's defence in depth — knowing prevention will sometimes fail, and making sure that when it does, you can still detect it, contain it, and recover. The attacker only has to win once. You have to be ready every single day.
