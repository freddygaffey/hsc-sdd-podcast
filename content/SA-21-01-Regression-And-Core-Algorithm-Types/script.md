NARRATOR: First, linear regression. It predicts a continuous number by fitting the best straight line through the data. The line equation is familiar — output equals a slope times the input, plus an intercept. In machine learning terms, the slope and intercept are the parameters, also called weights, and training means adjusting them until the line fits the data as closely as possible — minimising the error between what the line predicts and the actual values. The classic example: predict a house price from its size. You fit a line through past sales — size against price — and for a new house of a given size, the line gives a predicted price. The key fact for the exam: linear regression predicts a number — a continuous value. That's its defining trait. The line-fitting class is in Listing one. And here's the marquee line: linear predicts a number; logistic predicts a yes-or-no. Keep that — it's the most-tested distinction in the chapter.

NARRATOR: Second, logistic regression — the trap that catches everyone, so we hit it head-on. Despite the word "regression" in its name, logistic regression is not regression in the predict-a-number sense — it is classification. It predicts a category, typically a yes-or-no, by outputting a probability between zero and one and then thresholding it. It uses the sigmoid function — the same S-shaped squashing function you met as a neuron's activation last chapter — which maps any number to a value between zero and one, read as a probability. Above one-half, predict yes; below, predict no. The classic example: predict whether an email is spam from how many suspicious words it has — the model outputs "eighty percent likely spam", which thresholds to "spam". So the defining trait: logistic regression predicts a yes-or-no — a class — not a continuous number. The sigmoid-based class is in Listing two. Say the distinction once more, because it's worth a guaranteed mark: linear regression predicts a number; logistic regression, despite its name, predicts a class — a yes-or-no.

NARRATOR: Third, polynomial regression — the extension the building dot-point adds. Polynomial regression is linear regression's flexible cousin: instead of fitting a straight line, it fits a curve — a polynomial — so it can capture relationships that bend, where a straight line would miss the pattern. If your data curves, a polynomial can follow it. That sounds purely like an upgrade, but it's actually the gateway to the most important cautionary concept in the chapter, so hold that thought — polynomial regression is exactly how you can overfit, and we get there in a moment.

NARRATOR: Fourth, K-nearest neighbour — the K of L-L-K. K-nearest neighbour is a beautifully simple idea: to classify or predict for a new data point, find the K training examples closest to it — its nearest neighbours — and let them vote or average. K is just how many neighbours you consult. To classify a new email, find the, say, five most similar past emails and go with the majority label. It's "tell me your neighbours and I'll tell you who you are". The defining trait: K-nearest neighbour makes a prediction by looking at the most similar known examples — it doesn't fit a line or a curve, it just compares. One line: K-nearest neighbour predicts by finding the K most similar training examples and taking their majority class or average value. The K-N-N sketch is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Now the object-oriented angle, because the dot-point says "using O-O-P". You don't need new programming taught — you already know classes from Year 11 object-oriented programming, and you saw it again with the O-R-M in Programming for the Web. The point is just that machine learning models are naturally objects: you wrap each model type in a class that holds its learned parameters as attributes — the slope and intercept, or the weights — and exposes two methods every model shares. A fit method, which takes the training data and learns the parameters. And a predict method, which takes a new input and returns the prediction using those learned parameters. Fit then predict — that's the universal shape of a model object, and it's exactly the interface that scikit-learn uses, which is why every model in that library has dot-fit and dot-predict. So if an exam shows you a model class and asks you to complete the fit or predict method, you're filling in: fit learns the parameters from data; predict applies them to a new input. NARRATOR: Now the two ideas that separate a real model from a toy — and they're the high-value content. First, overfitting. This is the one I've been promising since the decision-trees episode. Overfitting is when a model learns the training data too well — it memorises the noise and the quirks of the specific examples instead of the general underlying pattern — so it scores brilliantly on the training data and then fails on new, unseen data. Polynomial regression is the perfect illustration: fit a high-degree polynomial and you can make the curve wiggle through every single training point exactly — zero training error — but that wiggly curve is contorting itself to match noise, and on a new point it predicts garbage. The opposite failure, by the way, is underfitting — a model too simple to capture the real pattern, like forcing a straight line through data that genuinely curves. The sweet spot is in between. The line you can write: overfitting is when a model fits the training data too closely, including its noise, and therefore generalises poorly to new data.

NARRATOR: So how do you reduce overfitting? The headline answers the exam wants: keep the model simpler — for example, use a lower-degree polynomial rather than a high one, or limit the depth of a decision tree, which is the pruning idea from last chapter; gather more training data; and use techniques like regularisation, which penalises overly complex models. But the single most important practical answer leads us to the second big idea.

NARRATOR: Second, the train-test split — and this is the one students forget, which costs marks. You must never evaluate a model on the same data you trained it on. Why? Because a model can memorise its training data, so scoring it on that data tells you nothing about whether it actually learned the pattern — it could be a pure overfitter and still get a perfect training score. The fix is the train-test split: you split your data into a training set, which the model learns from, and a separate test set, which it never sees during training and which you use only to measure how well it generalises. Good performance on the held-out test set is real evidence the model learned the pattern, not the noise. This is exactly how you detect overfitting: if a model scores high on training data but poorly on the test set, it has overfitted. So the rule, and write it down: train on one set, test on a separate unseen set — never evaluate on the training data. That pairing — overfitting plus the train-test split — is the conceptual core of this episode.

NARRATOR: Here's the story that makes overconfidence and overfitting unforgettable, and it pays off the seed from the applications episode. Zillow, the property company, ran a house-buying business on a machine learning price-forecasting model. The model had learned from historical data, and it was confident — but the housing market shifted into conditions its training data didn't represent, and the model's forecasts were systematically wrong. Zillow bought thousands of homes at the model's inflated estimates, couldn't sell them for what it paid, lost hundreds of millions of dollars, and shut the whole unit down. The lesson, perfect for an exam: a regression model is only reliable while new data resembles its training data; overconfidence in a model that has overfitted or whose world has changed is a real and expensive failure mode — which is exactly why you validate on held-out data and keep human oversight. That's overfitting and the train-test split, told as a four-hundred-million-dollar story.

NARRATOR: Model answer: linear regression predicts a continuous numeric value by fitting a straight line to the data and reading off the output — for example, predicting a house price from its size. QUESTION: Question two, recall. What is K-nearest neighbour, and how does it make a prediction? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: K-nearest neighbour is a machine learning algorithm that makes a prediction for a new data point by finding the K training examples most similar to it — its nearest neighbours — and taking their majority class for classification, or their average for a numeric prediction. K is the number of neighbours consulted. It does not fit a line or a curve; it predicts by comparison to the most similar known examples. Naming the find-the-K-most-similar mechanism is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain overfitting and one way to reduce it. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: overfitting occurs when a model learns the training data too closely, capturing its noise and specific quirks rather than the general underlying pattern, so it performs very well on the training data but poorly on new, unseen data — it fails to generalise. A common sign is a large gap between high training accuracy and low test accuracy. One way to reduce it is to simplify the model — for example, using a lower-degree polynomial instead of a high one, or limiting the depth of a decision tree — so it cannot contort itself to fit noise; gathering more training data and applying regularisation also help. QUESTION: Question four, application. A developer reports their regression model achieves ninety-five percent accuracy. What further information do you need before trusting this, and why? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: you need to know whether the ninety-five percent was measured on a separate test set that the model never saw during training, or on the training data itself. A high score on the training data is meaningless as evidence of quality, because a model can memorise its training data and still generalise poorly — it may be overfitted. Only strong performance on a held-out test set shows the model has learned the general pattern and will work on new data. So you must confirm a proper train-test split was used and ask for the test-set accuracy. Identifying the need for evaluation on unseen data and explaining why training accuracy is not enough is the mark.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A team fits a very high-degree polynomial that passes through every training point perfectly, and is delighted. Evaluate this decision. Pause, weigh it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the decision is poor and the delight is misplaced. A polynomial that passes through every training point perfectly has almost certainly overfitted — it has zero training error precisely because it has contorted itself to fit the noise and quirks of the specific training data, not the general pattern. As a result it will generalise badly and produce wildly inaccurate predictions on new, unseen data, which is the situation that cost Zillow hundreds of millions when its overconfident model met a changed market. A better approach is to use a simpler, lower-degree model, evaluate it on a held-out test set, and accept some training error in exchange for genuine generalisation. Reaching the judgement that perfect training fit signals overfitting, and recommending a simpler model evaluated on unseen data, is the full-mark answer.
