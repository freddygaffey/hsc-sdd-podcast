NARRATOR: Deep underground, in a hardened facility near the Iranian town of Natanz, there are halls full of spinning machines. Centrifuges — tall, slender aluminium tubes, taller than a person, that whirl at tens of thousands of revolutions a minute, fast enough that the rotor wall sits right at the edge of what metal can survive. Their job is to enrich uranium, to slowly concentrate the rare, light kind you need for a reactor — or, if you push it far enough, for a bomb. They are precious, finicky, and dangerous to run. A centrifuge that spins even slightly wrong can tear itself apart. And in late twenty-oh-nine and through twenty-ten, in those underground halls, the centrifuges started to die. Not all at once. A few here, a few there, spinning themselves to destruction for no reason the engineers could find. They'd swap out the broken ones, and more would fail. The technicians pulled the cascades apart looking for sabotage, for bad parts, for contamination. They pored over their control screens, and the screens told them everything was fine. Everything was normal. The numbers were all where they should be.

NARRATOR: The numbers were lying.

NARRATOR: This is the story of Stuxnet — the first piece of software that historians and security researchers will tell you, without hesitation, was a weapon. Not a tool for stealing money or secrets. A weapon, built by two of the most powerful governments on Earth, designed to reach across the world, cross into a building that had been deliberately cut off from the internet, and physically destroy machinery in the real world — all while whispering reassurances to the people watching the dials. To this day it is one of the most sophisticated pieces of malicious software ever discovered. Kaspersky Lab would later estimate it took a team of around ten coders something like two to three years to build. It was roughly half a megabyte of code — tiny by modern standards, smaller than a single photo on your phone — and into that half a megabyte its authors packed a level of craft the security world had simply never seen before. It changed forever what people understood a cyberattack could do. Because for decades, the worst a computer attack could do was, well, attack a computer. Steal the data. Wipe the disk. Lock the screen. Stuxnet broke that boundary. It reached out of the digital world and snapped physical metal.

NARRATOR: And here is the part that still unsettles the people who study it. It worked. It really, genuinely worked. Somewhere out there, the people who built this thing watched a foreign country's most secret machines tear themselves apart, on schedule, by remote control, without a single soldier crossing a border. So let me take you through how. Because the how is a masterclass — in patience, in deception, and in a single idea that should change the way you think about every system you will ever build.

QUESTION: How do you attack a computer that isn't connected to the internet, and never has been?

NARRATOR: That's the first problem you have to solve if you're the one building this — and it's a brutal one. Because the Natanz control systems weren't online. They sat behind what's called an air gap — and it's exactly what it sounds like. A literal gap of air between the sensitive systems and any outside network. No cable, no wi-fi, no Bluetooth, no connection of any kind to the internet. The reasoning is beautifully simple, and it feels airtight: if there's no wire, the attacker can't reach you. An air gap is supposed to be the ultimate defence for the most critical systems on Earth — power plants, weapons systems, nuclear facilities. You can't hack what you can't touch. So how do you get your weapon inside a sealed box?

NARRATOR: You don't. You get someone else to carry it in for you. Because people have to enter that building. Engineers, contractors, technicians, with their laptops and their tools. And to do their jobs, they carry things in and out — and one of the most ordinary things anyone carries is a U-S-B stick. A little flash drive. A U-S-B stick doesn't care about your air gap. It's a bridge with legs. Plug it into an internet-connected computer somewhere out in the world, let it quietly get infected, then carry it into the secure facility in your pocket and plug it into an air-gapped machine — and the gap has just been crossed. By a human being. On foot. Not over a wire. Up a flight of stairs.

NARRATOR: That is how Stuxnet got in. And the way investigators later proved it is one of the eeriest details in the whole affair. Every time Stuxnet infected a new computer, it wrote a little record of that machine into its own file — and it carried that ordered list of victims around inside itself, like a logbook. So when researchers later collected thousands of samples and read those logbooks, they could trace the family tree of the infection backwards. And every branch led back to the same place: five Iranian organisations, all working in industrial automation, all connected to Iran's nuclear program. Companies that built control systems, that wired up plants, that supplied the contractors. Patient zero wasn't Natanz itself. It was the people who serviced Natanz. The attackers didn't punch through the wall. They infected the workmen who walked through the gate every morning.

NARRATOR: The first of those infections has been dated to June of twenty-oh-nine. Then more, in March of twenty-ten. Then more again, in April. This was a campaign, run in waves, over more than a year — seed it into the supply chain, wait for the worm to ride a thumb drive across the gap, watch the centrifuges, refine the weapon, seed it again.

NARRATOR: And here's the first sign of how serious the people behind this were. To make that jump — from a U-S-B stick onto a fresh machine, and into a position of total control over it — Stuxnet didn't rely on one trick. It carried four separate zero-day exploits. That term matters, so let me be precise. A zero-day is a flaw in software that the makers don't know about yet — meaning there have been zero days to fix it, and no defence exists, anywhere, for anyone. Zero-days are rare and extremely valuable. On the black market a good one sells for hundreds of thousands of dollars. A serious attacker hoards a single one like treasure and uses it sparingly — because the moment you use it, you risk it being spotted and patched, and then it's gone forever, for everybody.

NARRATOR: Stuxnet burned four of them. Four unknown, unpatched Windows flaws, spent on a single operation. There was a flaw in the way Windows handled shortcut files — the little icons in a folder — so that simply opening the U-S-B drive and seeing its contents appear on screen was enough. Just the icons rendering triggered the infection. No double-click. No "are you sure." A bug in the print spooler, the service that handles printing, let it leap from machine to machine across a network. And two more flaws let it quietly escalate its own privileges — promoting itself from an ordinary user to total administrator control of the computer. On top of those four, it reused an older hole, the same one the Conficker worm had abused, and it carried a fifth, separate exploit aimed at the Siemens industrial software itself. Nobody spends four zero-days on a hobby project. Nobody spends four zero-days to steal credit cards — you'd be throwing away a fortune. Four zero-days, fired in one operation, is the signature of a government that does not care what things cost, aimed at a target it wants very, very badly.

NARRATOR: And there's a second tell, just as loud. When a piece of software wants Windows to trust it — to load a driver deep in the heart of the operating system without so much as a warning — it has to be digitally signed, cryptographically stamped by a company Windows already trusts. Stuxnet was signed. Not with a forged stamp — with real ones, stolen from two legitimate Taiwanese hardware companies, Realtek and JMicron, whose offices sat practically next door to each other in the same science park in Hsinchu. Someone had reached into those companies and lifted their private signing keys. So when Stuxnet installed its driver, Windows looked at the signature, saw a trusted, reputable name, and waved it straight through. The weapon didn't break the lock. It walked in holding a stolen key that the guard recognised.

NARRATOR: So picture the worm now — across the air gap, sitting inside a computer in the facility. Here's where it gets genuinely cunning, because Stuxnet was extraordinarily disciplined about what it touched. It spread widely. Wildly, even — onto tens of thousands of ordinary Windows computers, far beyond Natanz, all around the world. But on almost every single one of them, it did absolutely nothing. It looked around, decided "this isn't my target," and went back to sleep. It was hunting for one very specific thing, and it would ignore the entire planet until it found it.

QUESTION: If you only want to destroy one particular set of machines in one particular building, how does your code know when it has finally found them?

NARRATOR: It checks. Carefully, repeatedly, against a fingerprint. Stuxnet was looking for a particular kind of industrial controller — a P-L-C, a programmable logic controller. This is the hinge of the whole story, so let me make sure you can feel it. In the physical world — factories, power stations, water plants, that facility under Natanz — the actual machinery is not run by an ordinary PC. It's run by these small, rugged, specialised computers called P-L-Cs. A P-L-C sits between the control room and the physical equipment. It tells a motor to spin at a certain speed. It opens and closes valves. It reads the sensors and reports back up to the screens the operators watch. It is, quite literally, the bridge between code and the physical world — the muscle that turns a typed command into spinning metal.

NARRATOR: And Stuxnet wasn't looking for just any P-L-C. It was looking for a specific Siemens controller — the kind running Siemens' industrial software, the package called Step Seven, or Win-C-C — and it didn't stop there. It examined what that controller was wired up to. It counted. It was hunting for controllers connected to a particular number of variable-frequency drives — the units that govern motor speed — built by one of two specific manufacturers: a Finnish firm called Vacon, and an Iranian one called Fararo Paya. And it checked the frequencies those drives were running at, looking for a band between roughly eight hundred and seven and one thousand two hundred and ten hertz. That is not the range of a conveyor belt or a pump. That is the range of a gas centrifuge enriching uranium. If Stuxnet didn't find that exact constellation — Siemens controller, those drives, that frequency, that configuration — it stayed asleep. It would sit there, harmless, indefinitely. Only when it found the precise fingerprint of the cascades at Natanz did the weapon finally arm itself.

NARRATOR: And then it did two things at once. And that combination — those two things, happening together — is what makes Stuxnet not just an attack but a kind of dark masterpiece.

NARRATOR: The first thing: it reprogrammed the P-L-C. It quietly injected its own code into the controller, hijacking a Siemens system file — a library with the unlovely name s-seven-otbxdx-dot-d-l-l — so that it sat invisibly between the legitimate software and the actual hardware. A man in the middle, living inside the controller. And from there, instead of letting the centrifuges spin steadily at their safe operating speed of around one thousand and sixty-four hertz, it took the wheel. In one documented attack sequence, it slammed the rotors up to one thousand four hundred and ten hertz — far beyond their design limit — and held them there for about fifteen minutes. Then it let them recover. And then, weeks later — around twenty-seven days later — it did the opposite: it dragged the same rotors almost to a standstill, down near two hertz, and held them there for nearly an hour. Up too fast. Down too slow. Subtle, intermittent, separated by weeks, spread out over months, so it looked like random bad luck and brittle machinery rather than a deliberate hand. And driving a centrifuge wildly outside its tolerance is exactly how you destroy it. Push the rotor past what the aluminium can bear and it warps, it wobbles, the harmonics build, and the whole tube can fail — sometimes violently.

NARRATOR: But here is the problem, if you're the attacker. If that were all your code did, the engineers would catch it in seconds. The screens would show the centrifuges screaming at impossible speeds. Alarms would blare. Hands would slam the emergency stop. The whole game would be over in a single afternoon. So Stuxnet did the second thing — and this is the part that turns a clever attack into something genuinely chilling.

NARRATOR: Before it ever struck, it watched. While the plant was running normally, Stuxnet sat quietly inside the controller and recorded the real sensor data — captured what a stretch of healthy, ordinary operation actually looked like, around twenty-one seconds of it. And then, when it began the sabotage, it played that recording back. To the control room. On a loop. So while it was secretly dragging the centrifuges to their deaths, the screens the operators watched were being fed the prerecorded footage of a plant that was perfectly fine. The engineers sat at their stations, watching calm, normal, healthy numbers. Everything green. Everything nominal. While, a few metres away behind the wall, the machines were tearing themselves apart. The Siemens system was, in the most literal sense, lying about its own condition. The data the operators trusted to tell them the truth had been quietly swapped out for a comforting fiction — a recording of a yesterday when everything had been okay.

NARRATOR: Feel how devastating that is. The entire purpose of a control room — every sensor, every gauge, every screen — is to tell you the truth about your machinery so you can act on it. That is the whole job. Stuxnet didn't just sabotage the machines. It sabotaged the truth. It severed the wire between what was really happening and what the people in charge believed was happening. And so, in a crisis they could not see, the operators did the single worst thing — they did nothing. Not out of incompetence. Out of trust. Because nothing looked wrong. Why would you scram a reactor whose every instrument reads perfectly normal? By the time anyone pieced together what was going on, reportedly around a fifth of Iran's centrifuges had been wrecked — on the order of a thousand machines spun to death — and one analyst estimated the program had been set back by at least two years. All by an enemy that was never once physically in the room.

NARRATOR: So who built it? No government has ever formally and fully owned up to it. But the reporting is now broad and consistent, from multiple major news organisations and from former officials: Stuxnet was the product of a joint operation between the United States and Israel, a classified program codenamed Operation Olympic Games. It was conceived under President George W. Bush, who reportedly wanted an alternative to bombing Iran's nuclear sites, and it was expanded under President Barack Obama in the first months of his presidency. American intelligence — the N-S-A — and Israeli technical units are understood to have built it together. To replicate Natanz precisely enough to test the weapon, they are believed to have set up real centrifuges of the same design. This wasn't a basement project. This was an act of statecraft, with a budget to match, aimed across the world at a target a government had decided to break — without firing a shot, and, ideally, without anyone ever knowing it had been attacked at all.

NARRATOR: Which raises the obvious question: if it was so quiet, so surgical, so determined to stay hidden — how on earth did we ever find out about it?

NARRATOR: And the answer is almost an anticlimax. It got loose. Somewhere in the campaign — through a change in the code, or sloppy handling, or simply the unpredictable mess of the real world — Stuxnet stopped staying politely inside Natanz and started spreading the way worms do: everywhere. It rode out of the facility and into the wider internet, leaping from machine to machine, doing nothing, harming nothing, on hundreds of thousands of computers it had no interest in — but leaving its traces everywhere it went. A weapon built for one building had escaped into the world.

NARRATOR: And in June of twenty-ten, it landed on the desk of a man who had absolutely no idea what he was holding. His name was Sergey Ulasen. He worked at a small antivirus company called VirusBlokAda, in Belarus — not exactly the centre of the cybersecurity universe. A customer, with machines over in Iran, had filed a support ticket about something mundane and maddening: their computers kept rebooting. Bluescreening. Restarting for no reason anyone could explain. So Ulasen and his colleague Oleg Kupreev did what antivirus engineers do. They started pulling the thing apart. And the deeper they went, the stranger it got. The driver loading itself into the core of the system was digitally signed — by Realtek, a real, reputable company. That should have been impossible. Why would a respectable hardware firm's signature be on a piece of malware? They had stumbled, by pure chance, on the loose threads of a weapon built by two governments — and they pulled.

NARRATOR: VirusBlokAda raised the alarm. By the middle of July, word was spreading through the security world, and then the big guns moved in — and they were stunned by what they found. At Symantec, a team led by researchers including Liam O Murchu, Eric Chien, and Nicolas Falliere set about decoding it. They did something clever: the worm tried to phone home to a couple of command-and-control servers, so the researchers seized those addresses and pointed them at their own machines — a technique called sinkholing — so that every infected computer in the world would now report in to Symantec instead of to its masters. Within a week, around thirty-eight thousand machines had checked in, from dozens of countries. Before long it passed a hundred thousand. And the distribution told a story all by itself: more than sixty percent of the infections were inside Iran. A worm that had spread to a hundred countries was, overwhelmingly, concentrated in one. This was not a random pandemic. This was a spear that had glanced off its target and scattered.

NARRATOR: But understanding what Stuxnet did to Windows wasn't the same as understanding what it was for. That breakthrough came from a different place — from a German control-systems engineer named Ralph Langner, who looked past the Windows tricks and stared hard at the payload, the part that talked to the Siemens controllers. And Langner is the one who said out loud what everyone had been circling. This wasn't generic malware. The code was checking for one specific industrial configuration with the precision of a guided munition. He worked out that it targeted particular Siemens controllers — including the model managing the centrifuge cascades — and he noted a detail that gave it away: the code's structure matched the layout of a Natanz cascade, with its one hundred and sixty-four centrifuges. Langner went public, in plain language, with the conclusion that made the world's blood run cold: this was a precision weapon, built to sabotage one target, and that target was almost certainly the uranium enrichment plant at Natanz, Iran.

NARRATOR: And when the wider security community absorbed all of it — the four zero-days, the stolen certificates, the man-in-the-middle inside the P-L-C, the recorded-and-replayed sensor data, the surgical fingerprinting that ignored the entire planet to wake only for one set of drives running at one band of frequencies — they understood they were not looking at malware at all in the old sense. They were looking at a new category of object. Not theft. Not espionage. The deliberate, remote, physical destruction of specific industrial equipment in the real world, by a government, as an act of policy. Something that had never existed before. The first true cyber-weapon, lying in pieces on their workbenches.

NARRATOR: So let me draw out what this story is really about — because Stuxnet isn't just a thriller. It opens up an entire field, and it leaves you with ideas you should carry into everything you ever build.

NARRATOR: The deepest one is a single word: integrity. When people think about protecting a system, they usually reach first for secrecy — keeping data confidential, stopping it from being read by the wrong eyes. Stuxnet was about something else entirely. It attacked integrity — the trustworthiness of data, the guarantee that what the system tells you is true and hasn't been tampered with. The operators' confidentiality was never the issue. Nobody was reading their secrets. The issue was that their own instruments lied to them. The readings no longer reflected reality. And an integrity attack is, in a way, more dangerous than theft — because a system that's been robbed at least knows something is missing. It can tell. But a system whose integrity is broken doesn't know anything is wrong at all. It confidently reports that everything is fine, in calm green numbers, while it burns. So whenever you build something that makes decisions from data — and that is very nearly everything, every dashboard, every sensor, every automated control loop — you have to ask not only "can someone steal this data," but the harder question: "can someone make this data lie to me, and would I ever know?" Stuxnet is the answer to why that second question matters.

NARRATOR: Then there's where it struck: critical infrastructure. The systems that run the physical world — power grids, water treatment, factories, pipelines, nuclear facilities — and the industrial control systems and P-L-Cs that quietly operate them. For a long time those were treated as a separate, safe, sleepy world. Old. Specialised. Air-gapped. Boring. Nobody glamorous attacked a sewage pump. Stuxnet shattered that comfort overnight. It proved that an attack on these controllers isn't an abstract data breach with a press release and an apology — it has consequences in aluminium and concrete and human safety. Securing industrial control systems went, almost in a single summer, from a niche concern to a serious global priority — because the stakes aren't a leaked database. The stakes are whether the lights stay on and the dam holds and the water is clean.

NARRATOR: And notice how it got in, because this is the lesson people most want to forget. The air gap — the supposedly perfect defence — was not defeated by some impossible feat of hacking. It was defeated by a U-S-B stick carried by a person. The gap was real. But the people crossing it every day were the bridge. This is a supply-chain lesson wearing a disguise. The attackers didn't charge the fortress. They infected the contractors and the suppliers and the ordinary computers in the world around the target, the five companies that serviced it — and then they waited for a trusted human being to carry the weapon in for them, unknowingly, in a pocket. The strongest wall has a gate, and the gate is people. An air gap is not magic. All it does is change the question — from "how do I reach this over a wire" to "who do I get to walk it across the floor for me."

NARRATOR: And finally, the thing that makes Stuxnet a turning point rather than just an extraordinary hack: it was the birth of the cyber-weapon as a real instrument of war. Before it, a cyberattack meant data — stolen, deleted, leaked, held for ransom. After it, every government on Earth understood that code could be a weapon, built by states, aimed at physical targets, capable of destruction that previously required bombs, or saboteurs, or both. It opened a door that cannot be closed again. Every nation now knows this is possible. Every nation now builds for it — both to do it to others, and to defend against having it done to them. The genie is not going back in the bottle. We live, now, in the world Stuxnet made.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A weapon made of code, built by two governments, to destroy machines they could never physically touch. An air gap crossed not over a wire but on foot, by a human carrying a thumb drive across a sealed threshold. Four precious zero-days and two stolen signatures spent to seize total control of a machine. A worm patient enough to ignore a hundred thousand computers across a hundred countries, and wake for one — one exact arrangement of Siemens controllers and Finnish and Iranian drives, spinning in the narrow band of frequencies that means uranium. And then the masterstroke: it drove the centrifuges to their deaths while playing the operators a recording of calm, healthy readings — twenty-one seconds of a better yesterday, looped — so the people in charge watched their screens, saw that all was well, and did the only thing that calm green numbers ever ask of you: nothing. While their machines tore themselves apart behind the wall.

NARRATOR: The system didn't just fail. It lied about failing. And that — a system that confidently tells you the truth while the truth is a fabrication of someone else's making — is the most unsettling kind of attack there is. Build your systems as if someone, someday, might try to make them lie to you. Because at Natanz, someone did. And it worked.
