NARRATOR: Deep underground, in a hardened facility near the Iranian town of Natanz, there are halls full of spinning machines. Centrifuges — tall, slender metal tubes that whirl at tens of thousands of revolutions a minute, fast enough that the metal sits right at the edge of what metal can survive. Their job is to enrich uranium, to slowly concentrate the rare kind you need for a reactor — or, if you push it far enough, for a bomb. They are precious, finicky, and dangerous to run. A centrifuge that spins even slightly wrong can tear itself apart. And around twenty-ten, in those underground halls, the centrifuges started to die. Not all at once. A few here, a few there, spinning themselves to destruction for no reason the engineers could find. They'd swap out the broken ones, and more would fail. The scientists pored over their control screens, and the screens told them everything was fine. Everything was normal. The numbers were all where they should be.

NARRATOR: The numbers were lying.

NARRATOR: This is the story of Stuxnet — the first piece of software that historians and security researchers will tell you was a weapon. Not a tool for stealing money or secrets. A weapon, built by a nation-state, designed to reach across the world, cross into a building deliberately cut off from the internet, and physically destroy machinery in the real world — all while whispering reassurances to the people watching. To this day it is one of the most sophisticated pieces of malicious software ever discovered, and it changed forever what people understood a cyberattack could do. For decades, the worst a computer attack could do was, well, attack a computer. Stuxnet broke that boundary. It reached out of the digital world and snapped physical metal.

QUESTION: How do you attack a computer that isn't connected to the internet, and never has been?

NARRATOR: That's the first thing you have to solve if you're the one building this, because the Natanz control systems weren't online. They sat behind what's called an air gap — exactly what it sounds like. A literal gap of air between the sensitive systems and any outside network. No cable, no wireless, no connection of any kind to the internet. The reasoning is beautifully simple: if there's no wire, the attacker can't reach you. An air gap is supposed to be the ultimate defence for the most critical systems on Earth — power plants, weapons, nuclear facilities. You can't hack what you can't reach.

NARRATOR: Except. People have to get into that building. Engineers, contractors, technicians, with their laptops and their tools. And to do their jobs they carry things in and out — and one of the most ordinary things anyone carries is a U-S-B stick. A little flash drive. A U-S-B stick doesn't care about your air gap. It's a bridge with legs. Plug it into an internet-connected computer out in the world, let it quietly get infected, then carry it into the secure facility and plug it into an air-gapped machine — and the gap has just been crossed, by a human being, on foot. That is exactly how Stuxnet is believed to have gotten in. It spread out in the world, infecting ordinary computers belonging to contractors and suppliers connected to the Iranian nuclear program, and it waited on their U-S-B drives for one of them to be carried across the gap and plugged into the right kind of machine inside.

NARRATOR: And here's the first sign of how serious the people behind this were. To jump from a U-S-B stick onto a machine and take control, Stuxnet didn't rely on one trick. It carried several zero-day exploits. That term matters, so let me be precise. A zero-day is a flaw in software the makers don't know about yet — meaning there's been zero days to fix it, and no defence exists. Zero-days are rare and extremely valuable. A serious attacker hoards a single one like treasure and uses it sparingly, because the moment you use it, it can be discovered and patched, and then it's gone. Stuxnet burned four. Four unknown, unpatched flaws, spent on a single operation. One was so potent that simply having an infected U-S-B drive's contents appear on screen — just the icons showing up — triggered the infection, with no click required. Nobody spends four zero-days on a hobby project. Four zero-days is the signature of a government with deep pockets and a very specific target.

NARRATOR: So picture the worm, across the air gap, sitting inside a computer in the facility. Here's where it gets genuinely cunning, because Stuxnet was extraordinarily disciplined about what it touched. It spread widely — onto a great many ordinary Windows computers, far beyond Natanz, all around the world. But on almost every one of them, it did nothing. It looked around, decided "this isn't my target," and went dormant. It was hunting for one very specific thing.

QUESTION: If you only want to destroy one particular set of machines in one particular building, how does your code know when it's finally found them?

NARRATOR: It checks. Stuxnet was looking for a particular kind of industrial controller — a P-L-C, a programmable logic controller. This is the hinge of the whole story. In the physical world — factories, power stations, that Natanz facility — the actual machinery isn't run by an ordinary computer. It's run by these small, rugged, specialised computers called P-L-Cs. A P-L-C sits between the control room and the physical equipment. It tells a motor to spin at a certain speed, opens and closes valves, reads the sensors, and reports back. It is, quite literally, the bridge between code and the physical world — the muscle that turns a command into motion. And Stuxnet was looking for one exact configuration: a specific make of controller, wired up to drive motors spinning within a very particular range of frequencies. The range that matches uranium centrifuges. If it didn't find that exact fingerprint, it stayed asleep. Only when it found the precise setup at Natanz did the weapon arm itself.

NARRATOR: And then it did two things at once — and that combination is what makes Stuxnet a masterpiece of malice. First: it reprogrammed the P-L-C. It rewrote the controller's own code so that, instead of spinning the centrifuges steadily at their safe speed, it secretly messed with them. Spin them up too fast, far beyond their limits, then drop them, then later spin them up again — subtle, intermittent sabotage, spread over weeks and months so it looked like random bad luck rather than an attack. Spinning a centrifuge wildly outside its tolerance is exactly how you destroy it: the metal can't take it, and it fails, sometimes catastrophically.

NARRATOR: But if that were all it did, the engineers would have seen it instantly. The screens would have shown the centrifuges screaming at impossible speeds, alarms would have blared, and they'd have hit the emergency stop. So Stuxnet did the second thing, and this is the chilling part. While it was secretly driving the centrifuges to their deaths, it was also feeding the control room fake readings. It had recorded what normal, healthy operation looked like beforehand — and it played that recording back to the operators. So the engineers sat at their stations, watching screens that showed calm, normal, healthy numbers. Everything green. Everything fine. While, a few metres away behind the wall, the machines were tearing themselves apart. The system was lying about its own condition. The data the operators trusted to tell them the truth had been quietly replaced with a comforting fiction.

NARRATOR: Feel how devastating that is. The entire purpose of a control room — all those sensors and gauges and screens — is to tell you the truth about your machinery so you can act on it. Stuxnet didn't just sabotage the machines. It sabotaged the truth. It severed the link between what was really happening and what the people in charge believed was happening. And so they did the worst possible thing in a crisis they couldn't see: nothing. Because nothing looked wrong. By the time anyone understood, reportedly around a fifth of Iran's centrifuges had been wrecked — roughly a thousand machines spun to death — and the program set back significantly, all by an enemy that was never physically there.

NARRATOR: The worm was finally discovered in mid twenty-ten, not by the Iranians, but by a small antivirus company in Belarus that noticed something strange on computers in the region and started pulling it apart. And when the wider security world got hold of it, they were stunned. The sheer size and complexity. The four zero-days. The fact that it was signed with stolen but legitimate digital certificates to look trustworthy. The surgical targeting. And above all, the realisation of what it was for — not theft, not espionage, but physical destruction of specific industrial equipment in the real world. They knew immediately they were looking at something that had never existed before.

NARRATOR: So let me draw out what this story is really about, because Stuxnet is the case that opens up an entire field.

NARRATOR: The deepest idea is one word: integrity. When people talk about protecting a system, they usually think first about secrecy — keeping data confidential, stopping it from being read. Stuxnet was about something else. It attacked integrity — the trustworthiness of data, the guarantee that what the system tells you is true and hasn't been tampered with. The operators' confidentiality was never the issue. The issue was that their data lied to them. The readings no longer reflected reality. And an integrity attack is in some ways more dangerous than theft, because a system that's been robbed at least knows something is missing — but a system whose integrity is broken doesn't know anything is wrong at all. It confidently tells you everything is fine while it burns. Whenever you build something that makes decisions from data — and that's nearly everything — you have to ask not only "can someone steal this data" but "can someone make this data lie to me." Stuxnet is the answer to why that second question matters.

NARRATOR: Then there's where it struck: critical infrastructure. The systems that run the physical world — power grids, water treatment, factories, pipelines, nuclear facilities — and the industrial control systems and P-L-Cs that operate them. For a long time those were treated as a separate, safe world: old, specialised, air-gapped. Stuxnet shattered that comfort. It proved that an attack on these controllers isn't an abstract data breach — it has consequences in steel and concrete and human safety. Securing industrial control systems became, overnight, a serious global concern, because the stakes aren't a leaked database; the stakes are whether the lights stay on and the dam holds.

NARRATOR: And notice how it got in, because this is the lesson people most want to forget. The air gap — the supposedly perfect defence — was defeated not by some impossible feat but by a U-S-B stick carried by a human being. The gap was real, but the people crossing it every day were the bridge. This is a supply-chain lesson in disguise: the attackers didn't go at the fortress directly; they infected the contractors, the suppliers, the ordinary computers around the target, and let a trusted person carry the weapon in. The strongest wall has a gate, and the gate is people. An air gap is not magic. It just changes the question from "how do I reach this over a wire" to "who do I get to carry it across for me."

NARRATOR: And finally, the thing that makes Stuxnet a turning point rather than just an impressive hack: it was the birth of the cyber-weapon. Before it, a cyberattack meant data — stolen, deleted, leaked. After it, the world understood that code could be a weapon of war, built by states, aimed at physical targets, capable of destruction that previously required bombs or sabotage teams. It opened a door that cannot be closed. Every nation now knows this is possible, and builds for it — both to do it and to defend against it. The genie is out.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A weapon made of code, built by a government, to destroy machines it could never physically touch. An air gap crossed on foot, by a human carrying a U-S-B stick. Four precious zero-days spent to seize control. A worm patient enough to ignore the entire world and wake only for one exact set of industrial controllers. And then the masterstroke: it drove the centrifuges to their deaths while playing the operators a recording of calm, healthy readings — so the people in charge watched their screens, saw all was well, and did nothing, while their machines tore themselves apart behind the wall. The system didn't just fail. It lied about failing. And that — a system that confidently tells you the truth while the truth is a fabrication — is the most unsettling kind of attack there is.
