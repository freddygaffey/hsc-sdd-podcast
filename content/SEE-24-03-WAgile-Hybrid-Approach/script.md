NARRATOR: Recap first, and this one's essential — today's topic doesn't exist without the last two episodes. We've done both ends of the development-approach spectrum. Waterfall: the linear, sequential approach — Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance — run as strict stage-gates with heavy documentation and formal sign-off. "Waterfall Falls once": down the stages in order, going back up is expensive. Strong on predictability, documentation, and control; weak on flexibility, with late feedback and late testing. Suits stable, regulated projects. And Agile: the iterative, incremental approach — short time-boxed sprints, each delivering working software, with user stories in "As a, I want, so that" format, the P-S-R-R ceremonies — Plan, Standup, Review, Retro — and method tailoring. "Agile Goes around": looping with continuous feedback. Strong on adaptability and early feedback; weak on predictable scope and cost, and light on documentation. Suits uncertain, fast-moving projects.

NARRATOR: Hold those two side by side — Falls once versus Goes around, predictable-but-rigid versus adaptable-but-uncertain — because today's idea is: what if you don't want to choose? What if a project has stable, regulated parts and uncertain, evolving parts? That's most large projects. The answer is the third approach: WAgile, the hybrid. Today's outcome is S-E-twelve-oh-six, and the dot-point is short but specific: research WAgile as a hybrid model, with analysis of the "when" and "how" intervention is applied during the development lifecycle, and its scale and types of developments. Note those words — "when" and "how" — that's exactly what the exam tests, and the part students skip.

NARRATOR: Here's the core idea, and the hook to carry — reuse it unchanged: "WAgile equals Waterfall gates plus Agile sprints." Shorter still: "plan big, build small." WAgile structures the overall project with Waterfall's stage-gates — formal checkpoints where progress is reviewed and approval given to proceed to the next major phase — but inside each stage, the development work runs in Agile iterations, short sprints with feedback. So at the macro level you have Waterfall's governance, predictability, and documentation; at the micro level you have Agile's flexibility and feedback. Big linear phases on the outside, little loops on the inside. That's WAgile.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll explain what WAgile is as a hybrid; explain the "when" and "how" of applying each approach within the lifecycle; identify which parts of a project suit gates and which suit sprints; and recommend WAgile over pure Waterfall or pure Agile for a scenario, with justification. The classic questions are "explain how WAgile combines Waterfall and Agile" for around four marks, and the big one: "recommend an approach — Waterfall, Agile, or WAgile — for this scenario and justify," an extended response drawing on all three episodes.

NARRATOR: Make the structure concrete, because abstract "hybrid" answers lose marks. Picture a government citizen-services portal, eighteen months. WAgile lays four major stage-gates across that timeline: gate one at month three, requirements-and-compliance approval; gate two at month six, design-and-security approval; gate three at month twelve, core-implementation approval; gate four at month sixteen, production-readiness approval. Those gates are pure Waterfall — formal reviews, sign-offs, documentation, the governance executives and regulators need. Between the gates, the team works in two-week Agile sprints. Inside the requirements stage, sprint one runs stakeholder workshops, sprint two breaks down detailed stories and acceptance criteria, sprint three incorporates feedback and finalises the requirements document. The gate gives you the checkpoint; the sprints give you the iterative work to reach it. Gates for governance, sprints for delivery. That WAgile timeline is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now the heart of the exam: the "when" and "how" — when do you reach for Waterfall behaviour, and when for Agile, within one project? Here's the framework, and it's the mark-earner. Apply Waterfall-style elements when you need governance and predictability: when regulatory compliance demands formal documentation, when budget approval needs predictable milestones, when integrating with legacy systems requires detailed upfront planning, when stakeholders need formal progress reporting, and when scope-creep risk is high. Apply Agile-style elements when you need flexibility and feedback: when requirements are likely to change, when user feedback is essential, when technology choices may evolve, when the team needs room to respond to blockers, and when innovation and experimentation are valued. So the "when" is driven by which need dominates for that part of the work — control or adaptability.

NARRATOR: Here's how that plays out component by component — the most concrete way to answer a WAgile question. Take a school management system. The student-records module touches government data, integrates with legacy systems, and demands strict compliance — handle it Waterfall-heavy: detailed upfront design, comprehensive documentation, formal sign-off. The parent portal is about user experience, and its requirements evolve as parents react — handle it Agile-heavy: rapid prototyping, iterative user testing, frequent feedback. The reporting dashboard is a mix — some fixed compliance, some evolving usability — so run it balanced. Same project, different approaches per component, all under one set of overarching stage-gates. That's WAgile in practice: you don't pick one approach for the whole project; you intervene with the right approach where it fits.

QUESTION: Pause-the-player retrieval. For a hospital project, decide which approach — Waterfall-heavy or Agile-heavy — fits each of these two components, and give your reason: first, the module that stores legally-protected patient medical records; second, the appointment-booking interface patients use on their phones. Pause, decide both, then play.

NARRATOR: First, the patient-records module: Waterfall-heavy. It's safety-critical and legally regulated, so it needs comprehensive documentation, detailed upfront design, and formal sign-off — governance dominates, that's the "when" for Waterfall. Second, the appointment-booking interface: Agile-heavy. It's user-facing, its usability requirements evolve with patient feedback, so it benefits from iterative sprints and frequent user testing — adaptability dominates, that's the "when" for Agile. Both live under the same overarching stage-gates. Name governance-versus-adaptability as the deciding factor per component and you've got the "when and how" mark — exactly what the dot-point asks for.

NARRATOR: Advantages, for the balanced answer. WAgile gives the best of both: governance and risk management from the gates, plus adaptability and early feedback from the sprints. It reduces scope creep — the gates control it — while still allowing necessary change — the sprints absorb it. It satisfies different stakeholder groups: executives and regulators get formal milestones and documents; developers and users get iterations and feedback. And it scales: small teams use a couple of lightweight gates with sprint reviews doubling as mini-gates; large enterprise projects use four-to-six formal gates with executive review and heavier documentation. That scalability is the "scale and types of developments" part of the dot-point.

NARRATOR: Disadvantages — and there's a real trap. WAgile is more complex to manage than either pure approach: you're running two systems of working at once, which takes skill and coordination. Get it wrong and you don't get the best of both — you get the worst of both: the bureaucracy and slowness of Waterfall plus the unpredictability of Agile, with neither benefit. The gates can also block the sprints — a delayed gate review stalls iterative progress waiting for approval. And for a small, simple project, the overhead of gates may not be worth it; pure Agile is lighter. So name the trap explicitly: WAgile is not automatically best just because it "combines" the two. It only works if you justify the "when and how" — where the gates add value and where the sprints add value. A WAgile answer that just says "it combines the good parts of both" without explaining when and how is the band-four answer the marker is watching for.

NARRATOR: Interleave deliberately — WAgile is where this module's enterprise context shows up. Backward to Secure Software Architecture, chapter nineteen — the enterprise security material, the "benefits to an enterprise" P-I-P-P-B hook, the governance and audit thinking. WAgile's stage-gates are exactly the formal governance a real enterprise imposes; gate two in the citizen portal, the security-and-compliance gate, is where threat modelling and security review happen as a formal sign-off — shift-left given a checkpoint. Backward to both previous episodes — this whole topic is built on them; you can't explain WAgile without explaining what it hybridises. Forward to project management and Gantt charts, next episode: those stage-gates and sprint cycles are what you schedule and track on a Gantt, with the gates as milestones. And forward to quality assurance, twenty-four-oh-six: each gate is a quality checkpoint, a continual-checking moment — exactly the QA process. The thread: WAgile is the enterprise-realistic approach, it sits on top of Waterfall and Agile, and it feeds straight into how you schedule and quality-assure the project.

NARRATOR: Worked example for the recommendation question — "Waterfall, Agile, or WAgile — justify" is the extended-response peak of this chapter. Scenario: a large bank building an online banking platform, eighteen months, with strict financial regulation, a fixed budget, executive oversight, plus a customer-facing app where usability must be refined through feedback. Which approach? Pure Waterfall is wrong — the customer-facing parts need iteration and feedback that Waterfall's single late delivery can't give. Pure Agile is wrong — the regulator and the executive board need formal milestones, compliance documentation, and predictable budget gates that Agile's lighter governance doesn't provide. WAgile is the justified choice: lay formal stage-gates across the eighteen months for regulatory and budget sign-off, with the compliance-critical core like transaction processing handled Waterfall-heavy with full documentation, and the customer-facing app and interface components run in Agile sprints with continuous user feedback inside those gates. The justification names the "when" — gates where compliance and budget governance dominate, sprints where usability and feedback dominate — and the "how" — Waterfall-heavy components versus Agile-heavy components under one gate structure. That structure is the band-six answer.

NARRATOR: Hear the weak-versus-strong contrast. Weak answer to "explain how WAgile combines Waterfall and Agile": "WAgile uses some Waterfall and some Agile to get the benefits of both." That's the trap answer — true-ish, but no mechanism, no "when," no "how," band four. Strong answer: "WAgile structures the overall project with Waterfall stage-gates — formal review-and-approval checkpoints between major phases that provide governance, predictability, and documentation — while performing the development work within each stage in short Agile iterations that allow flexibility and continuous feedback. Waterfall-style governance is applied where compliance, formal reporting, and predictable milestones are needed; Agile-style iteration is applied where requirements are likely to change and user feedback is essential." Hear the difference — gates explained, sprints explained, the "when" of each named. Mechanism plus the when-and-how. That's the mark.

NARRATOR: Lock in today. WAgile is the hybrid: Waterfall gates plus Agile sprints — plan big, build small. Big linear stage-gates on the outside give governance, predictability, and documentation; little Agile iterations on the inside give flexibility and feedback. The "when": apply Waterfall behaviour where compliance, formal reporting, and predictable milestones dominate; apply Agile behaviour where requirements change and feedback is essential. The "how": run compliance-critical components Waterfall-heavy and user-facing components Agile-heavy, all under one gate structure. It scales from a couple of lightweight gates to six formal ones. Strength: the best of both. Trap: done badly it's the worst of both, and "it combines the good parts" without explaining when and how is the band-four answer. Always justify the when and how.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions, recall up to extended. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. What is the WAgile approach? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: WAgile is a hybrid software development approach that combines Waterfall and Agile. It structures the overall project using Waterfall stage-gates — formal review-and-approval checkpoints between major phases — while carrying out the development work within each stage in short Agile sprints. This gives the project Waterfall's governance, predictability, and documentation at the macro level, and Agile's flexibility and continuous feedback at the micro level — plan big, build small. Defining it as Waterfall gates plus Agile sprints, with the macro-governance, micro-flexibility split, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, the four-marker. Explain how WAgile combines the Waterfall and Agile approaches. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: WAgile applies Waterfall's stage-gates as the project's overarching structure — formal checkpoints at the end of major phases where progress is reviewed, documented, and formally approved before proceeding — which provides governance, predictable milestones, and the audit trail required by stakeholders and regulators. Within each of those stages, the team works in short, time-boxed Agile sprints, delivering increments and gathering feedback, which provides the flexibility to adapt and to incorporate user input continuously. So the two are combined at different levels: Waterfall governs the macro flow between phases, while Agile drives the micro work inside each phase. Explaining both the gate mechanism and the sprint mechanism, and that they operate at different levels, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three, application. Explain the "when" and "how" of applying intervention in WAgile, using an example. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the "when" is determined by which need dominates a part of the work. Waterfall-style intervention is applied when governance dominates — when regulatory compliance requires formal documentation, when budget or stakeholder reporting needs predictable milestones, or when scope-creep risk is high. Agile-style intervention is applied when adaptability dominates — when requirements are likely to change, when user feedback is essential, or when technology may evolve. The "how" is to handle different components with different emphases under one gate structure. For example, in a school management system, the student-records module — regulated and integrated with legacy systems — is handled Waterfall-heavy with detailed upfront design and formal sign-off, while the parent portal — user-facing with evolving requirements — is handled Agile-heavy with iterative prototyping and user testing, both progressing through shared stage-gates. Naming governance-versus-adaptability as the "when," and component-level emphasis under shared gates as the "how," with a concrete example, earns full marks.

QUESTION: Question four, the extended one. A large insurance company is building a new claims-processing system that must meet strict financial regulation but also includes a customer-facing claims app whose design will be refined through user feedback. Recommend Waterfall, Agile, or WAgile, and justify. Pause, plan across all three episodes, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: WAgile is the recommended approach. Pure Waterfall would be unsuitable because the customer-facing claims app needs iterative refinement and early, continuous user feedback, which Waterfall's single late delivery and rigidity cannot provide. Pure Agile would be unsuitable because the claims-processing core is subject to strict financial regulation requiring comprehensive, auditable documentation, formal compliance sign-off, and predictable budget milestones, which Agile's lighter governance does not deliver. WAgile resolves this: formal Waterfall stage-gates are laid across the project for regulatory and budget approval, and the regulated claims-processing core is developed Waterfall-heavy — detailed upfront design, full documentation, formal review — for compliance and predictability; meanwhile the customer-facing app is developed Agile-heavy in sprints with continuous user feedback for usability, all progressing through the shared gates. The "when": gates and Waterfall behaviour where compliance and governance dominate, sprints and Agile behaviour where usability and feedback dominate. The justification must explain not just that WAgile combines both, but specifically when and how each is applied to which part — that when-and-how analysis, plus the explicit rejection of the two pure approaches, is what earns full marks.
