NARRATOR: First, the shape of the block — the arc is itself a memory aid. Chapter twenty-three is defining: requirements and feasibility — what to build and whether you can; ideation and modelling — working the idea out on paper; and implementation methods — how you'll switch it on. Chapter twenty-four is planning: the three development approaches — Waterfall, Agile, WAgile; project management and the Gantt; the social, ethical, and communication side; and quality assurance. Define it, then plan how to build it. And remember the marker over all of it — this module rewards three verbs: justify, manage, document. Not "I wrote code," but "I made a defensible decision and I can show the paperwork." Every question today sits on that.

NARRATOR: Now the mnemonic dump — the whole block, in order, fast. Writing these in the margin in the first minute is worth real marks. FUN versus PERF — functional versus non-functional. TECO — the four feasibility dimensions. B-M-S — the ideation tools. DiP-PP — the four implementation methods. Waterfall Falls once; Agile Goes around. P-S-R-R — the Agile ceremonies. WAgile equals Waterfall gates plus Agile sprints. Scope-Time-Cost — the project triangle. I-F-N — the communication issues. PABT — the ethical issues. And the three Cs — quality assurance. That's the block on a few lines. Now let's reload each so the letters mean something.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-three, episode one — requirements and feasibility. FUN versus PERF: functional is what the system does, the features and verbs; non-functional, standing in as PERF, is how well it does it — performance, security, usability, reliability. The single most important property of any requirement: it must be measurable and testable, written with acceptance criteria. "It should be fast" is band four; "ninety-fifth percentile under three hundred milliseconds at two hundred users" is band six. Feasibility is TECO: Technical — do we have the skills and tech; Economic — do benefits beat costs, the cost-benefit and return-on-investment; Cost-and-schedule — can we deliver in time and budget with a risk buffer; Operational — will people actually adopt it. And boundaries — in, out, future — stop scope creep. The big idea that threads the whole module: the requirements you write here become your quality criteria and evaluation yardstick later.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-three, episode two — ideation and modelling. Ideation is B-M-S: Brainstorm to generate ideas without judging, quantity first; Mind-map to organise them around a central concept; Storyboard to walk the user's journey and catch usability problems early. Modelling turns the idea into a buildable design: the data dictionary documents every field, its type, and its constraints — removing ambiguity before you build, selecting appropriate types, Decimal not float for money; and algorithm design expresses the logic in NESA pseudocode first, because logic errors are cheap on paper. The verb is justify — name the tool, its mechanism, and why. The trap: jumping to code before modelling, the opposite of shift-left.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-three, episode three — implementation methods. DiP-PP: Direct, Phased, Parallel, Pilot. Direct is the big bang — cheap, fast, high-risk, no fallback. Phased rolls out feature by feature to everyone — medium risk. Parallel runs both systems at once — low risk, a safety net and a way to verify outputs, but expensive. Pilot tries the whole system on a small representative group first — low risk, but delays full rollout. The exam pairing: Direct is cheap and risky, Parallel is safe and costly. The distinction students lose: phased splits by feature, pilot splits by user group, parallel runs both at once. Knight Capital chose direct with no rollback and lost four hundred and forty million dollars in forty-five minutes; Denver airport chose direct cut-over of a giant untested system and opened sixteen months late.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-four, episode one — Waterfall. Falls once: linear stage-gates — Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance — the S-D-L-C run strictly, with heavy documentation and formal sign-off at each gate. Strengths: predictability, documentation, control. Weaknesses: rigidity to change, late feedback, late testing, all-or-nothing delivery. Suits stable, regulated projects; fails on uncertain, fast-moving ones. Healthcare-dot-gov: a fixed deadline plus weak testing equals meltdown.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-four, episode two — Agile. Goes around: iterative, incremental, short time-boxed sprints each delivering working software. Requirements are user stories — "As a, I want, so that" — with testable acceptance criteria. The ceremonies are P-S-R-R: Plan the sprint, Standup daily to surface blockers, Review the product with stakeholders, Retro on the team's process. Method tailoring is adapting practices to your context. Strengths: adaptable, early continuous feedback, frequent delivery. Weaknesses: less predictable scope and cost, lighter docs, needs client involvement. The trap: "Agile means no planning or documentation" — false; it plans every sprint and documents lightly.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-four, episode three — WAgile. Waterfall gates plus Agile sprints; plan big, build small. Waterfall stage-gates on the outside for governance, predictability, and documentation; Agile sprints on the inside for flexibility and feedback. The exam's key words are when and how: apply Waterfall behaviour where governance dominates — compliance, formal reporting, predictable milestones; apply Agile behaviour where adaptability dominates — changing requirements, essential user feedback. The how: run compliance-critical components Waterfall-heavy, user-facing components Agile-heavy, under shared gates. Trap: "it combines the good parts" with no when-and-how is the band-four answer; done badly, it's the worst of both.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-four, episode four — project management and Gantt. The triangle is Scope-Time-Cost, interdependent, quality in the middle, pick two. A Gantt chart is bars over time, arrows for dependencies, milestones as checkpoints. The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks — it sets the minimum project duration, and its tasks have zero slack, so a slip on the critical path slips the whole project, while a slip within a non-critical task's slack doesn't move the finish date. Tracking is plan-versus-actual. Collaboration tools — Jira, Trello, Microsoft Project, and version control — chosen and justified. This is the manage-and-document core.

NARRATOR: Chapter twenty-four, episode five — social, ethical, communication. Communication is I-F-N: Involve and empower through demonstrations and co-design; enable Feedback through channels, cycles, and tracking; Negotiate scope around the triangle by clarifying the need, offering options, and documenting. The ethical issues are PABT: Privacy, Accessibility, Bias, Transparency — every one a callback to another module. Collaboration brings V-D-Q benefits but raises fairness duties. The trap: treating communication and ethics as soft or optional.

NARRATOR: And chapter twenty-four, episode six — quality assurance. The three Cs: Criteria, Continual checking, Compliance. Criteria are measurable quality standards — C-U-P-S, Correctness, Usability, Performance, Security — drawn from the requirements. Continual checking verifies against them throughout, by reviews and aligned testing, catching defects early — shift-left for quality. Compliance is legally mandated — the Privacy Act, the Disability Discrimination Act and W-C-A-G, industry regulation. The central distinction: QA is the process that prevents defects; testing is one activity that detects them. That's the whole block reloaded. Now we earn the marks.

NARRATOR: Now the integrated scenario — the centrepiece, because real H-S-C extended questions hand you one brief and make you walk the whole block through it. The stimulus. A state health department wants an online appointment-booking and patient-records portal. It must comply with health-privacy law, it has a fixed eighteen-month deadline and budget, executives need formal progress reports, but the patient-facing booking interface must be refined through real user feedback. Walk it through the block. First, defining. Requirements: separate functional — "a patient can book an appointment," "a clinician can view a patient's records" — from non-functional, the FUN-versus-PERF split, and make the non-functional ones measurable: confidentiality via encryption and role-based access, availability twenty-four-seven, page loads under two seconds, full audit logging. Feasibility, TECO: technically demanding but doable; economically justified against the current manual system; cost-and-schedule tight at eighteen months, so build in a risk buffer; operational, it must fit how clinicians work or they'll route around it. Boundaries: records and booking in scope; billing out; a mobile app future — to stop scope creep.

NARRATOR: Same scenario, now ideation, modelling, and implementation. Ideation, B-M-S: brainstorm booking approaches, mind-map them, storyboard the patient's booking journey to catch friction early. Model it: a data dictionary pinning down patient, appointment, and clinician fields with appropriate types — a date-of-birth as a Date, never a String — and algorithm design in pseudocode for the slot-availability logic. Implementation method, DiP-PP: this is a critical health system, so no direct cut-over — that's the Knight Capital and Denver mistake. Recommend parallel, running the new portal alongside the existing system as a safety net and to verify outputs, preceded by a pilot in one clinic to contain any fault. The justification names the risk profile — critical, zero tolerance — and weighs cost against stakes.

NARRATOR: Same scenario, now planning — the chapter twenty-four layer, where the marks concentrate. Development approach: the textbook WAgile case. Pure Waterfall can't refine the patient interface through feedback; pure Agile can't give regulators and executives formal documentation and predictable milestones. So WAgile — formal stage-gates across the eighteen months for compliance and budget sign-off, the records core handled Waterfall-heavy for compliance, the patient-facing booking handled Agile-heavy in sprints with user feedback — explaining the when and how, not just "it combines both." Project management: schedule it on a Gantt with the stage-gates as milestones, map dependencies, watch the critical path, track plan-versus-actual; choose collaboration tools — version control plus an issue tracker. Communication, I-F-N: involve the department and clinicians through regular demos, enable patient feedback through usability sessions and surveys, negotiate scope around the Scope-Time-Cost triangle when new requests come in. Ethics, PABT: privacy of sensitive health data, accessibility for all patients including the elderly and disabled, bias if automated triage is added, transparency about how data is used. Quality assurance, the three Cs: define measurable criteria — C-U-P-S — from the requirements, check continually through reviews and aligned testing, meet compliance, the health-privacy law and W-C-A-G. That single answer — define, then plan, walking FUN-PERF, TECO, DiP-PP, the approach choice, the Gantt, I-F-N, PABT, and the three Cs through one brief — uses nearly every mnemonic in the block, and that breadth is what an extended "plan this project" question rewards.

QUESTION: Your turn to integrate. A university wants a new online exam system. In one structured answer, choose a development approach and an implementation method with justification, and define two quality criteria. Pause the player, plan across the block, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Development approach: WAgile, or defensibly Agile, but I'll argue WAgile — the exam-integrity and academic-regulation parts need governance and documentation, Waterfall-heavy, while the student-facing exam interface benefits from iterative feedback, Agile-heavy, under shared stage-gates; explain the when and how. Implementation method, DiP-PP: an exam system is high-stakes — a failure during a live exam is catastrophic — so recommend a pilot first, running the new system for one low-stakes quiz or one course before any high-stakes exam, then a phased or parallel rollout; reject direct cut-over, the Knight Capital lesson. Quality criteria, two from C-U-P-S, measurable: correctness — every submitted answer is saved and graded exactly per the marking key, verified by test cases; and security — students can only access their own exam, all access and submissions are logged, and the system resists cheating, verified by security review and penetration testing. A full-mark answer names the approach with its when-and-how, the implementation method matched to the high-stakes risk profile with justification, and two measurable, checkable quality criteria.

NARRATOR: Now the traps, gathered in one place, because cross-topic confusion is where marks leak across this block. Trap one: vague, untestable requirements — "it should be fast" — when every requirement needs measurable acceptance criteria. Trap two: jumping to code before modelling — model first, fixes are cheap on paper. Trap three: confusing the implementation methods — phased splits by feature, pilot splits by user group, parallel runs both at once; never recommend direct for a high-risk system. Trap four: "Waterfall is always bad" — it's right for the stable and regulated; balance it. Trap five: "Agile means no planning or documentation" — false, it plans every sprint. Trap six: WAgile "combines the good parts" with no when-and-how — the band-four answer. Trap seven, on a Gantt: not checking whether a slipped task is on the critical path before declaring the project delayed. Trap eight: treating communication and ethics as soft or optional — projects fail more from poor communication than from technical limits. And trap nine, the big one for this chapter: conflating quality assurance with testing — QA is the process that prevents defects; testing is one activity that detects them.

QUESTION: Quick-fire distinguish round, three in a row, the kind that opens an exam. Pause after each and answer before I do. First: distinguish functional from non-functional requirements.

NARRATOR: Model answer: a functional requirement specifies what the system must do — a behaviour or feature, such as "a teacher can record a grade." A non-functional requirement specifies how well it must do it — a quality attribute such as performance, security, or usability, such as "pages load in under two seconds." Functional is what it does; non-functional is how well. FUN versus PERF.

QUESTION: Second: distinguish the Waterfall and Agile approaches in one sentence each.

NARRATOR: Model answer: Waterfall is a sequential approach that completes each phase before the next, with all requirements planned up front — predictable and well-documented but rigid and slow to change; the water falls once. Agile is an iterative approach that delivers working software in short time-boxed sprints with continuous feedback and lighter documentation — adaptable but less predictable in scope and cost; Agile goes around.

QUESTION: Third: distinguish quality assurance from testing.

NARRATOR: Model answer: quality assurance is the overall, process-oriented framework — criteria, reviews, continual checking, compliance — that ensures the whole solution meets its quality requirements and prevents defects. Testing is one activity within quality assurance: running test cases against the built software to detect defects. QA is the process that prevents; testing is the activity that detects.

NARRATOR: Now pull the two famous failures together, because they map cleanly onto our content. Denver airport's automated baggage system: unbounded requirements and an infeasible plan — a TECO failure on technical and schedule feasibility, and scope creep from missing boundaries — plus a direct, big-bang cut-over of a giant untested system, the worst DiP-PP choice for its risk profile. Result: sixteen months late, hundreds of millions over. Healthcare-dot-gov: a waterfall approach under a fixed deadline with frozen, flawed requirements, late integration, and no real load testing — Waterfall's exact weaknesses, late feedback and late testing, amplified by a deadline with no slack. Result: a launch-day collapse. Same lesson in different costumes: the failures happened in defining and planning, this whole block, long before a single line of code was the problem. Get chapters twenty-three and twenty-four wrong and the build is doomed no matter how good your code. That's the through-line of the whole module's marker — justify, manage, document.

NARRATOR: Final exam-style set — one extended "plan this project" question and two distinguish-and-recommend questions, the review's last self-test. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, the extended one. A small charity wants a volunteer-management web app, built quickly on a tight budget, with requirements that are still evolving. Recommend a development approach and an implementation method, and outline how you would manage and quality-assure the project. Pause, plan across the whole block, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Development approach: Agile, because the requirements are still evolving and the charity needs quick delivery on a tight budget — Agile's short sprints embrace change and deliver working software early, whereas Waterfall's rigidity and late delivery would be wrong here, and full WAgile governance is overhead this small, low-regulation project doesn't need. Implementation method, DiP-PP: stakes and budget are low and speed matters, so direct or a light phased rollout is reasonable — the cost of a brief failure is tolerable, so the expense of parallel isn't justified. Management: schedule the sprints and key milestones on a simple Gantt, track velocity and plan-versus-actual, use a lightweight collaboration tool like Trello plus version control — chosen to fit a small Agile team. Communication, I-F-N: involve the charity through sprint reviews, enable feedback from volunteers each iteration, negotiate the evolving scope around the Scope-Time-Cost triangle. Quality assurance, the three Cs: define measurable criteria — C-U-P-S — from the requirements, check continually through code review and aligned testing, meet compliance, the Privacy Act for volunteer data and W-C-A-G for accessibility. A full-mark answer recommends Agile with justification, matches the implementation method to the low-risk profile, and ties management and QA to the right tools and the three Cs.

QUESTION: Question two. Distinguish direct from parallel implementation, and recommend one for a hospital pharmacy dispensing system. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: direct implementation switches entirely to the new system in a single changeover — fastest and cheapest, no duplicate systems run, but high-risk with no fallback if it fails. Parallel runs old and new simultaneously for a period — the safest, the old system a working fallback and outputs verifiable, but the most expensive. For a hospital pharmacy dispensing system, recommend parallel — a dispensing error could harm or kill a patient, so the system is safety-critical with zero tolerance for failure; parallel provides a guaranteed fallback and lets staff verify the new system's outputs against the known-correct old system before committing, and the higher cost is justified by the catastrophic consequences of an error. Stating the cost-versus-safety trade-off and matching parallel to the critical risk profile is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain how WAgile combines Waterfall and Agile, and give one situation where each kind of intervention is applied. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: WAgile structures the overall project with Waterfall stage-gates — formal review-and-approval checkpoints between major phases that provide governance, predictable milestones, and documentation — while performing the development work within each stage in short Agile sprints that allow flexibility and continuous feedback. Waterfall-style intervention applies where governance dominates — for example, a regulated component such as a patient-records module that requires formal compliance documentation and sign-off. Agile-style intervention applies where adaptability dominates — for example, a user-facing component such as a booking interface whose requirements will evolve with user feedback. Explaining both the gate and sprint mechanisms and giving a clear governance-dominant and adaptability-dominant situation — the when and how — earns full marks.
