NARRATOR: First — employment. Secure development reshapes work. On one side, it creates jobs and skills: a whole profession of security engineers, penetration testers, privacy officers, security champions — what the syllabus elsewhere calls the nature and skills required for employment. On the other side, automation of security — automated scanning, automated incident response, A-I-driven detection — changes and can eliminate roles, and demands constant reskilling; a developer today must know security in a way they didn't a decade ago, or they're left behind. The evaluate move: secure development both creates high-value careers and disrupts existing roles through automation and rising skill requirements, so the employment effect is a shift in the kind of work, not simply more or fewer jobs. Both sides, then a judgement — that's the technique for all six.

NARRATOR: Second — data security. The ethical and legal obligation to actually protect the data you hold. Ethically, when people trust you with their information, you have a duty of care to safeguard it — failing is a breach of trust with real human consequences: identity theft, fraud, exposure of medical or financial details that can ruin lives. Legally, that duty is now written down — mandatory data-breach notification laws requiring you to tell affected people and the regulator when their data is exposed, with penalties for failing to protect it. The evaluate move: data security is both an ethical duty of care and a hard legal requirement enforced by breach-notification laws and penalties, so a failure like Equifax is simultaneously a moral failing toward one hundred and forty-seven million people and a legal liability. The technical thing you've learned all module — the C-I-A triad's confidentiality and integrity — has this ethical and legal shadow.

NARRATOR: Third — privacy. Here's where sixteen-oh-two pays off as law. Privacy is whether collecting and using personal data is justified and consented to — distinct from data security, which protects it once held. The legal frameworks are the names to know: the G-D-P-R, the European data-protection regulation that made privacy-by-design a legal requirement and gave people the right to access and erase their data; and in Australia, the Privacy Act and the Australian Privacy Principles, the A-P-Ps. The evaluate move: privacy must be balanced against legitimate competing interests — security monitoring, fraud prevention, personalised services all want more data, while the individual's right to control their own information wants less; strong design like data minimisation resolves the tension by collecting only what's genuinely needed. Weigh the surveillance-versus-autonomy tension and land on minimisation — that's evaluating, not describing.

QUESTION: Quick check, because the marker tests this exact pair. Distinguish, as ethical and legal issues, between data security and privacy. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Data security is the ethical duty and legal obligation to protect personal data you already hold from unauthorised access and breach — keeping attackers out. Privacy is the distinct issue of whether you should collect and use that data at all, and the individual's right to control how it's handled — governed by laws like the G-D-P-R and the Australian Privacy Principles. You can have one without the other: a company can secure data flawlessly while still violating privacy by over-collecting and selling it. Keeping the protect-it versus should-you-hold-it distinction is the mark — it's the same privacy-is-not-security line from sixteen-oh-two, now as a legal distinction.

NARRATOR: Fourth — copyright. A specific legal protection: it automatically protects original creative works, including source code, from being copied or distributed without permission. It shows up constantly — when you use someone else's library or framework, you are using copyrighted work, and the licence is the permission slip. This connects straight to open-source licensing from Programming for the Web: a permissive licence like M-I-T lets you use the code freely with attribution; a copyleft licence like the G-P-L requires that derivatives also be open-sourced. The evaluate move: respecting copyright and licences is both a legal requirement and an ethical one toward the developers whose work you build on, and violating it — shipping G-P-L code in a closed product, or copying without permission — exposes the enterprise to legal action and reputational harm, however convenient it seemed. Weigh convenience against obligation, and land on compliance.

NARRATOR: Fifth — intellectual property, I-P, the broader category — copyright is one type of it, alongside patents, trademarks and trade secrets. Here's the trap the marker loves: students treat copyright and intellectual property as the same thing. They're not — copyright is one specific kind of I-P. In the security context, I-P includes your proprietary algorithms, your cryptographic implementations, your trade secrets, and protecting them is itself a security objective — confidentiality applied to the company's own crown jewels. The evaluate move: securing intellectual property protects competitive advantage and rewards investment in innovation, but it must be balanced against openness and collaboration — the same open-source ethos that x-z relied on, where shared code benefits everyone. Over-locking everything down stifles the collaboration that drives the field; under-protecting it gives away the value you built. Weigh protection against openness — evaluate, don't just define.

NARRATOR: Sixth — digital disruption, the broad social effect: secure software and the technologies around it transform whole industries and the way society works. On one side, it enables entirely new businesses and services — secure online banking, e-commerce, telehealth — that were impossible before, lifting convenience and access. On the other, it displaces older ways of doing things, concentrates power in the platforms that hold the data, and raises questions about who controls and is accountable for these systems. The evaluate move: secure software enables beneficial digital disruption — new services people genuinely value — but the same disruption displaces existing industries, deepens dependence on a few large providers, and shifts power toward whoever holds the secured data, so the social benefit must be weighed against these costs. Both sides, then a judgement.

NARRATOR: Now the through-line that ties this episode together — responsible disclosure, the ethical heart of security work, where x-z and Equifax both come home. Responsible disclosure is the ethical process for what you do when you find a vulnerability in someone else's software. The dilemma is genuine: announce it publicly straight away and you arm every attacker before the vendor can fix it; say nothing and the flaw stays exploitable and you've failed the users. The responsible path is to disclose it privately to the vendor first, give them a reasonable, agreed window to fix it, and only then publish — balancing the public's right to know against the harm of premature exposure. This is the ethical-versus-legal tension in miniature: hacking a system to find a flaw can be illegal even when your intent is good, which is why ethical hacking and penetration testing — remember pen testing from eighteen-oh-one — must be authorised in advance. And x-z is the case both ways: caught by a researcher who disclosed responsibly, yet itself an attack that abused the open-source trust we just discussed under I-P. Responsible disclosure is where Ed Picid's whole list — ethics, law, data security, the lot — gets resolved in a single decision.

QUESTION: Here's the high-value retrieval — pause the player and do this one, because it's a likely scenario question. You are a security researcher who discovers a serious vulnerability in a hospital's patient-records system. Walk through the ethical and legal considerations and decide what you should do. Pause now, weigh the issues, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's a full-mark answer. The situation engages several of the six issues. Data security and privacy are central — the records are sensitive personal and health data, so there is both an ethical duty of care and a legal obligation under the Privacy Act and Australian Privacy Principles to protect them, and the vulnerability puts that at risk. The ethical-versus-legal tension is acute: testing the flaw further, or accessing real records to prove it, could itself be illegal unauthorised access even with good intent, so I must not exploit it. The responsible course is responsible disclosure: report privately and promptly to the hospital and its vendor, provide enough detail to reproduce and fix it, agree a reasonable remediation timeline, and refrain from public disclosure until the fix is deployed, so attackers aren't armed before the patch. If the vendor ignores it and patients remain at risk, coordinated disclosure through a regulator or C-E-R-T balances the public's right to safety against the harm of premature exposure. The judgement: disclose responsibly, never exploit, prioritise the patients whose data is at stake. Naming the privacy and data-security obligations, the legality of unauthorised access, the responsible-disclosure process, and reaching a justified decision is band six — and the justification, weighing both sides, is what "evaluate" rewards.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Name the six social, ethical and legal issues affecting people and enterprises that result from secure software development. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the six are employment, data security, privacy, copyright, intellectual property, and digital disruption — E-D-P-C-I-D, Ed Picid. Employment concerns the creation, displacement and reskilling of roles; data security is the ethical and legal duty to protect held data; privacy is the right to control whether and how personal data is collected and used; copyright legally protects original works including code; intellectual property is the broader category covering patents, trademarks and trade secrets as well as copyright; and digital disruption is the transformation of industries and society by these technologies. Naming all six with a phrase each is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two. Distinguish between copyright and intellectual property. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: intellectual property is the broad legal category protecting creations of the mind, including several distinct types — copyright, patents, trademarks and trade secrets. Copyright is one specific type: it automatically protects original creative and literary works, including source code, from being copied or distributed without permission. So copyright is a subset of intellectual property, not a synonym for it — confusing the two is a common error. In software, respecting copyright means honouring licences such as M-I-T or G-P-L, while protecting intellectual property more broadly includes safeguarding proprietary algorithms and trade secrets. Stating that copyright is one type within the wider category of intellectual property is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain responsible disclosure and why it is the ethical course of action when a vulnerability is found. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: responsible disclosure is reporting a discovered vulnerability privately to the affected vendor first, allowing a reasonable agreed time to fix it, and publishing details only after a patch is available. It is ethical because it balances two competing harms: disclosing publicly straight away arms attackers before users can be protected, while staying silent leaves the flaw exploitable and fails the users. Reporting privately first protects users during the fix, while a coordinated eventual disclosure preserves the public's right to know and pressures the vendor to act. It also respects the law, since exploiting or further accessing the system without authorisation can itself be illegal even with good intent. Naming the report-privately-then-publish process and the balance-of-harms justification is the mark.

QUESTION: Question four, the extended one. Evaluate the social, ethical and legal implications of an organisation that suffers a major breach of customer data because it failed to apply a known security patch. Pause, plan a structured response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the implications span the legal, ethical and social. Legally, the organisation breaches data-protection law — in Australia the Privacy Act and Australian Privacy Principles — triggering mandatory breach notification to affected individuals and the regulator, and exposing it to substantial penalties and litigation; the failure is aggravated because the patch was known and available, mirroring the Equifax breach, where an unpatched framework vulnerability led to around one hundred and forty-seven million records exposed and settlements in the hundreds of millions. Ethically, the organisation violated its duty of care to the customers who trusted it with their data — a failure of data security that exposes those people to identity theft and fraud, a real human cost; leaving a known patch unapplied is a clear ethical lapse, not mere bad luck. Socially, the breach erodes public trust in digital services and can accelerate calls for stronger regulation, a form of digital disruption that affects the whole industry. Weighing these, the breach is not simply a financial setback but a compound failure of legal compliance, ethical responsibility and social trust — which is why investment in secure practices and timely patching is justified as both a commercial and an ethical obligation. A response that names the law, the ethical duty of care, the social effect, uses Equifax as evidence, and reaches a justified, weighed judgement is full marks.
