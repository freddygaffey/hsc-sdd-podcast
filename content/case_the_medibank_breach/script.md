NARRATOR: Somewhere in Australia, on an ordinary day, a person sits down at a home computer and opens their web browser. Maybe they check the news. Maybe they log into something. The browser, helpful as ever, has remembered all their passwords for them — the way yours probably does right now. It is the most mundane scene imaginable. A person, a laptop, a list of saved logins. Nothing about it looks like the beginning of one of the worst privacy disasters in Australian history. But it is. Because that home computer is quietly infected. There is a piece of malware sitting on it, a kind called an information-stealer, and its entire job is to do one thing: find every saved password on the machine and send them all to a stranger. And among those saved passwords are the keys to a company that holds the most intimate secrets of nearly ten million people.

NARRATOR: Not their money. Not their addresses. Their bodies. Whether they had ever been treated for a mental illness. Whether they had been through an addiction program. Whether they had H-I-V. Whether they had had an abortion, or a miscarriage, or a termination they never told anyone about. The company is Medibank. It is Australia's largest private health insurer, and to do its job — to pay people's medical claims — it has to hold the single most sensitive category of personal information that exists. And in the autumn of twenty twenty-two, all of that data walked out the front door. Not because someone broke a lock. Because the lock only needed one key, when it should have needed two.

NARRATOR: This is the story of the Medibank breach. And what makes it so haunting is not how clever it was. It is how ordinary it was. There is no zero-day here, no nation-state weapon, no exotic exploit. There is a stolen password, a missing second check, and a series of alarms that nobody answered in time. It is the story of how the most catastrophic things in security are very often the most boring — and of what happens when a company that knows the most painful facts about millions of people fails to do the one thing it had been warned, for a year and a half, that it needed to do.

QUESTION: When you log into something important — your bank, your email — and after your password it asks for a code from your phone, what is that second step actually protecting you against?

NARRATOR: Hold that question. The missing answer to it is the hole this whole disaster fell through, and we will come back to it when it matters most. But to feel the weight of it, you have to start where the attackers started, which was not inside Medibank at all. It was outside it, with a single human being who had legitimate access — and no idea he was about to become patient zero.

NARRATOR: His exact identity has never been made public, but the court documents describe him precisely. He was a contractor. Specifically, he worked at an I-T service desk — the kind of role that, by its very nature, has the keys to a lot of doors. To reset someone's password, to fix someone's access, to troubleshoot a system, an I-T service desk operator often needs broad reach across a company's network. And this contractor had exactly that. The credentials assigned to him reached into most, if not all, of Medibank's systems. Some of them weren't ordinary user accounts at all. They were elevated. Administrator-level. The accounts that don't just open one room but unlock the whole building.

NARRATOR: Now, here is the small, human, utterly relatable mistake at the root of everything. While working, this contractor saved his Medibank login details into a browser profile. You have almost certainly done the same thing — the browser pops up its little box, asks "would you like to save this password," and you click yes, because saying yes is convenient and saying no means typing the thing in every single time. But modern browsers do something else, too. They sync. Sign into the same browser profile on another device, and your saved passwords come with you — they follow you from machine to machine, so the password you saved at work appears, automatically, on your computer at home.

NARRATOR: And that is exactly what happened. The Medibank credentials, saved in the browser at work, synced across to the contractor's personal home computer. And that home computer — the one outside the company's walls, outside its security, outside anyone's control — was infected with information-stealing malware. The malware did what it was built to do. It swept up every credential the browser had stored, and among that haul were the keys to Medibank. The dates here matter, because they mark the beginning of the clock. This is believed to have happened around the seventh of August, twenty twenty-two. From that moment, a criminal somewhere held a valid Medibank username and password — including high-privilege access — and Medibank had no idea.

NARRATOR: Stolen credentials like these don't usually go straight to use. They get sold. There is an entire underground economy in exactly this — logs of harvested passwords, traded and brokered, bundled up and put on the market for whoever wants them. So the stolen Medibank keys passed into criminal hands, were recognised for the prize they were, and made their way to an attacker who understood precisely what they had. A way in. A legitimate, working login to a company holding the health records of nearly ten million Australians.

QUESTION: So the attacker has a username and password. They are sitting outside the company, on the internet. How do they actually get inside?

NARRATOR: Through the same door the contractor used. To let staff and contractors work from outside the office, Medibank ran what's called a V-P-N — a virtual private network. Now, you may know V-P-Ns as the consumer privacy products people use to hide their browsing. This is the other kind, the original kind: a corporate V-P-N is a secure, encrypted tunnel that lets an authorised person sitting at home reach into the company's internal network as if they were physically in the building. It is, quite literally, a remote front door. The specific product Medibank used was Palo Alto Networks' Global Protect. And on the twelfth of August, twenty twenty-two — five days after the credentials were stolen — the attacker started trying them out. They poked at Microsoft Exchange, the email system. And then they turned to that V-P-N. The remote front door.

NARRATOR: And here is the single decision this entire catastrophe hinges on. When the attacker presented that stolen username and password to Medibank's Global Protect V-P-N, the door opened. That was all it asked for. The investigation later put it in flat, devastating language: access to Medibank's Global Protect V-P-N did not require two or more proofs of identity. It did not require multi-factor authentication. The tunnel was configured so that a valid username and password — or a device certificate — was enough, on its own, to get in. One factor. Where there should have been two.

NARRATOR: So now picture it. The attacker, sitting at a keyboard who-knows-where, types in a password that belongs to someone else, on a connection coming from somewhere that contractor had never logged in from before — and the system says: welcome. Come in. The door swings wide. And just like that, an outsider is standing inside the network of Australia's largest health insurer, wearing the borrowed identity of someone with the keys to almost everything.

QUESTION: Hold on — why is a stolen password, on its own, such a fatal weakness? Plenty of systems use just a password. What makes that the whole disaster?

NARRATOR: Because of what kind of thing a password actually is. A password is a thing you know. A secret in your head. And the fundamental, unfixable problem with a thing you know is that it can be stolen without you ever feeling it go. Nobody picks your pocket. Nothing is missing in the morning. The secret is simply copied — phished out of you, or lifted off an infected laptop the way it was here — and now the thief knows exactly what you know. And once that's true, the password is useless as proof of identity, because it can no longer tell the difference between you and the person who stole it. Both of you can type the same string of characters. The door has no way to know which one is the real you. That is the flaw at the very heart of password-only authentication, and it is not a flaw you can patch with a longer password or a tougher one. A stolen long password is just as stolen as a short one.

NARRATOR: The fix for this has been industry standard for years, and it is conceptually simple. You require a second proof, of a completely different kind. Not another thing you know — a second thing you know is just a second secret that can also be stolen. You require a thing you have. A physical object that only the real person possesses. Your phone, generating a one-time code. A hardware security key in your pocket. A push notification that pops up on a device only you are holding. That is multi-factor authentication — M-F-A. Two factors, drawn from different categories: something you know, plus something you have. And the genius of it is brutally simple. Even if a criminal steals your password, they still do not have your phone. They have one factor. The door demands two. So the stolen password, on its own, opens nothing. It becomes a dead end. The second lock holds the line.

NARRATOR: This is the answer to the question I asked you at the very start — what is that code from your phone protecting you against? It is protecting you against exactly this. Against the day your password gets stolen and you never even know it happened. M-F-A is the thing that makes a stolen password worthless. And Medibank's remote front door did not have it.

NARRATOR: And that is the entire breach, right there. Everything else — all the horror that follows — is just the consequence of that one missing check. The stolen password should have been a dead end. Instead it was a master key.

NARRATOR: Now, the part that makes this sting even more. This was not an oversight nobody saw coming. Medibank had been told. Repeatedly. In black and white. As far back as the middle of twenty twenty, an external technology firm reviewing the company's defences had flagged the lack of multi-factor authentication as a critical defect. In August of twenty twenty-one, a separate review — by one of the big global consulting firms — found that M-F-A was not in place for privileged users. Privileged users. The exact category of account the attacker would go on to ride straight through the front door. The privacy regulator's case against Medibank later spanned the period from March twenty twenty-one all the way to the breach in October twenty twenty-two — a window of around eighteen months in which the company knew, on paper, in reports it had paid for, that this gap existed. Eighteen months of warnings about the absolute bare minimum of protection. And the second lock still wasn't fitted on the door that mattered.

NARRATOR: So on the twelfth of August, the attacker walks in. And what they do next is the thing that separates a real, professional intrusion from the movie version. They do not smash and grab. They do not panic and start dragging files. They do the patient, quiet, methodical thing. They get inside, they look around, and they begin to move — carefully — through the network, mapping it, learning it, working out where the valuable data actually lives. This is the part of an attack security people call lateral movement and reconnaissance. The break-in is just the start. The real work is finding the vault once you're through the door.

NARRATOR: And the attacker did find it. They located the systems where the real treasure was kept — two of them named in the court documents, a database called M-A-R-S and a system called M-P-L Filer. These held the heart of it: customers' personal details, yes, but also the health claims data. The diagnoses. The procedures. The conditions. Then, having found the vault, they began to empty it. Slowly. Steadily. Over weeks. Between roughly the twenty-fifth of August and the thirteenth of October, the attacker exfiltrated something on the order of five hundred and twenty gigabytes of data out of Medibank's network. Half a terabyte of the most private information about millions of people, siphoned out over a month and a half, a stream of stolen records flowing quietly out the door that one stolen password had unlocked.

QUESTION: But surely a company that size has alarms? Some system watching for exactly this kind of thing? Did nothing trip?

NARRATOR: Something did trip. And this is the second gut-punch of the whole story. Medibank had security monitoring. They had endpoint detection software — the kind of tool that watches the machines on a network for suspicious behaviour and raises an alert when something looks wrong. And around the twenty-fourth and twenty-fifth of August, as the attacker was getting established and starting to move, that software did exactly its job. It generated alerts. It flagged the suspicious activity. The alarm went off.

NARRATOR: And nobody answered it in time. The alerts were not properly triaged. They were not investigated with the urgency they deserved. The signal was there — the system worked, the warning fired — but the human response behind it failed. And so the attacker, undetected in practice even though they had technically been detected on paper, just kept going. Day after day, for weeks more, carrying out the records of nine point seven million people while the alarm that had already gone off sat unanswered. There is a hard lesson buried in that, and we'll come back to it: a security tool that fires an alert nobody acts on is not security. It is theatre. The camera caught the burglar. Nobody was watching the screen.

NARRATOR: It was not until the middle of October that the truth finally surfaced — and even then, partly by chance. Around the eleventh of October, Medibank's security team triaged a high-severity incident tied to a Microsoft Exchange vulnerability that was making headlines at the time. Pulling on that thread, and working with Microsoft's own threat-intelligence specialists, investigators uncovered the much larger horror underneath: the enormous volume of data that had already been quietly siphoned out of the network. On the thirteenth of October, Medibank detected the unusual activity, and shut down parts of its systems. The bleeding had been going on for nearly two months. Now, at last, someone had seen it.

NARRATOR: But seeing it was not the end. It was the beginning of the worst part — because the attacker still had the data. Half a terabyte of it. And on the seventeenth of October, they made contact. They reached out to Medibank to negotiate. They had what every extortionist needs: leverage. And the leverage was not that they'd locked Medibank out of its own files. It was that they held a copy of the most painful secrets of nearly ten million people, and they were willing to publish them.

QUESTION: Put yourself in the company's chair for a moment. The attacker has it all, and the message arrives: pay us, or we publish everything. What do you do?

NARRATOR: This is where the story stops being purely technical and becomes an ethical crisis, played out in front of an entire country. The demand, when it came, was around ten million U-S dollars. And then the attacker did something almost mockingly cruel — they offered a discount. Nine point seven million dollars. One dollar, they said, for each of the nine point seven million customers. A price tag, itemised, one human being's medical privacy per dollar. This is the modern face of extortion, and it is worth being precise about what kind it is. Classic ransomware scrambles your files and sells you the key to unlock them — an attack on your ability to reach your own data. This was the other kind. The attacker hadn't locked anything. They had already taken a copy, and now they threatened to publish it. The leverage wasn't denying Medibank its data. It was the shame and the harm that publication would inflict on millions of innocent people who had done absolutely nothing wrong except be insured.

NARRATOR: And Medibank made a decision that is still debated to this day. They refused to pay. The chief executive, David Koczkar, said so publicly, and the Australian government strongly backed the stance. The reasoning was this. There is no guarantee that a criminal keeps their word. Pay them, and they might publish anyway, and now you have funded a crime and gotten nothing. Worse, paying proves to every criminal watching that Australian companies pay — which paints a target on every other organisation in the country, and on Medibank's own customers, who might then be extorted directly. Koczkar called the prospect of paying a reward to criminals, and warned it could encourage them to extort customers one by one. He called the eventual data release disgraceful — a weaponisation of people's private information. The principle was clear: refusing to pay makes the attack worthless, and in the long run that makes everyone safer.

NARRATOR: But — and this is the unbearable "but" — refusing meant the data got published. And the attacker, true to the threat, began releasing it on a dark-web blog. They did not simply dump a single file and walk away. They were vicious, and they were deliberate, and they understood exactly how to maximise the cruelty. On the ninth of November, they posted files. They labelled them. One they called the "good list." Another, the "naughty list." The naughty list reportedly gathered together customers associated with treatment for H-I-V, for drug and alcohol problems, for mental-health conditions including eating disorders. They had sorted human beings by their most stigmatised diagnoses and published the categories like an index. And the next day, the tenth of November, they posted another file. They labelled it "abortions." It reportedly contained information on procedures customers had claimed for — terminations, miscarriages, ectopic pregnancies.

NARRATOR: Sit with how monstrous that is. This was not just a leak of data. It was data curated to do the maximum possible harm to the most vulnerable people in the entire database. The decisions a person makes about a pregnancy. The illness they fought in private. The addiction they were trying to recover from. The mental-health crisis they survived. The kinds of facts a person might never have told their own family — sorted into lists, given cruel labels, and posted on the internet for anyone to find, permanently, beyond any power to recall. Medibank later confirmed that detailed health claims data, with diagnoses and procedure codes, had been exposed for around a hundred and ninety-two thousand customers, out of the nine point seven million whose personal details were taken in total.

NARRATOR: So you have two genuinely defensible principles colliding head-on, with no clean resolution. Don't reward and fund criminals — the principle that protects everyone, in the future, by making attacks worthless. And protect these specific victims, right now, whose private suffering is about to be broadcast to the world. There is no answer that doesn't hurt someone. Hold the line and the vulnerable get exposed today. Pay and you endanger everyone tomorrow. That collision — a hard trade-off with real human stakes on both sides, and no exit that leaves everyone whole — is what makes this an ethics case, and not merely a security one.

NARRATOR: Now, what happened to the people behind it? For more than a year, the attackers were a shadow — a criminal group with links to Russian ransomware operations, but no name, no face. Investigators kept working. And on the twenty-third of January, twenty twenty-four, more than a year after the breach, three governments acted together. Australia, the United States, and the United Kingdom jointly named and sanctioned a man they accused of being central to the attack. His name was Aleksandr Ermakov — a thirty-three-year-old Russian national who had operated online under a string of aliases, names like GustaveDore, JimJones, and blade runner. He was linked to REvil, the notorious Russia-based ransomware crew. And the sanctioning was itself a landmark: Ermakov was the first person ever sanctioned under Australia's autonomous cyber-sanctions framework — a new legal power built precisely so the country could name and punish cybercriminals beyond its reach.

NARRATOR: The sanctions made it a serious crime, under Australian law, to give Ermakov money or to deal with his assets — including, pointedly, through cryptocurrency or ransom payments — punishable by up to a decade in prison. It was, in effect, the government closing the door on him for good, and warning anyone who might think of paying him. He was later reported to have been detained in Russia on domestic charges relating to malicious software. Whether real justice ever reaches him is another question — Russia does not extradite its nationals — but for the first time, the faceless attacker had a face, and a name, and a price on his ability to operate.

NARRATOR: So let me pull back now, and draw out what this story is really about — the things worth carrying long after the headlines have faded.

NARRATOR: Start with the cause, because it is so clear it almost hurts. The breach happened because authentication relied on a single factor — a password — and a password can be stolen. The entire nine-point-seven-million-person disaster traces back to one missing control: multi-factor authentication on the remote access. Everything downstream — the lateral movement, the half a terabyte siphoned out, the lists with the cruel labels, the abortions file, the impossible decision about the ransom — all of it flows from that one gap. A password proves you know a secret. It does not prove you are you, because secrets get stolen, silently, without a trace. Multi-factor authentication adds a second, different kind of proof — something you have, not just something you know — so that a stolen password, by itself, opens nothing at all. If you want one sentence to take from this entire story, make it this one: multi-factor authentication is what makes a stolen password worthless. Medibank is the textbook proof of what it costs to skip it.

NARRATOR: And notice the shape of the failure, because it is the most common shape in all of security. This was not an exotic attack. There was no genius hacker bending the laws of computing. There was a known weakness, flagged in writing, eighteen months before it was exploited, and simply not fixed. The most devastating breaches almost never come from some dazzling, unforeseeable technique. They come from the boring, known, already-warned-about gap that nobody got around to closing. The glamorous part of security is imagining sophisticated attackers. The part that actually saves you is the unglamorous discipline of acting on the warning you already have, before someone else does.

NARRATOR: Then there is what was lost, because not all data is equal. Health information is the most sensitive category of personal data there is, precisely because of what publishing it does to a person. You can cancel a stolen credit card. The bank can issue you a new number, and within days the harm is undone. You cannot un-publish the fact that someone had an abortion, or fought an addiction, or was treated for a mental illness. There is no reissuing that. Once it is out, the discrimination, the shame, the danger, the damage to relationships and to a sense of safety — those cannot be reversed by anyone. That is why the law protects health data most heavily of all, and why the duty of care on an organisation that holds it is so very heavy. The cost of getting it wrong isn't measured in dollars. It is measured in human lives quietly upended.

NARRATOR: There is a thread about responding, too, and it is one people skip past too quickly. Prevention is not the whole of security. The attacker was inside Medibank's network for weeks, and the alarms that fired were not acted on in time. Good defence is not only the locks on the doors — it is whether anyone is watching the cameras, and whether anyone moves the instant something trips. Detecting an intrusion early and responding fast is the difference between a catastrophe and a near-miss. The same intrusion, caught and contained on day one instead of week six, is a footnote instead of a national scandal. A security tool that raises an alert nobody answers has bought you nothing. The detection has to be wired to a response, or the detection is just decoration.

NARRATOR: And finally, the hardest layer, the one without a tidy answer: the ethics. Whether to pay an extortion demand is a genuine dilemma, with strong arguments on both sides, and reasonable people still disagree about whether Medibank made the right call. But notice this. The dramatic, agonising question — should we pay the criminals — only arises because the earlier, quiet, boring thing didn't get done. The most ethical moment in this entire story was not the public decision about the ransom, made under a national spotlight. It was a quiet decision, more than a year earlier, that nobody would ever have made a headline out of: whether to enforce a second factor on a remote-access login. Get that small, dull thing right, and the dramatic ethical crisis never happens at all. The real ethics of security usually live in the unglamorous decisions made long before anyone is watching.

NARRATOR: So here it is, whole, in one breath. A contractor's saved passwords, synced from work to a home computer, and lifted off that home computer by malware. A criminal market that turned those keys into a sale, and an attacker who knew exactly what they were worth. A remote front door — a corporate V-P-N — that asked for a password and nothing else, one factor where there needed to be two, in spite of eighteen months of written warnings to fit the second lock. A patient, professional intruder who walked in, found the vault, and siphoned out half a terabyte of the most private data imaginable over six unhurried weeks. Alarms that fired and went unanswered. Nine point seven million people's most intimate health records, carried out the door. A ransom demand priced at one dollar per human being, a refusal made on principle, and then the cruellest publication imaginable — the suffering of the most vulnerable people, sorted into labelled lists and posted to the world, forever. And finally, more than a year later, a name and a face for the man behind it.

NARRATOR: But strip all of it away, and what remains is one stolen password that should have been a dead end. It would have been a dead end — harmless, useless, worthless — behind one more lock. The whole catastrophe lived in the gap between one factor and two. Build your systems as though the password will be stolen. Because someday, somewhere, it will be — silently, without a sound, without a trace. And the only thing standing between that theft and a disaster like this one is the second lock you remembered to fit before anyone ever came knocking.
