NARRATOR: It is just after half past eight in the evening, on the second of November, nineteen eighty-eight, and a twenty-two-year-old graduate student is about to do something he will regret for the rest of his life. He is sitting at a terminal at Cornell University, in upstate New York, but the program he is about to release will not go out from Cornell. He has logged in, across the network, to a computer at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, hundreds of kilometres away in Cambridge. He is launching it from there, from a machine that is not his own, so that if anyone ever comes looking, the trail will start in the wrong place. That single precaution tells you he already knew, on some level, that what he was doing was not entirely innocent. Then he runs the program. And for a few minutes, nothing seems to happen at all.

NARRATOR: The student's name is Robert Tappan Morris. The program he has just set loose is about ninety-nine lines of clever, compact C code, and his stated intention — the thing he would maintain for years afterward — was almost gentle. He wanted to count the internet. He wanted to know how big this network of networks really was, so he wrote something that would crawl quietly from machine to machine, copying itself, tallying the computers as it went, like a census-taker walking from house to house in the dark. That was the idea. What actually happened, over the next twelve hours, was that his census-taker went insane. It broke into thousands of computers across the United States, copied itself again and again and again until the machines choked on copies of it, and ground a tenth of the entire internet to a dead halt. It was the first time the network had ever been attacked at scale. It was the first time most people on it even realised that was possible. And almost everything we now call cybersecurity — the whole discipline, the whole industry — can trace a line back to this one night.

NARRATOR: But to feel why this night matters, you have to understand what the internet was in nineteen eighty-eight, because it was nothing like the thing you carry in your pocket. There was no web. There were no browsers, no websites, no companies. It was a small, quiet, trusting network of universities, research labs, and a handful of military and government sites. By the best estimates of the time, there were only about sixty thousand computers connected to it in the entire world. Sixty thousand. The people on it largely knew each other, or knew someone who knew each other. It had been built by academics, for academics, on a single foundational assumption: that everyone connected to it was a colleague, acting in good faith. There were locks on some doors. But an enormous number of doors were simply left open, because why on earth wouldn't they be? You were among friends. There were no burglars, because nobody had ever thought to be one.

NARRATOR: Robert Morris was the first burglar. And the deeply strange thing — the thing that makes this story sit so uneasily — is who he was. Because Robert Tappan Morris was the son of Robert Morris senior, one of the most important computer security experts alive. The father had spent decades at Bell Labs, where he helped build the Unix operating system itself. He wrote the program that encrypted passwords on Unix systems. And in nineteen seventy-nine, he co-authored, with the legendary Ken Thompson, a paper called "Password Security: A Case History" — a foundational document about exactly how people choose weak, guessable passwords and how attackers exploit that. By nineteen eighty-eight, Robert Morris senior was the chief scientist at the National Computer Security Center, which is the public face of the National Security Agency, the N-S-A — America's most secretive electronic spying organisation. So the worm that taught the world about weak passwords was written by the son of the man who literally wrote the paper on weak passwords. You could not invent that. We'll come back to it, because it matters more than it first appears.

QUESTION: Here's the question that makes this whole thing historic. How does a program copy itself from one computer to another, all on its own, with no human being helping it along — nobody clicking, nobody installing it, nobody even awake?

NARRATOR: Answering that question is, quite literally, the definition of a worm — and the Morris worm is the textbook case the word was built around. We throw the term "virus" around for any nasty software, but there is a real, precise distinction here, and you should hold onto it, because it is exactly the kind of thing an exam wants you to get right. A virus is like a biological virus: it needs a host and it needs help. It hides inside a file or a program, and it only spreads when a human being runs that infected thing — carrying it along like a passenger, opening the attachment, clicking the link. A virus is patient and parasitic; it waits to be picked up. A worm needs no passenger at all. A worm spreads itself across a network, on its own, under its own power. It finds another computer, breaks into it, copies itself across, and then from that fresh machine it immediately starts hunting for the next one, and the next, and the next, automatically, with no human in the loop anywhere. It propagates by itself. That self-propagation is the entire essence of a worm — and the entire reason it is so much more dangerous than a virus. A virus moves at the speed of human clicks. A worm moves at the speed of the network.

NARRATOR: So the real question — the one Robert Morris had to solve to make any of this work — is brutally specific. How does the program break into each new machine, with no person there to let it in? And his answer was not one trick. It was a small, vicious toolkit of them. The worm tried several different ways into every computer it found, one after another, like a burglar trying the front door, then the back door, then the windows, then the cat flap. And here is what should make the hairs on your neck stand up: every single one of those break-in techniques teaches a lesson that is still, nearly four decades later, exactly how systems get broken into today. The technology has changed beyond all recognition. The mistakes have not changed at all.

NARRATOR: The first way in is the most technically famous, and its name is one you will hear again and again in security for the rest of your life: a buffer overflow. Now, the idea is simpler than the jargon makes it sound, so let me make you see it. Imagine a paper form, and on that form there is a box that says "name," with room for, say, twenty letters. You are meant to write your name in it. But the box is badly designed — there's no edge that stops you. If you just keep writing past the end of the box, your extra letters don't get rejected; they spill out and write over whatever was printed on the rest of the form below. And now imagine that the stuff printed below the box happens to be the form's own instructions — the part that says "when you're done, do this next." By overflowing the box, you have overwritten those instructions with your own words. That is a buffer overflow.

NARRATOR: In a real computer, a "buffer" is just a fixed-size patch of memory that a program sets aside to hold some incoming data — the box on the form. When a program carelessly accepts more data than the buffer was built to hold, the excess spills out and overwrites the memory sitting right next to it. And in that adjacent memory, among other things, sits the program's own record of what to run next — the address it will jump to when it finishes. So a carefully crafted overflow — too much data, precisely shaped, exactly the right length — does not just crash the program. It overwrites that "what to do next" address with a pointer to the attacker's own code, which the attacker has helpfully smuggled in as part of the oversized input. The input the attacker sent becomes the code the machine runs. The data turns into commands.

NARRATOR: The Morris worm found a program on these Unix computers that had exactly this flaw. It was a small, mundane network service called fingerd — the "finger daemon" — and its job was completely harmless. If you wanted to know whether your colleague was logged in, you could "finger" their machine, and fingerd would politely report back who was on it. Utterly boring. But fingerd had been written using a notorious C function called gets — g-e-t-s — which reads input from the network into a buffer without ever checking how much input is arriving. The buffer was five hundred and twelve bytes long. fingerd just kept reading, trusting that nobody would ever send more than that. So the worm sent it five hundred and thirty-six bytes — deliberately, precisely too long — and those extra bytes spilled past the end of the buffer and overwrote the program's instructions with a command to start a shell, a command-line. Instead of answering a harmless question about who was logged in, the machine handed the worm a fully open command prompt, running with the program's own privileges. No password. No permission. No human. The worm was simply in, because the program had trusted its input not to be too big.

QUESTION: Where have you heard that shape before — a program trusting data from outside that turns out to be a weapon in disguise?

NARRATOR: Everywhere. That is the point. It is the same fundamental shape as a huge fraction of all the attacks across the decades since: a program trusts data coming in from the outside world, and that trust is betrayed. The buffer overflow is the memory-level version of it — the input is too big and spills into territory it should never have been allowed to reach. It comes down to a single property called memory safety: whether a program rigorously guards the boundaries of its own memory, refusing to let any input run even one byte past the edge of the space set aside for it. The languages and tools of nineteen eighty-eight made this catastrophically easy to get wrong — C will let you walk straight off the end of a buffer and say nothing — and fingerd got it wrong. And here is the part that should genuinely bother you: decades later, we are still fighting buffer overflows. They remain one of the oldest and most persistent classes of vulnerability there is. The Morris worm is their grand public debut, the first time one was used at scale to break into the world. The same bug is still being found in shipping software this year.

NARRATOR: But the worm didn't rely on the overflow alone. Its second route in was a flaw in a completely different program — the mail program, sendmail, the software that moved electronic mail between machines. Sendmail had a feature called debug mode, meant to help the people who developed it test their changes. The trouble was that debug mode was switched on by default on an enormous number of systems, and when it was on, it let you do something it absolutely should never have allowed: instead of just naming a person to deliver the mail to, you could send a command, and the machine would run it. The worm connected to sendmail, slipped into debug mode, and instead of delivering a message, it delivered an instruction: pull down the rest of my code, and run it. The mail server, trying to be helpful, obliged. A diagnostic convenience that someone had left switched on became a wide-open door. If that sounds familiar — a powerful debugging or administrative feature accidentally left enabled in production, where the whole world can reach it — it should, because that exact mistake is still handing attackers control of systems today.

NARRATOR: And the third route in is, if anything, the most human of all. The worm tried to guess passwords. It carried, built into itself, a list of around four hundred and thirty common passwords — obvious, predictable ones, the kind people picked precisely because they were easy to remember. It tried them, one by one, against the accounts on each machine. It also tried the username itself as the password, and the username spelled backwards, and other simple variations, because people did that constantly. And when that failed, it could reach for the system's own dictionary — the word list used for spell-checking — and grind through that too. It worked, over and over again, because in nineteen eighty-eight — and, let me be blunt, in twenty twenty-six — an enormous number of people protect their accounts with a password a machine can guess in seconds. Once the worm guessed a valid password, there was no clever exploit needed at all. It just walked in the front door and used the account as if it owned it. This is weak authentication: relying on a secret that is trivially guessable. And remember whose son wrote this. The man who co-authored the founding paper on weak Unix passwords raised the boy whose worm conquered the internet partly by guessing weak Unix passwords. The lesson the father had tried to teach the whole field, the son demonstrated to the whole world, by accident, in a single night.

NARRATOR: So picture the worm now, alive and loose. It lands on a machine — through the overflow, or the mail debug hole, or a guessed password. It copies its code across. It covers its tracks, deleting the temporary files it used to get in. And then, from that new foothold, it immediately starts scanning the network for more machines to attack, trying all three tricks again on each one. One machine becomes two. Two become four. The thing fans out across the country at the speed of the wires. And up to this point, you could almost call it a successful experiment. A bit reckless, a bit rude, but it was spreading, quietly counting, doing more or less what its author intended.

NARRATOR: And then one design decision turned the experiment into a catastrophe.

QUESTION: If you were Robert Morris, and you'd written a worm that spreads from machine to machine, what's the one obvious problem you'd worry about — the thing that could ruin your nice clean census?

NARRATOR: Reinfection. He worried about reaching a machine that already had a copy of the worm on it. Because if the worm politely refused to reinfect a machine that was already running it, then a clever system administrator could defend themselves trivially: just fake it. Set a flag on your machine that says "already infected," and the worm would believe you, skip you, and move on. Your machine would be immune, and your immunity would also corrupt his count. So Morris built in a defence against that fake-out. He made the worm reinfect a machine sometimes, even when a copy appeared to be already there. Specifically — and this number is the whole tragedy in a single statistic — roughly one time in seven, about fourteen percent of the time, the worm would install a fresh copy of itself regardless of whether one was already running. He reasoned that this would defeat anyone trying to immunise their machine with a fake flag.

NARRATOR: What it actually did was turn every infected computer into a place where copies of the worm piled up without limit. Think about what one in seven really means once thousands of machines are all attacking each other constantly. A machine gets infected. Then another copy of the worm comes knocking, and one time in seven, it gets in too. Now there are two. Then a third arrives, and the dice roll again. Copies accumulated — two, then five, then dozens — each one a full running program, each one eating memory, each one consuming the processor, each one spawning still more attempts. Computers in nineteen eighty-eight were not powerful. A machine running dozens of copies of an aggressively scanning worm doesn't slow down gracefully. It seizes. The processor is one hundred percent consumed by the worm. Legitimate work stops dead. The machine becomes, in the words of one observer that week, dead in the water — useless until somebody disinfects it. The worm didn't damage a single file. It didn't steal anything. It didn't read anyone's secrets. It just multiplied, and the multiplication itself was the weapon. It ate the computers alive with copies of itself.

NARRATOR: So across that night and into the next day, system administrators all over the country watched their machines bog down, freeze, and fall over, with no idea why. A student at the University of California, Berkeley, sent an email into the dark that night with three words that capture the whole moment: "We are currently under attack." But here is the cruellest twist of the night. The network that connected all these machines — the thing that let the administrators email each other to coordinate, to warn each other, to share a fix — was the very same network the worm was travelling along. And the worm was faster than the humans. As people tried to raise the alarm, their warnings crawled through a network so clogged with worm traffic that the messages barely moved. The fire and the fire alarm were running down the same wire, and the fire was winning. Some sites, in desperation, did the only thing that obviously worked: they pulled the plug. They physically disconnected themselves from the wider network. That protected them — but it also cut them off from every warning and every fix that anyone else was trying to send. The internet itself fractured into islands that night, as terrified administrators severed their own connections to survive.

NARRATOR: One witness, the astronomer and security writer Clifford Stoll, later recorded that around two thousand computers were infected within the first fifteen hours, and that each one took something like two days of work to clean up and restore. By the estimates of the time, the worm ultimately hit somewhere around six thousand machines. And here's where that famous figure comes from, because it's worth being honest about: nobody did a precise count. Someone reasoned that there were roughly sixty thousand computers on the internet, guessed that the worm had hit about ten percent of them, and ten percent of sixty thousand is six thousand. So the number is an estimate built on an estimate. But whatever the true figure, the proportion is staggering. For a network of sixty thousand machines, taking out something on the order of six thousand of them, in a single night, was an extraordinary, unprecedented blow. The damage in lost time and frantic recovery was later estimated, very roughly, at anywhere from one hundred thousand dollars to ten million dollars, depending on whom you asked and how you counted.

NARRATOR: And Robert Morris, by every account, was horrified at what he had unleashed. This was not what he had wanted. As it became sickeningly clear how fast and how badly his program was spreading, he did try to stop it. He reached out to a friend, and together they composed an anonymous message — instructions, sent out across the network, explaining how administrators could kill the worm and protect their machines. It was the right thing to do. It was also far too late, and undone by the very disaster it was trying to fix. Because the network was now so thoroughly clogged with the worm's own traffic that the message explaining how to stop the worm could barely get through the congestion the worm had caused. The cure couldn't reach the patient, because the disease had blocked the road. The instructions arrived, for most people, long after they'd already worked it out the hard way.

NARRATOR: And here is where this story becomes a genuine first in another sense entirely — a legal one. Because Robert Morris was identified. The investigation traced the worm back through the network, and despite his attempt to launch it from M-I-T to disguise its Cornell origin, the trail led to him. And what followed had never happened before to anyone. Two years earlier, in nineteen eighty-six, the United States Congress had passed a brand-new law called the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, which made it a federal crime to gain unauthorised access to computers. It had barely been used. Robert Tappan Morris became the first person ever convicted under it. The first felony conviction for what we would now simply call hacking. He was found guilty in nineteen ninety, and his conviction was upheld on appeal. He was sentenced to three years of probation, four hundred hours of community service, and a fine of just over ten thousand dollars, plus the costs of his supervision. Releasing a program onto the network had, in that courtroom, formally become a crime — with a young man standing up to answer for it. The line between an experiment and an offence had been drawn, in law, for the very first time.

NARRATOR: And then comes the epilogue, which is so improbable it would be rejected from a novel for being too neat. Robert Tappan Morris did not vanish in disgrace. He finished his doctorate. And in nineteen ninety-nine, he became a professor of computer science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology — at M-I-T, the very institution whose network he had used to launch the worm that brought the academic computing world to its knees. He went on to co-found a successful software company, and then to co-found Y Combinator, one of the most influential technology investors in the world. The first person ever convicted of a computer crime became a celebrated and tenured computer scientist at one of the finest universities on Earth. Make of that what you will.

NARRATOR: So let me draw out what this old, old story is really about — because for something that happened before most of the internet existed, before the web, before nearly everyone listening to this was born, it is astonishingly, almost eerily modern. Every lesson in it is a lesson we are still, expensively, relearning.

NARRATOR: The clearest one is about availability. We instinctively think of security as keeping secrets safe — stopping data from being stolen, read, or leaked. But notice: the Morris worm stole nothing. It read nothing. It broke no confidentiality whatsoever. It didn't even alter the data on the machines it hit — so it didn't attack integrity either. What it destroyed was availability — the simple, fundamental ability to actually use the systems. The computers were all still there. The data was all still sitting on the disks, untouched, perfectly intact. But you couldn't get a single thing done, because the machines were drowning under copies of the worm. And the lesson there is permanent: a system you cannot use is, for every practical purpose, a system that has failed. It doesn't matter how perfectly your secrets are kept if nobody can reach the service at all. Keeping a system up and responsive — defending its availability against whatever floods it until it collapses — is one of the three core pillars of information security, every bit as much as keeping its secrets or keeping its data correct. The Morris worm is the founding example of an availability attack. And you can draw a straight, unbroken line from this night in nineteen eighty-eight to the modern denial-of-service attacks that knock services offline by burying them in traffic — the same idea, the same crime: not breaking in to steal, but flooding until the lights go out.

NARRATOR: Then there are the three ways it got in, and not one of them has gone out of date. The buffer overflow in fingerd is the ancestor of an entire dynasty of memory-safety bugs — the failure to guard the boundaries of memory, to check that input actually fits the space you set aside for it before you accept it. The sendmail debug mode is the eternal reminder that a powerful feature left switched on by default, where the world can reach it, is a gift to attackers — convenience and security are forever pulling against each other, and someone left convenience winning. And the weak passwords are the oldest lesson of all: authentication is only ever as strong as the secret behind it, and a password a machine can guess from a short list is barely a lock at all. The very first internet attack we have a record of came in through a memory bug, a misconfigured default, and a guessable password — and those same three doors are still, decades later, among the most common ways attackers get in, every single day. The technology changed beyond recognition. The human mistakes did not change one bit.

NARRATOR: And finally — the thing that makes this the true origin story, the night the whole field was born. Until this night, the connected world ran purely on trust, and the worm shattered that trust in a single evening. Suddenly everyone understood, all at once, that the network had enemies, or could be harmed by a friend, and that nobody was watching the gate, because nobody had ever thought a gate was needed. And the reaction was immediate, and it is still with us. In direct response to the chaos of the Morris worm, the United States government funded the creation of the very first coordinated incident-response team — a central group, set up at Carnegie Mellon University, to be the place you call when this happens again. A place to coordinate the response, to warn everyone, to organise the cleanup, so that the next time, the people defending the network would not be a thousand panicking administrators on severed islands, but a coordinated body with a plan. We now call these teams C-E-R-Ts — Computer Emergency Response Teams. Nearly every country and every major organisation has one now, and they are all, in a sense, descended from that very first one, stood up in the smoking aftermath of this single worm. The idea that when an attack hits you don't panic alone — that you have a planned, practised, coordinated response and people whose actual job is to answer the call — that whole discipline of incident response was born here, in the wreckage.

NARRATOR: So here it is, whole, in one breath. A small, trusting network of sixty thousand machines, built by academics who assumed everyone was a friend. A curious twenty-two-year-old — the son, of all people, of the N-S-A's chief computer scientist — who wanted to count the network, and wrote a program that could spread on its own to do the counting. A worm, by the truest definition of the word. Three old, eternal mistakes for it to ride in on: a program that didn't check whether its input fit the memory it had, a powerful feature left carelessly switched on, and accounts protected by passwords a machine could guess from a short list. And one single fatal decision — reinfect anyway, just in case someone tries to fake immunity — that turned a quiet census into an uncontrolled flood, piling copies upon copies until the machines seized and died. It stole nothing. It read nothing. It broke no secrets and altered no data. It simply took away the ability to use the computers at all — and in doing so it taught the entire world, in a single night, three things it has never been allowed to forget: that the network had enemies, that availability is something you have to fight to keep, and that someone, somewhere, needs to be ready to answer the call when the next one comes. The first internet attack, and the first organised defence against attacks, were born the very same night. We have been living in the world that night made ever since.
