The first three are the C-I-A triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — answering "what are we protecting?" NARRATOR: Objectives up front, so your attention knows what to catch. By the end you'll define each of the six precisely; distinguish the two pairs people always confuse — confidentiality versus integrity, and authentication versus authorisation; and name a concrete control for each. The classic exam questions are "distinguish between authentication and authorisation" and "for each element of the C-I-A triad, describe one threat and one control" — so we'll drill exactly those.

NARRATOR: Start with the triad — what we protect. First property, confidentiality. Confidentiality means information is accessible only to those authorised to see it — keeping secrets secret. The textbook example is medical records: viewable by the patient, their doctor, and authorised staff, nobody else. The threats are the ones that disclose data to the wrong eyes: a data breach, where someone reads a database they shouldn't; eavesdropping, intercepting network traffic in transit; social engineering, tricking a person into handing over information; and the insider threat, an authorised user looking at data beyond their role. The controls are encryption — both at rest, so a stolen database is unreadable, and in transit, which is exactly what T-L-S and H-T-T-P-S gave us back in Programming for the Web — plus access controls that only let the right people in. One line for the exam: confidentiality is preventing unauthorised disclosure, achieved mainly through encryption and access control.

NARRATOR: Second property, integrity. Integrity means data stays accurate, complete, and unmodified by anyone unauthorised — the data you read is the data that was written, untampered. The textbook example is a bank balance: if someone can quietly change the number, that's fraud. The threats are the ones that alter data: tampering with stored records; malicious code injection, like S-Q-L injection rewriting your database — the injection family we fix in chapter sixteen; a man-in-the-middle attack altering data as it crosses the network; and malware modifying files. The controls are hashing, digital signatures, and input validation. And a marquee callback — remember "hashing is one-way, encryption is two-way"? Hashing is the integrity workhorse: you hash the data, store the fingerprint, and re-hash later — if the fingerprints differ, the data was tampered with. We build that out fully next episode. One line: integrity is preventing unauthorised modification, achieved through hashing, digital signatures, and validation.

NARRATOR: Now the trap, because the exam loves it. Confidentiality and integrity are not the same thing, and they fail independently. Confidentiality is about who can *read* the data; integrity is about whether the data can be *changed*. A vandal who can't read your file but can scramble it has broken integrity while confidentiality held. A spy who reads your file but changes nothing has broken confidentiality while integrity held. The marker wants them kept distinct: confidentiality is secrecy, integrity is trustworthiness. Don't blur them into "keeping data safe" — name which one.

NARRATOR: Third property, availability. Availability means the system and its data are accessible to authorised users when they need them. There's no point having perfectly confidential, perfectly intact data if nobody can reach it. The textbook example is online banking, which has to be up twenty-four-seven. The threats knock the system over: denial-of-service attacks that flood a server with bogus requests until it can't serve real ones; resource exhaustion, like a memory leak or a full disk; hardware failure; and plain software bugs — an infinite loop, a deadlock, a crash. The controls keep it running: rate limiting, to throttle a flood; redundancy and failover, so a backup takes over; the circuit-breaker pattern, to stop one failing service dragging the rest down; and backups for recovery. This is a direct interleave back to Programming for the Web episode eleven-oh-four, big data and web architectures. Remember why we chose microservices and a content delivery network? So one component failing doesn't take everything with it. That whole architecture conversation was, in security terms, an availability conversation. And remember the forward hook I planted there — the Dyn D-N-S outage of twenty-sixteen, where a flood of traffic from hijacked devices took down the name servers a huge chunk of the web depended on? That's an availability attack, a story we'll cash in later. One line: availability is keeping the service reachable, achieved through rate limiting, redundancy, and recovery planning.

QUESTION: Quick check before we switch from "what" to "how". For each of the three — confidentiality, integrity, availability — name the one-word version of what it protects. Pause, say all three out loud, then play.

NARRATOR: Confidentiality protects secrecy — who can read it. Integrity protects accuracy — whether it can be changed. Availability protects access — whether you can reach it. Secrecy, accuracy, access. If you can rattle those three off, the triad is yours. Now the triple-A — how we actually protect those three properties — and this is where the most-confused pair in the whole syllabus lives.

NARRATOR: Fourth property, authentication. Authentication is verifying that users are who they claim to be, before granting any access — proving identity. The student logging into the school portal proves it's really them. How? Through authentication factors — a classic "list them" target, so here's the hook. Three classic factors plus a fourth: something you Know, something you Have, something you Are — and somewhere you Are. Know, Have, Are, plus Where. Something you know is a password or P-I-N. Something you have is your phone or a hardware token. Something you are is biometric — a fingerprint or face. And somewhere you are is location, like an I-P restriction. Multi-factor authentication — M-F-A — means combining two or more of these, so stealing one factor isn't enough. And authentication is where secure password storage lives — you never store the password itself, you store a salted hash of it: the "hashing is one-way" idea again, and the home topic of next episode. One line: authentication proves who you are, using factors you know, have, or are.

NARRATOR: Fifth property, authorisation. Authorisation determines what an authenticated user is allowed to do. Authentication let you in the front door; authorisation decides which rooms you can enter. The textbook example is crisp: a student can view their own grades, but cannot change them and cannot view anyone else's. The dominant model — and the one to name in an answer — is role-based access control, R-B-A-C: you attach permissions to roles, like student, teacher, admin, and attach users to roles, so the teacher role carries "read all grades" and "write grades" while the student role carries only "read own grades". There are other models — attribute-based, where the decision uses attributes and context — but role-based is your default answer. Riding on top is least privilege: give each role the minimum permissions it needs, nothing more, so a compromised student account can't touch the gradebook. One line: authorisation controls what you may do, usually through role-based access control with least privilege.

QUESTION: This is the one. Pause the player and actually answer it, because it is the most common distinguish question in the entire module. What is the difference between authentication and authorisation? Give me a one-sentence distinction and an example of each. Pause now, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here it is. Authentication is verifying *who you are*; authorisation is determining *what you are allowed to do*. Authentication comes first and answers identity — username and password, then M-F-A. Authorisation comes second and answers permission — once we know it's you, are you allowed to open this record? The example that nails it: logging into the school portal successfully is authentication; once in, seeing your own grades but not changing them or seeing others' is authorisation. The marker's red flag is using the words interchangeably, or writing "the system authorises the user's login" when you mean authenticates. Lock the order in: authenticate, then authorise. Who, then what. And a clean memory line — authentication is who you are; authorisation is what you can do — the exact pair I promised back in Programming for the Web, now formal.

NARRATOR: Sixth property, accountability. Accountability means every security-relevant action is logged and traceable to a specific user — so you can answer "who did what, and when?" after the fact. The textbook example: when a grade is changed, the system records who changed it, when, the old and new value, and from which I-P address. The components are audit logging — recording the security events; non-repudiation — making it so a user cannot credibly deny an action they took, which is exactly what a digital signature gives you, coming in episode fifteen-oh-two; forensic analysis — reconstructing an incident from the logs; and compliance reporting. Accountability pays off after everything else has done its job: even with perfect authentication and authorisation, people make mistakes and insiders go rogue, and the audit trail is how you detect it, investigate it, and prove it. One line: accountability is logging actions so they're traceable and undeniable.

NARRATOR: Now see the triple-A as a pipeline, because that's the structure markers reward. A single secure action runs the three in order. Authentication: prove who you are. Authorisation: check whether you're allowed to do this specific thing. Then the action happens. Accountability: log that it happened, with who and when. Authenticate, authorise, act, log. That's a teacher updating a grade: the system confirms it's the teacher, confirms the teacher role may write grades for this class, writes the grade, and records the change in the audit log. Narrate that four-step flow and you can answer any "how do authentication, authorisation and accountability work together" question. The full version of that flow is in Listing two, and the exam-style pseudocode for it is in Listing four — but you don't need to see them: the four words are the whole idea, authenticate, authorise, act, log.

NARRATOR: Now the worked example, because the highest-value question on this dot-point is "take a real system and name a control for each property." The scenario: an online banking application. Walk all six. Confidentiality: encrypt account data at rest and force H-T-T-P-S so balances can't be read in transit. Integrity: hash or sign each transaction record so a tampered balance is detectable, and validate every input. Availability: rate-limit login attempts and run redundant servers so a denial-of-service flood or a crash doesn't take banking offline. Authentication: require a password plus a one-time code — multi-factor — to prove identity. Authorisation: ensure a logged-in customer can see and move money in their *own* accounts only, never anyone else's — role and ownership checks. Accountability: log every transfer with user, amount, timestamp, and I-P, so any disputed transaction can be traced. Six properties, six concrete controls — that structure, property then control, is the band-six answer.

NARRATOR: Now the weak-versus-strong contrast, because it's the difference between a four and a six. Take one property — confidentiality of bank data. A weak answer: "the bank keeps the data secure so hackers can't get it." Vague — it names no property and no mechanism. A strong answer: "confidentiality is maintained by encrypting account data at rest and using T-L-S to encrypt it in transit, so that even if data is intercepted or a database is stolen, it cannot be read by an unauthorised party." Hear the difference — the strong answer names the property, names the control, and states the effect. Property, control, effect. Do that six times and you've written a full-mark extended response.

NARRATOR: One more high-value trap, beyond the two distinctions. Don't confuse authorisation with authentication's *result*. A logged-in user is authenticated, but that doesn't mean they're authorised for everything — "logged in" is not "allowed". We'll see this fail for real in chapter eighteen as broken access control, where a user changes a number in a U-R-L and reaches a record they were authenticated to the site for but never authorised to see. Authentication without proper authorisation is one of the most common real-world breaches, so keep the two jobs separate.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — five questions, recall up to extended. Pause and attempt each one properly before the model answer; this is your retrieval practice over the whole episode.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. What do the letters C-I-A-A-A stand for, and which group is "what we protect" versus "how we protect it"? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: C-I-A is the triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — defining what we protect. A-A-A is Authentication, Authorisation, Accountability, defining how we protect it. Confidentiality is preventing unauthorised access to data; integrity is preventing unauthorised modification; availability is keeping systems accessible to authorised users; authentication verifies identity; authorisation controls permitted actions; accountability logs actions so they're traceable. Naming all six correctly, and splitting them into the protect-versus-how groups, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, the classic. Distinguish between authentication and authorisation, with an example of each. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: authentication is the process of verifying that a user is who they claim to be — for example, logging in with a username, password, and a one-time code. Authorisation is the process of determining what an authenticated user is permitted to do — for example, allowing a student to view their own grades but not to change them or view other students' grades. Authentication establishes identity and happens first; authorisation establishes permissions and happens second. Stating both definitions, the who-versus-what contrast, and the order is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Distinguish between confidentiality and integrity. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: confidentiality ensures data is accessible only to authorised parties — it protects against unauthorised reading or disclosure. Integrity ensures data remains accurate and unmodified by unauthorised parties — it protects against unauthorised changing or tampering. They are independent: data can be confidential but altered, or intact but exposed. Confidentiality is about secrecy; integrity is about trustworthiness. Naming the read-versus-change distinction is the mark.

QUESTION: Question four, application. For the availability element of the C-I-A triad, describe one threat and one control. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: a threat to availability is a denial-of-service attack, in which an attacker floods a server with bogus requests so that it can no longer respond to legitimate users. A control is rate limiting — capping how many requests a client may make in a time window — combined with redundancy and failover so that traffic can be absorbed or shifted and the service stays reachable. Naming a real availability threat and a control that directly counters it, with the effect, is the mark. The same shape works for confidentiality — threat: eavesdropping; control: encryption in transit — and integrity — threat: tampering; control: hashing or digital signatures.

QUESTION: Question five, the extended one. An online banking system must protect customer accounts. Explain how authentication, authorisation, and accountability work together to secure a money transfer. Pause, plan a structured response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the three operate as a sequence. First, authentication verifies the customer's identity before any access is granted — for example, a password plus a one-time code sent to their phone, which is multi-factor authentication combining something they know and something they have. Second, once authenticated, authorisation determines what that customer may do — role and ownership checks ensure they can only move money from their own accounts, applying least privilege so they cannot access anyone else's. Third, accountability records the action — every transfer is written to an audit log with the user, amount, timestamp, and I-P address, providing non-repudiation so the customer cannot later deny the transaction and investigators can trace any dispute. Together they answer who you are, what you are allowed to do, and what you did: authentication and authorisation prevent unauthorised transfers, and accountability provides the evidence trail if something goes wrong. A response that names all three, puts them in order, ties each to a concrete banking control, and states that they cover identity, permission, and traceability is a full-mark answer.
