NARRATOR: Recap first. Last episode, implementation methods — the four cut-over strategies, DiP-PP: Direct, Phased, Parallel, Pilot. Direct is the big bang, cheap and fast but high-risk with no fallback. Phased rolls out feature by feature to everyone, medium-risk. Parallel runs both systems at once for safety and verification, low-risk but expensive. Pilot tries the whole system on a small representative group first, low-risk but delays full rollout. The exam pairing: Direct is cheap and risky, Parallel is safe and costly; the skill was matching the method's risk profile to the scenario's stakes; and the cautionary tales were Knight Capital and Denver airport — both chose direct cut-over for systems that could least afford it.

NARRATOR: And the two before: ideation and modelling — ideation was B-M-S, and the data dictionary plus algorithm design turned ideas into a buildable design; and requirements and feasibility — FUN versus PERF split functional from non-functional, everything had to be measurable, and feasibility was TECO. Now we shift from the individual stages to the big picture: the development approach — the overall shape of how the whole project runs from start to finish. Three approaches in this chapter; today is the first and oldest, Waterfall. Today's outcome is S-E-twelve-oh-six.

NARRATOR: A development approach is the overall model for how a project moves through its lifecycle. Waterfall is the classic, linear one: complete one phase fully, then move to the next, like water flowing down a series of steps — and water doesn't flow back up. Each phase is finished and signed off before the next begins, with a formal review at each gate. The memory hook is a marquee one for this module; reuse it unchanged: "Waterfall Falls once." You go down the stages in order, and going back up is expensive and disruptive. Hold that against next episode's "Agile Goes around," and you've got the central contrast of this chapter.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll name the waterfall phases in order, explain its advantages and disadvantages, and — the application skill — identify a project that suits waterfall and one that doesn't, and justify why. The classic questions are "outline the stages of the waterfall model" and "explain one advantage and one disadvantage of waterfall" for around four marks, often extended into "is waterfall suitable for this scenario?"

NARRATOR: The phases first, because "outline the stages" is a guaranteed exam target. Waterfall flows through these in strict order: Requirements analysis, then Design, then Implementation — the coding — then Testing, then Deployment, then Maintenance. Six stages. You already know these: this is the software development lifecycle, the S-D-L-C, the same one you learned in Year 11 and met again in Secure Software Architecture fourteen-oh-two. There the eight-step version had the hook "Really Smart Developers Don't Ignore Testing In Maintenance" — Requirements, Specifications, Design, Development, Integration, Testing, Installation, Maintenance. Waterfall is just the S-D-L-C run strictly linearly, one phase fully done before the next. So you don't need a new mnemonic for the stages — you need the one you already have, plus the new idea that in waterfall they're sequential stage-gates with no going back.

NARRATOR: Requirements analysis: gather, document, and validate all functional and non-functional requirements up front — that's episode one of this module, FUN versus PERF, done exhaustively before anything else. The output is a Requirements Specification Document, signed off by stakeholders. Design: turn those requirements into the technical blueprint — system architecture, database design, interface design. The output is a System Architecture Document and the database specifications. Implementation: write the code, following the design with minimal deviation, with unit testing as you go. Testing: verify the whole system against the requirements — integration testing, user acceptance testing, performance and security testing. Deployment: install it in production, migrate data, train users. Maintenance: ongoing bug fixes, monitoring, and enhancements — the longest phase of the software's life. Six phases, each fully completed and reviewed before the next. The phase list and key documents are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: The defining feature — what makes waterfall waterfall — is documentation and formal sign-off at every gate. Each phase produces comprehensive documents — the Requirements Specification, the System Architecture Document, the test plans — and each gate requires formal approval before you proceed. That heavy, deliberate documentation ties straight to this module's marker, S-E-twelve-oh-nine, manage and document the project. Waterfall is the most document-heavy approach, which is both its great strength and part of its cost.

QUESTION: Quick check before advantages and disadvantages. Name the six waterfall phases in order. Pause, list them, then play.

NARRATOR: Requirements analysis, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance. Down the waterfall, one to six, no going back up cheaply. If you can produce those six in order, you've got the "outline the stages" mark.

NARRATOR: Advantages now — and the exam wants balance, because the big trap with waterfall is treating it as simply outdated and bad. It is not. Advantage one: clear structure and predictability. Because phases and deliverables are defined up front, you get predictable timelines, clear milestones, and clear roles — managers can plan resources and budgets with confidence. Advantage two: comprehensive documentation. The detailed requirements and design documents reduce ambiguity, serve as a knowledge base for maintenance, and provide an audit trail — exactly what regulated industries need. Advantage three: quality through formal reviews. The phase-gate reviews catch problems before you move on, and upfront sign-off reduces scope creep, because changing a signed-off requirement requires a formal change-control process. So waterfall's strengths are predictability, documentation, and control.

NARRATOR: Disadvantages — the real ones, the reason agile exists. Disadvantage one, the big one: limited flexibility for change. Because water falls once, a requirements change discovered late — in testing, say — is expensive and disruptive, since you'd have to flow back up through design and implementation. Waterfall assumes you got the requirements right at the start, which is often wrong. Disadvantage two: late feedback and late testing. The client typically doesn't see working software until near the end, and testing happens after everything's built — so a fundamental misunderstanding can sit undiscovered for months and surface only when it's hugely expensive to fix. Disadvantage three: all-or-nothing delivery. Value arrives only at the end, so a project cancelled at eighty percent has produced no usable software, and long cycles risk building something the market no longer wants. One-line summary: waterfall is rigid, and its feedback comes too late.

NARRATOR: Now the story — the most instructive waterfall failure of the modern era, a case study this module cashes in across several episodes. Healthcare-dot-gov — the U.S. federal health-insurance marketplace website, launched October two thousand thirteen. It was built essentially as waterfall against a hard, politically fixed launch deadline. Requirements were rushed and kept changing, but the linear process and the fixed date meant contractors built to a frozen, flawed spec; integration of the many sub-systems was left until the end, as waterfall does; and there was no real load testing before launch — they tested late, and lightly. On launch day, the site was meant to handle huge volumes and collapsed almost immediately — only a tiny fraction of users could even create an account. It took months and an emergency "tech surge" to fix. The lesson, in this episode's exact terms: waterfall's late-integration and late-testing weaknesses, plus a fixed deadline that left no slack and a requirements process that couldn't iterate, produced a launch meltdown. We'll return to Healthcare-dot-gov when we do testing — twenty-six-oh-one, the no-load-testing failure — and evaluation, twenty-six-oh-three, the post-mortem. For now: the cautionary tale of waterfall plus a fixed deadline plus weak testing.

NARRATOR: But — and this is the balance that earns marks — waterfall is the right choice for some projects, and saying so is what separates a band-six answer from "waterfall is bad." Waterfall suits projects with stable, well-understood requirements that won't change much; projects with regulatory or compliance requirements demanding comprehensive documentation and an audit trail; projects using mature, predictable technology; and risk-averse environments that value predictability over speed. The textbook's strong-fit example is a government compliance system — requirements stable and dictated by regulation, documentation mandatory, predictability over rapid iteration. The poor-fit example is a startup mobile app — requirements unstable, market moving, deadline pressure, and you need early user feedback. That contrast is the application question.

NARRATOR: Lock in today. Waterfall is the linear, sequential development approach: Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance — the S-D-L-C run as strict stage-gates with heavy documentation and formal sign-off. "Waterfall Falls once" — down the stages in order, and going back up is expensive. Strengths: predictability, comprehensive documentation, control through phase-gate reviews. Weaknesses: rigidity to change, late feedback and late testing, all-or-nothing delivery. It suits stable, well-understood, regulated projects; it fails on uncertain, fast-moving ones — Healthcare-dot-gov plus a fixed deadline plus weak testing equals meltdown. The trap: "waterfall is always bad" — it's right for the regulated and the stable.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions, recall to extended. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Outline the stages of the waterfall model. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the waterfall model progresses through sequential phases, each completed before the next begins. Requirements analysis gathers and documents all functional and non-functional requirements. Design transforms those requirements into the system architecture, database design, and interface design. Implementation writes the code according to the design. Testing verifies the complete system against the requirements through integration, system, and user acceptance testing. Deployment installs the system in production and migrates data and trains users. Maintenance provides ongoing support, bug fixes, and enhancements. Each phase ends in a formal review and sign-off before progressing — the water falls once and does not flow back. Naming the six phases in order, with a one-line purpose each, and noting the sequential stage-gate nature is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, the four-marker. Explain one advantage and one disadvantage of the waterfall approach. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: one advantage is comprehensive documentation and predictability — because requirements and design are fully documented up front and each phase passes a formal review, the project has predictable timelines, clear milestones, and an audit trail, which is especially valuable for regulated or compliance-driven projects. One disadvantage is limited flexibility for change — because each phase is completed and signed off before the next, and the model does not flow back up cheaply, a requirements change discovered late, such as during testing, is expensive and disruptive to incorporate, and the client sees working software only near the end. Stating each point with its specific feature and consequence is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three, application. Describe one type of project for which waterfall is well-suited and one for which it is poorly-suited, justifying each. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: waterfall is well-suited to a regulated system with stable requirements — for example, government compliance software or medical-device control software — because the requirements are well-understood and unlikely to change, comprehensive auditable documentation is mandatory, and predictability is valued over speed; these match waterfall's strengths exactly. Waterfall is poorly-suited to an innovative, user-facing product with evolving requirements — for example, a startup's new mobile app — because the requirements are uncertain and will change with user feedback, the market moves quickly, and early and frequent feedback is essential; waterfall's rigidity and late feedback would make change expensive and risk building the wrong thing. Justifying each by the stability-and-regulation versus uncertainty-and-feedback distinction earns full marks.

QUESTION: Question four, extended. Healthcare-dot-gov was developed largely with a waterfall approach under a fixed launch deadline and failed badly at launch. Explain how the characteristics of waterfall contributed to this outcome. Pause, plan across the episode, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: several waterfall characteristics combined with the fixed deadline to produce the failure. First, waterfall's reliance on getting requirements right up front clashed with a requirements set that was rushed and changing — but the linear process and frozen deadline meant the system was built to a flawed specification with no cheap way to iterate. Second, waterfall defers integration of sub-systems until late in the lifecycle, so the many components were stitched together near the end, where integration problems are hardest and most expensive to fix. Third, waterfall tests late, after the system is built, and in this case load testing was inadequate before launch — so the system's inability to handle real traffic wasn't discovered until launch day, when it collapsed under demand. The fixed deadline removed the slack that might have allowed re-work, and waterfall's all-or-nothing delivery meant the failure was total and public rather than caught in an earlier increment. The lesson: waterfall's late feedback, late integration, and late testing make it dangerous for a high-traffic, evolving, deadline-driven project — an agile or hybrid approach with earlier, incremental testing and feedback would have surfaced these problems sooner. An answer that ties the specific waterfall characteristics — frozen requirements, late integration, late testing, all-or-nothing delivery — to the specific failures, and weighs the role of the fixed deadline, earns full marks.
