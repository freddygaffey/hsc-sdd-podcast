NARRATOR: It is just after seven in the morning on the east coast of the United States, the twenty-first of October, two thousand sixteen, and millions of people are doing the first thing they do every day. They reach for the phone on the nightstand, still half-asleep, and they open something. Twitter. The weather. A news site. And one by one, the apps spin. And spin. And nothing comes. The little loading wheel turns, and turns, and then gives up. Try again. Same thing. Twitter will not load. Reddit will not load. Spotify plays the song it had already cached and then falls silent. Netflix shows a spinner where the show should be. People do what people always do — they assume it is their own connection. They toggle wi-fi off and on. They restart the router. None of it helps. Because the problem is not in their house. It's not even at Twitter, or Netflix, or Spotify. Every one of those companies is sitting there, healthy, servers humming, ready to answer. Their machines are fine. The websites are fine.

NARRATOR: The problem is that, for millions of people, the internet has forgotten how to find them.

NARRATOR: This is the story of the day a single obscure company most people had never heard of was buried alive under a flood of traffic — and took a huge slice of the internet down with it. Twitter, Reddit, Netflix, Spotify, GitHub, PayPal, Amazon, The New York Times — dozens of the biggest names on the web, dark or crippled for hours, across much of the United States, in three brutal waves over a single day. And it is the story of the weapon that did it — an army of hundreds of thousands of soldiers, except the soldiers were baby monitors, and security cameras, and home routers, and digital video recorders. Ordinary little gadgets, sitting in ordinary homes, that their owners had bought, plugged in, and completely forgotten. And it is the story of the young men who built that army — not spies, not a foreign government, but college kids — and what they were actually trying to do, which was something so much smaller and stranger than crashing the internet that you almost won't believe it. So let me show you how a handful of kids and a pile of cheap webcams made the internet forget its own name.

QUESTION: When you type a website's name into your browser, what actually happens in the half-second before the page loads?

NARRATOR: That question is the whole key to this story, because the answer is a piece of plumbing that runs underneath everything you do online, invisibly, billions of times a second — and almost nobody ever thinks about it. When you type a name into your browser — the name of a website, in plain human words — that name means absolutely nothing to the machinery of the internet. Computers don't talk to each other using names. They talk using numbers. Numerical addresses, called I-P addresses, like a long phone number for every machine on the network. So before your browser can fetch a single thing, it has to take the human-friendly name you typed and look up the actual numerical address of the computer that name belongs to. And that translation service has a name. It is the Domain Name System. Everyone just calls it D-N-S. And the simplest way to picture it is this: D-N-S is the phone book of the internet.

NARRATOR: Think about what a phone book does. You know a person's name, but to call them, you need their number. So you look the name up, the book gives you the number, and now you can make the call. D-N-S does exactly that, automatically, every time you go anywhere online. Your device quietly asks D-N-S, "what's the number for this name?" D-N-S answers with the I-P address. And only then — only once it has the number — can your device reach out and connect. It happens in a fraction of a second, and you never see it. But here is the thing to hold onto: if that lookup fails — if the phone book is unavailable, if nobody answers when your device asks for the number — then it does not matter one bit that the website itself is perfectly healthy. You cannot reach a machine whose address you cannot find. The site is up. You just can't get its number. So you can't make the call.

NARRATOR: Now, here is the second piece, and it is the one that turns one company's bad day into the whole internet's bad day. Most companies do not run their own phone book. Running D-N-S well — reliably, globally, fast, defended against attack — is a specialist job, so the big sites pay a specialist to do it for them. And one of the largest of those specialists, handling D-N-S for an enormous slice of the most popular sites on the internet, was a firm called Dyn. Spelled D-Y-N, pronounced like the start of "dynamic" — Dynamic Network Services, based, of all the unglamorous places, in Manchester, New Hampshire. When you tried to reach Twitter that morning, your phone was, behind the scenes, asking Dyn for Twitter's address. Reddit, GitHub, Netflix, Spotify — Dyn, Dyn, Dyn, Dyn. An astonishing number of household-name services had all, quite reasonably, outsourced the same critical job to the same single company.

NARRATOR: So sit with the shape of that, because it is the whole vulnerability in one sentence. One company was the phone book for a huge fraction of the web's most-used services. Which means you don't have to attack Twitter to take down Twitter. You don't have to find a flaw in Netflix to make Netflix go dark. You just knock out the phone book. Take down Dyn, and you don't take down one website — you make hundreds of unrelated websites unreachable at once, all of them working perfectly the entire time, all helpless, because the world has suddenly lost the ability to look up their numbers. It is a single point of failure hiding in plain sight. And on the twenty-first of October, two thousand sixteen, somebody found it, and hit it.

QUESTION: So how do you actually take down a company like that? You can't exactly walk in and unplug it.

NARRATOR: No. You drown it. The attack on Dyn was what's called a distributed denial-of-service attack — D-DoS for short — and it is worth pulling that name apart, because every word in it is telling you exactly what's happening. Start at the end. Denial of service. The goal is not to steal anything, not to read anything, not to break in and look around. It is simply to deny people the service — to make it unavailable. Now the front. Distributed. A plain denial-of-service attack comes from one machine, which is easy to deal with — you see all that junk pouring in from one address, you block that address, you're done. Distributed means the attack does not come from one place you can block. It comes from a vast, scattered army of machines, all over the world, all hammering the target at the same instant, from tens of thousands of directions at once.

NARRATOR: So picture Dyn's servers as a shop with a door, and through that door come the legitimate customers — your phone, my phone, millions of devices, each politely asking "what's the number for Twitter, please?" On a normal day they handle that flood beautifully. Now imagine that suddenly, instead of millions of real customers, there are tens of millions of fake ones, all shoving through the door at once, all shouting nonsense — so many, so fast, that the real customers can't get near the entrance. That's a D-DoS flood. The phone book is still standing. It's just that ten million fake callers are jamming every line, and you, the one real caller, can never get through. Dyn itself later said it was seeing malicious requests from what looked like tens of millions of separate addresses. Media reports put the peak of the flood at around one terabit of data per second — among the largest the internet had ever witnessed. Dyn's own chief strategist, Kyle York, would call it "very complex and unlike everyday D-DoS attacks." This was not someone's idle prank. This was a tidal wave.

QUESTION: An attack that big needs an absolutely enormous number of machines, all pointed at one target at the same moment. So where on earth do you get an army like that?

NARRATOR: And this is the part of the story that should make you walk around your own home and look at your own devices differently. Because the attackers did not buy that army. They did not rent a thousand servers or break into a thousand data centres. The army was already out there, sitting in people's living rooms, waiting. It was made of webcams. Baby monitors. Home wi-fi routers. Digital video recorders — those boxes under the television recording the feeds from security cameras. The Internet of Things: the great sprawling mass of cheap, internet-connected gadgets people buy, plug in, watch work, and then never think about again. Tens of thousands — eventually well over a hundred thousand — of these little devices, scattered across homes and small businesses in dozens of countries, had been silently hijacked. Taken over without their owners noticing a thing. Welded into a single remote-controlled weapon. And on that October morning, an unseen hand pointed all of them at Dyn at once.

NARRATOR: A network of hijacked machines like that has a name. It's called a botnet — a robot network, an army of infected computers under the secret command of one operator, doing its bidding while their actual owners have no idea anything is wrong. And here's the unsettling thing: nothing breaks on the hijacked device. Your security camera keeps showing you your front porch. Your router keeps serving you Netflix — right up until Netflix goes dark for a completely different reason. The little machine works exactly as it always did, while in the background it fires a relentless stream of attack traffic at a target halfway around the world. The camera watching your driveway could have been part of the assault that took down half the American internet that day, and you would never have known. You'd have looked at the blinking light and thought: working fine.

NARRATOR: The software that built this army out of other people's gadgets had a name worth knowing, because it became one of the most infamous pieces of malware of the decade. It was called Mirai. M-I-R-A-I. It's the Japanese word for "future" — and according to chat logs investigators later recovered, it was named after a Japanese anime series, Mirai Nikki, "Future Diary." It first appeared in the wild around August of two thousand sixteen, just a couple of months before the Dyn attack. And the thing that makes Mirai so important, and so frightening, is how stupendously, almost embarrassingly, simple it was.

QUESTION: How do you hijack a hundred thousand devices? Some brilliant exploit, some unbreakable piece of code?

NARRATOR: No. Nothing of the kind. Mirai didn't break any encryption. It didn't exploit some deep, exotic flaw. It did something so basic it's almost insulting. It guessed passwords. That's it. That's the whole trick. Here is the problem it preyed on. When a manufacturer makes a cheap internet-connected camera, or a budget router, or a D-V-R, that device needs a way for you to log into it and set it up. So it comes from the factory with a username and password already set — a default. Something like "admin" and "admin." Or "root" and "password." And here's the killer detail: it's very often the same default on every single unit the factory ships. Every camera of that model, in every home, with the identical credentials baked in. The manual, somewhere on page forty, politely suggests you change it. And almost nobody does. Why would they? It's a camera. You plug it in, the picture appears on your phone, it works, you're done. You never log into it again. The default password sits there, unchanged, for the entire life of the device.

NARRATOR: So Mirai went hunting. It roamed the internet — scanning constantly, automatically, on the network ports these devices use to accept logins, the Telnet ports, numbers twenty-three and twenty-three-twenty-three — firing probes at random addresses, asking, in effect, "is anybody home?" And whenever something answered — a camera, a router, a D-V-R — Mirai would try to log in, working through a short, hardcoded list of the most common factory-default username and password combinations. Just over sixty of them. "admin, admin." "root, root." "root, twelve-three-four-five-six." "support, support." A little list of the laziest credentials in the world. And on device after device after device, the owner had never changed the default — so one of those sixty guesses simply worked. The door swung open. Mirai walked straight in, installed itself, and — this is the worm-like part — immediately turned that freshly infected gadget into a new scanner, sending it out to find and infect the next victim. One becomes ten. Ten become a hundred. A hundred become a hundred thousand. It spread like a contagion, every infected device recruiting the next, until the botnet was vast enough to point at a target and simply bury it.

NARRATOR: And Mirai had a nasty refinement that shows its makers knew exactly what they were doing. Once it had taken over a device, it would look around for any rival malware already living there, kill it, and lock the doors behind itself — closing the very ports it had come in through — so that no other attacker could hijack the gadget it had just claimed. It was territorial. It cleaned house and barricaded the place, keeping the device for itself.

NARRATOR: Now, to really understand the Dyn attack, you have to back up about a month, because Dyn was not the first time the world saw Mirai's teeth. It was the third. And the earlier two are how we eventually learned who built this thing. In mid-September of two thousand sixteen, the website of Brian Krebs — a well-known security journalist who spends his career exposing cybercriminals — came under one of the largest D-DoS attacks ever recorded to that point: a flood of around six hundred and twenty gigabits per second, so enormous that the company protecting his site for free could no longer afford to absorb it, and Krebs was knocked offline for days. Then, almost immediately after, a French web-hosting company called O-V-H got hit even harder — a flood reported to approach a full terabit per second. And the target inside O-V-H, the thing the attackers were actually aiming at, was a server hosting the video game Minecraft. Hold onto that detail. Minecraft. We'll come back to it, because it is the secret engine underneath this entire story.

NARRATOR: And then, on the thirtieth of September, two thousand sixteen, came the move that turned a private weapon into a public catastrophe. A user calling themselves Anna-senpai — "senpai," the Japanese honorific, another anime nod — went onto a notorious online forum called Hack Forums and posted, for anyone in the world to download, the complete source code of Mirai. The entire weapon. The scanner, the password list, the command-and-control machinery, all of it, free, open, public. Now, criminals do this for a cynical reason: when investigators start sniffing too close, you dump your source code into the wild, so that suddenly thousands of people possess it — and you are no longer the only person who could have written the thing the police are hunting for. You hide in the crowd you just created. But the consequence was that Mirai stopped being one person's botnet. Within days, copies and variants were sprouting everywhere, run by anyone who could download a file and follow instructions. The weapon had been handed to the entire world.

NARRATOR: And three weeks later, somebody who had downloaded that public code pointed their own home-grown Mirai botnet at Dyn.

NARRATOR: The attack came in waves, and that, too, tells you something. The first wave hit around eleven in the morning, U-T-C — roughly seven a.m. on the U.S. east coast — and for about two hours, the eastern United States lost great chunks of the internet. Dyn's engineers scrambled, beat the flood back, and brought the phone book online. People exhaled. And then, in the early afternoon, a second wave crashed in, bigger and broader, reaching further across the country and out toward Europe. They fought that one off too. And then, in the evening, a third. Three separate assaults across a single day, each a fresh tidal wave of junk traffic from that hijacked army of cameras and routers. For Dyn's engineers it must have felt like bailing out a boat while someone kept punching new holes in the hull. By late evening, after the third wave was finally beaten down, it was over. But the damage was done, and the lesson had landed with a thud heard around the world: the internet had a soft underbelly, and it was made of the cheap, forgotten, badly secured gadgets sitting in all of our homes.

QUESTION: So who did it? A foreign government? Some shadowy hacker collective with a grand plan to break the internet?

NARRATOR: This is where the story takes a turn I find genuinely fascinating, because the truth is so much smaller, and so much more human, than the scale of the damage. In the immediate aftermath, the usual suspects came forward to claim the glory. A group calling itself New World Hackers said they'd done it. Accounts linked to the Anonymous collective claimed it was revenge over the treatment of the WikiLeaks founder, Julian Assange. None offered real evidence, and researchers were deeply skeptical. The threat-intelligence firm Flashpoint, which had been tracking Mirai closely, looked at the technical fingerprints of the attack and came to a conclusion that almost sounded like an anticlimax. This did not have the hallmarks of a nation-state or a sophisticated criminal syndicate. This looked, in their assessment, like the work of "script kiddies" — their words — relatively unskilled people using a powerful tool somebody else had built and handed out for free. President Obama himself, asked about it days later, said plainly that investigators still did not know who was behind it. The most powerful country on earth had just watched its internet stagger, and it had no idea who'd thrown the punch.

NARRATOR: And the eventual answer, which took years to nail down, was exactly that. The Dyn attack was not launched by the people who created Mirai. It was launched by someone who had simply downloaded the leaked code. In December of two thousand twenty — more than four years later — a young man pleaded guilty to taking part in the Dyn attack. His name was never released, because he had been a juvenile when he did it. A teenager, using a weapon he found lying on the internet, had switched off Twitter, Netflix, and Reddit for much of a continent. That is the terrifying democratisation at the heart of this whole story. The hard part — building the weapon — had already been done by somebody else, and then given away. All that was left was to aim it.

QUESTION: So who did build it? And here's the question that always gets me — why? What were they actually trying to do?

NARRATOR: This is my favourite part of the entire saga, because the answer is so completely disproportionate to the consequences. The people who created Mirai were three young men in their late teens and early twenties, in America. Paras Jha, from Fanwood, New Jersey. Josiah White, from Washington, Pennsylvania. And Dalton Norman, from Metairie, Louisiana. College kids. And they did not build the most powerful botnet the world had ever seen to attack the internet, or to make a political point, or to serve a foreign power. They built it, fundamentally, to win at Minecraft.

NARRATOR: It sounds like a joke, and it is completely true. Minecraft, the block-building video game, is played on servers — individual machines that host worlds for players to log into. And popular Minecraft servers make real money: donations, perks, a busy one pulling in tens of thousands of dollars a month. Which means there is a fierce, cutthroat economy around them. And one of the dirtiest tactics in it is to D-DoS a rival server — flood it with junk so it lags and crashes, so the frustrated players abandon it and migrate to yours. Where this gets almost beautifully cynical is that Paras Jha and Josiah White also ran a company. ProTraf Solutions. And what did ProTraf sell? D-DoS protection. Defence against exactly the kind of attack they were running. They would attack Minecraft servers with Mirai, then turn around and sell those same owners protection from the attacks. They were the arsonists running the fire brigade. They built the most fearsome botnet on the planet as a tool of extortion in a turf war over Minecraft hosting.

NARRATOR: That's what Mirai was for. The hijacked cameras, the hundred thousand enslaved devices, the record-shattering floods — the original purpose of all of it was to muscle in on a video-game side hustle. Jha, the one who'd posted the source code as Anna-senpai, was a computer science student at Rutgers University in New Jersey, and in a detail that's almost too perfect, he had repeatedly attacked his own university's network with D-DoS floods — knocking out the systems students used to register for classes — apparently, in part, to make his own mitigation services look necessary. He attacked the institution he was attending, to drum up business for the company defending against attacks.

NARRATOR: They were caught. The investigation, which Brian Krebs himself contributed to from the very site Mirai had knocked offline, eventually identified all three. In December of two thousand seventeen, Paras Jha, Josiah White, and Dalton Norman pleaded guilty to federal charges under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. And here's the coda that says a lot about how the modern world handles its most talented young offenders. When they were sentenced, in Alaska, in September of two thousand eighteen, they did not go to prison. They got five years of probation each, two thousand five hundred hours of community service, and were ordered to pay around a hundred and twenty-seven thousand dollars in restitution. The leniency came because, between the plea and the sentencing, the three had gone to work for the very people who'd caught them — providing what the court called "extraordinary cooperation," spending hundreds of hours helping the F-B-I track down other cybercriminals. The kids who built the weapon became consultants for the side defending against it.

NARRATOR: So that's the whole arc of it. A weapon built by college students for a video-game feud. Released into the wild to muddy a criminal investigation. Picked up by a teenager who pointed it at the phone book of the internet. And for a single October day in two thousand sixteen, much of America couldn't reach the websites it lived on — not because those websites broke, but because the world briefly lost the ability to look up their names. Now let me pull out what this story actually teaches, because underneath the chaos there is a tidy set of lessons you should carry into every system you ever build.

NARRATOR: Start with the thing that was actually attacked, because it's the part people most often get wrong. This attack stole nothing. It read no one's data. It broke no encryption, cracked no password belonging to Twitter or Netflix, leaked no secrets. By the usual picture of a "cyberattack," nothing was even taken. And yet it was devastating. So what did it destroy? It destroyed availability — the property that a system is actually there, reachable, and working when the people who need it come knocking. Security people talk about three great pillars holding up everything we do with information. Confidentiality, keeping data secret from those who shouldn't see it. Integrity, keeping data correct and untampered with. And availability, making sure the data and the service are reachable when they're genuinely needed. We spend most of our breath on the first two. The Dyn attack is the great, screaming argument for the third. A service can be perfectly confidential, with its secrets locked down tight, and perfectly intact, with not a byte altered, and still be completely, uselessly dead — because nobody can get to it. A denial-of-service attack ignores the first two pillars entirely and takes a wrecking ball to the third.

NARRATOR: Then look at how the army was built, because this is the lesson that should actually change your own behaviour, today, at home. Every single one of those hundred thousand hijacked devices was taken over for one reason: insecure defaults and weak authentication. The manufacturers shipped their products with a known, identical, factory-set password — the same one on every unit — and either never forced the owner to change it, or made changing it so obscure that almost nobody bothered. That is a design failure, and a profound one. The deep principle here is about authentication — the act of proving you're allowed in — and the brutal truth is that authentication is worthless if the credential is public knowledge. A password printed in a manual, identical across a million devices, sitting on a page anyone can find online, is not a password at all. It's a "welcome" mat. The fix is a principle worth tattooing somewhere: the secure choice has to be the default choice and the easy choice. A device should refuse to even start working until you've set a real, unique password. Security that depends on the user reading page forty of the manual is security that does not exist.

QUESTION: Before I give you the next one — here's the sharpest, most uncomfortable lesson in the whole story, and I want you to actually stop and work it out. The people whose devices were hijacked lost nothing. Their cameras kept working. So who paid the price for their carelessness? Pause the player, think it through, then come back.

NARRATOR: Did you get it? The answer is: everybody else. This is the twist about responsibility that makes the Dyn attack so morally interesting. The person who bought the cheap webcam and never changed its password suffered no consequence whatsoever. Their camera kept showing them their porch. From their point of view, nothing was ever wrong. The people who paid the price were Twitter, and Netflix, and Dyn, and the millions of strangers who couldn't reach the services they relied on — none of whom had done anything careless at all. So here is the principle, one the industry took years to truly absorb: an insecure device doesn't just endanger its owner. It becomes ammunition aimed at everyone else. Your unguarded gadget can be quietly conscripted into a weapon used to harm total strangers who never had any say in how secure your camera was. Security, this attack proved, is not a purely private matter.

NARRATOR: And then there is the structural lesson, the one most directly aimed at you as a future builder of systems. Too many eggs in one basket. The reason a flood against one company could darken hundreds of unrelated websites is that all of those websites had quietly outsourced the same critical function — D-N-S — to the same single provider. Dyn was a single point of failure: one component, sitting in the middle of everything, that, if it went down, took an enormous amount with it. None of those companies had done anything obviously foolish. Outsourcing D-N-S to a competent specialist is sensible. The failure was quieter — it was that they had all made the same sensible decision, and in doing so had bet their availability on a single shared link, a link that, when it snapped, snapped for all of them at once.

NARRATOR: The fix is the opposite of concentration. It's redundancy. Spread the dependency. Use more than one D-N-S provider, so that if one is drowning, your traffic quietly fails over to the other and your users never even notice. And that is precisely what many of the big sites caught in the Dyn attack did afterward — they added a second, independent D-N-S provider, so that no single flood could take them all down again. So whenever you design a system, get into the habit of asking a single, ruthless question: what is the one thing that, if it dies, takes everything with it? Find that single point of failure before an attacker does. Because availability isn't only about being strong enough to survive a direct hit. Just as often, it's about not having quietly staked your entire service on one fragile thing you forgot you were depending on.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A phone book for the internet, run by one company most people had never heard of. An army of a hundred thousand hijacked cameras and routers and baby monitors, taken over not by genius but by guessing sixty default passwords nobody had bothered to change. A weapon called Mirai, built by college kids to win a turf war over Minecraft servers, dumped onto a public forum to throw investigators off the scent — and picked up by a teenager who aimed it at that phone book and pulled the trigger three times in one day. And every one of those darkened websites sat there the whole time, perfectly healthy, perfectly intact, and completely, uselessly out of reach.

NARRATOR: The websites didn't fail. The phone book did. And that's the thing I want you to carry out of this. We spend so much energy locking the data away and keeping it correct, and we forget the simplest, most fragile promise a system makes: that when you come looking for it, it will actually be there. Build your systems so that no single thing, falling over, can take all the rest down with it. Set a real password on every device you own, because the cheap forgotten gadget in the corner is exactly the kind of thing that gets turned into someone else's weapon. And the next time a page won't load and you're sure it must be your own connection — remember the day the whole internet forgot its own name.
