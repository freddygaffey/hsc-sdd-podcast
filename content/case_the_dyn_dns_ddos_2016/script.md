NARRATOR: On the morning of the twenty-first of October, two thousand sixteen, people on the east coast of the United States woke up, reached for their phones, and found that large parts of the internet had simply vanished. Twitter wouldn't load. Netflix wouldn't play. Reddit, Spotify, Airbnb, PayPal, GitHub, The New York Times — gone, or crawling. These sites hadn't crashed. The companies' own servers were running perfectly fine. The problem was stranger, and more frightening: people's computers could no longer find them. The internet had, for millions of people, forgotten how to look up its own addresses.

NARRATOR: To see how, you need one piece of plumbing almost nobody thinks about, because it works invisibly all the time. When you type a website's name into your browser, that name means nothing to the machinery of the internet. Computers don't talk to names; they talk to numbers — numerical addresses. So before your browser can fetch anything, it has to translate the human-friendly name into the machine address behind it. That translation service is the Domain Name System, or D-N-S. It is, quite literally, the phone book of the internet. Every time you visit a site, your device quietly asks D-N-S, "what's the number for this name?" — and only when it gets an answer can it connect.

NARRATOR: Most companies don't run that phone book themselves. They pay a specialist. And one of the biggest specialists, handling D-N-S for an enormous slice of the most popular sites on the internet, was a company called Dyn. When you tried to reach Twitter, your device was, behind the scenes, asking Dyn for Twitter's address. So here's the setup for the disaster: one company was the phone book for a huge fraction of the web's most-used services. Knock out Dyn, and you don't take down one website — you make hundreds unreachable at once, even though every one of them is working perfectly. People can't look up the number, so they can't make the call.

NARRATOR: And that is exactly what someone did. They drowned Dyn in traffic — a distributed denial-of-service attack. Unpack the name, because it tells you precisely what's happening. Denial of service: the goal is to deny legitimate users the service, to make it unavailable. Distributed: the attack doesn't come from one machine you could just block, it comes from a vast, scattered army of machines all at once. They flooded Dyn's servers with a tidal wave of bogus requests — junk traffic — so overwhelming that the real requests, yours trying to reach Twitter, couldn't get through. The phone book was buried under so many fake callers that nobody real could get a line. At its peak, the flood hitting Dyn measured over a terabit of data every second — one of the largest such attacks the world had ever seen.

QUESTION: An attack like that needs an enormous army of machines, all pointed at one target at the same time. So where did the attackers get an army that big? Did they buy thousands of computers? Hack into a thousand data centres?

NARRATOR: No. And this is the part that should make you look differently at the gadgets in your home. The army was made of ordinary devices. Webcams. Baby monitors. Home routers. Digital video recorders — the boxes hooked up to security cameras. The internet-connected odds and ends people buy, plug in, and forget about. Tens, eventually hundreds of thousands of these little devices, scattered across homes and offices worldwide, had been silently hijacked and turned into soldiers in a botnet — a network of infected machines, controlled remotely, that their owners had no idea was being used as a weapon. Your security camera, the one watching your front porch, could have been part of the attack that took down half the internet, and you'd never have known.

NARRATOR: The software that built this army had a name: Mirai — say it "my-rye." And Mirai was almost insultingly simple. No brilliant exploit. No broken encryption. It just guessed passwords. Here's the thing about cheap internet-connected cameras and recorders: most leave the factory with a default username and password set by the manufacturer. Something like "admin" and "admin," or "root" and "password." The same default on every single unit. The instructions tell you to change it. Almost nobody does. Why would they? It's a camera. You plug it in, it works, you forget about it.

NARRATOR: So Mirai roamed the internet, knocking on the door of every device it could find, trying a short list of those factory-default passwords — maybe sixty common combinations. "admin, admin." "root, root." "support, support." On device after device, the default had never been changed, so the door swung open. Mirai walked in, installed itself, added that device to the army — then used it to go looking for more. It spread like a contagion, each infected gadget recruiting the next, until the botnet was vast enough to point at a target like Dyn and simply bury it.

NARRATOR: The attack came in waves through that October day. Dyn's engineers would fight off one assault and another would surge in. For hours, depending on where you were and which sites you wanted, the internet was a broken mess. And then it subsided. But the message had landed: the internet had a soft underbelly, and it was made of the cheap, forgotten, badly secured devices in all of our homes.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So let's name what this story teaches, because under the chaos there's a tidy set of lessons.

NARRATOR: First, the property under attack. Security isn't only about keeping secrets. This attack stole nothing. It read no data. It broke no encryption. What it destroyed was availability — the property that a system is there and reachable when legitimate users need it. A service can be perfectly confidential and perfectly intact and still be useless if nobody can get to it, and a denial-of-service attack goes straight for that third pillar. This is the textbook picture of an availability attack: not theft, but denial — making something unreachable. And the distributed version, powered by a botnet, is the hardest kind to stop, because you can't just block one source — the flood comes from everywhere at once.

NARRATOR: Second, how the army got built — and this is the lesson that should change your own behaviour. Every hijacked device was taken over because of insecure defaults and weak authentication. The manufacturers shipped products with a known, identical, factory-set password, and either never forced the owner to change it or made it painful enough that nobody bothered. That's a design failure. The secure choice should be the default choice and the easy choice: a device that refuses to operate until you've set a real password, that doesn't ship with the same credentials as a million others. The deeper principle: authentication — proving who's allowed in — is worthless if the credential is public knowledge printed in a manual. A password everyone already knows is no password at all.

NARRATOR: And there's a sharp twist about responsibility. The people who suffered weren't the people who were careless. The owner of the hacked webcam lost nothing — their camera kept working fine. Twitter, Netflix, and ultimately all of us paid the price. Insecure devices don't just endanger their owners; they become ammunition aimed at everyone else. Security is not purely a private matter — your unguarded gadget can be conscripted into harming strangers.

NARRATOR: And third, the structural lesson — too many eggs in one basket. A flood against a single company could darken hundreds of unrelated websites because all of them depended on the same D-N-S provider. Dyn was a single point of failure — a concentration of risk, where one component, if it goes down, takes a huge amount with it. The fix is the opposite of concentration: spread the dependency, use more than one provider, build in redundancy so no single failure can cascade. Many of the big sites that went dark that day learned exactly that, and started using multiple D-N-S providers afterward. Whenever you design a system, keep asking: what's the one thing that, if it fails, takes everything with it? Find that single point of failure before an attacker does — because availability isn't just about surviving an attack on you. It's about not having quietly bet your entire service on one fragile link in the chain.
