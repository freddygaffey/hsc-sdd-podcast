NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll describe a Gantt chart and its components; identify the critical path and explain what happens when a task on it slips; explain task dependencies; describe the project management triangle; and — the headline skill — read a Gantt chart in a stimulus question, one of the most common practical items on this dot-point. Let's start with the constraint every project lives under.

NARRATOR: The project management triangle. Every project is bounded by three things that pull against each other: Scope — how much you build, the features and requirements; Time — how long you have; and Cost — the budget and resources. The hook, a marquee one for this module: "Scope, Time, Cost." The law of the triangle is that you can't change one without affecting the others — they're connected. If a stakeholder expands scope mid-project but keeps the deadline fixed, then cost must rise — more people — or quality drops. The classic phrasing is "pick two": you can have it fast and cheap, but not also large-scope; large and fast, but not also cheap. Quality sits in the middle, squeezed by all three. This is the frame behind every scheduling decision, and it ties straight back to feasibility from episode one — TECO's economic and schedule dimensions are the cost and time corners of this very triangle. When you negotiate scope with a client next episode, this triangle is what you're negotiating around.

NARRATOR: Now the main tool: the Gantt chart, said "gant," hard G. A Gantt chart is a visual schedule that shows the project's tasks as horizontal bars laid against a timeline. I can't show it to you, and you might not be looking, so picture it in words: time runs left to right along the top — days, weeks, or months; each task is a row; each task's bar starts at its start date and its length shows its duration. A four-week task is a bar four units long, positioned where it starts. The memory line for what a Gantt contains: "bars over time, arrows for dependencies, the long chain is the critical path." Each component: the bars are the tasks and their durations. Dependencies are arrows linking tasks — an arrow from task A to task B means B can't start until A finishes. Milestones are key checkpoints or deliverables, often drawn as diamonds — in a WAgile project, your stage-gates are milestones on the Gantt. And progress is shown by shading the bars to indicate completion. I've put a task-and-dependency table that maps to a Gantt in Listing one if you want to read it later.

NARRATOR: Dependencies are crucial, and the exam tests them. A dependency is a relationship where one task relies on another. The most common type — the one to know — is finish-to-start: task B can't start until task A finishes. You can't start coding until the design is approved; you can't do integration testing until the modules are built. There are others — start-to-start, finish-to-finish — but finish-to-start is the default. Why they matter: dependencies determine the order, and therefore the minimum possible duration of the project. Tasks with no dependency between them run in parallel — overlapping bars — which shortens the schedule; tasks chained by dependencies run in sequence, which lengthens it. Mapping dependencies correctly is what makes a schedule realistic. And it leads to the single most important concept in this episode.

NARRATOR: The critical path. The critical path is the longest sequence of dependent tasks through the project — and because it's the longest chain, it determines the minimum time the whole project can possibly take. The key property: tasks on the critical path have zero slack — zero flexibility in their timing. Delay a critical-path task by a week and the entire project slips by a week; there's no give. Tasks not on the critical path have slack — they can be delayed somewhat without moving the finish date, because they're not the bottleneck. So the critical path is the chain you watch like a hawk. The exam consequence: if a stimulus shows a Gantt and asks "what happens if this task slips," the answer hinges entirely on whether that task is on the critical path. On it: the whole project slips by the same amount. Off it, within its slack: no effect on the finish date. That distinction is the mark.

QUESTION: Let me give you a worked Gantt to read, then you'll answer. Here's a small project. Task A, requirements, two weeks, starts at the beginning, nothing before it. Task B, design, three weeks, can't start until A finishes. Task C, build, four weeks, can't start until B finishes. Task D, write the user manual, two weeks, can also start as soon as A finishes — it only needs the requirements, not the design or build. Task E, testing, two weeks, needs both C and D finished. Pause the player and work out two things: the critical path, and how long the whole project takes. Then play.

NARRATOR: Let's trace it. There are two paths from start to finish. Path one: A, then B, then C, then E — two plus three plus four plus two equals eleven weeks. Path two: A, then D, then E — two plus two plus two equals six weeks. The critical path is the longest, path one, A-B-C-E, at eleven weeks, so the whole project takes eleven weeks. Now the payoff. Task D, the user manual, is not on the critical path — its path is only six weeks, so D has slack: it could be delayed up to five weeks and the project would still finish in eleven, because E is waiting on C anyway. So if D slips by two weeks, the finish doesn't move at all. But if task C, the build, slips by two weeks, it's on the critical path, so the whole project slips to thirteen weeks. Same-sized slip, completely different consequence — and the difference is the critical path. That's the exact reasoning a Gantt stimulus question wants.

NARRATOR: Tracking — the second half of the dot-point, "scheduling and tracking." Once the schedule exists, you track actual progress against it. You record completion for each task, compare planned versus actual — the gap is the schedule variance — and raise alerts when tasks fall behind, prioritising critical-path tasks because those directly threaten the finish date. This is continuous, the same shape as Agile's velocity tracking and the "measure, then act" idea you've seen all year. The point for the marker: project management isn't drawing the schedule once; it's the ongoing comparison of plan to reality and adjusting — the "manage the project" verb of S-E-twelve-oh-nine.

NARRATOR: Collaboration tools, named in the dot-point. These are the software for scheduling, tracking, and working together. Know a few real ones and where they fit. To hold the set, the hook is J-T-M-P-V, "Jump To My Project, Version one" — Jira, Trello, Microsoft Project, Primavera, Version control. Jira is the standard for Agile software teams — issue tracking, backlogs, sprints. Trello is a lightweight Kanban-board tool — cards on columns — good for a small team. Microsoft Project and Primavera P6 are heavyweight tools with full Gantt and resource management, suited to large, complex projects. And version control is your direct callback — shared repositories from Programming for the Web twelve-oh-six and from Year 11: fork, branch, pull request. A pull request is itself a collaboration-and-review tool, and a shared Git repository is the backbone of how a software team coordinates work. So when the dot-point says "collaboration tools," version control is squarely one of them — we go deep on it in twenty-five-oh-four. The exam move when asked to select a tool: justify it against the project — Trello for a small Agile team, Microsoft Project for a large structured one. That's S-E-twelve-oh-six and oh-nine together: pick the tool, justify it, use it to manage.

NARRATOR: Worked example for the descriptive question. "Describe how a Gantt chart supports project management." A weak answer: "a Gantt chart shows the tasks and when they happen." True but thin — band three. A strong answer: "a Gantt chart supports project management by visually scheduling all tasks as bars against a timeline, showing each task's duration and start, the dependencies between tasks as linking arrows, and milestones as checkpoints. This lets a manager see the critical path — the longest chain of dependent tasks that sets the minimum project duration — so they know which tasks have zero slack and must not slip. By shading bars to show progress, it also enables tracking of actual versus planned schedule, so delays, especially on critical-path tasks, are spotted early and managed." The strong answer names the components, the critical path, and the tracking function, and says what each enables. Components, critical path, tracking — that's the four-mark structure.

NARRATOR: The traps, named. Trap one: a Gantt chart without dependencies. A schedule that lists tasks in time but doesn't show what depends on what is nearly useless — you can't find the critical path, and you discover too late that task C couldn't start because B wasn't done. Dependencies are what make a Gantt a management tool rather than a pretty calendar. Trap two: ignoring the critical path. Treating all delayed tasks as equally urgent wastes effort; the slip that matters is the one on the critical path. Trap three, subtler: confusing slack tasks with critical tasks when answering a slip question — always check whether the slipped task is on the critical path before you say the project is delayed.

NARRATOR: Lock in today. The project management triangle is Scope, Time, Cost — change one and the others move; quality is in the middle. A Gantt chart is a visual schedule: bars over time, arrows for dependencies, milestones as checkpoints, shaded bars for progress. The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks — it sets the minimum project duration, and its tasks have zero slack, so a slip on the critical path slips the whole project, while a slip within a non-critical task's slack doesn't move the finish date. Tracking is the ongoing plan-versus-actual comparison. Collaboration tools — J-T-M-P-V, "Jump To My Project, Version one," Jira, Trello, Microsoft Project, Primavera, version control — are chosen and justified to fit the project. This is S-E-twelve-oh-nine, the manage-and-document core. Traps: Gantt without dependencies; ignoring the critical path.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions, recall up to stimulus. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Describe how a Gantt chart supports project management. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: a Gantt chart is a visual project schedule that displays tasks as horizontal bars against a timeline, showing each task's start and duration, the dependencies between tasks as linking arrows, and milestones as key checkpoints. It supports project management by making the schedule and task relationships clear, allowing identification of the critical path — the longest chain of dependent tasks that determines the minimum project duration — so managers know which tasks cannot slip. By shading bars to indicate completion, it also supports tracking of actual progress against the plan, so delays, particularly on critical-path tasks, can be detected and addressed early. Naming the components, the critical path, and the tracking function, with what each enables, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, recall. What is the critical path, and why does it matter? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the critical path is the longest sequence of dependent tasks through a project, and because it's the longest chain, it determines the minimum possible duration of the whole project. It matters because tasks on the critical path have zero slack — no flexibility in their timing — so any delay to a critical-path task delays the entire project by the same amount, whereas tasks off the critical path have slack and can be delayed within that slack without affecting the finish date. Managers therefore monitor critical-path tasks most closely. Defining it as the longest dependent chain that sets project duration, and explaining the zero-slack consequence, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three, the stimulus. A project has these tasks: A, four weeks, no predecessor; B, two weeks, after A; C, five weeks, after A; D, three weeks, after both B and C. What is the critical path and the project duration, and what happens if task B is delayed by one week? Pause, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: there are two paths. Path A-B-D is four plus two plus three equals nine weeks. Path A-C-D is four plus five plus three equals twelve weeks. The critical path is A-C-D at twelve weeks, so the project duration is twelve weeks. Task B is on the shorter path, A-B-D at nine weeks, so B has three weeks of slack — D can't start until C finishes at week nine anyway. Therefore, if B is delayed by one week, it stays within its slack and the project finish does not change; it remains twelve weeks. The mark is identifying A-C-D as the critical path, twelve weeks total, and correctly reasoning that B's one-week slip is absorbed by its slack with no effect on the deadline.

QUESTION: Question four, application. A client adds a major new feature halfway through a project but insists the deadline doesn't move. Using the project management triangle, explain the consequences. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the project management triangle holds that scope, time, and cost are interdependent, with quality in the middle, so you cannot change one constraint without affecting the others. Here the client is increasing scope while fixing time, so to keep the deadline the project must either increase cost — by adding developers or resources to do more work in the same time — or sacrifice quality — by cutting testing, documentation, or polish to fit the extra feature in. If neither cost nor quality is allowed to move, the deadline will in reality slip despite the insistence, because the work simply takes longer. The correct management response is to make this trade-off explicit to the client and negotiate — for example, defer the new feature to a later phase, drop a lower-priority feature to make room, or agree to extend time or budget. Explaining that fixed time plus increased scope forces cost or quality to give, and recommending an explicit negotiation, earns full marks.
