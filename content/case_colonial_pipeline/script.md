NARRATOR: Some time around five in the morning, on Friday the seventh of May, two thousand twenty-one, an employee in a control room at a company called Colonial Pipeline noticed something on a screen that should never have been there. A ransom note. A message, left by strangers, sitting on a computer inside the network of one of the most important pieces of machinery in the United States. And the message said, in effect, we are inside, we have locked your systems, we have stolen your data, and if you ever want it back, you will pay us. By the end of that day, a company most Americans had never heard of would do something it had never done in its entire history. It would shut down the largest fuel pipeline in the country. Not a section of it. All of it. Five and a half thousand miles of pipe, carrying nearly half of all the fuel consumed on the eastern seaboard of the United States, switched off, deliberately, by the people who ran it. And the thing that set all of this in motion — the cause of the petrol queues, the panic, the planes being rerouted, the President of the United States declaring a state of emergency — was not a missile, or an army, or even a particularly clever piece of hacking. It was a single password. One password, for an account nobody was even using anymore, on a system that had been quietly forgotten. That is the whole story, in one sentence. And it's worth understanding in detail, because the gap between how small the cause was and how enormous the consequences were is one of the most important lessons in security.

NARRATOR: Let me first make you feel the size of the thing that got switched off, because if you don't understand the pipeline, none of the panic makes sense. There's a river of fuel that runs underneath the eastern United States, and almost nobody who depends on it every day has ever heard its name. The Colonial Pipeline stretches roughly five and a half thousand miles, from the great refineries on the Gulf Coast of Texas, up through the south-east, all the way to the New York area. Through it flows gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel — and not a trickle. Something on the order of forty-five percent of all the fuel burned on the entire east coast of America travels through this one system. The petrol in the cars in Atlanta, the diesel in the trucks on the interstate, the jet fuel in the airliners at the big southern airports — a vast share of it spent part of its journey inside the Colonial Pipeline. This is what people mean, in the most literal possible sense, by the words critical infrastructure. A system that, if it stops, a region stops with it. And on the seventh of May, it stopped.

NARRATOR: So who left that note? A criminal group that called themselves DarkSide. And it matters to be precise about what they were, because they were not what most people picture when they hear the word hackers. Not a government. Not soldiers in some foreign cyber-army. They were a business — a criminal business, run for money, with a chilling degree of professionalism. DarkSide operated what the security world calls ransomware-as-a-service. And that phrase tells you how the modern criminal underworld actually works. DarkSide were the developers. They wrote the ransomware — the malicious software that breaks into a company, encrypts all its files, and holds them hostage. But they didn't carry out every attack themselves. They rented their software out to other criminals, called affiliates, who did the breaking-in, and took a cut of whatever the affiliate extorted, like a grim franchise. They even ran something resembling customer service — a support portal for victims, a leak site to publish stolen data, a press operation. It was crime, organised like a software start-up.

NARRATOR: And one detail about DarkSide tells you almost everything about where they sat in the world. Their software, before doing anything else on a freshly infected machine, performed a quiet little check. It looked at the computer's language settings. And if it found the machine was configured for Russian, or for the languages of several neighbouring former Soviet states — the group of countries known as the Commonwealth of Independent States — the ransomware would stop, delete itself, and do no harm at all. DarkSide were widely assessed to be operating out of Eastern Europe, most likely Russia. And the reason their own code refused to attack computers in those countries is the oldest reason in the criminal book: you do not rob the houses on your own street, because that's the one place the police might actually come for you. And in the spring of two thousand twenty-one, one of their affiliates went looking for a way into Colonial Pipeline. They did not have to look very hard.

QUESTION: A pipeline that carries nearly half a continent's fuel should be one of the hardest targets on Earth to break into. So how did a criminal gang get inside the network of something that critical?

NARRATOR: They logged in. That's the uncomfortable, anticlimactic truth of it. They didn't smash through a firewall. They didn't unleash some exotic, never-before-seen weapon. They found a valid username and password, and used the front door. Specifically, they got into a V-P-N — a virtual private network. A V-P-N is the standard, everyday tool that lets an employee working from home, or on the road, connect securely into their company's internal network, as if they were at a desk in the office. It builds an encrypted tunnel across the open internet, straight into the heart of the corporate network — a door from the outside world into the protected inside, and it's supposed to be a very well-guarded door. The attackers found a working account for Colonial's V-P-N, and it let them straight through onto the internal network. The forensic investigators who later reconstructed the attack — a firm called Mandiant — traced the earliest evidence of the intruders being inside to the twenty-ninth of April, more than a week before the ransom note ever appeared. They had been quietly walking around inside the network for days before anyone had the faintest idea.

NARRATOR: But here is where the story gets genuinely instructive, because the details of that account are a catalogue of ordinary, forgivable, human mistakes — the kind that exist in thousands of companies right now. First: the account was not even supposed to be in use anymore. It was a legacy account — an old V-P-N profile, belonging to an employee, that the company believed was no longer active. But "no longer active" and "no longer working" are two completely different things, and that distinction is the whole ballgame. Nobody had switched it off. Nobody had deleted it. It was a forgotten door, still unlocked, still leading inside — and a forgotten door is the most dangerous kind there is, because nobody is watching it.

NARRATOR: Second — and pay attention, because this part defies the cliché — the password on that account was not weak. This is what people get wrong about the Colonial story. They imagine the password was something stupid, like the word "password," or "one-two-three-four-five-six." It was not. When Mandiant's chief technology officer, a man named Charles Carmakal, later testified to the United States Congress about exactly how this happened, he described it as a relatively complex password — a decent length, a mix of cases, special characters. By the ordinary rules we all get nagged about, it was a good password. So how did the criminals know it? Because that same password had been used somewhere else. On some other website, at some point, the employee had reused it — and that other place had been breached. When a website gets breached, the stolen passwords don't just vanish. They get bundled into enormous lists and dumped across the criminal corners of the internet — billions of leaked credentials, all searchable. The attackers found it in one of those troves, tried it against Colonial's V-P-N, and it worked. So the lesson isn't "pick a stronger password." The password was strong. The lesson is that a strong password reused in two places is exactly as weak as the weakest place you put it. The strength of the lock means nothing if you hand a copy of the key to a stranger and they leave it lying in the street.

QUESTION: All right — but a single password should never be enough to walk into the network of national critical infrastructure. There is supposed to be a second line of defence for precisely this situation. So what was missing?

NARRATOR: What was missing was multi-factor authentication. And this is the absolute heart of the Colonial Pipeline story, so let me be precise about what it is and why its absence was catastrophic. When you log into something, you are proving who you are — the technical word is authentication. And there are, broadly, three categories of proof you can offer. Something you know — a password, a P-I-N. Something you have — a physical object only you possess, like your phone, or a hardware key that generates codes. And something you are — a part of your body, like your fingerprint or your face. Now, a password is purely something you know. And the fatal weakness of "something you know" is that knowledge can be copied without you ever noticing. Someone can learn your password — by stealing it, guessing it, phishing it, or, as here, finding it in a leak from somewhere else you used it — and the instant they know it, they are you. The system cannot tell the difference. Nothing about a password proves the person typing it is the person it belongs to.

NARRATOR: Multi-factor authentication fixes this by demanding proof from more than one of those categories. The classic combination is something you know plus something you have: your password, and then a six-digit code from an app on your phone, or a tap on a physical security key. And the beauty of it is in the arithmetic of the attack. To get in, the criminal now needs both things at once — to know your password and to physically possess your phone. Stealing a password from a leaked database is trivial. Stealing a password and the specific physical phone in a specific employee's pocket, on the other side of the world, at the exact moment you want to log in — that is an exponentially harder problem. Multi-factor authentication takes a stolen password and renders it almost useless on its own. It turns a single point of failure into two independent locks an attacker would have to defeat simultaneously. It is, by a wide margin, the single most effective ordinary security control there is. Cheap, well understood, everywhere.

NARRATOR: That legacy V-P-N account at Colonial Pipeline did not have it. A password, and nothing else. One factor. No code on a phone, no hardware token, no second gate of any kind standing between that one leaked string of characters and the inside of the network of a company that fuels half a coast. So when the attackers found the password, that was the entire defence. There was no second lock to stop at. They typed in the username, they typed in the password, and the door swung open. One factor, compromised, was everything. And this is the lesson the whole world took from Colonial Pipeline, because it's so painfully clean: critical access must never depend on a single factor, because any single factor can fail, and eventually will. The defence that would have stopped the entire crisis dead — that would have turned that leaked password into a useless dead end — was a control so standard, so cheap, so unglamorous, that its absence on that one account is genuinely shocking. It just wasn't switched on.

NARRATOR: So now the attackers are inside, with days to spare and nobody watching. What does a ransomware crew actually do once they're in? They don't fire the moment they arrive. The smart ones, and DarkSide were smart, take their time. They map the network, figure out what's valuable, hunt for the systems that, if taken away, would hurt the most. And crucially, before they lock anything, they steal. Over the days they were inside, the attackers quietly copied out — exfiltrated — nearly one hundred gigabytes of the company's data. And that theft is a deliberate tactic with a name: double extortion. The first lever is the encryption — we've scrambled your files, pay us for the key. The second lever is the theft — we've also got a copy of your private data, so even if you restore everything from your own backups, pay us anyway, or we publish it all to the world. Extortion stacked on extortion. No matter how you wriggle, there's a reason to pay.

NARRATOR: And then, in the early hours of the seventh of May, having stolen what they wanted, they pulled the trigger. They detonated the ransomware across Colonial's systems, encrypting them, freezing them, and leaving behind the ransom note the control-room employee found that morning. And here we arrive at one of the most subtle and important technical points in this whole story — a point students get wrong constantly, and markers love because it separates a shallow understanding from a real one.

QUESTION: The ransomware locked up the company's computers. But it never actually took control of the pipeline's physical machinery — so why on Earth did the entire pipeline have to be shut down?

NARRATOR: To answer that, you have to understand that a company like Colonial runs two very different kinds of computer system, in two different worlds. On one side is information technology — I-T. The business side: the email, the billing, the accounting, the corporate network, the spreadsheets that track who ordered how much fuel and who owes what for it. The ordinary office stuff. On the other side is operational technology — O-T. The industrial side: the specialised computers and controllers that physically run the pipeline — that open and close the valves, drive the pumps, monitor the pressure and the flow of fuel through the pipes. The machinery that actually moves the oil. In a well-designed company, those two worlds are kept carefully separated, precisely so a problem on the messy, internet-connected office side cannot spill over into the machinery that runs the physical world.

NARRATOR: And as far as the investigation could determine, that separation held. The ransomware hit Colonial's I-T systems — the business network, the billing. It did not, as best anyone could establish, take over the operational technology that physically controls the flow of fuel. The hackers got the office. They did not, directly, get the pumps. So if the pumps were fine, why shut the pipeline? Two reasons, both instructive. The first is caution under uncertainty. When you discover intruders have been roaming your network for days and have detonated ransomware across your business systems, you cannot be confident, in that first frightening moment, how far they got. Maybe they only touched I-T. But maybe they had a foothold into the operational side too — and a pipeline run by compromised controllers is a genuinely dangerous physical object. Faced with that uncertainty, shutting down is the safe choice: you stop everything and work out what's actually happened from a position of safety, rather than letting fuel flow under machinery you no longer fully trust.

NARRATOR: But the second reason really shows you how a purely digital attack reaches out and touches the physical world. The systems that got encrypted included billing. And Colonial could not run its pipeline without billing, because it could not account for the fuel it would be delivering — it couldn't measure who was getting what and charge them for it. You cannot ship hundreds of millions of dollars of fuel if you have no working system to track and invoice a single drop. The business of moving the fuel was frozen, even though the machinery for moving the fuel was fine. So they stopped the pipeline. Sit with that, because it's the whole point: an attack that only ever touched the back-office computers — the billing, the spreadsheets, the boring administrative plumbing — brought the physical flow of fuel to forty-five percent of a coast to a dead halt. The attackers never went near a valve. They never needed to.

NARRATOR: And so, on the seventh of May, the great river of fuel under the eastern United States simply stopped flowing. And the world above the pipe started to come apart.

NARRATOR: At first, there was a buffer. Pipelines don't deliver instantly; there's fuel already in storage, already in transit, so for a day or two the petrol stations kept running and most people had no idea anything was wrong. But word got out. And the moment the public understood the pipeline supplying their fuel had been shut down by hackers, and nobody could say when it would come back, human nature did what it always does in a shortage. It panicked. And panic-buying, in a fuel crisis, is uniquely vicious, because it's self-fulfilling. The moment everyone, all at once, rushed to top up their tanks, to fill every jerry can, to grab fuel they didn't even need yet just in case — demand spiked far beyond anything the system was built for, and the stations ran dry not because there was no fuel coming, but because everyone tried to buy a month's worth in an afternoon. The fear of a shortage manufactured the shortage.

NARRATOR: And the numbers got bad, fast. Within days, temporary fuel outages were reported across eleven states and Washington, D.C. The south-east was hit hardest. In North Carolina, around two-thirds of all gas stations ran dry. In Charlotte, more than seven in ten were out. In Washington, D.C., by the middle of the following week, nearly nine out of ten stations had nothing left in the pumps. Lines of cars snaked around blocks. The average national price of gasoline climbed to its highest level in more than six years. And then there was the detail that, more than any other, captured the sheer irrationality of a population in panic. The United States Consumer Product Safety Commission — a government agency — felt compelled to issue a public warning that read like satire but was deadly serious: do not fill plastic bags with gasoline. People, desperate to hoard fuel, were filling plastic shopping bags with petrol and putting them in their cars. A government had to officially tell its citizens not to do something so obviously, catastrophically dangerous, because enough of them were doing it. That is what a fuel panic looks like.

NARRATOR: The crisis went all the way to the top. On the ninth of May, two days after the attack, the United States government declared a regional state of emergency, relaxing the rules on transporting fuel by road, to try to route around the frozen pipeline with tankers on the highways instead. Airlines had to adjust some flights and refuelling plans over jet-fuel concerns at southern airports. A criminal gang, on the other side of the world, using one leaked password, had triggered a national emergency in the most powerful country on the planet. And inside Colonial Pipeline, the company's leadership faced the single hardest decision of the whole affair.

QUESTION: You're the chief executive of the company. The country is in chaos, your systems are locked, and the criminals are demanding millions. Do you pay them?

NARRATOR: They paid. The chief executive of Colonial Pipeline, a man named Joseph Blount, authorised the ransom, and they paid it within hours of the attack: seventy-five Bitcoin, worth roughly four point four million dollars that day, handed to a criminal gang. Now, this is a genuinely controversial decision. The official advice, from the F-B-I and most security experts, is: do not pay. Paying funds the criminals directly; it proves to them and every other gang watching that this kind of target pays out; and it makes the next attack more likely. Every payment is an investment in the ransomware industry. Blount knew all of that, and paid anyway. His reasoning, which he later defended in front of Congress, was that he did not know the full extent of the attack, did not know how long recovery would take, and with a critical national fuel supply in the balance, was not willing to gamble the country's fuel on his own ability to recover unaided. In his words, it was the right thing to do for the country. You can disagree with him. Many people did. But you can also see the impossible position he was in.

NARRATOR: And here is the bitter punchline of that decision, the detail that turns it almost darkly comic. In exchange for the four point four million dollars, DarkSide handed over a decryption tool — the software to unlock the encrypted files. And it was so slow as to be nearly useless. It worked, technically, but it crawled. So slow that Colonial, in the end, recovered most of its systems not from the tool they'd paid millions for, but from their own backups. They paid a multi-million-dollar ransom and then largely restored themselves anyway, from copies of their own data they already had. The thing they paid a fortune for was less useful than the thing they already owned.

NARRATOR: The pipeline began restarting on the twelfth of May, and returned to normal operations by the fifteenth. The physical crisis lasted about a week. But the story has one more remarkable turn — a rare piece of good news in a field that doesn't offer many. Because the criminals had made a mistake of their own, a mistake of trust. They'd assumed Bitcoin was untraceable, that money moved in cryptocurrency simply vanishes into the dark. It does not. Every Bitcoin transaction is recorded, permanently and publicly, on a shared ledger called the blockchain. The coins are pseudonymous — tied to wallet addresses, not names — but the trail of where they move is there for anyone to follow forever. And the F-B-I followed it. A specialised unit traced the ransom as it moved between wallets, identified the specific wallet holding a large chunk of it, obtained a court order, and — in a way never fully explained in public, but which involved getting hold of the cryptographic private key for that wallet — seized the money straight back out of it. On the seventh of June, just a month after the attack, the United States Department of Justice announced it had clawed back sixty-three point seven of the seventy-five Bitcoin — the bulk of the ransom. Because Bitcoin's price had fallen in the intervening weeks, that recovered share was worth about two point three million dollars. The criminals who thought they'd vanished with the money watched a chunk of it pulled back out of their own wallet.

NARRATOR: As for DarkSide — the heat became too much. Within days of the attack, with the full attention of the United States government now fixed on them, the group announced it was shutting down, claiming it had lost access to its own infrastructure. Whether that was a genuine takedown or a convenient excuse to disappear with their earnings and rebrand later — which is exactly what such groups tend to do — only they know. But the franchise, at least under that name, went dark.

NARRATOR: So let me draw out what this story teaches, because for one forgotten password, it teaches an extraordinary amount.

NARRATOR: The headline, the thing you carry out of this above all else, is authentication, and specifically the case for multi-factor authentication. The entire catastrophe — the panic, the queues, the national emergency, the millions in ransom — turns on one missing control. A password alone is a fragile defence, because passwords leak: through reuse, through breaches elsewhere, through guessing, through phishing. Multi-factor authentication exists for precisely this scenario — to ensure a stolen password, on its own, is not enough. Had that one V-P-N account required a second factor, the leaked password would have been a dead end. And the most powerful version of this lesson is the contrast: the password was strong, and it didn't matter, because strength was not the property that mattered. Singleness was. One factor, however strong, is one point of failure. It is as clean a demonstration as exists anywhere that critical access must never rest on a single factor, because any single factor can, and eventually will, be compromised.

NARRATOR: The second lesson is about access management — the quiet one people skip past, but markers reward. That account was dormant, and yet still live, still valid, still a working door into the network. Good security means knowing, at all times, exactly which accounts exist, who they belong to, whether they're still needed, and ruthlessly disabling the ones that aren't — removing access the moment it's no longer required. Every account that lingers past its usefulness is an unguarded, unwatched door you've forgotten you have. The Colonial attackers didn't break a door down. They walked through one nobody watched.

NARRATOR: The third lesson is availability, and the special weight it carries when the target is critical infrastructure. Think about what this attack actually destroyed. The thing the world felt — the cars lining up, the empty pumps, the state of emergency — was a loss of availability. Availability is one of the three great pillars of information security: confidentiality, keeping data secret; integrity, keeping data correct and untampered; and availability, simply being able to use your systems when you need them. We obsess over the first two and treat the third as an afterthought. Colonial Pipeline is the argument for the third. Because when the unavailable thing is the fuel supply for half a country, availability becomes a matter of public order and national security. That is exactly why securing critical infrastructure — the power, the water, the fuel, the hospitals — has become one of the most serious concerns in the entire field. The stakes are not a leaked spreadsheet. They are whether a continent can move.

NARRATOR: The fourth lesson is about what you do when prevention fails — because it will, no defence is perfect, and what separates a survivable incident from a catastrophe is how well you can respond and recover. The discipline has names: incident response and business continuity. The decision to shut down to contain the threat; the agonising question of whether to pay; and, above all, that devastating detail — that their own backups did much of the real recovery, decryption tool or not. The organisations that weather an attack like this planned for it before it ever happened: a tested incident-response plan, recent backups kept somewhere the attacker can't reach and encrypt too, and a clear plan for keeping critical functions running while they recover. You cannot prevent every breach. What you can do is decide, in the calm before, how you'll detect it, contain it, and come back — so one stolen password doesn't get to write the whole ending for you.

NARRATOR: And there's a fifth thread worth pulling, because it reframes how you think about the threat itself. The people who did this were not a government and they were not geniuses. They were a criminal business, renting out their software, taking a cut. The barrier to entry for causing a national emergency has collapsed. You no longer need an army or a spy agency or a stockpile of secret exploits to shut down critical infrastructure — you need a leaked password, a piece of rented ransomware, and a target that left one ordinary control switched off. The terrifying thing about Colonial Pipeline is not how sophisticated it was. It is how ordinary it was.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A pipeline carrying nearly half the fuel of a continent. A forgotten account that should have been deleted, still alive, still a working door. A password that was strong, and reused, and therefore worthless — found in the wreckage of some other website's breach. No second factor, nothing between that leaked password and the inside of the network. A criminal franchise that walked through the front door, stole a hundred gigabytes, locked the business systems, and demanded millions. A company that shut down its own pipeline not because the pumps were hacked, but because it could no longer bill for the fuel — a digital attack on the back office halting the physical flow of an entire region's energy. A nation panic-buying petrol into plastic bags. A multi-million-dollar ransom paid for a decryption tool so slow they restored from their own backups anyway. And, for once, a clawback — the money pulled back out of the criminals' wallet, because the blockchain they trusted to hide them remembered everything.

NARRATOR: One password. No second factor. And a continent ran short of fuel. If you take a single thing from this story, let it be this: the smallest, cheapest, most boring security control there is — a code on a phone, the thing you grumble about every time it asks you — was the one thing standing between a leaked password and a national emergency. And it simply wasn't turned on. The most devastating attacks are almost never the cleverest ones. They are the ordinary ones, against the door nobody bothered to lock. So build your systems as if the attacker already has one of your passwords. Because somewhere out there, in a leaked list nobody told you about, they very probably already do.
