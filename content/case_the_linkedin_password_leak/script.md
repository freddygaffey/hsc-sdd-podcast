NARRATOR: On the fifth of June, twenty twelve, a file appeared on a Russian website called InsidePro. InsidePro was not a place ordinary people went. It was a forum for people who crack passwords for sport and for profit — for the kind of person who finds beauty in turning scrambled gibberish back into someone's secret. And on that day, somebody uploaded a plain text file and attached a request. The file held six and a half million long strings of letters and numbers. Each one looked like noise — a forty-character smear of hexadecimal, the sort of thing you'd get if a cat walked across a keyboard and the keyboard only spoke in zero through nine and A through F. And the request that came with it was short, and almost casual. Help me crack these.

NARRATOR: The people on that forum did not know, at first, where the file had come from. They didn't need to. To them it was a puzzle, and a generous one — six and a half million puzzles in a single download. So they pointed their machines at it. And within hours, the strings started turning back into words. Real words. Real passwords. And as more and more of them fell, a pattern emerged in the cracked ones that told everybody, very quickly, exactly whose passwords these were. People had used the name of the site as their password. Over and over, the cracked words contained the same theme — link, linkedin, linkedinpassword, the kind of thing a person types when a website nags them to make an account they didn't really want. These were the passwords of LinkedIn. The professional networking site. The place where, by then, more than a hundred and fifty million people kept their careers, their colleagues, their contacts, and the carefully managed face they showed to the working world.

NARRATOR: This is the story of those six and a half million fingerprints. It has a strange shape, because the disaster you can see in twenty twelve turns out to be only the corner of a much larger disaster that nobody would fully understand for four more years. It is a story about one technical shortcut — made years earlier by engineers who almost certainly thought they were being responsible — that turned a stolen database from an embarrassment into a catastrophe rippling outward across the entire internet, into accounts on websites LinkedIn had never even heard of. And underneath all of that, it is a story about a deep and beautiful idea in computer science: an idea about one-way doors, and about what happens when you build a one-way door and then forget to lock it.

QUESTION: Before any of that — let me get the obvious objection out of the way. LinkedIn didn't store everyone's password as plain readable text, did they? Surely a company that size knew better than that.

NARRATOR: They did know better. And no — they were not so reckless as to keep a giant spreadsheet of everyone's actual password in a database. If they had, this would be a much shorter and much dumber story. What they had done was the thing you are supposed to do. They had hashed the passwords. And to understand why this story is a tragedy rather than a farce — why LinkedIn got the big idea right and still ended up with a hundred million people exposed — you have to understand what hashing actually is. So let me build it up properly, because this single concept is the beating heart of the whole thing.

NARRATOR: When you create an account on a well-built website, the site faces a problem that is genuinely hard when you think about it. It needs to check, every time you come back, that you are you — that the password you typed today matches the one you chose when you signed up. But it also, ideally, does not want to know your password, because anything the site stores can be stolen. A database can be copied. A disk can be carried out the door. An employee can peek. So the dream is a kind of magic trick: a way to verify a password without ever holding onto it. And that magic trick exists. It is called a hash function.

NARRATOR: A hash function is a one-way scrambler. You feed it some input — your password, say, the word "sunshine" — and it produces a fixed-length output, a string of characters that looks completely random. We call that output a hash, or a digest, or, the word I'll keep using because it captures the feel of it: a fingerprint. And it has two properties that make it almost miraculous. The first is that it is deterministic — the same input always produces the exact same fingerprint. Feed it "sunshine" today, you get a certain string; feed it "sunshine" in ten years on the other side of the planet, you get that identical string. The second property is the magic one. It is one-way. You can go forwards — password to fingerprint — instantly and easily. But you cannot go backwards. There is no reverse gear. Given the fingerprint, there is no formula, no clever rearrangement, that hands you back the original password. The information needed to reverse it simply isn't there anymore.

NARRATOR: So now the magic trick works. When you sign up, the site hashes your password and stores only the fingerprint — never the password itself. The real password evaporates the instant you've finished typing it; the site keeps just the smear. And next time you log in, the site takes whatever you typed, hashes it with the same function, and compares the two fingerprints. Match, and you're in. The site has confirmed you know the password without ever knowing it itself. And the prize, the whole point, is this: if someone steals the database, they don't get passwords. They get fingerprints. And fingerprints can't be reversed. The thief is holding a list of smears that lead nowhere.

NARRATOR: That is exactly what LinkedIn had done. The six and a half million strings on that forum were not passwords. They were SHA-one hashes — fingerprints produced by a well-known, perfectly legitimate hash function called the Secure Hash Algorithm, version one. So LinkedIn had stored fingerprints, not passwords. They had built the one-way door. By the logic I just walked you through, that file on InsidePro should have been useless — six and a half million dead ends.

NARRATOR: And yet within seventy-two hours, by the crackers' own boasting, ninety percent of them had been turned back into the original passwords. Ninety percent. The one-way door, it turned out, had a way through it after all. So the real question — the question this entire case study exists to answer — is this. If a fingerprint genuinely cannot be reversed, how on earth did they reverse six million of them in a weekend?

QUESTION: Right, that's the thing I don't get. You just spent five minutes telling me hashing is a one-way street with no reverse gear. So how did the attackers drive back up it?

NARRATOR: They didn't drive back up it. That's the trick, and it's a beautiful, horrible trick once you see it. They never reversed a single hash. Reversing is impossible, and they knew it. So they didn't try. Instead, they ran the door forwards — millions and millions of times — and watched what came out.

NARRATOR: Here is the attack, and I want you to feel how simple it is, because its simplicity is the point. The attacker has the stolen list of fingerprints. They cannot turn a fingerprint into a password. But they can do the easy direction — turn a password into a fingerprint, because that's the direction the door opens freely. So they make a guess. They take a candidate password — let's say "password one two three" — and run it through SHA-one themselves. Out comes a fingerprint. Now they search for it in the stolen list. And if it's there — if that exact smear appears anywhere in those six and a half million lines — they have just learned that somebody on LinkedIn used the password "password one two three", because that person's password produced this exact fingerprint, and here it is. They didn't crack anything. They guessed a password, hashed their guess, and found a match. Same input, same output, always — so a match is proof.

NARRATOR: And then they do it again. And again. They don't guess randomly — they use enormous dictionaries of likely passwords, built from every previous breach in history, every common word and name and keyboard pattern, with numbers and symbols swapped in the predictable human ways. They feed millions of candidates through the hash function and check every result against the stolen list, harvesting matches as they go. It is not code-breaking. It is a guessing game played at unimaginable speed. And that phrase — at speed — is where LinkedIn's first real mistake lives.

NARRATOR: Because the speed of that guessing game is decided entirely by how fast the hash function runs. Every guess costs you one trip through the function, so if the function is fast, you can make a staggering number of guesses per second. And SHA-one is fast. Blisteringly fast. That was the whole reason it was invented. SHA-one was designed to fingerprint files — to verify that a multi-gigabyte download arrived intact and uncorrupted. For that job you want raw speed, to chew through enormous amounts of data in an eyeblink. And it delivers. On ordinary hardware of that era, using the graphics card — the same chip that draws video games, which happens to be fiendishly good at doing the same small calculation millions of times in parallel — an attacker could compute somewhere between tens of millions and over a hundred million SHA-one hashes every single second. Per machine.

QUESTION: Hold on. Why would anyone ever want their password scrambler to be slow? Slow sounds like a flaw. Fast is good. What am I missing?

NARRATOR: This is the inversion that catches everyone, so sit with it, because it's one of the most counterintuitive ideas in all of security. For almost everything in computing, fast is the goal. But for hashing a password, speed is the enemy — because of that guessing attack I just showed you. Run the numbers. If your hash function lets the attacker make a hundred million guesses per second, then a dictionary of a billion likely passwords takes them ten seconds. That's why ninety percent fell in three days — three days is an eternity at a hundred million guesses a second. Now imagine the function were deliberately slow instead — each single hash costing, say, a quarter of a second of real computer effort. Suddenly a hundred million guesses doesn't take one second; it takes most of a year. A billion guesses takes the better part of a decade. The same attack, the same dictionary, the same hardware — but the guessing game has become hopeless, because every guess now costs real time, and the attacker has to pay that cost on every single one.

NARRATOR: So slowness is not a defect. For password storage, slowness is the whole defence. There are hash functions built specifically for this — built to be deliberately, tunably, expensively slow. They have names like bcrypt and scrypt and Argon two, and their entire reason for existing is to take a meaningful slice of time and computer power for each and every hash, so that the easy direction — the direction the attacker needs — stops being easy. They turn the guessing game from a weekend's work into something measured in centuries. LinkedIn used none of them. They used SHA-one, the fast file-fingerprinting function, to store passwords. They took the one tool whose greatest strength was its speed, and used it for the one job where speed is fatal. That was the first mistake. But on its own, honestly, it might have been survivable. It was the second mistake that turned the lights out.

NARRATOR: The second mistake was that LinkedIn's hashes were unsalted. And to feel the weight of that word, I need to introduce the hero who was missing from this entire story — a small, humble, almost trivial-sounding thing called salt.

NARRATOR: A salt is a chunk of random data — unique to each individual user — that you mix into the password before you hash it. That's all. Before you run "sunshine" through the function, you generate a fistful of random characters, staple it onto the password, and hash the combination. Then you store the fingerprint and the salt together. The salt isn't a secret — it sits right there next to the hash in the database. It seems almost pointless at first glance. But watch what it does, because it quietly destroys two of the attacker's most powerful weapons at once.

NARRATOR: Here's the first thing it kills. Without salt, two users who happen to choose the same password get the identical fingerprint — same input, same output, always. So in LinkedIn's database, every person who chose "sunshine" had the exact same smear next to their name as every other "sunshine". And in a database of a hundred million people, a popular password like that appears thousands and thousands of times — the same fingerprint, repeated, like a watermark. Which means the attacker doesn't crack passwords one at a time. They crack them in bulk. The instant they discover that a certain fingerprint means "sunshine", they have simultaneously cracked every account that shares it — thousands of people, unlocked by one guess. Now add salt. Each user's "sunshine" gets a different random salt mixed in, so each produces a completely different fingerprint. The watermark vanishes. The thousands of identical smears become thousands of unique ones. And cracking one "sunshine" now tells you nothing about the others — you have to attack every account separately, from scratch, redoing all the work every time.

NARRATOR: And here's the second weapon salt destroys, which is even bigger. There's a technique attackers love called a precomputed table — you may have heard the fancier name, a rainbow table. The idea is devastating in its laziness. Long before stealing any database, the attacker takes a giant dictionary of passwords and patiently hashes every one, building an enormous lookup table that maps every fingerprint back to the password that made it. All the expensive computation is done up front, at their leisure. Then, the day they steal a database, they don't guess at all. They don't hash anything. They look up each stolen fingerprint in their pre-built table and read off the answer. Millions of accounts cracked in minutes, with no real work, because the work was all done in advance. Against fast, unsalted SHA-one, a precomputed table is apocalyptic. It turns cracking into a search.

NARRATOR: Salt annihilates that attack completely. Because now the thing being hashed isn't "sunshine" — it's "sunshine" plus a random salt the attacker couldn't have known in advance. Their precomputed table is full of fingerprints for plain passwords, and none of them match, because every real fingerprint in the database includes a unique random salt that wasn't in the table. Years of precomputation become worthless in an instant. The attacker is dragged back to the slow, one-account-at-a-time guessing game — which, if the hash were also slow, would be utterly hopeless. Salt doesn't make any single password uncrackable. It forbids the bulk attack, and forces the work to be redone, in full, for every user. And LinkedIn had no salt. None. Not a grain.

NARRATOR: So put the two mistakes side by side and feel how they compound. A fast hash — so each guess is nearly free. And no salt — so each guess, when it lands, unlocks every duplicate at once, and a precomputed table can do most of the guessing in advance. Fast and unsalted. It is, almost precisely, the worst of every possible world. And there's a detail in how the crackers tore through that file that I find quietly chilling. As they cracked the hashes, they marked the ones they'd finished — overwrote the start of each broken fingerprint with a row of leading zeros, as a flag to the others: don't bother with this one, it's done. And when researchers later counted the flagged ones, more than three and a half million hashes — three million, five hundred and twenty-one thousand, one hundred and eighty of them — began with those zeros. Already cracked and crossed off, in the version that became public. More than half. This wasn't a struggle. It was a harvest, and the harvesters were so efficient they were tidying up after themselves so as not to duplicate effort.

NARRATOR: Now. That was twenty twelve. Six and a half million hashes, most of them cracked, a bad week for LinkedIn, a flurry of news stories, a forced password reset, a public apology. And then the world more or less moved on. LinkedIn fixed their practices — they began salting, they moved off bare SHA-one — and for four years, this looked like a closed chapter. A company got embarrassed, learned its lesson, and the six and a half million number sat in everyone's memory as the size of the damage.

NARRATOR: It was not the size of the damage. The six and a half million was a fragment. And in May twenty sixteen, the rest of the iceberg surfaced.

QUESTION: Four years later? What could possibly turn up four years after a breach that everyone thought was over?

NARRATOR: A salesman. In May twenty sixteen, on a dark web marketplace called The Real Deal — one of those hidden corners of the internet, reachable only through anonymising software, where stolen data and worse change hands — a seller appeared. He went by the handle Peace; sometimes the longer version, peace of mind. And what Peace was selling, for the almost insulting price of five bitcoin — around two thousand two hundred US dollars at the time, the price of a decent laptop — was the LinkedIn breach. The real one. Not six and a half million records. The listing offered the email addresses and passwords of around a hundred and seventeen million LinkedIn accounts. And the total dataset, in some accountings, ran to about a hundred and sixty-seven million records — the difference being users who had no password stored because they'd signed up through Facebook or the like.

NARRATOR: Sit with that jump. The world had spent four years believing the LinkedIn breach was six and a half million accounts. The truth was more than a hundred million — the front-page figure from twenty twelve wasn't even five percent of the real number. What had leaked onto InsidePro back then was apparently just a slice, the portion one cracker pulled out to ask for help with. The whole haul had been sitting in private hands the entire time, quietly, for four years, and only now was being put up for public sale. Every one of those hundred and seventeen million was a person who, for four years, had no idea their LinkedIn password — and, as we'll see, very possibly the password to far more than LinkedIn — had been in a criminal's pocket the whole time.

NARRATOR: A data-breach indexing service called LeakedSource got hold of the full set and analysed it, and what they found put a brutally fine point on everything I've been describing about fast, unsalted hashing meeting human beings. They reported that just fifty passwords — fifty — accounted for more than two point two million of the hundred and seventeen million accounts. And the most common password in the entire dataset, the single most popular secret guarding the professional identities of millions of people, was the number sequence one, two, three, four, five, six. The crackers didn't need to be clever. The defence was a fast unsalted hash, and the secrets behind it were one two three four five six, repeated two million times over.

QUESTION: All right — it's bad for LinkedIn, it's bad for those hundred and seventeen million users. But here's the part I actually want to understand. Why should someone who barely uses LinkedIn care that their LinkedIn password leaked? It's just one site.

NARRATOR: Because it was never just one site. This is the twist that takes a story about one company's bad engineering and turns it into a story about you, and your bank, and your email — and it's the single most important practical lesson in this entire episode. The reason a LinkedIn breach becomes everyone's problem has a name, and the name is password reuse.

NARRATOR: Think honestly about how people actually behave. A person has dozens of online accounts — email, bank, shopping, social media, a forum they posted on once in twenty fourteen. Remembering a different strong password for every one is genuinely hard, so most people don't. They pick one password they like, maybe two or three, and reuse them everywhere — the same secret on LinkedIn, on Gmail, on their online banking, on Amazon. Maybe with a small tweak, a one on the end here, an exclamation mark there, but fundamentally the same secret guarding everything. And once you understand that, you understand the real value of that hundred-and-seventeen-million-line file. It is not a list of LinkedIn passwords. It is a list of email addresses paired with the passwords those people probably use everywhere.

NARRATOR: So the attacker who buys that file doesn't waste time on LinkedIn. They write a simple program that takes each email-and-password pair from the dump and tries it — automatically, at machine speed — against other websites entirely. The LinkedIn password on that person's Gmail. On their PayPal. On their bank. On their company's login portal. The vast majority fail, because the person didn't reuse the password there, or the account doesn't exist. But a meaningful fraction succeed. And when you're trying a hundred and seventeen million pairs, even a one-percent success rate is over a million broken-into accounts — on services that were never breached, never hacked, belonging to companies that had never heard of the LinkedIn incident. This technique has a name you should know cold for any security exam, and for life: credential stuffing. Stuffing stolen credentials, in bulk, into other people's login forms, betting on reuse.

NARRATOR: And the LinkedIn dump, alongside a small handful of other gigantic breaches from the same era, became premium fuel for exactly that. For years afterward, security teams watched waves of automated login attempts wash over their services — attacks that had nothing to do with any flaw in their own systems, powered entirely by passwords that had leaked elsewhere and been reused. Somebody's forgotten LinkedIn account from twenty twelve became the unlocked back door into their email in twenty sixteen, and from their email — which can reset the password on almost everything else — into their whole digital life. The breach didn't stay inside LinkedIn. It leaked outward, through the simple human habit of reusing a password, into millions of lives.

NARRATOR: So who actually did this? Unusually for a breach of this scale, this story has a culprit with a face and a verdict. His name is Yevgeniy Nikulin — a Russian hacker, and according to US prosecutors the man behind the original twenty twelve intrusion. And the way he got in is its own small lesson, almost an anticlimax after all the talk of hashes and salts: he didn't break any clever cryptography. He stole a LinkedIn employee's username and password and used those legitimate credentials to walk into the corporate network and help himself to the database. The front door, opened with a borrowed key. And — fittingly, given everything we've just said about reuse — he didn't stop at LinkedIn. The same campaign, across twenty twelve, hit Dropbox, where he made off with the details of tens of millions of accounts, and the social site Formspring as well.

NARRATOR: Nikulin was arrested in Prague in October of twenty sixteen — pulled in by Czech police while he was out at a restaurant. There followed a long, strange extradition fight, with both the United States and Russia trying to claim him, before he was sent to America, convicted in twenty twenty, and sentenced to eighty-eight months in prison — more than seven years — for the intrusions into LinkedIn, Dropbox, and Formspring. One of the very few breaches of this magnitude where the person responsible was actually caught, tried, and locked up.

NARRATOR: So let me draw out what this story is really teaching — because behind the drama, the LinkedIn breach is one of the cleanest, most precisely examinable lessons in all of security. It is, fundamentally, a lesson about how to store a password correctly. There are three rules, and the breach is what happens when you get them wrong.

NARRATOR: The first rule, the one people muddle constantly: you hash passwords, you do not encrypt them. Hold the distinction firmly, because it matters and it earns marks. Encryption is two-way, designed to be reversible — whoever holds the key can decrypt the data and get the original back. That's exactly what you want for a private message that needs reading later. But it is precisely what you do not want for a password, because if the data can be decrypted, then a stolen key, a careless insider, or a court order gets every password back in plaintext. Hashing is one-way by design. No key, no reverse gear. The site can check a password without ever being able to recover it. LinkedIn, to their credit, understood this — they hashed. They got the first rule right. That's why it could have been so much worse.

NARRATOR: The second rule: every password gets a unique salt. Random, per-user, mixed in before hashing. Its job is to make identical passwords produce different fingerprints, and to render precomputed rainbow tables completely useless. Salt forbids the bulk, crack-them-all-at-once attack and forces the work to be redone for each account. Unsalted hashing was the single biggest reason this breach was a massacre instead of an inconvenience — two million people sharing the fingerprint for one two three four five six is the absence of salt made visible.

NARRATOR: And the third rule, the counterintuitive one: the hash function must be slow and expensive on purpose. A general-purpose hash like SHA-one is built for speed — wonderful for verifying a downloaded file, catastrophic for storing a password. For passwords you reach for a function deliberately designed to be slow and costly — bcrypt, scrypt, Argon two — so each guess takes real, painful time, and an attacker's hundred million guesses per second collapses into a useless trickle. Slow is the feature. Slow is the wall.

NARRATOR: One-way hashing, a unique salt per user, a deliberately slow function. Get those three right and a stolen password database becomes a minor embarrassment — the attacker holds fingerprints they cannot turn back into passwords at any useful scale, and goes home with nothing. LinkedIn got the first idea right, and the second and third completely wrong, and a hundred and seventeen million people paid for the gap.

NARRATOR: But there's a final lesson, and it's the one actually in your hands tonight, because it doesn't depend on any company doing anything. The most dangerous thing about the LinkedIn breach was never the cracked LinkedIn passwords themselves. It was that they unlocked other doors — through credential stuffing, through reuse — into accounts on services that had done everything right. No website can protect you from that. The only defence is yours: a different password on every single site, ideally generated and remembered by a password manager so you never hold them in your head, so that one company's bad day can never become the master key to your entire life. Every password you reuse is a spare copy of your house key, quietly scattered across the internet — and somewhere out there, on a forum like InsidePro or a market like The Real Deal, is a list of every house it opens, just waiting for one of those houses to be robbed.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A company that did the responsible thing and hashed its passwords — and then chose a fingerprint function so fast, and skipped a defence so simple, that the one-way door it had built turned out to have a way straight through it. Six and a half million fingerprints on a Russian forum, ninety percent of them cracked in a weekend, not by reversing anything but by guessing at the speed of a graphics card and watching the matches pour in. A breach the world filed away as six and a half million that was, in truth, more than a hundred million — surfacing four years later from a seller called Peace, for the price of a laptop. Two million people guarding their professional lives with one two three four five six. And then the quiet, devastating spread — out through password reuse, into Gmail and PayPal and bank accounts on sites that were never breached at all, because the secret that leaked from LinkedIn was the same secret people used everywhere.

NARRATOR: The fingerprint was never supposed to be reversible. That was the whole promise, and the promise was real. But a one-way door you build fast, leave unsalted, and forget to make expensive is just an invitation written in a language the attacker happens to speak fluently. Build your systems as if someone, someday, will steal the whole database and have all the time in the world to guess. Because at LinkedIn, someone did. And it only cost them a weekend.
