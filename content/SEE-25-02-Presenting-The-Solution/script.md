NARRATOR: Last episode, twenty-five oh-one, building the solution. The hook was REFF — Risk reduction, Early feedback, Flexibility, Frequent value — the four reasons you build incrementally instead of big-bang. The quality pillars were S-R-I: Standards, Review, continuous Integration. The big idea: building "the right way" reuses the whole course — back-end flow from Programming for the Web, secure coding from Secure Software Architecture, code quality from Year eleven — and the SEE marker rules even at the keyboard: justify, manage, document, not just "it works."

NARRATOR: And reaching back to twenty-four oh-five, the communication episode, because today is where it pays off: I-F-N — Involve and empower the client, enable Feedback, Negotiate. Presenting your solution is the most concentrated act of "involve and empower" in the whole project — the moment you put working software in front of stakeholders and either earn their trust or lose it. Hold I-F-N; we'll use the "I" all episode.

NARRATOR: Today's outcome is S-E-twelve-oh-nine — applies methods to manage and document the development of a software project — the manage-and-document core. The syllabus dot-point is one line: present a software engineering solution using presentation software. It sounds soft, but the marker is sharp, so don't dismiss it as "make some slides." The home topic: how to present a software solution so an audience understands it, believes it works, and decides to use it.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll tailor the same solution to four different audiences; structure a presentation as a narrative arc; explain why the live demo is the most important part and how to demo for trust; handle questions without losing credibility; and answer "describe the features of an effective presentation of a software solution." Hear that question now, so you're listening for the answer.

NARRATOR: Core idea, the one students miss: a brilliant solution nobody understands is worthless. Presenting isn't about showing how clever you are. It's about making your audience confident your solution solves their problem. Everything flows from that reframing — they are the centre of every slide, not your code.

NARRATOR: First memorisable list: know your audience. One presentation won't work for everyone, and naming the four audience types — with what each cares about — is a clean four-mark answer. The hook is T-B-E-D, said "tee-bed," like the four beds you have to make. T, Technical stakeholders — developers, I-T staff — care about architecture, security, and maintenance: "How does authentication work? What database? How do we maintain it?" B, Business stakeholders — principals, managers — care about cost, efficiency, and return on investment: "How much? What's the payback?" E, End users — the people using it daily — care about ease of use and time saved: "Is this easier than what we have now?" D, Decision makers — boards, funding bodies — care about strategic fit, risk, and compliance: "Why this over alternatives? What are the risks?" T-B-E-D — Technical, Business, End users, Decision makers. Tailor the message to whichever bed you're making.

NARRATOR: The tailoring made concrete, for one attendance-tracking system. To the principal — business — "this cuts attendance processing by seventy percent and ensures compliance with departmental reporting." To I-T — technical — "built in Python with S-Q-L-ite, integrates via C-S-V export, no external dependencies, quarterly backups." To teachers — end users — "three taps instead of five minutes." To the board — decision makers — "saves fifteen thousand dollars a year, lifts data accuracy from eighty-seven to ninety-nine point five percent." Same solution, four angles, each audience hearing what matters to them. That's twenty-four oh-five's stakeholder thinking made operational.

QUESTION: Quick check. You built a homework-submission system. You're pitching it to the I-T manager who'll maintain it. What two things do you lead with, and what do you not waste their time on? Pause, decide, then play.

NARRATOR: You lead with what the technical audience cares about: how it runs and how it's maintained. "It runs on the existing school web server, uses S-Q-L-ite so there's no new database server, all student data stays on-premises, and maintenance is automated backups plus basic monitoring — I'll provide full documentation." What you don't waste their time on is the emotional "students will love it" pitch or the return-on-investment numbers — that's the end-user and business angle, the wrong bed. Matching content to audience is the whole skill, and the trap is one technical deck for everyone.

NARRATOR: Second memorisable list, the spine of the talk: the narrative arc. Don't open with your solution; open with the problem. Five beats, and the cleanest way to remember them is as a story: Problem, Solution, Demo, Benefits, Next steps. P, the Problem — two to three minutes, quantified. Bad opening: "Today I'll present my attendance system built in Python." Good opening: "Every morning teachers spend five minutes on paper rolls; across fifty teachers that's over twenty hours a week wasted." Make them feel the pain with real numbers. S, the Solution — three to five minutes, high-level: what is it, how does it work, what makes it different. D, the Demo — the biggest beat, we'll come to it. B, the Benefits — tie back to the problem with the same numbers: "those twenty hours a week become two." N, Next steps — timeline, cost, training, so nobody's left asking "what now?"

NARRATOR: Now the most important beat: the demo. Show, don't tell. In a twenty-minute presentation, the demo should get about half the time — that's where decisions are made. The key technique: structure the demo around user scenarios, not features. The weak demo says "first the database schema, then the authentication, then the validation code" — you lose the room instantly. The strong demo says "let's follow Sarah, a Year ten teacher, on Monday morning — she opens the app, sees her class, taps three names, done." Real workflow, realistic data, relatable. Hear the contrast: features-first is band four; a user-scenario walkthrough that proves the workflow is band six. This is "involve and empower" — you're showing the client their own world, improved.

NARRATOR: And demos fail, so plan for it — this is a maturity marker the HSC rewards. Have a backup: a second device, and crucially a pre-recorded walkthrough video. If the live demo breaks, don't spend five minutes fighting connectivity in front of stakeholders. Say "the server's not responding — let me show you the recorded walkthrough" and move on. Recovering smoothly builds more trust than a flawless demo, because it shows you've thought about risk — the same risk-mitigation instinct as a rollback plan in version control, which is our next episode.

NARRATOR: Handling questions and objections. Reframe it: questions aren't attacks, they're engagement — a good question means the audience is imagining using your thing. Answer technical questions directly and briefly, then offer documentation later rather than derailing. For the skeptical "will teachers actually use this?" — don't argue; show user-testing feedback or propose a pilot. And the most important rule: if you don't know, say so. "Great question, I don't have that data right now, but I'll research it and get back to you by Friday." Never make up an answer — a fabricated answer that's later exposed destroys the whole pitch, and your credibility is worth more than appearing to know everything. Honesty about limitations — "it doesn't support biometric login yet, that's a planned enhancement" — demonstrates maturity.

NARRATOR: Let me weave the rest of the course in. Forward: presenting is a rehearsal for your final evaluation report in twenty-six oh-three — both translate technical work into evidence and value for an audience, and both are graded on communication, not just the thing itself. Back: the slide-design principle — one idea per slide, visuals not walls of text — is the same usability thinking as Nielsen's heuristics in the prototype episode, twenty-five oh-six: reduce cognitive load, show the essential, cut the clutter. A slide with fifteen bullet points fails for the same reason a cluttered interface fails. And building credibility by showing your process — "I interviewed twelve teachers, I piloted with Year seven for two weeks" — is the evidence-based habit that runs through the whole evaluation half of the module.

NARRATOR: Now the worked example. The brief: a five-minute pitch of a canteen pre-ordering system to the principal and the canteen manager. The weak pitch opens "I built a web app in Flask with a S-Q-L database and a responsive front-end," lists features for four minutes, no demo, ends "any questions." Band four: tech-first, audience ignored, no narrative, no proof. The strong pitch opens with the problem, quantified — "four hundred students queue twelve minutes each at lunch; that's eighty student-hours a day lost, and some Year sevens never reach the counter." Then the solution in one sentence — "students pre-order on their phones, the canteen sees orders ahead of time." Then the demo as a scenario — "watch a student order in thirty seconds, then watch the canteen manager see the morning's orders in one screen." Then benefits in the same numbers — "queue time drops from twelve minutes to two." Then next steps — "pilot with Year seven next term, fifty dollars a month hosting." That structure — Problem, Solution, Demo, Benefits, Next steps, audience-tailored, demo-centred — is the band-six shape, and there's no code on a single slide because the audience is non-technical.

NARRATOR: Here they are. Audiences, T-B-E-D: Technical — architecture, security, maintenance; Business — cost, efficiency, return on investment; End users — ease of use, time saved; Decision makers — strategy, risk, compliance. The arc, in order: Problem — quantified, felt; Solution — high-level, what makes it different; Demo — the biggest beat, user scenarios not features; Benefits — tied back to the problem's numbers; Next steps — timeline, cost, training, a clear decision. If you got the four audiences and at least four of the five beats, you can answer the exam question cold.

NARRATOR: Lock in today. A presentation's job is to make the audience confident your solution solves their problem — they're the centre, not your code. Know your audience: T-B-E-D. Follow the arc: Problem, Solution, Demo, Benefits, Next steps. The demo is the most important part — user scenarios, not features, and have a backup. Handle questions as engagement, be honest about what you don't know, and build credibility with evidence and your process. The marker, S-E-twelve-oh-nine: this is managing and documenting the project's communication, not a soft afterthought. Traps: a tech-first demo with no narrative; one deck for every audience; jargon at non-technical stakeholders.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Identify four audiences for a software presentation and one thing each cares about. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: technical stakeholders care about architecture, security, and maintenance; business stakeholders care about cost and return on investment; end users care about ease of use and time saved; and decision makers care about strategic fit, risk, and compliance. Naming all four — Technical, Business, End users, Decision makers, the T-B-E-D set — each with a distinct concern, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, recall. Outline the structure of an effective software solution presentation. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: an effective presentation follows a narrative arc. It opens with the problem, quantified so the audience feels its impact; introduces the solution at a high level, including what makes it different; gives a live demonstration structured around realistic user scenarios — the most important and longest part — to prove it works; states the benefits, tied back to the original problem using the same metrics; and closes with clear next steps such as timeline, cost, and training, so the audience can decide. Naming the five beats in order — Problem, Solution, Demo, Benefits, Next steps — and noting the demo is central is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three, the harder one. Describe the features of an effective presentation of a software solution. Pause, plan a structured answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: an effective presentation is, first, audience-tailored — the same solution is framed for technical, business, end-user, or decision-maker concerns rather than delivered as one generic deck. Second, it follows a clear narrative arc — problem, solution, demo, benefits, next steps — opening with a quantified problem rather than the technology. Third, it is demonstration-centred: a live, scenario-based demo proves the solution works in real workflows, and the presenter prepares a backup such as a recorded walkthrough in case the demo fails. Fourth, it uses clean visual design — one idea per slide, visuals over walls of text — to reduce cognitive load. Fifth, it handles questions honestly, treating them as engagement, providing evidence such as pilot data, and admitting when an answer isn't known rather than fabricating one. The full-mark answer names several distinct features and explains why each builds the audience's confidence and the presenter's credibility.

QUESTION: Question four, application. A developer's live demo crashes in front of the school board. Explain what they should do and why. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the developer should acknowledge the failure briefly and immediately switch to a prepared backup — a pre-recorded walkthrough video or a backup device — rather than spending minutes fixing it live, and should not panic or apologise at length. This matters because dwelling on a technical failure erodes confidence and burns limited time, whereas a calm, prepared recovery demonstrates professionalism, risk awareness, and respect for the audience's time, which can actually increase trust. Having planned for the demo to fail is itself evidence of the mature, risk-aware mindset decision makers want before committing. The mark is for the smooth switch to a planned backup and the justification in terms of credibility and risk mitigation.

NARRATOR: That's presenting the solution. Make the audience the centre, know which of T-B-E-D you're addressing, run the Problem-Solution-Demo-Benefits-Next-steps arc, and let the scenario-based demo carry the trust.
