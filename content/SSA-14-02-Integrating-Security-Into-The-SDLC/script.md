NARRATOR: Recap first. Last episode opened Secure Software Architecture with the business case. The two benefits of secure software are D-A — protect Data, prevent Attacks. Security is a business *enabler*, not just a cost: it builds trust, ensures compliance, differentiates the product, and manages risk. You justify investment with a cost-benefit argument — prevention is far cheaper than response, because a breach carries huge *indirect* costs like customer churn and reputation damage, not just fines. And you prioritise with risk equals likelihood times impact. Hold "prevention is far cheaper than response" tight, because today's whole episode is the practical expression of it.

NARRATOR: The bridge. Last episode argued *that* you should invest in security. Today answers *when* and *where* in the process — and the answer is everywhere, from the very start, not bolted on at the end. Today's dot-point: interpret and apply the fundamental software development steps to develop *secure* code — requirements definition, determining specifications, design, development, integration, testing and debugging, installation, and maintenance. The home topic is security woven through every phase of the software development life cycle, the S-D-L-C.

NARRATOR: Open with a backward interleave to Year 11. You already know these eight development steps — they're the standard software development life cycle from the programming-fundamentals course. Nothing about the *steps* is new. What's new is the *lens*: at each step you now ask "what's the security activity here?" This episode isn't teaching you the S-D-L-C — you have it — it's teaching you to run security through it. That reframing is the exam skill.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front: name the eight S-D-L-C steps and a security activity at each; explain "shift left" and why early security is cheaper; and apply threat modelling. At speed: the eight steps with security, shift-left, and threat modelling. The headline exam question is "explain how security is addressed across the software development life cycle", worth around six marks — so you march through the phases.

NARRATOR: First, the eight steps — you must be able to list them — with a hook for the order: "Really Smart Developers Don't Ignore Testing In Maintenance." Really — Requirements definition. Smart — Specifications. Developers — Design. Don't — Development. Ignore — Integration. Testing — Testing and debugging. In — Installation. Maintenance — Maintenance. Really Smart Developers Don't Ignore Testing In Maintenance — eight words, eight steps, in order. Now the idea that turns Year 11 recall into a Secure Software Architecture lesson: security-by-design. Security-by-design means security is considered at *every* one of those phases, built into the foundations — the opposite of bolting it on at the end.

QUESTION: Pause the player. Why is it so much more expensive to fix a security flaw after the software is built and deployed than to design it out at the start? Work out the reasoning, then play.

NARRATOR: Because in production, fixing it means emergency patches, possible downtime, re-testing everything it touches, and potentially a breach response if attackers found it first — whereas at the design stage it's just a decision on a whiteboard. The textbook puts a number on it: fixing a flaw in production costs roughly sixty-three times what it costs to prevent it at the design phase. That's the same "prevention is far cheaper than response" idea from last episode, now *inside* the development process. And it has a name to use: "shift left." Picture the S-D-L-C as a timeline running left to right, requirements on the left, maintenance on the right. Shifting left means moving security effort *earlier* — to the left — where flaws are cheap to fix. The mark-earning line: addressing security early in the life cycle — shifting left — is far cheaper than fixing vulnerabilities after deployment, because early fixes are design decisions while late fixes require emergency patches, re-testing, and incident response. Shift left: fix it early, fix it cheap.

NARRATOR: That gives the first named trap: the "bolt-on" approach — treating security as something you add after the application is built. It's expensive, ineffective, and often breaks existing functionality, because you're retrofitting protections the design never anticipated. "Bolt security on at the end" is the wrong answer the exam wants you to reject. Security-by-design is the right one.

NARRATOR: Now walk security through the eight phases — narrating that *is* the six-mark answer. I'll anchor it on one feature, a user login, so it's concrete. At Requirements: identify the security needs — confidentiality of credentials, who must be authenticated, what must be logged. At Specifications: write those as testable requirements — "passwords stored hashed", "lock account after five failed attempts". At Design: choose the security controls and map the trust boundaries — multi-factor authentication, role-based access, where data crosses from untrusted to trusted. This is also where threat modelling happens. At Development: apply secure coding — hash the password properly, use parameterised queries, validate input — the techniques from the rest of this module. At Integration: make sure the security controls still work when components are joined — the login talks securely to the database and the session store. At Testing and debugging: actively test the security — try to bypass the login, attempt injection, check authorisation. At Installation: harden the deployment — enforce H-T-T-P-S, lock down configuration, restrict database access. And at Maintenance: keep it secure over time — patch vulnerabilities, monitor for incidents, update dependencies. Eight phases, a security activity at each — that's the answer, and the login thread makes it concrete.

NARRATOR: Two of those phases deserve interleaving. First, Maintenance — patching and dependencies — because a story lives exactly there. In twenty-twenty-four, an attacker spent two years patiently earning the trust of the volunteer maintainer of a small compression library called xz, became a co-maintainer, and slipped a hidden backdoor into it — a library that flows, through dependencies, into the secure-shell logins of millions of Linux servers. It was caught by sheer luck, days before shipping widely. That's the *maintenance and dependencies* phase as an attack surface — the software supply chain we flagged in Programming for the Web with open source. We tell it in full as a case study; for now, file it under "maintenance security is real, and it includes the code you didn't write." Second, Testing — the security testing toolbox is a whole episode later, eighteen-oh-one. The named trap there: do *not* equate testing with security. Testing is *one* phase. If your entire security strategy is "we'll test it at the end", you've missed the other seven — that's the bolt-on trap wearing a lab coat. Security spans the whole life cycle; testing is one slice.

QUESTION: How do you actually figure out what threats to design against, at the requirements and design stage? It feels open-ended.

NARRATOR: With threat modelling — a structured way to identify what could go wrong, done early when fixes are cheapest. The process: model your system, identify the threats, assess each by risk — likelihood times impact, from last episode — and define countermeasures. To make "identify the threats" systematic rather than a guessing game, there's a framework whose name is its own mnemonic: STRIDE. Six threat categories. S — Spoofing: pretending to be someone else. T — Tampering: unauthorised modification of data. R — Repudiation: denying you did something you did. I — Information disclosure: leaking data to people who shouldn't see it. D — Denial of service: making the system unavailable. E — Elevation of privilege: gaining more access than you should. STRIDE — Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege. For each component, you ask "how could each of these six happen here?", and you've systematically surfaced your threats. There's a STRIDE threat-model table for a login in Listing 1. The mark-earning point: threat modelling, using a framework like STRIDE, identifies potential attacks during design — when they're cheapest to fix — rather than discovering them in production.

NARRATOR: The worked example contrast, because "explain how security is addressed across the S-D-L-C" rewards structure. Weak answer: "you make sure the code is secure and you test it for bugs before release." That touches maybe two phases vaguely — low marks. This security-by-design or shift-left approach is far cheaper than fixing flaws after deployment." Hear the difference — the strong answer marches the phases, names an activity in each, and lands the shift-left principle. That's the six-marker.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — five questions, recall to extended. Pause and attempt each, then the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. What does "security-by-design" or "shift left" mean? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: security-by-design, or shifting left, means integrating security considerations into every phase of the software development life cycle from the very start — beginning at requirements and design — rather than adding security after the software is built. This makes systems inherently more secure and is far cheaper than fixing flaws later. Naming "every phase, from the start, not bolted on" is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two. Name three software development life cycle phases and a security activity for each. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: at requirements, gather security requirements such as confidentiality and authentication needs; at design, perform threat modelling and select security controls like access control and encryption; at testing, conduct security testing such as attempting injection and verifying authorisation. Other valid pairs include secure coding at development and hardening plus patching at installation and maintenance. A phase with a matching activity, three times, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Why is it cheaper to fix a security flaw during design than in production? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: at the design stage a flaw is corrected by changing a decision before any code depends on it, whereas in production it requires emergency patches, system downtime, re-testing of everything affected, and possibly breach response if attackers have already exploited it — costing roughly sixty-three times more. This is why security is shifted left to early phases. Naming the production overhead and the cost multiplier is the mark.

QUESTION: Question four. What is threat modelling, and what does the STRIDE framework provide? Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: threat modelling is a structured process of identifying potential attacks early in development by modelling the system, identifying threats, assessing their risk, and defining countermeasures. STRIDE provides six categories to systematically identify threats — Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, and Elevation of privilege — so that threats are surfaced comprehensively rather than guessed at. Naming the process and the six STRIDE categories is the mark.

QUESTION: Question five, the extended one. Explain how security is addressed across the software development life cycle. Pause, plan a structured response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: security is integrated into every phase of the life cycle under a security-by-design approach. At requirements definition, security needs such as confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and compliance are identified. At specifications, these are written as testable requirements. At design, threat modelling using a framework like STRIDE identifies potential attacks, trust boundaries are mapped, and security controls such as authentication, access control, and encryption are selected. During development and integration, secure coding practices are applied — input validation, parameterised queries, secure password hashing — and code is peer-reviewed and statically analysed. At testing and debugging, the application is actively security-tested, including attempts at injection, authentication bypass, and unauthorised access. At installation, the deployment is hardened — enforcing H-T-T-P-S, restricting access, and configuring security headers. At maintenance, vulnerabilities are patched, dependencies monitored, and incidents responded to. Addressing security at every phase — shifting left — is far cheaper and more effective than bolting security on at the end, since fixing a flaw in production can cost over sixty times its prevention cost. That response marches all phases with concrete activities and lands the shift-left principle — a full-mark answer.
