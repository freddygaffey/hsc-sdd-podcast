NARRATOR: Imagine a filing cabinet. An enormous one. Tall as a building, stretching off into the dark further than you can see, drawer after drawer after drawer, ten million of them, and every single drawer holds a person. Not a file. A person. Their full legal name. The day they were born. The street they live on, the unit number, the suburb. Their phone, their email. And in a great many of those drawers, tucked behind the everyday details, the documents that prove who they are to the entire world — the number on their passport, the number on their driver's licence, the number on their Medicare card. Now imagine that this cabinet has no lock. Not a broken lock. Not a weak lock. No lock at all. The drawers aren't even labelled with names — they're numbered, one, two, three, four, counting up in a tidy line. And the cabinet is sitting on the footpath, out in the open, facing the street, where anyone walking past can stop, pull open drawer number one, read everything inside, slide it shut, and pull open drawer number two. And it has been sitting there, like that, for months. And nobody has noticed.

NARRATOR: That filing cabinet is real. It belonged to one of the largest companies in Australia. And in September of twenty twenty-two, somebody walked past it, tried a drawer, and realised what they were looking at.

NARRATOR: This is the story of the Optus breach. By the numbers it is one of the largest data breaches in Australian history — the personal information of roughly nine point eight million people, current and former customers of the telecommunications giant Optus. That is close to one in every three people in the entire country. Stop and hold that. Not one in three users of some niche app. One in three Australians. And what makes this story worth telling is not that the company was clever or the attacker was a genius. It is the exact opposite. There was no genius here. There was no master criminal with custom tools and unknown exploits. There was a single person, by every account since, poking at the edges of a giant company's systems — and finding a mistake so basic, so fundamental, that a competent first-year programming student would be told never, ever to make it. To understand this breach, you do not need to understand hacking. You need a door, a key, and a list of numbers.

QUESTION: When you open the app for your phone company and ask to see your own account, how does the system actually know that the details it's about to show you are yours — and not the details of a complete stranger?

NARRATOR: Hold that question, because it is the entire story, and we are going to come back to it. To answer it, you first have to understand how a company like Optus is actually built underneath, because it is not what most people picture. Most people imagine one big program — the Optus program — sitting on one big computer. That is not how it works, and it hasn't been for a long time. A modern company runs its systems as dozens, often hundreds, of small separate services, each doing one job, all talking to one another constantly over the network behind the scenes. The app on your phone holds almost nothing. It is a thin shell. When you open it and tap to see your account, the app does not look up your data itself — it can't, the data isn't on your phone. Instead, it reaches out across the internet to a server and asks for it.

NARRATOR: And the way it asks is through something called an A-P-I — an application programming interface. That phrase sounds technical, so let me give you the picture I want you to carry through the whole story. An A-P-I is a service window. Picture the drive-through window at a fast-food place. You pull up in your car, you say what you want through the window, and the window hands it out to you. That's it. That's an A-P-I. The Optus app pulls up to one of these service windows and says, in effect, "give me the account details for customer such-and-such," and the window passes the details back. Every app you have ever used works like this. When your banking app shows your balance, when your maps app loads a street, when your messaging app fetches your texts — somewhere, an app is pulling up to a service window and asking, and a window is handing something out. This is completely normal. This is the architecture of the modern internet. The A-P-I is the front counter of almost everything.

NARRATOR: Now. Here is what is supposed to happen at that window, every single time it is asked for something. Before it hands anything out, it runs two checks. The first check is: who are you? Prove it. You don't get served just because you pulled up — you have to show that you are a real, logged-in user and not a random stranger who wandered off the street. That check has a name, and it is a name the marker wants you to know precisely: authentication. Authentication is proving identity. It's the login, the password, the token your app carries that says, "yes, this request is genuinely coming from this account holder." The second check happens only after the first one passes, and it is: all right, you are who you say you are — but are you allowed to have this particular thing? That check also has a precise name: authorisation. Authorisation is checking permissions. Even once the window knows for certain that you are customer A, it must still refuse to hand you the records of customer B. Authentication asks who are you. Authorisation asks are you allowed. Two different questions, two different checks, and both of them are supposed to run on every single request that comes to the window.

NARRATOR: At Optus, on one particular service window, neither check was running. Not the first. Not the second. Neither.

NARRATOR: I want you to really sit inside that sentence, because the rest of the disaster falls out of it like dominoes. The reporting afterwards — and the best of it came from an Australian security journalist named Jeremy Kirk, who actually got the person claiming to be the attacker to talk — described an A-P-I endpoint, one of these service windows, that was reachable from the open public internet, and that asked the visitor for nothing. No login. No password. No token. No account. No proof of any kind. You did not have to be an Optus customer. You did not have to be a person at all — a script would do fine. You simply walked up to the window, asked for a customer's details, and the window answered. The attacker later put it almost exactly that way: the data, they said, was all open on the internet for anyone to use.

NARRATOR: Security professionals have a blunt, official name for this, and it is worth knowing because the syllabus and the industry both use it: a broken access control. Access control is the whole machinery that stands at the door and decides who is allowed in and what they're allowed to touch. Here, that machinery was not weak, and it was not bypassed. It was simply absent. The guard who was meant to stand at the window had never been posted. The door, to go back to our filing cabinet, had no lock fitted at all.

NARRATOR: But an open door, on its own, is only half of a catastrophe. An unlocked drawer is a problem for one person. What turns the leak of one record into the leak of millions is the second half of the story — and it is just as simple, and just as avoidable, as the first.

NARRATOR: To ask the service window for a customer's details, you obviously had to tell it which customer you wanted. You couldn't just say "give me a customer" — you had to point at one. And you pointed by giving the window an identifier. A number. In the leaked data this field had a name — it was called the contact i-d. Every customer had one. And here is the fatal detail: those numbers were not random. They were not long, scrambled, unguessable strings. They were sequential. They counted up by one. Customer number, then the next number, then the next, in a neat, orderly, completely predictable line. One, two, three, four, five.

QUESTION: So picture it. The window hands a customer's full record to anyone who asks, no questions, and the customers are numbered one, two, three, four in order. If you were standing in front of that window, what is the very first thing you would try?

NARRATOR: You'd ask for number one. Then number two. And when both of them came back — loaded with a real person's name, their date of birth, their home address, their email, their phone number, and for a great many of them a passport or licence or Medicare number sitting right there in the response — you would realise, in that instant, that you do not have to guess anything. You don't have to be clever. You don't have to break a thing. You can just count. Start at one. Go up by one. Forever. Or until you run out of customers.

NARRATOR: This pattern, this exact failure, has a formal name in security, and it is one I genuinely want you to be able to say out loud because it sounds intimidating and is in fact dead simple. It is called an insecure direct object reference. People shorten it to I-DOR, said "eye-door." Let me break the phrase apart so it never confuses you. "Direct object reference" means the system lets you point straight at a record using its raw, real identifier — its actual internal number — rather than something safe and meaningless. "Insecure" means the system then fails to check whether you, the person asking, are actually allowed to point at that particular record. Put it together: you change the number in your request, and you get a different person's data. Change it again, get another person. It is the precise digital equivalent of that filing cabinet where every drawer is unlocked and the drawers are numbered in order. You don't even have to hunt for the one you want. You just walk down the row, pulling drawers, one after another.

NARRATOR: And that is exactly, mechanically, what happened. The attacker did not sit there typing numbers in by hand like some hacker in a film. Why would you? You write a tiny program — a loop, the most ordinary thing in all of programming. Set a counter to one. Ask the window for that customer. Save whatever comes back. Add one to the counter. Ask again. Save. Add one. Ask again. A machine running that loop can do it thousands of times a minute, tirelessly, marching straight up the entire line of every customer the company had ever had. And nothing — nothing — stopped it. There was no login to defeat, because there was no login. There was no rate limit — no safeguard that says "hang on, one single source just asked for ten thousand different customer records in five minutes, that is obviously not a human being, slam the window shut." There was no alarm wired to go off when the records started pouring out the window faster than any legitimate use could ever explain. The loop simply ran. And the records simply came. Roughly nine point eight million of them, drawer after drawer after drawer, counted out of an open window that asked nobody anything.

NARRATOR: Now I need you to think hard about what was actually in those drawers, because this is the part that lifts the Optus breach out of the ordinary and into something genuinely frightening. When a website leaks a list of passwords, it's bad, but there's a floor under how bad it gets, because you can change a password. You pick a new one, the old one is worthless, you move on with your day. The data that came out of Optus had no such floor. Names. Dates of birth. Home addresses. Phone numbers. Email addresses. And for a very large slice of those customers — the company eventually confirmed around two point one million people — at least one piece of government-issued identity documentation. Around a hundred and fifty thousand passport numbers. Tens of thousands of Medicare numbers — there was even a public squabble over the exact count, the government putting it near thirty-seven thousand, Optus putting the valid figure lower, but tens of thousands either way. And driver's licence numbers running into the millions. Of those two point one million people with ID numbers exposed, the company said around one point two million had at least one current, valid identity number taken, and the other nine hundred thousand had numbers from documents that had since expired.

NARRATOR: Here is why that specific combination is so dangerous, and it comes back to a single idea: you cannot change these things. You cannot change the day you were born. You cannot, with a click, re-roll your passport number because a phone company misplaced it — getting a new passport is slow, it costs money, and the process is genuinely infuriating. And these particular pieces of information, taken together — your name, your birthday, your address, the number off a government ID — are the exact ingredients somebody needs to pretend to be you. To open an account in your name. To pass the identity questions a bank or a government office asks when they want to be sure they're talking to the real you. To commit the kind of identity fraud that doesn't end when the news cycle ends — that can follow a person around for years, surfacing again and again every time their stolen details get bought and sold and reused. This is precisely why the law treats this category of data — sensitive personal information — as deserving the highest level of protection. Losing it is not embarrassing. It is dangerous, to real people, for a long time.

NARRATOR: And then came the move that turned a serious data breach into a genuine national emergency, the thing that put it on the front page of every paper in the country.

NARRATOR: The attacker, now sitting on this colossal haul of nine point eight million people's lives, went to an online criminal forum — a place called BreachForums, where stolen data gets traded — and posted under the handle "optus data." To prove they were holding the real thing and not bluffing, they published a sample: a batch of genuine customer records, around ten thousand of them, real names and real addresses and real ID numbers belonging to real Australians, dumped into the open for anyone to grab. And then they made their demand. Pay a ransom — about one million United States dollars, set in the cryptocurrency Monero, which is favoured by criminals precisely because it is hard to trace — or the entire trove of nine point eight million records goes public. A countdown, in effect, on the identities of a third of the country.

NARRATOR: For several days the whole nation held its breath, because it genuinely looked as though every one of those records might be tipped out onto the open internet for every scammer on Earth to scoop up. And then something almost stranger happened. The attacker got scared. The attention had become enormous — wall-to-wall media, the federal government furious and public, and the Australian Federal Police now very much involved and very much hunting. And whoever was behind "optus data" apparently realised they had grabbed something far hotter than they could handle. They deleted their posts. They claimed they had destroyed the only copy of the data. And, extraordinarily, they posted an apology — to Optus, and to the Australians whose records they'd taken, saying it had been a mistake. But the apology changed nothing about the fundamental reality. A sample of thousands of real people's records, complete with Medicare numbers, had already been released into the wild. And data does not come back. You cannot un-leak it. Once it's out, it is out forever, copied and re-copied across machines you will never find. The genie does not go back into the bottle, no matter how sorry anyone is.

QUESTION: So how did a service window that handed out a third of the country's personal data end up sitting on the open internet, unlocked, in the first place? Surely nobody decided to build it that way on purpose?

NARRATOR: No. And the answer is almost more unsettling than if someone had. Because it points at how these disasters really happen — not through a single villainous decision, but through ordinary, well-meaning steps that quietly add up to catastrophe. The reporting, again largely from Jeremy Kirk's investigation, painted a picture not of a sophisticated attack on a hardened system, but of human error and misconfiguration. The endpoint appears to have lived on a domain — a web address — that was, by the analysis of public records, reachable from the internet for months. There is a well-known and very human pattern behind this kind of mistake, and it goes like this. When developers build an A-P-I, the security controls — the authentication, the authorisation — are very often the last thing bolted on. The reason is practical and almost sympathetic: while you are building and testing, security gets in your way. If something is functionally broken, it is far easier to find the bug with all the guards switched off, so you can see clearly what the window is doing. So you build it open, you test it open, and you intend — you fully intend — to lock it all down before it ever touches the real world.

NARRATOR: But there are two iron rules that are supposed to make that safe, and the lesson of Optus is what happens when they slip. The first rule: you test against a dummy database. Fake people. Mock data. Made-up names and made-up numbers that, if they leaked, would harm precisely nobody. You never, ever point a half-finished, unsecured window at the real production database full of real customers. The second rule: before anything faces the public internet, the security goes back on, and somebody checks that it did. The Optus failure is the story of those rules failing together — an endpoint without its access controls, somehow reachable from the open internet, somehow connected to live customer data, somehow left that way, somehow unnoticed, for long enough that someone strolling past could find it and count their way through ten million lives. There was no master plan. There was a window that got built open, the way windows often are, and then nobody closed it. And the records behind it were real.

NARRATOR: I want to be absolutely clear about this, because it is the spine of why this story gets taught in classrooms rather than just gossiped about over coffee. Nothing the attacker did was sophisticated. Let me list what they did not do, because the list is the lesson. They did not break any encryption. They did not crack a single password — there was no password to crack. They did not phish an employee, did not trick anyone into clicking anything, did not sneak a piece of malware onto any server, did not exploit some rare unknown flaw hoarded by a government. They found a window that asked them nothing and answered everything, in front of records that were numbered in a tidy, countable row — and they counted. The hard part of an attack, the part that is supposed to be hard, the part our entire security industry exists to make hard — had already been done for them. Not by the attacker. By the people who built and ran that window. The breach didn't happen because the attacker was strong. It happened because the door was open.

NARRATOR: So let me pull out what this story is really teaching — because the Optus breach is, in a way, the purest, cleanest worked example of secure design failure you will ever come across, and it leaves you with a small handful of ideas you should carry into every system you ever build.

NARRATOR: The deepest one is about doors — about how many of them there really are. Every one of those service windows, every A-P-I endpoint a system exposes to the world, is a door to your data. A real, full-sized, dangerous door. And the single rule that this entire nine-point-eight-million-person disaster comes down to is this: the lock has to be on every single door. Not most of them. Not the front one everybody can see. Not just the ones that feel important. Every one. And here is why that is so much harder than it sounds, and why even good engineers get it wrong. When you build a system out of dozens or hundreds of separate little services, it is genuinely easy to secure the front entrance — the login page, the main app, the obvious door that everyone uses and everyone is watching — and then leave some side window unlocked. Some endpoint that sounds internal. Some service that was "only ever meant for testing." Some address that you assumed nobody would ever find because you never published it anywhere. And that is precisely the one the attacker comes through. They do not come through the front door you spent all your effort guarding. They come through the one you forgot you had. The professional name for the right mindset here is secure A-P-I design, and at its heart it means a single ruthless assumption: assume every endpoint you expose will be found, and will be probed, by someone hostile — and therefore put authentication and authorisation on each one, by default, as the standard, rather than hoping nobody stumbles onto the one you left open.

NARRATOR: And keep those two checks crisp in your head, because losing the difference between them is the exact mistake at the centre of this. Authentication: who are you. Authorisation: are you allowed to have this specific thing. The Optus window failed the first one completely — it never even asked who was knocking. But I want you to understand that even a system which does ask who you are can still be destroyed by the second failure. Picture a different version of Optus, a better one, where the window does demand a login. You can only reach it if you're a genuine, authenticated customer. Sounds safe. But if that window still lets you ask for any contact i-d you like, and still doesn't check that the record you asked for actually belongs to you — then a logged-in customer A can simply change the number in their own perfectly authenticated request and pull back customer B's record. That is the insecure direct object reference all over again, and it survives authentication. The fix is that whenever a system lets you reach a record by its raw identifier, something must verify that the record actually belongs to the person asking. Skip that check, make your identifiers neat and sequential and guessable, and you have built a filing cabinet that anyone with an account can read drawer by drawer.

NARRATOR: There's a smaller, sharper lesson tucked in here too, and it's about those identifiers. Why were the records numbered one, two, three? Because sequential numbers are the easy, natural thing to reach for when you're building a database — the system just hands out the next number each time. But predictable identifiers are an invitation. If the attacker can't guess the next valid identifier, enumeration — that march up the line, counting through every record — becomes vastly harder. This is why serious systems hand out long, random, unguessable identifiers instead of one, two, three. It is not the lock — the access control is still the lock, and it is still the thing that actually mattered most here — but unpredictable identifiers are a second layer, and a system worth trusting has more than one layer. Defence in depth: you assume any single protection can fail, so you stack them, so that when one slips, another is still standing. At Optus, there was no lock and the numbers counted up in a line. Every layer that could have failed, did.

NARRATOR: And then there is the deepest point of all, underneath the technical one, about what that data actually was. This was not game scores. It was not browsing history or marketing preferences. It was people's identities — the irreplaceable, unchangeable, deeply sensitive documents that, once they're loose, can never be recalled, never reset, never made safe again. And that changes the nature of the responsibility. Building a system that holds that kind of information is not merely a technical task with a technical failure mode. It carries a duty of care to the human beings whose lives are sitting in your database — people who never read your code, never saw your architecture, and simply trusted, by handing over their passport number to sign up for a phone plan, that you would keep it safe.

NARRATOR: Australia absorbed that lesson loudly, and at every level. There were consequences for individual people: the breach is woven into the story of the chief executive, Kelly Bayer Rosmarin, who had led Optus since twenty twenty, and who resigned in November of twenty twenty-three, in the wake of the breach and a separate, catastrophic network outage that knocked out service for around ten million customers — unable, in some cases, to even call emergency services. There were consequences for the company: Optus set aside on the order of a hundred and forty million dollars to deal with the fallout — replacing passports, paying for credit-and-identity monitoring for affected customers, commissioning external reviews. And there were consequences in law. The nation's privacy regulator, the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner, took Optus to the Federal Court, alleging that for years — from around twenty nineteen up to the breach — the company had seriously interfered with the privacy of roughly nine and a half million Australians by failing to take reasonable steps to protect their personal information. And the penalties on the table, the maximums the court could impose, were counted not as one fine for one breach, but potentially per person affected — which, multiplied across millions, is a number large enough to genuinely threaten a company's existence. The breach also became a catalyst for sharpening Australia's privacy laws more broadly, lifting the maximum penalties dramatically, so that losing millions of people's identities can never again be quietly written off as a minor cost of doing business. The privacy of personal data stopped being an afterthought on a compliance checklist and became, very publicly and very expensively, the company's legal problem.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in a single breath. One person. No special tools, no genius, no exotic flaw. A service window facing the open internet that never once asked who was knocking — authentication, simply missing. Behind it, the records of nine point eight million people, numbered one, two, three, four, in a neat predictable row, with no check that the person asking for a record had any right to it — authorisation, simply missing. A short, dull, ordinary loop, counting up the line, with no rate limit and no alarm to stop it. And out of that open window poured a third of a nation's names, birthdays, home addresses, and the identity documents they can never simply replace — passports, licences, Medicare numbers — into the hands of someone who then tried to ransom the country with it. The attacker did not pick the lock. There was no lock to pick. They did not defeat the guard. There was no guard. They just counted.

NARRATOR: And that, in the end, is the thing I most want you to carry away from this story — more than any clever exploit ever could teach you. We tend to imagine that the worst breaches come from the most brilliant attackers, that catastrophe requires genius on the other side. The Optus breach is the permanent, ten-million-drawer reminder that it does not. The most dangerous vulnerability in a system is almost never the exotic one. It is the obvious check that was supposed to be standing at every door — the simple question, who are you, and are you allowed — and that, on one forgotten window, somebody quietly never put there at all. Build your systems as though every door will be tried. Because at Optus, one was. And it counted all the way to ten million.
