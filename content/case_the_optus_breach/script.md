NARRATOR: In September twenty twenty-two, somebody on the internet went looking for a way into one of the biggest companies in Australia. Not a government. Not a team of elite hackers with custom tools and zero-day exploits. By every account since, this was one person, poking around at the edges of a telecommunications giant called Optus — a company that, between its mobile plans, its broadband, and its business contracts, holds the personal details of roughly ten million Australians. That's close to one in every two adults in the country. And what this person found was not a clever flaw buried deep in the system. They found a door — an ordinary door, sitting out in the open, facing the public internet. And the door was unlocked. It had been unlocked for months.

NARRATOR: This is the story of the Optus breach. It matters because of how big it was — the personal data of about nine point eight million people, including passport numbers, driver's licence numbers, and Medicare numbers, the kind of identity documents you can't just cancel and reissue like a credit card. But it matters even more because of how it happened. No genius. No master criminal. Just a piece of software doing exactly what a competent first-year student would tell you never to build. To understand it, you don't need to understand hacking. You need a door, a key, and a list of numbers.

QUESTION: When you log into your phone account and ask to see your own details, how does the system know that those details are yours to see, and not your neighbour's?

NARRATOR: Hold onto that question, because it is the whole story. A modern company like Optus doesn't have one giant program. It has hundreds of little services that talk to each other over the internet, behind the scenes. The app on your phone doesn't hold your data; it asks a server for it. The way it asks is through something called an A-P-I — an application programming interface. Think of an A-P-I as a service window, like the window at a fast-food drive-through. You pull up, you say what you want, and the window hands it out. The phone app pulls up to one of these windows and says, in effect, "give me the account details for customer number such-and-such," and the window hands them back. That's normal. That's how every app you've ever used works.

NARRATOR: Now, here's what should happen at that window, every single time. Before it hands anything out, it checks two things. First: who are you? Prove it. That's authentication — proving your identity, usually with a login or a token, something that says "yes, this request really is coming from a logged-in user, not a random stranger." Second, once it knows who you are: are you allowed to have this particular thing? That's authorisation — checking your permissions. Even if you've proven you're customer A, the window must refuse to hand you the details of customer B. Authentication asks "who are you?" Authorisation asks "are you allowed?" Two different checks, both essential, both meant to run on every request.

NARRATOR: At Optus, on this particular service window, neither check was running.

NARRATOR: Let that sink in. The reporting afterwards described an A-P-I endpoint — one of these service windows — reachable from the open internet, that asked the visitor for no proof of identity. No login. No password, no token, no account, nothing. You just walked up to the window and asked, and it answered. Security people have a blunt name for this: a broken access control. The control that's supposed to stand at the door, checking who comes and goes, was simply not there. The door had no lock at all.

NARRATOR: But an open door, by itself, is only half the disaster. Here's the second half — the part that turns a leak of one record into a leak of millions. To ask the window for a customer's details, you had to tell it which customer you wanted. And you said that with an identifier — a number. Customer number one. Customer number two. And those numbers, it turned out, were not random. They were sequential. They went up by one. One, two, three, four, five, in a neat, predictable line.

QUESTION: So if the window hands out any customer's record to anyone who asks, and the customers are numbered one, two, three, four — what's the very first thing a curious person tries?

NARRATOR: They try number one. Then number two. And when both come back loaded with somebody's real name, date of birth, home address, email, phone number, and in many cases their passport or licence or Medicare number — they realise they don't have to guess at all. They can just count. This pattern has a formal name in security, worth knowing precisely because it sounds technical and is so simple. It's called an insecure direct object reference — often shortened to I-DOR. "Direct object reference" means the system lets you point straight at a record using its raw identifier, its number. "Insecure" means it doesn't check whether you're allowed to point at that one. So you change the number and get a different person's record. Change it again, get another. It's the digital equivalent of a filing cabinet where every drawer is unlocked and the drawers are numbered in order — you don't even have to hunt. You pull them open, one after another, down the row.

NARRATOR: And that's exactly what happened. The attacker didn't sit there typing numbers by hand. You write a tiny script — a loop. Start the counter at one. Ask the window for that customer. Save what comes back. Add one. Ask again. Over and over, a machine doing it thousands of times a minute, marching straight up the line of every customer the company had. Nothing stopped it. No login to defeat, no rate limit slamming the window shut after too many requests, no alarm going off because one address was pulling down records faster than any human possibly could. The loop just ran, and the records poured out. Roughly nine point eight million of them.

NARRATOR: Think about what was in those records, because this is what made it so much worse than a stolen password list. Names. Dates of birth. Home addresses. Phone numbers and email addresses. And for a large slice — reportedly more than two million — at least one piece of government identity document. Around a hundred and fifty thousand passport numbers. Tens of thousands of Medicare numbers. Driver's licence numbers in the millions. When a password leaks, you change it and move on. But you can't change your date of birth. You can't re-roll your passport number on a whim — getting a new passport because a company lost yours is slow, expensive, and infuriating. These are the exact pieces of information someone needs to impersonate you: to open accounts in your name, to pass the security questions a bank asks, to commit identity fraud that follows you around for years. That's why this kind of data is treated, under the law, as so sensitive. It isn't just embarrassing to lose. It's dangerous.

NARRATOR: Then came the part that made it a national story. The attacker, sitting on this enormous haul, went to an online forum, posted a sample as proof, and demanded a ransom — pay up, in cryptocurrency, or the rest goes public. For a tense few days it looked like all nine point eight million records might be dumped onto the open internet for any scammer in the world to grab. Then, abruptly, the person got cold feet — there was a great deal of attention by then, including from law enforcement — deleted their posts, claimed they'd destroyed the data, and apologised. But the damage was done. A sample of real customers' records, including Medicare numbers, had already been released. The genie does not go back in the bottle.

NARRATOR: Now I want to be very clear, because it's the heart of why this story is taught and not just gossiped about. Nothing the attacker did was sophisticated. They did not break encryption. They did not crack a password. They did not trick an employee or sneak malware onto a server. They found a window that asked them nothing and answered everything, behind which the records were numbered in a tidy, countable row — and they counted. The hard part of the attack — the part that's supposed to be hard — had already been done for them, by the people who built and ran that window. The breach didn't happen because the attacker was strong. It happened because the door was open.

NARRATOR: There's a lesson here bigger than Optus, and it's the part worth carrying away. Every one of those service windows — every A-P-I endpoint a system exposes — is a door to your data. And the one rule this entire disaster comes down to is that the lock has to be on every single door. Not most of them. Not the ones that feel important. Every one. It's astonishingly easy, building a system out of dozens of services, to secure the front entrance everyone sees — the login page, the main app — and then leave some side window, some internal-sounding endpoint, something "only ever meant for testing," sitting out there with no check at all. The attacker doesn't come through the front door you guarded. They come through the one you forgot. Secure A-P-I design means assuming every endpoint will be found and probed by someone hostile, and putting authentication and authorisation on each one by default — proving who's asking, and checking they're allowed — rather than hoping nobody notices the unlocked one.

NARRATOR: And notice the two checks, because losing track of the difference is exactly the mistake here. Authentication — who are you. Authorisation — are you allowed to have this specific thing. The Optus window failed the first completely: it never even asked who was knocking. But even a system that does ask who you are can still fall to the second failure — the insecure direct object reference — where it knows you're logged in as customer A, yet cheerfully hands you customer B's record because you changed a number in your request and nobody checked the record was actually yours. Whenever a system lets you reach a record by its raw identifier, something has to verify that record belongs to that requester. Skip the check, make the identifiers predictable, and you've built a filing cabinet anyone can read drawer by drawer.

NARRATOR: There's a deeper point underneath, about what that data actually was. This wasn't game scores. It was people's identities — the sensitive personal information that, once it's loose, can't be recalled or reset. Building a system that holds that kind of information isn't just a technical job; it carries a duty of care to the people whose lives sit in your database. Australia learned that loudly. The breach triggered a federal investigation, an enormous public reckoning, and a sharpening of the laws around how companies must protect personal data and what happens when they don't — with penalties scaled up dramatically, so that losing millions of people's identities is no longer something a company can treat as a minor cost of doing business. The privacy of personal data stopped being a box-ticking afterthought and became, very publicly, the company's legal problem.

NARRATOR: So here's the whole thing in one breath. One person. No special tools. A service window on the open internet that never asked who was knocking, in front of records numbered one, two, three, in a neat predictable row. A short loop counting up the line. Nine point eight million people's names, birthdays, addresses, and the identity documents they can never simply replace — poured out through a door that was never locked. The attacker didn't pick the lock. There was no lock to pick. And that, more than any clever exploit ever could, is the thing worth remembering: the most dangerous vulnerability in a system is the check that was supposed to be there, on every door, and quietly wasn't.
