NARRATOR: The first thing you write when the exam starts, before you read a single question: C-I-A-A-A, said "see-eye-ay, triple-A". Six foundational properties. C-I-A is the triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — what we protect. Triple-A is Authentication, Authorisation, Accountability — how we protect it. Confidentiality is secrecy, who can read it. Integrity is accuracy, whether it can be changed. Availability is access, whether you can reach it. Authentication is who you are. Authorisation is what you may do. Accountability is what you did. That's chapter fifteen, episode one, and it is the spine of the entire module — every vulnerability is one of these six being broken, every defence is one being upheld. If you remember one thing from the whole module, it's CIA-AAA in the margin.

NARRATOR: Now the whole arc fast, chapter by chapter, re-surfacing every hook — hearing them in one pass is the point of a review. Chapter fourteen — the mindset. Fourteen-oh-one, the business case: D-A, protect Data and prevent Attacks; security is an enabler, not just a cost — prevention is far cheaper than response, and risk equals likelihood times impact. Fourteen-oh-two, the software development life cycle, the eight steps — Really Smart Developers Don't Ignore Testing In Maintenance: Requirements, Specifications, Design, Development, Integration, Testing, Installation, Maintenance — security built into every phase, which is shift left, fix it early where it's cheap; and threat modelling with STRIDE — Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege. Fourteen-oh-three, user-centred design: the security-usability paradox — difficult security gets bypassed, so usability is a security requirement — and the principle, secure default, easy right path.

NARRATOR: Chapter fifteen — the principles. Fifteen-oh-one is CIA-AAA, which we just dumped. Fifteen-oh-two, cryptography, two parts. The most repeated line in the module: hashing is one-way, encryption is two-way. Hashing gives integrity — a fixed-size fingerprint you can only check, never reverse; encryption gives confidentiality — reversible with a key. Password storage: hash it, salt it, slow it — salt stops the rainbow, defeating rainbow tables. Part two: symmetric equals same key, fast but a sharing problem; asymmetric equals a pair, slow but solves sharing; and the digital-signature rule — encrypt with public, decrypt with private, but sign with private, verify with public — giving non-repudiation, which is accountability. Plus key management — Good Spies Rotate Disguises, Generate, Store, Rotate, Destroy — and sandboxing, a playpen the code can't climb out of.

NARRATOR: Chapter sixteen — practice on data. Sixteen-oh-one, defensive input handling: V-S-E — Validate, Sanitise, handle Errors. Never trust input. Validation rejects, sanitisation cleans — keep those distinct. Allow-list beats block-list. Injection and X-S-S are the same disease — input becoming code — fixed by parameterised queries for the database and output encoding for the browser. And safe errors: log it private, show it generic. Sixteen-oh-two, privacy by design: PER — Proactive, Embed, Respect — and collect less, keep less. The distinction to hold: privacy is not security — you can be perfectly secure and still violate privacy.

NARRATOR: Chapter seventeen — systems and A-P-Is. Seventeen-oh-one, secure A-P-I design: A-A-R-T — Authenticate, Authorise, Rate-limit, Transport-secure — with S-A-F-E sessions underneath — Strong IDs, cookie Attributes like HttpOnly and SameSite, Fixed lifetime, regenerate on Escalation — and the line that matters everywhere: authenticated is not authorised. CORS relaxes the same-origin policy; it is not access control. Seventeen-oh-two, secure execution: M-S-E — Memory, Session, Exception management — where Heartbleed was a memory over-read from trusting an unvalidated length. Plus the back-door attacks: dot-dot-slash climbs out via path traversal, and the secret leaks through timing, so compare in constant time.

NARRATOR: Chapter eighteen — testing and vulnerabilities. Eighteen-oh-one, testing: the chant CR-SAST-DAST-VA-PEN — Code Review, Static, Dynamic, Vulnerability Assessment, Penetration testing — Static is Source, Dynamic is Doing — and the incident lifecycle, H-B-D, Harden, Breach-handle, Disaster-recover, serving business continuity. Eighteen-oh-two, the user-action vulnerability master list: B-X-I-R — Broken authentication and session, Cross-site, Invalid forwarding and redirect, Race conditions. X-S-S injects a Script; C-S-R-F rides a Session. Race conditions are T-O-C-T-O-U, Time-Of-Check to Time-Of-Use, and the server is always concurrent. Then chapter nineteen, the context chapter: nineteen-oh-one, collaboration, V-D-Q — Views, Delegate by expertise, Quality. Nineteen-oh-two, enterprise benefits, PIP-PB — Products, Influence, Practices, Productivity, Business interactivity. Nineteen-oh-three, the social, ethical and legal issues, E-D-P-C-I-D, Ed Picid — Employment, Data security, Privacy, Copyright, Intellectual property, Digital disruption. And nineteen-oh-four, evaluation, M-A-I — Measure, Audit, Improve, continuously. That's the whole module in one breath. Now we stop listing and start integrating — that's where the marks are.

NARRATOR: First the traps the marker exploits across the module — the places students confuse one topic for another. These are guaranteed mark-droppers, so hear them together. One: authentication versus authorisation — who you are versus what you may do, and the order, authenticate then authorise; "logged in is not allowed". Two: confidentiality versus integrity — reading versus changing; they fail independently. Three: hashing versus encryption — one-way versus two-way; passwords are hashed, not encrypted. Four: validation versus sanitisation — reject versus clean. Five: SAST versus DAST — source not running versus app running. Six: X-S-S versus C-S-R-F — inject a script versus forge a request. And two more from chapter nineteen: privacy versus security — should you hold it versus is it protected; and copyright versus intellectual property — copyright is one type of I-P, not the whole. Eight distinctions. Rattle those off and you've inoculated yourself against the most common marking traps in the module.

NARRATOR: Here they are, fast. Authentication verifies who you are; authorisation determines what you may do — authenticate first, then authorise. Confidentiality is preventing unauthorised reading; integrity is preventing unauthorised changing. Hashing is one-way and gives integrity; encryption is two-way and gives confidentiality. Validation rejects input that breaks the rules; sanitisation cleans input so it's safe to use. SAST analyses the source without running it; DAST attacks the running application from outside. X-S-S injects a script that runs in a victim's browser; C-S-R-F forges a request that rides a victim's logged-in session. Privacy is whether you should collect the data at all; security is protecting the data once you hold it. And copyright is one type of intellectual property, alongside patents, trademarks and trade secrets. Eight clean distinctions — that's eight traps disarmed.

NARRATOR: Now the three case studies, pulled together — the module has three running threads and a review is where you see how they connect. The x-z backdoor: in twenty-twenty-four an attacker spent two years becoming a trusted maintainer of a tiny compression library deep in Linux, then inserted a backdoor as an insider; it was caught days before going worldwide by one engineer who noticed a half-second slowdown. That single story touches the whole module: a supply-chain failure in the maintenance phase of the S-D-L-C, fourteen-oh-two; caught by code review, eighteen-oh-one; a collaboration and trust story, nineteen-oh-one — an isolated maintainer let it in, a second point of view caught it; and open-source ethics and responsible disclosure, nineteen-oh-three. The Equifax breach: twenty-seventeen, a single unpatched vulnerability, one hundred and forty-seven million records, hundreds of millions in settlements. It's the business case, fourteen-oh-one; the missed vulnerability assessment and patch, eighteen-oh-one; the enterprise cost of losing PIP-PB, nineteen-oh-two; and the legal and ethical fallout under the Privacy Act, nineteen-oh-three. And Heartbleed: twenty-fourteen, a memory over-read in the TLS library leaked private keys — sound cryptographic maths undone by a buggy implementation. It's TLS from fifteen-oh-two, memory management from seventeen-oh-two, testing from eighteen-oh-one. Three stories, the whole module — the marker loves a case study as evidence, so keep one ready for each major theme.

NARRATOR: Now the integrated stimulus — the heart of a review and the shape of the hardest exam questions. I'll describe one vulnerable system and we'll find every CIA-AAA violation across multiple chapters, because real H-S-C extended questions never test one dot-point alone. Picture this. An online health portal. Patients log in with just a username and password. The login page is served over plain H-T-T-P, port eighty. Passwords are stored using fast unsalted M-D-five hashing. The search feature takes whatever you type and builds the database query by gluing your text straight into the S-Q-L string, and echoes your search term back onto the page without encoding it. Any logged-in user can view any patient's record by changing the I-D number in the U-R-L. The sign-up form demands your full date of birth, home address, and Medicare number, all kept forever. When something goes wrong, the app shows the user a full stack trace including the database structure. And there's no logging of who views which record. Hold that picture.

NARRATOR: Let's walk it, flaw by flaw — notice every chapter shows up. Plain H-T-T-P on the login: confidentiality broken in transit — credentials cross in clear text. Fix: T-L-S, H-T-T-P-S, port four-four-three — fifteen-oh-two and A-A-R-T's transport-secure. Fast unsalted M-D-five password storage: a confidentiality failure waiting to happen — one breach and every password is cracked. Fix: hash it, salt it, slow it — bcrypt or Argon2 with a per-user salt; hashing is one-way, salt stops the rainbow — fifteen-oh-two. String-built S-Q-L: that's S-Q-L injection, an integrity and confidentiality break — input becoming code. Fix: parameterised queries — V-S-E, sixteen-oh-one. The search term echoed back unencoded: reflected X-S-S, integrity and confidentiality — a script running in the victim's browser. Fix: output encoding plus a Content Security Policy — eighteen-oh-two, and X-S-S injects a Script. Any user reading any record by changing the U-R-L I-D: broken access control, an authorisation failure — authenticated is not authorised — the insecure direct object reference. Fix: authorisation and ownership checks on every request — A-A-R-T, seventeen-oh-one. Only a password for a health system: weak authentication. Fix: multi-factor — something you know plus something you have — fifteen-oh-one. Over-collecting date of birth, address and Medicare number and keeping them forever: a privacy failure. Fix: data minimisation, collect less, keep less, with a retention policy — PER, sixteen-oh-two, and a legal issue under the Privacy Act, nineteen-oh-three. The stack trace shown to users: information disclosure — a confidentiality failure. Fix: log it private, show it generic — sixteen-oh-one and M-S-E exception management, seventeen-oh-two. No logging of record access: an accountability failure — you can't answer "who viewed what". Fix: audit logging — fifteen-oh-one. That's nine flaws, every one of the six CIA-AAA properties, drawing on six chapters. That single answer, structured as flaw, property, fix, mnemonic, is a full-mark extended response — and exactly how the hardest stimulus questions are built.

NARRATOR: One more integrated worked example, the cross-chapter extended type, so you've heard the full shape. The question: a startup is building a new e-commerce platform. QUESTION: Question one, the big stimulus. A company's web application stores passwords as unsalted M-D-five hashes, builds its database queries by concatenating user input, serves its login over plain H-T-T-P, lets any logged-in user access any account by changing a U-R-L parameter, and keeps no record of who accesses what. Identify the security flaws, the property each breaks, and a fix for each. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Unsalted M-D-five password storage breaks confidentiality — a fast unsalted hash is trivially cracked after a breach; fix by hashing with a salt and a slow algorithm such as bcrypt or Argon2, since hashing is one-way and salt stops the rainbow. Concatenated queries are S-Q-L injection, breaking integrity and confidentiality as input becomes code; fix with parameterised queries that pass values separately. Plain H-T-T-P breaks confidentiality in transit, exposing credentials; fix with T-L-S over H-T-T-P-S. Any user accessing any account via a U-R-L parameter is broken access control, an authorisation failure — authenticated is not authorised; fix with per-request authorisation and ownership checks. No record of access is an accountability failure; fix with audit logging. A response that names each flaw with its precise term, maps it to the correct CIA-AAA property, and gives a specific fix is full marks.

QUESTION: Question two, distinguish-and-evaluate. Compare the contribution of secure development practices to a small development team with their benefit to the wider enterprise, and evaluate one ethical issue that arises. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. For the team, collaboration delivers V-D-Q: various points of view catch flaws in an individual's blind spot, which is why code review and pair programming work; delegation by expertise means each security-critical part is handled by someone with depth; and the quality of the solution rises, with fewer vulnerabilities and shared knowledge. For the enterprise, secure practices deliver PIP-PB: improved products as a value proposition, influence on future development through reusable secure patterns, improved work practices, higher productivity as prevention frees time from firefighting, and business interactivity through the trust that enables safe transactions and integrations. Evaluating an ethical issue — privacy: the enterprise gains commercially from collecting customer data, but has an ethical duty and a legal obligation under the Australian Privacy Principles to collect only what is needed and protect it; weighing commercial benefit against the individual's right to control their data, the justified course is data minimisation, which serves both. A response that distinguishes team from enterprise benefits with the correct mnemonics and weighs both sides of an ethical issue to a judgement is full marks.

QUESTION: Question three, the synthesis. Explain how an organisation can both build secure software and know that its security remains effective over time. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Building secure software means applying security across the entire software development life cycle — shifting left with threat modelling at design, upholding the six CIA-AAA properties through encryption, hashing, validation, multi-factor authentication, least-privilege authorisation and audit logging, handling input defensively with validation, sanitisation and error handling, securing A-P-Is and sessions, applying privacy by design, and testing with the full toolbox of code review, static and dynamic testing, vulnerability assessment and penetration testing while planning for incidents through hardening, breach handling and disaster recovery. Knowing that security remains effective over time means evaluating it continuously through the measure-audit-improve loop: measuring actionable, outcome-based metrics such as mean time to detect and vulnerability remediation time, auditing independently against a recognised standard such as I-S-O twenty-seven-thousand-and-one, and improving by closing gaps and feeding lessons back — repeated indefinitely because the threat landscape constantly changes. Building security and evaluating it are two distinct skills; the first creates protection, the second verifies and sustains it. A response that covers secure construction across the life cycle and the continuous evaluation loop, and distinguishes the two, is full marks.
