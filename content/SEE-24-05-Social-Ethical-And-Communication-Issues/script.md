NARRATOR: Recap first. Last episode: project management and Gantt charts. The project management triangle is Scope, Time, Cost — change one and the others move, with quality squeezed in the middle. A Gantt chart is a visual schedule: bars over time, arrows for dependencies, milestones as checkpoints. The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks; it sets the minimum project duration, and its tasks have zero slack, so a slip there slips the whole project, while a slip inside a non-critical task's slack doesn't. And tracking is the ongoing plan-versus-actual comparison. Hold onto that triangle especially — today it becomes the thing you negotiate around.

NARRATOR: The bigger arc behind us: the three development approaches — Waterfall falls once, Agile goes around, WAgile combines the two. That was all process and schedule, the mechanics of running a project. Today we shift to the part that's hardest in real software engineering: the people. Stakeholders, ethics, and communication. The outcome is S-E-twelve-oh-five — explains the social, ethical and legal implications of software engineering on the individual, society and the environment. There's no algorithm here. The marks come from naming the issues precisely and from structured answers about working with people. The two dot-points: explore social and ethical issues associated with project work — working individually, collaboratively, and responding to stakeholders — and explore communication issues — involving and empowering the client, enabling feedback, and negotiating.

NARRATOR: Objectives up front. By the end you'll be able to describe ethical issues in project work; explain how to involve and empower a client; explain how to enable structured feedback; explain how to negotiate scope with a difficult client; and avoid the trap of treating communication as optional. The classic questions are "explain how a developer can effectively involve a client during development" for around six marks, and "describe an ethical issue that can arise in collaborative project work."

NARRATOR: Start with the headline, because the communication strand is the heart of this episode and the most-examined part: I-F-N. Involve, enable Feedback, Negotiate. Those three are the syllabus' own communication issues — involving and empowering the client, enabling feedback, and negotiating — and I-F-N is your structure for any "how do you communicate with the client" question. Teach each, do the ethics, then put it together.

NARRATOR: I — Involve and empower the client. The big idea the marker wants: client involvement is not a one-off at the start. A weak developer gathers requirements in week one, disappears for six months, and delivers at the end. A strong developer builds an ongoing partnership where the client feels heard, informed, and in control throughout. Concretely: regular demonstrations of working software — show, don't just tell — which is exactly Agile's sprint review from last week's approaches, the feedback loop every couple of weeks. Co-design activities, where the client helps make design decisions rather than just receiving them. Transparent communication about progress, challenges, and decisions — the bad news too, not just the good. And "empower" specifically means giving the client genuine influence over the outcome — their input actually changes the product — not just informing them after the fact. Empowerment is involvement with teeth. The exam phrasing that scores: involve the client continuously through demonstrations and co-design, and empower them by giving their feedback real influence over decisions.

NARRATOR: F — enable Feedback. Involvement is only useful if feedback flows and gets acted on, so you build structured ways to collect it. Multiple channels: surveys, interviews, usability sessions, informal conversations — different stakeholders surface different things in different settings. Regular feedback cycles: scheduled collection points aligned with your development iterations, so feedback comes in steadily, not in one overwhelming dump. Categorisation: organising input by type, priority, and feasibility, so you can act on it sensibly. And response tracking: documenting how each piece of feedback actually changed a decision — which closes the loop and shows the client they were heard. That last point matters for the marker: enabling feedback isn't just collecting it; it's having a process to synthesise and act on it. We go deeper on analyse-and-respond in twenty-six-oh-two, with the hook C-S-A — Collect, Synthesise, Act. For now, F means: build real channels and cycles so feedback flows and is acted on.

NARRATOR: N — Negotiate. Here last episode's triangle comes back. Clients ask for more — that's normal, not a failure — and your job is to negotiate scope against the Scope-Time-Cost reality. The structured process: first, clarify the underlying need — what problem is the client actually trying to solve? Often the literal request isn't the best solution to their real need. Second, assess impact and feasibility — cost in time and effort, dependencies, risk. Third, explore options — full implementation, a simplified version, an alternative approach, or defer to a future release. Fourth, document the agreement — what was decided and why, and what was deferred. The textbook's example: a client wants a native mobile app, a hundred and twenty hours the project can't afford; you clarify the real need is "access on phones," and propose a mobile-responsive web design at forty hours that meets the core need within budget. That's negotiation — not "yes" to everything, not a flat "no," but clarify, assess, offer options, document. And the link: this is the developer-side mirror of scope creep from episode one. Negotiation is how you protect the boundaries you set, against the triangle's constraints.

NARRATOR: Now the social and ethical issues, the other dot-point. The syllabus frames it around working individually, collaboratively, and responding to stakeholders. The four ethical areas the textbook uses, with a hook: PABT, "pab-it" — Privacy, Accessibility, Bias, Transparency. Privacy and data protection: what personal data does the system collect, how is it stored and shared, and do users genuinely understand and consent? That's privacy-by-design from Secure Software Architecture sixteen-oh-two — the P-E-R principles, collect less, keep less — applied to your project. Accessibility and inclusion: can people with visual, auditory, or motor impairments use the system; does it work with assistive technology? That's W-C-A-G and POUR from Programming for the Web, here as an ethical obligation, not just a feature. Bias and fairness: could the system discriminate against particular groups; are any algorithms trained on representative data? That's S-H-L-M and the bias toolkit from Software Automation twenty-two-oh-three. And transparency and control: can users understand how the system makes decisions, and do they control their data and any automated decisions? So PABT — Privacy, Accessibility, Bias, Transparency — is your checklist of ethical issues in project work, and every one is a callback to another module. That's the point: this is the synthesis module, and ethics is where the threads tie together.

NARRATOR: The textbook anchors this in one scenario worth remembering — a story is easier to recall under exam pressure than an abstract list. A school wants a student performance analytics system that tracks academic progress, attendance, and behavioural incidents to flag at-risk students for early intervention. Sounds good — early help. But look at the tensions across PABT. Privacy: it collects sensitive behavioural data on minors — who sees it, for how long, with what consent? Bias: if the model is trained on biased historical data, it could unfairly flag certain groups — the algorithm isn't neutral, S-H-L-M. Transparency: a student labelled "at-risk" by an opaque system, with a self-fulfilling-prophecy risk where the label itself shapes how teachers treat them. And the stakeholders have competing interests: administration wants the data, teachers worry about bias, parents want help but also privacy, students fear being labelled. That single scenario lets you discuss every PABT issue and the stakeholder conflict — exactly what an ethics question rewards.

NARRATOR: Now the collaboration angle, the "working individually versus collaboratively" part. Collaboration brings real benefits — a direct callback to Secure Software Architecture nineteen-oh-one, the V-D-Q hook: various points of view catch blind spots, delegate by expertise, and quality improves. A solo developer reviews with the same blind spots they built with; a second person reaches the gap. So collaboration produces better, safer, fairer software. But it raises ethical and communication demands — give credit fairly, share workload equitably, communicate clearly within the team, handle disagreement professionally. The exam point for "an ethical issue in collaborative work": name unfair attribution of others' work, unequal contribution, or — pulling V-D-Q in — the responsibility to use diverse perspectives to avoid building something biased or exclusionary. Name a specific issue; don't just say "people might not get along."

NARRATOR: Now the trap, and it costs the most marks on this dot-point: treating communication and ethics as soft or optional — the "real work is the code" mindset. The history of software failure says otherwise. Healthcare-dot-gov failed partly because stakeholder communication and feedback broke down under a fixed deadline. Projects fail far more often from poor communication and ignored stakeholders than from technical impossibility. In an exam, never write communication off as a nice-to-have. Frame it as central to success — a project that ignores stakeholder feedback builds the wrong thing efficiently. The marker is watching for students who dismiss the human side; flip it, and treat communication and ethics as core engineering discipline.

NARRATOR: Worked example, because "explain how a developer can effectively involve a client" is a six-marker and I-F-N is built for it. Scenario: you're building a patient portal for a hospital on a twelve-month timeline. How do you involve and empower the client throughout? Apply I-F-N. Involve: run requirements workshops at the start, then schedule regular demonstrations — at each quarterly milestone and at sprint reviews — where the client sees working software and helps make design decisions through co-design, like user-journey mapping; keep communication transparent, including challenges, not just wins. Feedback: provide multiple channels — usability sessions with actual patients across the age range, surveys, interviews — on regular cycles aligned to iterations, and track how each piece of feedback changed the product. Negotiate: when the client requests new features, clarify the underlying need, assess the impact on the Scope-Time-Cost triangle, offer options like deferring to a later phase, and document what's agreed. That scores because it's specific, named, and tied to the syllabus terms, not a vague "talk to them regularly."

NARRATOR: Hear the weak-versus-strong contrast. Weak answer to "how do you involve a client during development": "you should keep talking to the client and ask them what they think." Vague, no structure, band three. Strong answer: "involve the client through an ongoing partnership rather than one-off requirements gathering: hold regular demonstrations of working software so they see progress and give feedback early; use co-design activities so they influence design decisions directly, which empowers them; maintain transparent communication about progress and challenges; provide multiple structured feedback channels on regular cycles and document how their feedback shaped decisions; and negotiate new requests by clarifying the need, weighing the impact, and agreeing trade-offs." Hear the difference — named practices for involve, empower, feedback, and negotiate, each tied to a mechanism. That structure, I-F-N spelled out, is the six-mark answer.

NARRATOR: Exam-style finish — four questions, recall up to extended. Pause and attempt each before the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Describe an ethical issue that can arise in collaborative project work. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: one ethical issue is the fair attribution of work and equitable contribution — in a collaborative project, all team members should receive appropriate credit for their contributions and share the workload fairly; failing to credit a contributor, or one member taking credit for others' work, is an ethical breach that damages trust and fairness. A second, drawn from the benefits of collaboration, is the responsibility to use diverse perspectives to avoid building biased or exclusionary software — a team has an ethical duty to consider various points of view so the solution doesn't disadvantage particular user groups. Naming a specific issue — fair attribution, equitable contribution, or the duty of inclusive design — rather than a generic "people might disagree," is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two, application. Explain how a developer can enable effective feedback during a project. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: a developer enables effective feedback by building structured processes to collect, organise, and act on stakeholder input. They provide multiple feedback channels — surveys, interviews, usability sessions, and informal conversations — so different stakeholders can contribute in the way that suits them; they schedule regular feedback cycles aligned with development iterations, such as sprint reviews, so feedback flows steadily rather than all at once; they categorise feedback by type, priority, and feasibility so it can be acted on sensibly; and they track how each piece of feedback influenced project decisions, closing the loop so stakeholders see they were heard. The mark is describing feedback as a structured, two-way process — channels, cycles, categorisation, and acting on it — not merely "ask people what they think."

QUESTION: Question three, the six-marker. Explain how a developer can effectively involve and empower a client during the development of a software solution. Pause, plan a structured answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: involving and empowering a client means treating them as an ongoing partner throughout development, not just a source of requirements at the start. The developer holds regular demonstrations of working software — for example at each iteration or milestone — so the client sees real progress and can give feedback early, while problems are cheap to fix. They use co-design activities, such as user-journey mapping, so the client helps make design decisions directly, which empowers them by giving their input genuine influence over the product rather than informing them after the fact. They maintain transparent communication about progress, challenges, and decisions, including bad news, so the client trusts the process. They provide structured feedback channels on regular cycles and document how the client's feedback shaped the solution, closing the loop. And when the client requests changes, they negotiate around the project constraints — clarifying the underlying need, assessing impact, and agreeing trade-offs — so the client stays in control of priorities. An answer structured around involve, empower, feedback, and negotiate, with a concrete practice and mechanism for each, earns full marks.

QUESTION: Question four, scenario. A client keeps requesting new features mid-project but refuses to extend the deadline or budget. Explain how you would respond. Pause, plan an answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: I would respond by negotiating, not by simply refusing or silently accepting, applying a structured process around the Scope-Time-Cost triangle. First, I would clarify the underlying need behind each request — often the literal feature isn't the only way to solve the client's real problem, and a lighter alternative may meet it. Second, I would assess the impact and feasibility of each request — its effort, dependencies, and risk. Third, I would explain the triangle transparently: increasing scope while fixing time and cost means something must give — quality suffers or the deadline slips — and I would present options: implement a simplified version, swap out a lower-priority feature to make room, or defer the new feature to a future phase. Fourth, I would document whatever is agreed and why. This protects the project boundaries while keeping the client involved and in control of priorities, and it frames the conversation as a shared trade-off rather than a conflict. Explaining the structured clarify-assess-offer-document negotiation, grounded in the triangle, and avoiding both blanket-yes and blanket-no, earns full marks.
