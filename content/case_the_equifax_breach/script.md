NARRATOR: There is a company that knows you better than your bank does. Better, in some ways, than your own family. It knows your full name, your date of birth, every address you have lived at, and the one number that, in America, is the master key to a person's entire financial life — the Social Security number. It knows how much you owe, who you owe it to, whether you pay on time, and how desperate you got the month you missed a payment. You never signed up for this company. You never agreed to anything. You have, in all likelihood, never spoken to it in your life. And yet it holds a thick, intimate file on you, built quietly over years, and it sells judgments about you to anyone willing to pay — to lenders, to landlords, to the people who decide whether you get a house, a car, a credit card, a job. The company is called Equifax. It is one of the three great credit bureaus of the United States. And in the spring and summer of two thousand seventeen, while everyone inside it believed the vault was locked, the door at the back of it stood wide open for seventy-six days, and strangers walked in and out, carrying off the financial souls of nearly half the adult population of America.

NARRATOR: A hundred and forty-seven million people. That is the number that matters in this story, and it is worth saying slowly. A hundred and forty-seven million names, dates of birth, addresses, and Social Security numbers, lifted from a company whose single reason to exist is to be trusted with exactly that. The people whose data walked out the door had never knowingly handed Equifax anything. They were not customers. They were the product. And here is the part that turns this from a tragedy into a lesson you should carry into every system you ever build: the hole the attackers walked through had a free fix available, published to the entire internet, three days before they first stepped inside. Equifax knew about the fix. They were warned, by name, by the United States government. They sent the email telling their own staff to apply it. And it didn't get applied, on the one server that mattered, because of a chain of small, dull, human failures, each one forgivable on its own, that lined up into a catastrophe.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how that happened. It is not a story about a genius hacker. It is a story about maintenance, and monitoring, and the boring, unglamorous discipline that is the difference between a closed door and an open one. So let me take you back to the beginning — to a quiet warning that went out across the internet, months before anyone at Equifax understood what was about to happen to them.

NARRATOR: The seventh of March, two thousand seventeen. The Apache Software Foundation, which maintains some of the most widely used free software on Earth, issued an emergency alert. There was a critical flaw in a piece of their software called Struts. Now, Struts is what's called a framework — scaffolding that developers build web applications on top of. Think of every form you have ever filled in on a website: a login box, a search bar, a complaint form. Something on the server has to receive what you typed and act on it. Struts is one of the things that does that job, and it sits underneath thousands of corporate websites, quietly handling the traffic, almost never thought about.

NARRATOR: The flaw had a name, because that is how the security world keeps track of these things. Every significant vulnerability gets a public catalogue number so the whole industry can talk about the same bug without confusion. This one was catalogued as C-V-E twenty-seventeen, dash, five-six-three-eight. And to understand why it was about as dangerous as a flaw can be, you need to understand one small, ordinary thing about how the web works.

QUESTION: When you submit a form on a website, what actually gets sent to the server besides the words you typed?

NARRATOR: More than you'd think. Along with your data, your browser attaches a set of little labels describing it — metadata, the data about the data. One of those labels is called the content type. It is a humble thing. Its only job is to tell the server what kind of data is arriving — is this plain text, is this an image, is this a file upload — so the server knows how to handle it. The server is supposed to glance at that label and move on. But in the vulnerable versions of Struts, there was a piece of code — the part that handles file uploads, called the Jakarta Multipart parser — that mishandled that content-type label in a very specific, very fatal way. If the value in that label was malformed, Struts would throw an error. And when it built the error message to report the problem, it took the bad content-type value and, instead of treating it as inert text, it fed it into an expression language the framework uses internally — something called O-G-N-L. Which meant that if you wrote your poison not as plain text but as an O-G-N-L expression, Struts would not just read it. It would evaluate it. It would run it. As a command. On the server.

NARRATOR: Sit with that, because it is the whole heart of the story. A stranger, anywhere in the world, could type a carefully crafted, malicious string into the content-type label of a web request, hit send, and the server on the other end would obediently execute their instructions — as if that stranger were sitting at the keyboard. No password. No login. No break-in in the way you'd picture it. The front door opened because the door had been built, by accident, to open for anyone who knocked in exactly the wrong way. The industry has a name for the worst possible outcome of a flaw like this, and this flaw delivered it in full: remote code execution. Untrusted input — data from an outsider you should never blindly trust — turned directly into running code on your machine. It is the cardinal sin of programming, and here it was, sitting in software running underneath thousands of the world's biggest websites.

NARRATOR: But here is the good news, the part that makes everything that follows so painful. When the Apache Foundation announced the flaw on the seventh of March, they did not just announce it. They released the fix the very same day. A patch — an updated, repaired version of Struts with the hole sealed shut. And the message to the world could not have been clearer. This is critical. This is being exploited in the wild already. If you run this software, update it now. Today.

NARRATOR: One of the organisations running that software was Equifax. Specifically, one of their public-facing web applications was running a vulnerable version of Apache Struts. It was a portal called A-C-I-S — the Automated Consumer Interview System. In plain terms, it was the website where ordinary people went to dispute errors on their credit report. If a bank wrongly reported you'd missed a payment, this was where you'd go to argue about it. Which means it was, by design, a door facing the open internet — a door the public was invited to walk up to and interact with. And behind that door, threaded through Equifax's network, sat the databases holding the most sensitive financial information of hundreds of millions of people. The portal was the front gate. And the front gate was running the broken version of Struts.

NARRATOR: So the clock starts on the seventh of March. The flaw is public. The fix exists. The warning is loud. Let's watch what Equifax does, because this is where a giant company's defences should have caught fire and they didn't.

NARRATOR: The very next day, the eighth of March, the United States Department of Homeland Security emailed Equifax directly, to make sure the warning had landed. This was not a generic bulletin lost in a feed. The government reached out, by name, and said: there is a critical flaw, you need to deal with it. The day after that, the ninth of March, an internal email went around inside Equifax — the message reaching the right teams: there is a critical patch, it needs to be applied. So far, this is exactly how it's supposed to work. The warning came in, the warning was passed on, the right people were told. The system, on paper, was functioning.

NARRATOR: Then, on the fifteenth of March, Equifax's security team did the next correct thing. They ran a scan. This is a standard tool — software whose whole job is to sweep across all your systems and find any machine still running the vulnerable software, so you know what needs fixing. The scan ran. And it came back clean. It reported that it had found nothing vulnerable.

QUESTION: So that's it, surely? They were warned, they passed it on, they scanned, they were clear. Story over? Pause for a second and ask yourself — what could possibly have gone wrong here, when every step looks right?

NARRATOR: The scan was wrong. It missed the vulnerable server entirely. The one machine that was running the broken version of Struts — the A-C-I-S dispute portal, the one facing the open internet — was somehow not caught by the search that was supposed to catch it. We don't know precisely why; the scan may not have reached that part of the network, or wasn't configured to look there. But the effect is the only thing that matters: the internal email said "fix this," and the scan said "there is nothing to fix," and those two messages never met on the one server that needed them both. So nobody patched it. The dispute portal kept running the broken software, wide open to the internet, day after day, while everyone who had touched the warning quietly believed the problem had been handled. That is the most dangerous state a system can be in — not "we're vulnerable," but "we're vulnerable and we're certain we're not."

NARRATOR: The attackers found the open door before Equifax did. Forensic reconstruction later placed the first intrusion at the tenth of March — just three days after the patch was released, and the same day security researchers around the world first noticed hacking groups out there scanning the internet for unpatched Struts servers. Whoever they were, they were hunting. They found Equifax's dispute portal, fired the content-type exploit at it, ran their code, and got a foothold inside. Three days. The fix had existed for three days.

NARRATOR: And here the story shifts into something slower and, in its way, more chilling — because a foothold is not a heist. Getting onto one web server is the beginning, not the end. The attackers were standing in the entrance hall, not the vault. What happened next is a masterclass in something the field calls lateral movement — the patient, quiet work of spreading sideways through a network, from the machine you broke into toward the machines that actually hold the treasure.

NARRATOR: From that first compromised web server, the intruders began to explore. And they found that Equifax, on the inside, was soft. The network was flat — poorly divided, with too few internal walls between the public-facing portal and the sensitive systems behind it. Once you were in the entrance hall, far too many doors led deeper without resistance. The attackers moved from the three databases they could first reach and pushed onward, eventually gaining access to more than forty-eight separate databases inside Equifax. And as they went, they found a gift — the kind of thing that should never exist on a well-run system. Sitting on a file share, in a plain, readable file, was a set of credentials. Usernames and passwords. In clear text. Not encrypted, not hashed, not protected in any way — just typed out, sitting there, for the taking. Those credentials unlocked still more systems. The attackers had walked in through one broken door and then found the keys to the rest of the building left hanging on a hook.

NARRATOR: And then they got to work. Over the following weeks they ran query after query against those databases — by the official count, around nine thousand database queries — methodically asking the systems to hand over their records. They were searching for unencrypted personal information, and they found it, the investigators later determined, some two hundred and sixty-five times. They bundled what they found and shipped it out of the network, a steady stream of the most sensitive data in the country flowing quietly outward to servers somewhere far away. This was not a smash and grab. This was someone living inside the vault for weeks, calmly loading the truck, query by query, file by file.

QUESTION: Now here is the question that should be screaming at you. Equifax was a major financial company. They had monitoring — security tools whose entire job is to watch the traffic leaving the network and raise the alarm when something looks wrong. Gigabytes of the nation's credit data should not just be quietly streaming out to some unknown server in the distance without anyone noticing. So where, in all of this, was the alarm?

NARRATOR: The alarm was switched off. Not deliberately — nobody chose to blind the company. It was switched off through neglect, and the way it happened is the single most damning detail in the whole affair. A lot of that outbound traffic was encrypted. That's normal and even good — encryption protects data in transit. But it creates a problem for your own security tools, because a device watching for stolen data can't see inside traffic that's scrambled. To inspect encrypted traffic, that monitoring device needs a digital certificate — a credential that lets it decrypt the data, peek inside to check for anything suspicious, and re-encrypt it before sending it on its way. It's a legitimate, standard piece of how these systems work. And that certificate, on the device watching the A-C-I-S traffic, had expired.

NARRATOR: Not expired by a few days. Not by a few weeks. According to the United States Government Accountability Office — the G-A-O, the federal agency that later investigated the whole disaster — that certificate had been expired for nineteen months. For a year and a half, the device meant to watch for exactly this kind of theft had been staring at scrambled noise, seeing nothing, understanding nothing, completely blind. The guard was at his post the entire time. His eyes had been taped shut for nineteen months, and nobody had noticed. So the attackers moved their stolen data out, day after day, week after week, straight past a security tool that could not see a single byte of what was passing in front of it.

NARRATOR: And so the breach ran. For seventy-six days, by the forensic timeline, strangers extracted the financial identities of a nation, using ordinary encrypted web traffic, past a blindfolded guard, while everyone at Equifax believed all was well. It only ended on the twenty-ninth of July, two thousand seventeen — and it ended almost by accident. Someone finally renewed that expired certificate. The monitoring device blinked back to life. Its eyes opened. And in the very first moments of being able to see again, it spotted it: strange, suspicious traffic on the dispute portal. Equifax pulled the server offline. They applied the patch — months late, the patch that had been free and waiting since March. And then they began the grim work of counting what had walked out the door.

NARRATOR: The number, when it finally settled, was about a hundred and forty-seven million people. A hundred and forty-seven million names, dates of birth, and addresses. Social Security numbers for the overwhelming majority of them. Driver's licence details for many. And for roughly two hundred and nine thousand people, credit card numbers as well. To put that in proportion: that is on the order of half of every adult in the United States. Most of them, as we said, had never knowingly given Equifax a thing. The single most sensitive identifier an American possesses — the Social Security number, the one you cannot easily change, the one that follows you for life and unlocks loans and accounts and identity itself — was now loose, for half the country, in the hands of people unknown. This was not a leaked list of email addresses. This was the raw material of identity theft, harvested at the scale of a nation.

NARRATOR: And then Equifax made it worse. They discovered the breach at the end of July. They did not tell the public until the seventh of September — roughly six weeks later. The company would say it needed that time to understand the scope, which is a fair argument up to a point. But in that same gap, three Equifax executives sold off company stock worth nearly two million dollars, before the breach was public and before the share price collapsed under the news. The company insisted those sales were unrelated, that the executives didn't know. One of them, an executive named Jun Ying, who was in line to become the company's global chief information officer, was later charged and convicted of insider trading over exactly those sales — sentenced to months in prison for dumping his stock once he'd worked out what had happened. Whatever the truth of the others' intentions, the optics were catastrophic: a company whose product is trust, whose executives appeared to protect their own wallets before warning the public whose data they'd lost.

NARRATOR: And when the announcement finally came, it landed like a second disaster. Equifax set up a special website for worried people to check whether they'd been affected. The site was a shambles. It lived on a strange, separate domain that looked exactly like the kind of phishing site you'd be warned never to trust — so much so that web browsers and security software started flagging Equifax's own official website as suspicious. The tool gave inconsistent answers. Some of its fine print appeared, at first, to ask people to waive their right to sue simply for checking. A company whose entire business was being a trustworthy guardian of data had, at every single stage — prevention, detection, disclosure, and response — comprehensively failed at the one thing it sold.

NARRATOR: So let's step back from the timeline now, because under every beat of this story sits a security principle with a proper name — and these names are the spine of how the field thinks about protecting systems.

NARRATOR: Start with the wound itself — what was actually lost. What Equifax lost was confidentiality. That's the property that says sensitive information is seen only by those authorised to see it. It is the first of the three great pillars of information security, and the one people think of first when they imagine a "data breach." Here, confidentiality failed for a hundred and forty-seven million people at once. The whole catastrophe, in the language of the field, is one colossal confidentiality breach — the exposure of private data to people who had no right to it.

NARRATOR: Now the way in — the technical root. The flaw was untrusted input becoming running code. A stranger's data — the value in a content-type label — was taken at face value and executed as a command. This is the danger behind everything you will ever learn about input validation and sanitisation: the discipline of never trusting data that comes from outside your program, of checking and cleaning every piece of it before you let it anywhere near a decision your code makes. The entire reason that discipline exists is to stop precisely this — untrusted input escalating into remote code execution. Equifax's portal trusted a stranger's label, and the stranger wrote a command into it.

NARRATOR: But here is the part that should make you grind your teeth, and it is the most important lesson in the whole story. This was not a brilliant, unstoppable zero-day — not some secret flaw nobody on Earth knew about, the kind of thing that takes a nation-state's research budget to discover. The opposite. The flaw was publicly known, and the fix already existed. The patch was published the same day as the vulnerability — three days before the attackers got in. The failure was not ignorance. It was maintenance.

NARRATOR: Keeping software up to date — applying patches for known vulnerabilities promptly — is part of the maintenance phase of the software development life cycle. That's the long, quiet, unglamorous stretch that happens after a system ships, when the exciting work of building is over and all that's left is the discipline of keeping it alive and safe. People imagine that security is clever code, written cleverly, up front. A huge amount of it is something far more boring: knowing exactly which versions of which software you are running, watching the security advisories as they come out, and patching reliably and fast, before an attacker can weaponise a hole the vendor has already publicly admitted to and fixed. This even has a name as a discipline — patch management. And Equifax's patch management didn't fail because the warning never came. It came three times — from Apache, from Homeland Security, from their own internal email. It failed because the patch sat un-applied on the one server that mattered, while a faulty scan told everyone the coast was clear. Patch management is not paperwork. It is the literal difference between a closed door and an open one.

NARRATOR: Then there's the blindfold — the expired certificate. And this teaches the second great pillar of the story: that prevention alone is never enough. No matter how good your defences, you must assume that someday one of them will fail — and so you build a second line whose job is to detect that something is wrong while it's happening, and to respond. That second line is monitoring, and the logging and watching that lets you answer the question "who is doing what, right now, inside my system." Equifax had that capability. They had the device. They had the monitoring built and installed. And they let it rot — let its certificate sit expired for nineteen months, turning a working alarm into an expensive ornament. The lesson here is sharper than "build the alarm." It is "keep the alarm working." A monitoring tool that is silently broken is in some ways worse than no monitoring at all, because it lets you believe you are being watched over when you are not. Equifax thought it had eyes on its network. It had been blind for over a year and didn't know it.

NARRATOR: And there are two quieter structural failures the G-A-O investigation drew out, both worth knowing by name. The first is the lack of network segmentation. A well-built network is divided into compartments, with internal walls and locked doors between them, so that breaking into the public-facing portal does not hand you the whole building. Equifax's network was flat — too open on the inside — which is exactly why the attackers could move so freely from that first web server into forty-eight databases. The second is data governance, and the gift the attackers found: usernames and passwords sitting in plain, readable text on a file share. Sensitive credentials should never exist unprotected like that. Both of these are the same idea wearing different clothes — the principle of defence in depth, the assumption that any single barrier can be breached, and so you layer them, so that no one failure is fatal. Equifax had, in effect, one barrier: the patch that didn't get applied. Once that fell, there was almost nothing behind it.

NARRATOR: And finally, the cost — because security looks like an expense right up until the moment it doesn't, and then it looks like the cheapest thing the company never bought. Equifax paid, and it paid enormously. In July of two thousand nineteen, the company reached a global settlement with the Federal Trade Commission, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, and nearly every state in the country. The figure was up to seven hundred million dollars — at least five hundred and seventy-five million, with provisions that could push it past seven hundred — making it, at the time, one of the largest data-breach settlements in history. And the human cost at the top was just as total. The chief executive, Richard Smith, retired in the immediate aftermath. The chief information officer, David Webb, retired. The chief security officer, Susan Mauldin, departed. The careers at the top of the company were, effectively, ended.

NARRATOR: And here is the brutal arithmetic, the thing I most want you to carry out of this story. The flaw that caused all of it — the lost identities of a hundred and forty-seven million people, the seven-hundred-million-dollar settlement, the ruined careers, the years of identity-theft risk for half a nation — that flaw had a free fix, available to download, three days before the attackers ever got in. The patch cost nothing. The breach cost the better part of a billion dollars and the trust of an entire country. When you hear someone argue that security is too expensive, that patching is a chore, that monitoring is overhead, that the boring maintenance work can wait until next quarter — this is the story to put in front of them. Because the real expense is never the patch. The real expense is always, always on the other side of the breach.

NARRATOR: There is a final twist, and it's worth telling, because it changes the shape of the threat. For years, the question of who did this hung open. Then, in February of two thousand twenty, the United States Department of Justice indicted four members of the Chinese People's Liberation Army — named as Wu Zhiyong, Wang Qian, Xu Ke, and Liu Lei — accusing them of carrying out the Equifax breach on behalf of the Chinese military. If that attribution is right, then this was not ordinary criminals hunting for credit-card numbers to sell. The credit-card data, oddly, was barely used. This was a nation-state, hoovering up the personal details of nearly every adult in a rival country — the kind of vast dossier an intelligence service builds to find, profile, and recruit, to know who is in debt, who is vulnerable, who can be leaned on. Which makes the original failure all the more sobering. The door that a faulty scan left unlocked, and a blindfolded guard failed to watch, may not have let in thieves at all. It may have let in a foreign army.

NARRATOR: So here is the whole thing, in one breath. A free fix, published to the world with a screaming warning, three days before the attackers walked in. A company warned three separate times, that sent the email, ran the scan, and was failed by a single missed server. A flat internal network and plaintext passwords on a file share that turned one open door into the keys to forty-eight databases. And, watching over all of it, a security camera whose lens had been capped for nineteen months — so that for seventy-six days, the financial identities of a hundred and forty-seven million people streamed quietly out into the dark, past a guard who could not see, while everyone in the building was certain the vault was locked.

NARRATOR: The most expensive breaches in history are almost never the work of unstoppable genius. They are the work of known flaws, left unpatched, behind alarms left broken, by organisations that believed — sincerely, comfortably, and completely wrongly — that they had already taken care of it. The patch was free. The certificate cost a few hundred dollars to renew. The discipline to apply them both, on time, every time, on every machine, is the whole of the job. Equifax had every chance to be a footnote. It became a warning instead. Build your systems — and run them, and watch them, and patch them, for all the long boring years after they ship — as if the door you forgot to check is the one someone is already walking through.
