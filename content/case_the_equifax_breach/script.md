NARRATOR: On the seventh of March, two thousand seventeen, a quiet warning went out across the internet. The Apache Software Foundation, which maintains some of the most widely used web software on Earth, announced a flaw in a piece of their software called Struts. Struts is a framework — scaffolding that lets developers build the web applications you fill in on a website. Think of every form you've ever submitted: a login box, a complaint form, a search bar. Something on the server has to take what you typed and act on it. Struts is one of the things that does that job, and it runs underneath thousands of corporate websites.

NARRATOR: The flaw had a name. Vulnerabilities get catalogued with a public reference number so the whole industry can talk about the same bug. This one was catalogued as C-V-E twenty-seventeen, dash, five-six-three-eight. And it was about as bad as a vulnerability gets. When you submit a form, your browser attaches little labels to the data describing what kind it is — one of them is called the content type. Normally a server glances at that label and moves on. But in vulnerable versions of Struts, if you wrote a specially crafted, malformed value into that content-type label, Struts would try to interpret it as a command — and run it. On the server. As if you were sitting at the keyboard.

NARRATOR: Let that sink in, because it's the heart of the whole story. A stranger, anywhere in the world, could type a poisoned string into a web form, hit submit, and the server would obediently execute their instructions. No password. No break-in. The front door would open because the door was built to open for anyone who knocked in exactly the wrong way. The industry has a phrase for the worst version of this: remote code execution. Untrusted input — data from a stranger you should never blindly trust — turned directly into running code on your machine.

NARRATOR: The good news, on the seventh of March, was that Apache didn't just announce the flaw. They released the fix the same day — a patch, an updated version of Struts with the hole sealed. The instructions to the world were simple: update now. This is urgent. Anyone running this software needs to apply this patch immediately.

NARRATOR: One of the organisations running that software was Equifax.

NARRATOR: You may never have signed up for Equifax, but if you live in America, Equifax almost certainly has a file on you anyway. Equifax is one of the three big credit bureaus. Their entire business is collecting the most sensitive financial details of hundreds of millions of people — names, dates of birth, addresses, and the single most valuable number in American identity, the Social Security number — and selling assessments of how trustworthy you are with money. They are, in effect, a vault holding the keys to the financial identity of half a country. And one of their public web applications — a portal where consumers could dispute errors on their credit report — was running a vulnerable version of Apache Struts.

NARRATOR: So the clock starts on the seventh of March. The fix exists. The warning is out. What does Equifax do?

NARRATOR: The day after the announcement, the United States Department of Homeland Security emailed Equifax to make sure they knew. The next day, an internal email went round the company: there's a critical patch, apply it. So far, so good — the message reached the right people. A few days later, the security team ran a scan across their systems, a tool meant to find any machine still running the vulnerable software so it can be fixed. The scan came back clean. It found nothing.

QUESTION: So that's it — they checked, they were clear, story over? If only. What do you suppose was actually wrong here?

NARRATOR: The scan was wrong. It missed the vulnerable server entirely. We don't know exactly why, but the effect is what matters: the one machine that needed the patch was the one the search didn't find. So nobody patched it. The internal email said "fix this." The scan said "nothing to fix." And the two never met on that server. The dispute portal — facing the open internet, holding a path into Equifax's data — kept running the broken version of Struts, wide open, day after day.

NARRATOR: The attackers found it before Equifax did. Forensic reconstruction put the first intrusion at the tenth of March — three days after the patch was released. Three days. Whoever they were, they were watching for unpatched Struts servers, and they walked straight in through that content-type flaw, ran their code, and got a foothold inside.

NARRATOR: And here the story gets worse, because a foothold is not the same as a heist. Getting onto one web server is the beginning, not the end. From that first server the intruders began to move — quietly finding other systems, hunting for where the real data lived. They found databases. Dozens of them. They found, sitting on a file share, a set of credentials — usernames and passwords in plain readable text — that let them reach still more databases. For weeks they roamed, running query after query, scooping up records and bundling them to ship out of the network. By the forensic timeline this ran into the middle of May and beyond — months of someone living inside the vault, loading up the truck.

QUESTION: Here's the question that should be screaming at you. Equifax had monitoring. They had tools whose entire job is to watch the traffic leaving the network and shout when something looks wrong — gigabytes of credit data shouldn't just be quietly flowing out to some server in the distance. So where was the alarm?

NARRATOR: The alarm was switched off. Not deliberately — through neglect. A lot of that outbound traffic was encrypted, and to inspect encrypted traffic, the monitoring system needs a digital certificate — a credential that lets it decrypt, peek inside, and re-encrypt the data as it passes. That certificate had expired. And not by a few days. By the reporting afterward, it had been expired for something like nineteen months. For more than a year and a half, the device meant to watch for exactly this kind of theft had been blindfolded, seeing only scrambled noise. The intruders moved data out, day after day, past a guard who couldn't see.

NARRATOR: It was only on the twenty-ninth of July, when someone finally renewed that certificate and the monitoring tool blinked back to life, that Equifax suddenly saw it: strange, suspicious traffic on the dispute portal. They pulled the server offline. They applied the patch — months late. And then they began to count what had walked out the door.

NARRATOR: The number, when it settled, was about a hundred and forty-seven million people. A hundred and forty-seven million names, dates of birth, addresses. Social Security numbers for the great majority of them. Driver's licence details. For a couple of hundred thousand, credit card numbers as well. That's not a list of customers. That's roughly half the adult population of the United States — most of whom had never knowingly given Equifax anything. Their data was held about them, and lost about them, without their say.

NARRATOR: And the public didn't find out for another six weeks. Equifax discovered the breach at the end of July and didn't announce it until the seventh of September. In that gap, awkwardly, several Equifax executives sold off shares — sales the company insisted were unrelated, but which became their own scandal. When the announcement finally came, it landed badly: the website Equifax set up for people to check if they were affected was itself riddled with problems, at one point flagged by browsers as suspicious. A company whose product is trust had comprehensively failed at it.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So let's step back from the timeline — because under the drama, every beat of it is a security lesson with a proper name.

NARRATOR: Start with the wound. What Equifax lost was confidentiality — the property that says sensitive information is seen only by those authorised to see it. Here it failed for a hundred and forty-seven million people at once. The whole disaster is, in the language of the field, one enormous confidentiality breach.

NARRATOR: Now the way in. The flaw was untrusted input becoming running code — a stranger's data, the value in a web form, taken at face value and executed as a command. That is the cardinal sin of secure programming: trusting input from outside. Everything you'll learn about validating and sanitising user input, about never letting external data steer the behaviour of your program, exists precisely to stop this — to stop input turning into remote code execution.

NARRATOR: But notice the saddest part. The flaw was known, and the fix already existed. This wasn't a brilliant zero-day nobody could have stopped. The patch was published the same day as the vulnerability — three days before the attackers got in. The failure wasn't ignorance; it was maintenance. Keeping software up to date — applying patches for known vulnerabilities promptly — is part of the maintenance phase of the software development life cycle, the unglamorous, ongoing work that happens after a system ships. People imagine security is clever code written up front. A huge amount of it is just this: tracking which versions you run, watching the advisories, and patching reliably, before the attackers do. Equifax knew, and the patch sat un-applied on the one server that mattered. Patch management isn't paperwork. It's the difference between a closed door and an open one.

NARRATOR: Then the expired certificate — the blindfold. Even when prevention fails, you're supposed to detect that something's wrong while it's happening, and respond. That's the value of monitoring and accountability — the logging and watching that lets you answer "who is doing what, right now, in my system." Equifax had that capability and let it rot for nineteen months, so the breach ran for months instead of being caught in hours. The lesson isn't only "build the alarm." It's "keep the alarm working" — a monitoring tool that's silently broken is worse than none, because it lets you believe you're being watched over when you're not.

NARRATOR: And finally, the cost — because security looks like an expense right up until the moment it doesn't. Equifax paid. They settled with regulators and consumers for an amount that climbed past seven hundred million dollars, with provisions pushing it well beyond. Their chief executive, chief information officer, and chief security officer all left. Their reputation took a hit a company built on trust may never fully recover from. And the flaw that caused all of it had a free fix available three days before the break-in. That's the brutal arithmetic of insecurity: the patch was free, and the breach cost the better part of a billion dollars and the careers of the people at the top. When you hear someone argue that security is too expensive, this is the story to remember — the real expense is always on the other side of the breach.
