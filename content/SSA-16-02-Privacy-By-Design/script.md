NARRATOR: Draw the privacy-versus-security line right at the top, because it's the number-one confusion and the marker loves it. Security is protecting data from unauthorised access — keeping the wrong people out. Privacy is the appropriate handling of personal data — what you collect, why, how you use it, how long you keep it, and whether the person agreed. The killer point: you can have perfect security and still violate privacy. Imagine a company that encrypts everything flawlessly, has airtight access control — perfect security — but secretly harvests your location every five seconds, sells it to advertisers, and keeps it forever. Nothing was breached. Security held completely. But privacy was trampled. Security asks "is the data protected from attackers?"; privacy asks "should we even have this data, and are we using it as the person expects?" Never write that privacy is just "keeping data secure." That's the band-four answer.

By the end you'll be able to explain the privacy-by-design approach and its three named principles; explain data minimisation and why it reduces risk; and distinguish privacy from security cleanly. NARRATOR: The core idea. Privacy by design means building privacy into a system from the very start — into its architecture and its defaults — rather than adding it on later as a feature or a checkbox. If that rings a bell, it should: it's exactly security-by-design from episode fourteen-oh-two, the same shift-left philosophy, pointed at personal data instead of attacks. Build it in at the start, where it's cheap and structural, not bolted on at the end where it's expensive and leaky.

NARRATOR: Now the three named principles, and here's your hook, because "name the privacy-by-design principles" is a guaranteed list question. The syllabus names three: Proactive not reactive, Embed privacy into design, and Respect for user privacy. Take the first letters — P, E, R — and you get PER. Privacy by design is PER: Proactive, Embed, Respect. Say it: P-E-R. Three principles, one tiny word. Unpack each so you can say more than the label.

NARRATOR: First, Proactive not reactive. You anticipate privacy risks and prevent them before they happen, rather than reacting after a breach or a complaint. A reactive company waits until data leaks, then scrambles. A proactive company runs a privacy impact assessment — a P-I-A, a structured review of what could go wrong with personal data and how to prevent it — during design, before a single record is collected. Proactive is "prevent the harm"; reactive is "clean up the harm." Same prevention-is-cheaper-than-response logic from the business case in fourteen-oh-one, now about privacy. The exam line: proactive not reactive means anticipating and preventing privacy risks by design, rather than responding to them after they occur.

NARRATOR: Second, Embed privacy into design. Privacy is a core part of the system's architecture and default settings, not an optional add-on. The phrase the marker rewards is privacy as the default. When a user signs up, the privacy-protective option should already be switched on — sharing off, tracking off, visibility limited — so a user who never touches a setting is still protected. Contrast the dark pattern where everything is shared by default and you have to hunt through menus to opt out. Embedded privacy means the safe choice is the default choice — which is exactly "secure default, easy right path" from user-centred design in fourteen-oh-three, now about privacy. The exam line: embed privacy into design means privacy is built into the system architecture and is the default setting, requiring no action from the user to be protected.

NARRATOR: Third, Respect for user privacy. Keep the user's interests central — be transparent about what you collect and why, give genuine control through real consent, and let them see, correct, and delete their data. The keyword is consent — real consent, freely given and specific, not "consent theatre" where a giant pre-ticked box says "I agree to everything" and you can't use the site otherwise. Respect also means honouring data subject rights: the right to access your data, to correct it, and to have it erased. The exam line: respect for user privacy means keeping users informed and in control, through transparency, genuine consent, and rights to access, correct and delete their data.

QUESTION: Quick check. Name the three privacy-by-design principles and give the one-phrase essence of each. Pause, say all three, then play.

NARRATOR: PER. Proactive not reactive — prevent privacy harm before it happens, by design. Embed privacy into design — privacy is built in and on by default, no user action needed. Respect for user privacy — transparency, genuine consent, and the rights to access, correct and delete. If you can rattle off PER and one phrase each, you've got the list question cold.

NARRATOR: Now the practical technique that earns its own marks and underpins all three: data minimisation. Data minimisation means collecting, processing and keeping only the personal data genuinely necessary for the specific purpose — and nothing more. Two halves, one hook: collect less, keep less. Collect less is the moment of collection — don't ask for a date of birth, home address and phone number on a sign-up form for a discussion app that only needs an email. Keep less is retention — don't hoard data forever; set a retention period and delete data when the purpose is done. Collect less, keep less.

NARRATOR: Here's the band-six insight on why minimisation reduces risk, because "why does it reduce risk?" is the real question. The data you never collect cannot be breached, cannot be misused, cannot be subpoenaed, cannot leak. Every extra field and every extra month of retention is more attack surface, more liability, more to protect. So minimisation isn't just polite — it's a direct security control: it shrinks the blast radius of any future breach. Tie it back to the business case from fourteen-oh-one: less data held means a cheaper, smaller breach if one happens. The cleanest exam line: data minimisation reduces risk because data that is never collected or has already been deleted cannot be exposed, misused or stolen — it shrinks the attack surface and the impact of any breach. The trap is the opposite — retaining data "just in case." Data you might want someday is a liability sitting there waiting to leak.

NARRATOR: Two more techniques to name so your answer sounds expert. Purpose limitation: data collected for one purpose may only be used for that purpose — you can't quietly repurpose the email someone gave you for account login into a marketing list. That's "purpose creep," a privacy violation. And data retention policies: a defined schedule for how long each kind of data is kept and when it's automatically deleted — the "keep less" half of minimisation, made systematic. Purpose limitation says use it only for what you said; retention says don't keep it longer than you need.

NARRATOR: One note on the legal layer, because the dot-point's sibling content is "data protection and regulatory compliance," and a name earns credit. The flagship privacy law is the European Union's G-D-P-R, the General Data Protection Regulation, which made privacy by design a legal requirement and gave people rights to access and erase their data. In Australia, the equivalent is the Privacy Act and its Australian Privacy Principles. You don't need chapter and verse — but naming G-D-P-R or the Australian Privacy Principles, and saying privacy by design is now a legal obligation, not just good manners, lifts an answer. We return to the legal and ethical fallout properly in nineteen-oh-three.

QUESTION: This is the one to lock in — pause the player and answer it, because it's a guaranteed marker and the trap is sitting right inside it. A messaging app encrypts all messages end-to-end but also records and stores every user's precise location continuously, forever, and sells it. Has it failed at security, privacy, or both? Explain. Pause now, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the full-mark answer. It has not failed at security — the data is encrypted and protected from unauthorised access, so confidentiality holds; nothing is breached. But it has badly failed at privacy: it collects far more personal data than necessary, violating data minimisation; it uses that data for a purpose the user never agreed to — selling it — violating purpose limitation and respect for user privacy; and it retains the data forever, violating retention limits. The key insight: security and privacy are distinct. Security protects data from unauthorised access; privacy governs whether the data should be collected at all and how it may be used. You can have strong security and still violate privacy. Naming "security holds, privacy fails," and tying the privacy failures to minimisation, purpose limitation and respect, is the band-six answer.

NARRATOR: Worked example — the weak-versus-strong contrast on "explain data minimisation," because hearing the boundary is worth more than another definition. Picture a sign-up form for a simple to-do app that asks for full name, date of birth, home address, phone number, and gender — when it only needs an email and a password. A weak answer says: "data minimisation means don't collect too much data, to protect privacy." Vague — no mechanism, no reasoning about risk. A strong answer says: "data minimisation means collecting only the personal data necessary for the stated purpose; here the to-do app only needs an email and password, so the date of birth, address, phone and gender should not be collected. This reduces risk because data that is never collected cannot be breached, misused or leaked — minimising collection directly shrinks the attack surface and the impact of any future breach." Hear the difference: the weak answer gestures; the strong answer applies the principle to the specific fields, then explains why it reduces risk. Apply it, then justify with the attack-surface reasoning — that's the mark.

QUESTION: Question one, recall. Name the three principles of privacy by design and give the essence of each. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: the three are proactive not reactive — anticipating and preventing privacy risks by design rather than responding after they occur; embed privacy into design — building privacy into the system architecture and making it the default so no user action is needed to be protected; and respect for user privacy — keeping users informed and in control through transparency, genuine consent, and the rights to access, correct and delete their data. Remember them as PER. Naming all three with a correct one-line meaning each is the mark.

QUESTION: Question two. Distinguish between privacy and security. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: security is concerned with protecting data from unauthorised access, modification or disclosure — keeping attackers out. Privacy is concerned with the appropriate handling of personal data — what is collected, why, how it is used, how long it is retained, and whether the individual has consented. They are distinct: a system can have strong security yet still violate privacy by collecting excessive data or using it for purposes the user did not agree to. Naming "security protects access; privacy governs collection and use," and that one can hold while the other fails, is the mark.

QUESTION: Question three. Describe data minimisation and explain why it reduces risk. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: data minimisation is the practice of collecting, processing and retaining only the personal data that is necessary for a specific stated purpose, and no more — collect less, and keep less. It reduces risk because data that is never collected, or that has already been deleted under a retention policy, cannot be breached, misused or stolen; minimisation therefore shrinks the system's attack surface and limits the impact of any breach that does occur. Naming "only what's necessary," both collection and retention, and the "can't lose what you don't hold" reasoning is the mark.

QUESTION: Question four, application. A school portal's sign-up form collects students' full names, home addresses, phone numbers and dates of birth, but the portal only delivers timetables and assignments. Apply privacy by design to redesign this. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: applying data minimisation, collect only what the purpose requires — a name and a school-issued login are needed to deliver timetables and assignments, but the home address, phone number and date of birth are not, so they should not be collected. Apply embed-privacy-into-design by making privacy-protective settings the default and limiting who can view a student's data. Apply respect-for-user-privacy with a clear, honest explanation of what little is collected and why, and a way to access or correct it. Apply proactive-not-reactive by conducting a privacy impact assessment before launch. Set a retention policy so data is deleted when the student leaves. Naming the specific fields to drop, then applying the PER principles and minimisation, is the band-six answer.

QUESTION: Question five, the extended one. Explain the privacy-by-design approach and how it would guide the development of a new health-tracking application. Pause, plan a structured response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer: privacy by design means building privacy into the application from the outset rather than adding it later, and it rests on three principles — proactive not reactive, embed privacy into design, and respect for user privacy. For a health-tracking app, which handles especially sensitive data: proactively conduct a privacy impact assessment during design to identify and prevent risks before any data is collected; embed privacy by making the most protective settings the default — health data shared with no one unless the user explicitly turns sharing on — so the user is protected even if they change nothing; and respect the user through clear, specific, freely given consent for each kind of processing, plus rights to view, export and delete their data. Throughout, apply data minimisation — collect only the health metrics the feature genuinely needs and retain them only as long as necessary, which both respects the user and shrinks the harm of any breach. The approach also helps meet legal obligations under laws such as G-D-P-R or the Australian Privacy Principles. A strong response names the three principles, applies them concretely to the sensitive health context, brings in minimisation and the legal layer, and explicitly contrasts privacy with mere security. That's full marks.
